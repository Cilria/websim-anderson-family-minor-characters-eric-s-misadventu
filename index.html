US, France and UK launch airstrikes against Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel as forces advance toward Abuja

International coalition responds to unprecedented jihadist expansion with coordinated military action as caliphate forces besiege Kaduna, 50km from Nigeria's capital
By Larry Madowo & Stephanie Busari | CNN Africa

Washington DC/Niamey — The United States, France and United Kingdom have launched coordinated airstrikes against Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel targets across West Africa, with Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth announcing that "military operations have begun" against the jihadist group that has seized control of multiple capital cities and is now advancing toward Nigeria's capital, Abuja.

President Trump announced the military action in a series of tweets Wednesday morning, declaring that "America is back and stronger than ever" as US warplanes began striking caliphate positions across Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger. The operation marks the largest US military intervention in Africa since the 1993 Battle of Mogadishu.

"This terrorist group has taken over West Africa and threatens global security," Trump tweeted. "We will crush them quickly and decisively."

US warplanes conduct airstrikes against Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel positions in Mali

Secretary Hegseth, appearing at the Pentagon Wednesday afternoon, confirmed that "military operations have begun" against the caliphate, describing the campaign as "a sustained effort to degrade and ultimately destroy this terrorist organization that threatens not just West Africa, but global security."

"We are working in coordination with our French and British allies to ensure maximum effectiveness," Hegseth said. "This is not a short-term operation. We are committed to seeing this through until the caliphate's ability to project terror beyond West Africa is eliminated."

Coordinated international response

The international military response began early Wednesday morning with French Rafale fighter jets striking caliphate positions around Bamako, while British Typhoon aircraft targeted jihadist infrastructure in northern Mali. US F-35 and F-16 fighters conducted simultaneous strikes across multiple countries.

"This is the most significant multilateral military operation in Africa since the 2011 intervention in Libya," said Dr. Comfort Ero, CEO of the International Crisis Group. "The speed of the international response reflects the unprecedented nature of the threat posed by the caliphate's expansion."

French Defense Minister Sébastien Lecornu announced that France had deployed aircraft carrier Charles de Gaulle to the West African coast, bringing significant naval aviation capability to the operation. "France will not stand idle while a terrorist organization threatens the stability of an entire region," Lecornu said.

British Defense Secretary Ben Wallace confirmed UK participation, stating that "the United Kingdom stands shoulder to shoulder with our American and French allies in confronting this threat to international security."

Caliphate advances continue

Despite the international military response, caliphate forces continued their advance Wednesday, with jihadist fighters besieging Kaduna, a major Nigerian city just 50 kilometers from the capital, Abuja. The city of approximately 1 million people has been under siege since Tuesday night, with residents reporting heavy fighting on the outskirts.

"They are at the gates of the city," said Musa Bello, a civil servant in Kaduna who spoke to CNN via WhatsApp. "The Nigerian military is trying to hold them back, but they keep coming. We can hear the aircraft overhead, but the fighting on the ground continues."

"We are working in coordination with our French and British allies to ensure maximum effectiveness. This is not a short-term operation. We are committed to seeing this through until the caliphate's ability to project terror beyond West Africa is eliminated."

In Ivory Coast, the strategic city of Korhogo fell to caliphate forces Wednesday morning after a 12-hour battle that saw government forces retreat south toward the capital, Yamoussoukro. The capture of Korhogo gives the caliphate control of a major transportation hub that connects northern Ivory Coast to Burkina Faso and Mali.

"The timing of the international strikes, while welcome, may have come too late to prevent the caliphate from achieving its strategic objectives," said Dr. Akinola Olojo, a West Africa security analyst at the University of Lagos. "They already control multiple capital cities and are now threatening the most populous country in Africa."

Global operations launched

Beyond the immediate military strikes in West Africa, US and allied intelligence agencies have launched coordinated operations to disrupt the caliphate's global network, with raids conducted in multiple countries targeting suspected financiers, recruiters, and logistical supporters.

FBI Director Christopher Wray announced Wednesday that US authorities had arrested 23 individuals across 11 states for allegedly providing material support to the caliphate, while the Treasury Department imposed sanctions on 47 individuals and entities accused of facilitating the group's operations.

"This is not just about bombs and bullets," Hegseth said. "We are targeting the entire ecosystem that enables this terrorist organization to function, from its financial networks to its propaganda machinery."

European intelligence agencies conducted simultaneous raids across France, Germany, Belgium, and the Netherlands, arresting dozens of suspected caliphate supporters and seizing weapons, communications equipment, and financial records.

Humanitarian crisis deepens

The military intervention comes as the humanitarian crisis across West Africa continues to worsen, with more than 20 million people now living under caliphate control and facing severe shortages of food, medicine, and other essential supplies.

The United Nations estimates that more than 8 million people have been displaced by the fighting across the seven countries affected, with refugee camps in neighboring states overwhelmed by the influx of people fleeing the violence.

"We are seeing the largest displacement crisis in African history," said Martin Griffiths, the UN's humanitarian chief. "The international military response, while necessary to address the security threat, must be accompanied by a massive humanitarian effort to prevent famine and disease from claiming more lives than the fighting itself."

Next phase uncertain

Despite the dramatic start to the international military campaign, questions remain about the long-term strategy for defeating the caliphate and restoring stability to West Africa. Military analysts warn that airstrikes alone may not be sufficient to dislodge the group from the major cities it controls.

"The caliphate has embedded itself within civilian populations, making traditional military operations extremely complex," said Dr. Comfort Ero. "We may be looking at a prolonged campaign that could last months or even years."

As night fell across West Africa Wednesday, the sound of aircraft could still be heard over multiple cities, while on the ground, caliphate forces continued their advance toward Abuja, raising the possibility that Nigeria's capital could fall within days.

"This is just the beginning," Hegseth said. "The American people should prepare for a sustained effort. We are committed to defeating this threat, but it will require patience, resources, and the support of the international community."

For residents of Kaduna and other cities now in the caliphate's path, the international intervention offers hope, but also fear of what may come next.

"We pray the bombs find their targets and not us," said Amina Yusuf, a mother of three in Kaduna. "But we also know that when elephants fight, it is the grass that suffers."
