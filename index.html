George Wallace III announces Alabama governor bid, immediately dominates polling among state's oldest voters

Son of segregationist governor promises 'traditional Democratic campaign' focused on states' rights
By CNN Staff • Updated 6:23 PM ET, March 11, 2027

MONTGOMERY, Alabama (CNN) — George Wallace III, the 77-year-old son of the late segregationist governor George Wallace, officially announced his candidacy for Alabama governor Monday, immediately shaking up the 2027 race with a commanding lead among voters over 80 years old.

Wallace III, making his first bid for public office, declared his intention to run a "traditional Democratic campaign" focused on states' rights and what he termed "Alabama's right to govern itself without federal interference."

A CNN/Alabama Media Group poll conducted in the 24 hours following Wallace's announcement shows the candidate capturing an remarkable 84% support among male voters over 80, and 78% among women in the same age bracket.

Key Polling Numbers
Men over 80: Wallace III leads with 84% support
Women over 80: Wallace III leads with 78% support
Voters 70-79: Wallace III leads with 61% support
Overall Democratic primary: Wallace III leads with 34% support

"The people of Alabama know my father's name stands for standing up to Washington," Wallace told supporters at his campaign kickoff in Montgomery. "I intend to carry on that legacy of fighting for our state's sovereignty."

The announcement has drawn immediate parallels to his father's controversial tenure, though Wallace III has attempted to distance himself from the most polarizing aspects of George Wallace's legacy while embracing what he calls "the spirit of states' rights advocacy."

"My father was a complex man who loved Alabama," Wallace III said when asked about his father's 1963 "segregation now, segregation tomorrow, segregation forever" speech. "But this campaign is about the future of Alabama, not the past."

Who is George Wallace III?
  • Born December 11, 1949, eldest son of George and Lurleen Wallace
  • Former insurance executive and real estate developer
  • Never previously held elected office
  • Married to wife Patricia for 52 years, has three children
  • Active in various Alabama business associations

Political analysts note that Wallace III's base of support skews heavily toward older voters who remember his father's era, while younger Alabama voters appear less receptive to his message.

"He's essentially cornered the market on voters who remember the original George Wallace administration," said Dr. Sarah Mitchell, political science professor at the University of Alabama. "But among voters under 50, he's polling in single digits."

"The Wallace name still carries significant weight among older Alabamians who view his father through the lens of economic populism and resistance to federal authority, rather than the racial politics that defined his national reputation."

— Dr. Sarah Mitchell, University of Alabama

The Democratic primary field currently includes five candidates, with Wallace III leading despite his late entry. His closest competitor, State Senator Maria Rodriguez, trails by 12 points overall but leads among voters under 40 by a 3-to-1 margin.

Wallace III's campaign platform emphasizes reducing federal oversight of state matters, strengthening Alabama's business climate, and what he terms "protecting Alabama values from Washington interference."

"We don't need Washington telling us how to run our schools, our businesses, or our lives," Wallace declared to applause from supporters, many of whom appeared to be over 70.

The campaign has drawn mixed reactions from Alabama Democrats. Some see Wallace III as a potential unifying figure who could appeal to conservative-leaning older voters, while others worry about the national implications of another Wallace candidacy.

"Alabama Democrats are trying to move forward, not backward," said State Democratic Party Chairman Thomas Booker. "While we respect Mr. Wallace's right to run, we hope this campaign focuses on Alabama's future, not its past."

Wallace III faces several challenges beyond his age demographic advantage. He lacks political experience, has limited name recognition among younger voters, and must navigate the complex legacy of his father's administration.

His campaign strategy appears focused on maximizing turnout among older voters while hoping economic populism resonates beyond his base. Recent polling suggests his message may have limited appeal among Alabama's growing suburban electorate.

The Democratic primary is scheduled for June 2027, with the general election following in November. Current Governor Bill Johnson, a Republican, is term-limited and cannot seek re-election.

Political observers note that Alabama's shifting demographics and political landscape may present challenges for a Wallace-style candidacy, even as the name recognition provides significant advantages in a crowded primary field.
