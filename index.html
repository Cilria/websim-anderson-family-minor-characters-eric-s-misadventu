Oil price crash sends Saudi Arabia into economic free fall as prices hit historic lows

Riyadh, Saudi Arabia (CNN) — Oil prices have collapsed to unprecedented lows, triggering a severe economic crisis in Saudi Arabia that experts are calling the worst since the Great Depression. Brent crude fell to $7.42 per barrel Monday, the lowest price in modern history, sending shockwaves through global markets and devastating the kingdom's oil-dependent economy.

The crash has pushed Saudi Arabia's inflation rate to a staggering 900%, according to data released by the Saudi central bank. The kingdom's sovereign wealth fund has reportedly lost more than $400 billion in value over the past month as oil revenues, which typically account for over 80% of government income, have evaporated.

Key context: This represents the most severe oil price collapse since records began in the 1860s, surpassing even the 2020 negative price event.

"This is an economic earthquake," said Omar Al-Rashid, former deputy governor of the Saudi Arabian Monetary Authority. "The kingdom has never faced such a dramatic collapse in its primary revenue source. We're looking at complete economic devastation."

The crisis has sparked widespread protests across major Saudi cities, including Riyadh and Jeddah. Initially sparked by soaring prices for basic goods, the demonstrations have grown increasingly tense as citizens express frustration over the government's handling of the economic crisis.

"We can't afford bread. We can't afford electricity. The government promised us stability and prosperity, but they've delivered nothing except empty promises."
— Ahmed Al-Saud, protester in Riyadh

Videos shared on social media show thousands of protesters gathering in central Riyadh, chanting slogans against government policies and demanding economic reforms. Saudi security forces have been deployed in large numbers, though protests have remained largely peaceful thus far.

The Saudi government has announced emergency measures, including price caps on essential goods and direct cash transfers to citizens. However, with inflation running at 900%, these measures appear insufficient to address the scale of the crisis.

"The kingdom's entire economic model is built on oil revenue," explained Sarah Mitchell, Middle East analyst at Oxford Economics. "When that disappears overnight, there's simply no easy solution. We're looking at a prolonged economic depression that could last years."

The crisis has also triggered concerns about regional stability, with fears that economic collapse could lead to broader political unrest across the Gulf region.
