Persian social media users spark regional outrage with Iraq memes

Iranian internet users joke about "rightful Persian territory" prompting furious Arab response and religious condemnation

10 July 2027 • By Jasmine Rahmani, BBC Persian Service • Beirut

A seemingly innocuous social media trend started by young Persian users has ignited a firestorm of controversy across the Middle East, with Arab ultranationalists and religious leaders denouncing what they call "Persian imperialist sentiment" and "betrayal of the Islamic world."

The controversy began last week when Persian-language social media users began sharing memes suggesting that Iraq would be better off as part of a "Greater Persian Empire." The posts, which were clearly intended as humorous rather than serious political statements, featured tongue-in-cheek arguments such as "better food," "prettier borders," and "access to proper saffron rice."

"You will get better food, the borders will look nicer, and we can finally agree on proper tea preparation methods"

What started as a niche internet joke quickly spiraled into a regional diplomatic incident. Within 48 hours, the hashtag #PersianImperialism began trending across Arab social media platforms, with users sharing increasingly inflammatory responses and calling for boycotts of Iranian goods.

The situation escalated dramatically when Sheikh Ahmed al-Rashidi, a prominent Imam in Baghdad's Sadr City district, delivered a fiery Friday sermon condemning the memes. "These so-called Persians are traitors to the Islamic world and to Allah himself," he declared to thousands of worshippers. "They seek to divide the ummah with their ancient grudges and imperial ambitions."

[Image: Screenshot collage of viral Persian memes about Iraq]

From jokes to jihad

The BBC has reviewed dozens of the original Persian posts and found them to be clearly satirical in nature. One popular meme showed a map of the Middle East with Iraq colored in Persian green, captioned: "Look how much cleaner the borders would be. Also, Baghdad gets proper kebabs."

However, the humor was lost in translation as screenshots circulated through Arab social media networks, stripped of their original context and presented as evidence of Iranian expansionist ambitions.

Dr. Layla al-Mansouri, a professor of Middle Eastern studies at the University of Baghdad, explains the sensitivity: "The idea of Persian territorial claims over Iraq taps into very deep historical wounds. The Arab-Persian rivalry is over a millennium old, and memories of Persian empires stretching into Arab lands are still very much alive in the collective consciousness."

By Tuesday, the backlash had spread beyond social media. Small protests erupted in several Iraqi cities, with demonstrators burning Iranian flags and chanting anti-Persian slogans. The Iraqi government, caught between maintaining relations with its powerful neighbor and appeasing domestic sentiment, issued a carefully worded statement calling for "mutual respect between brotherly nations."

"These so-called Persians are traitors to the Islamic world and to Allah himself"

Tehran's response

The Iranian government's response has been notably restrained. Foreign Ministry spokesperson Sara Mousavi issued a brief statement calling for "understanding and brotherhood between Muslim peoples" while noting that "social media posts do not represent official policy."

However, the controversy has revealed fault lines within Iranian society itself. While the original memes were created by Persian users, Iranian Arabs, Kurds, and other ethnic minorities have used the opportunity to highlight their own grievances with what they see as Persian cultural dominance within Iran.

Ahwazi Arab activists have seized on the moment to draw attention to discrimination in Iran's Khuzestan province, while Iranian Kurds have pointed to the irony of Persians joking about territorial expansion while Kurdish regions remain marginalized.

The digital divide

The incident highlights the growing power of social media to inflame regional tensions in an already volatile part of the world. What might have been dismissed as harmless banter a decade ago now has the potential to spark real-world consequences.

Mohammed al-Saadi, a social media analyst based in Dubai, notes: "The speed at which this escalated from internet jokes to street protests shows how thin the line is between online discourse and real-world tensions. Young Iranians making memes don't realize they're playing with matches in a fireworks factory."

The controversy comes at a particularly sensitive time, with regional tensions already high following recent disputes over water rights, religious pilgrimage routes, and competing influence in post-war Syria and Lebanon.

Some Persian social media users have since deleted their original posts and issued apologies, claiming they never intended to cause offense. Others have doubled down, arguing that Arabs are being overly sensitive about what was clearly intended as humor.

Regional implications

The incident has prompted calls for greater digital literacy and cross-cultural understanding in the region. Several NGOs have proposed initiatives to promote dialogue between young Iranians and Arabs, though such efforts face significant hurdles given the current political climate.

Meanwhile, the memes continue to circulate, now weaponized by various political actors for their own purposes. Iranian opposition groups have used them to criticize Tehran's regional policies, while Arab nationalist movements have pointed to them as evidence of Persian cultural arrogance.

As the situation continues to evolve, the episode serves as a reminder that in the digital age, a joke made in Tehran can spark protests in Baghdad, and that ancient rivalries die hard in the modern Middle East.

Additional reporting by: Ahmed Hassan in Baghdad, Zahra Karimi in Tehran, and the BBC Monitoring team.

Why this matters

The Persian-Arab rivalry spans over 1,400 years, dating back to the early Islamic conquests. Modern tensions often flare over cultural, religious, and political differences.

Iraq's large Shia population and its proximity to Iran make it particularly sensitive to perceived Persian influence, whether real or imagined.
