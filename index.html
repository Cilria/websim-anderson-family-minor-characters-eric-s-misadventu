Gulf Airlines Race to Secure Airbus's New Large Aircraft as Emirates Claims Launch Customer Status

Emirates and Singapore Airlines position themselves as early adopters of next-generation aircraft; Airbus accelerates development timeline amid unprecedented demand

By David Gelles and Michael J. de la Merced | July 11, 2028

DUBAI - Emirates has formally declared itself the launch customer for Airbus's New Large Aircraft, intensifying competition among Gulf carriers to secure early delivery slots for the European planemaker's most ambitious commercial project since the A380, according to people familiar with the matter.

The Dubai-based carrier's announcement comes as Singapore Airlines simultaneously positioned itself as the launch customer for the A350neo, creating a two-pronged endorsement of Airbus's next-generation aircraft programs that industry analysts say could reshape long-haul aviation for the next two decades.

Airbus has responded to the surge of interest by accelerating development of both programs, with the A350neo's development timeline compressed by nearly six months to meet what executives described as "unprecedented demand" from airlines worldwide, according to people briefed on the company's plans.

"The level of interest from airlines has exceeded our most optimistic projections," said a senior Airbus executive who requested anonymity because the discussions are private. "We are essentially sold out of launch customer positions for both programs through 2035."

The New Large Aircraft, which has been internally designated as the NLA-1000, represents Airbus's reentry into the ultra-high-capacity market segment abandoned with the A380's discontinuation. The aircraft will feature a single-aisle configuration despite its massive scale, accommodating between 465 and 500 passengers in a three-class layout, making it larger than Boeing's 777-10, currently the largest variant of the 777X family.

Technical specifications obtained by The Times reveal the NLA-1000 will utilize the same Rolls-Royce Ultrafan engines selected for the A350neo, though configured with additional thrust to accommodate the aircraft's maximum takeoff weight of approximately 380 tons. The aircraft will have a range of 7,800 nautical miles, enabling nonstop service on routes such as Dubai-Los Angeles and Singapore-Newark.

Emirates' interest in the NLA program reflects its strategy to replace its aging A380 fleet while maintaining capacity on high-density routes connecting Dubai with London, Sydney, and New York. The carrier operates 116 A380s, more than half of the global fleet, and has been pressing Airbus for a true A380 replacement since the superjumbo's production ended in 2021.

"Emirates sees the NLA as the natural successor to the A380 for our hub operations," said a person familiar with the carrier's thinking. "The economics are compelling - 15% lower fuel burn per seat than the A380 with similar passenger capacity on our core routes."

The competition for launch customer status has been intense, with Qatar Airways and Etihad Airways also vying for early delivery positions. However, Emirates' willingness to commit to 50 aircraft with options for an additional 50 has effectively secured its position as the program's anchor customer, according to industry analysts.

Meanwhile, Singapore Airlines' selection as A350neo launch customer represents a strategic victory for Airbus in the Asia-Pacific market, where the carrier has built its reputation on operating the most advanced long-haul aircraft. The Singapore flag carrier was the A380's launch customer in 2007 and has maintained close relationships with Airbus throughout multiple aircraft programs.

"Singapore Airlines wants to maintain its position as the premium long-haul carrier in Asia," said Brendan Sobie, an independent aviation analyst based in Singapore. "The A350neo's improved economics will be crucial for maintaining profitability on ultra-long-haul routes as fuel prices remain volatile."

The accelerated A350neo development timeline reflects Airbus's confidence in the program's technical maturity and market demand. The aircraft will enter service in 2033, six months earlier than previously announced, with production rates ramping up to 10 aircraft per month by 2035, according to people familiar with the production plans.

"We are witnessing a fundamental shift in long-haul aviation economics," said Richard Aboulafia, managing director at AeroDynamic Advisory. "These next-generation aircraft will make routes that are currently marginal suddenly viable, and make profitable routes even more lucrative."

The NLA program's single-aisle configuration represents a departure from traditional wide-body designs, utilizing advanced composite materials and aerodynamic innovations to achieve efficiency gains while maintaining passenger comfort. The aircraft will feature a 3-4-3 economy class layout, similar to the A380's main deck, but with wider seats made possible by the single-aisle design's optimized fuselage cross-section.

Rolls-Royce's Ultrafan engine, which will power both the A350neo and NLA-1000, has completed more than 2,000 hours of ground testing and is on track for certification in 2029. The engine's geared turbofan design delivers a 10-15% improvement in fuel efficiency compared with current generation powerplants, according to Rolls-Royce executives.

Industry analysts estimate the combined market for the A350neo and NLA-1000 at approximately 3,000 aircraft over the next 20 years, representing potential revenue of $500 billion for Airbus and its suppliers. The programs are expected to create more than 50,000 jobs across Europe and the United States.

Airbus is expected to formally launch both programs at the Farnborough International Airshow next week, with announcement ceremonies planned for Tuesday morning. The company has scheduled investor briefings for Monday evening to discuss the programs' financial implications.

The timing of the launches reflects Airbus's confidence in the commercial aviation market's recovery from the pandemic-induced downturn. Global passenger traffic is expected to return to 2019 levels by late 2028, with long-haul travel recovering even faster as business travel resumes and leisure travelers seek more distant destinations.

For Emirates and Singapore Airlines, securing launch customer status provides competitive advantages in terms of delivery timing, pricing, and customization options. Launch customers typically receive aircraft 6-12 months earlier than subsequent customers and benefit from preferential pricing and support terms.

The race to secure next-generation aircraft positions represents a strategic shift in airline competition, with carriers betting that fuel efficiency improvements and passenger capacity optimization will determine profitability in the coming decade. As one airline executive put it: "In this business, being first with the right aircraft can be worth billions in revenue advantage."
