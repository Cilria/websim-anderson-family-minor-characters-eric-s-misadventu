COL Robert Harland Jemison's Ancestors: The Jemison, Harland, and Johnson families of Alabama

Alabama Civil War Plantation History Banking Families Posted by u/Alabama_Archivist • 8 hours ago

After my deep dive into Colonel Jemison's military career, I'm now researching his family background. Born 1884 in Bibb County, Alabama, but I need to go back further. What I've found so far:

Parents: William Thomas Jemison (1855-1918) and Mary Elizabeth "Molly" Harland (1858-1934). The Harland family had Harland Creek named after them in Bibb County - they were that established.

Marriage: Lillian Mae Johnson (1882-1954), daughter of John William Johnson, a banker in San Antonio. This banking connection explains his later board appointments.

What I'm Missing: William Thomas Jemison's parents and siblings - I can't find them in 1860/1870 census. Also need to trace the Harland family back further than just "they had a creek named after them." Plus the Johnson banking family of San Antonio - how did a rural Alabama boy meet a Texas banker's daughter in 1908?

Looking for anyone connected to these Alabama families or familiar with pre-Civil War plantation records in Bibb County.

Found William Thomas Jemison's parents! Thomas Jefferson Jemison (1830-1905) and Sarah Ann "Sally" Wilson (1832-1899). Thomas was a substantial planter in Bibb County - 1860 slave schedule shows he owned 47 enslaved people and 1,200 acres. The family plantation was called "Oak Grove," located on the Cahaba River about 5 miles from Centreville. After the Civil War, he diversified into lumber and survived Reconstruction by selling timber rights.

u/Bibb_County_Historian • 5 hours ago
Oak Grove was a working plantation until 1875. The main house burned in 1868, and Thomas rebuilt a smaller house using lumber from his own sawmill. Family papers at Alabama Department of Archives show he sold 800 acres to pay debts, kept 400 acres for farming. The transition was brutal - went from being worth $50,000 in 1860 to $8,000 in 1870. But he adapted: bought a steam-powered sawmill in 1872 and made a fortune selling crossties to the railroad.

🏅 u/Harland_Family_Genealogist • 5 hours ago
The Harland family goes way back! Mary Elizabeth Harland's father was Colonel James Harland (1812-1887) - yes, another Colonel! He commanded the 42nd Alabama Infantry (CSA) during the Civil War. Before that, he was a state legislator and major landowner. His father was Reverend Thomas Harland (1778-1855), a Methodist circuit rider who settled Bibb County in 1819. Harland Creek was indeed named after them - they owned all the land around it. The family came from South Carolina originally, part of the migration that opened up Alabama's Black Belt.

u/Harland_Family_Genealogist • 4 hours ago
Colonel James Harland fought at Shiloh, Chickamauga, and was wounded at Franklin in 1864. His regiment was part of Clayton's Brigade, famous for holding the line at Chickamauga. After the war, he became a leader in the "Lost Cause" movement, helping organize Confederate veteran reunions. His military bearing and leadership clearly influenced his grandson Robert - the military tradition skipped a generation (William Thomas was too young for Civil War, too old for Spanish-American War).

Found the Johnson banking connection! John William Johnson (1855-1928) founded the San Antonio National Bank in 1893. His daughter Lillian Mae was actually born in Alabama - the family moved to Texas in 1890 when she was 8. They probably met at family gatherings before Robert enlisted in 1902, then reconnected when he was stationed at Fort Sam Houston in 1910.

The bank survived the Panic of 1907 and became First National Bank of San Antonio in 1922. u/Jemison_Siblings_Research • 3 hours ago
William Thomas Jemison had 5 siblings:
1. James Wilson Jemison (1857-1933) - married Martha Johnson (Lillian's aunt)
2. Mary Ann Jemison (1859-1941) - married into the prominent Moore family
3. Sarah Elizabeth Jemison (1861-1920) - married a doctor, moved to Mobile
4. Martha Louise Jemison (1863-1935) - never married, became a schoolteacher
5. Thomas Jefferson Jemison Jr. (1865-1944) - took over the lumber business

The family was close-knit - they all stayed in Bibb County and intermarried with other planter families. The Johnson-Jemison marriage that created the cousin connection was typical of how these families maintained wealth and status.

u/Plantation_Daily_Life • 2 hours ago
Found details about Oak Grove's daily operations: The 1850s plantation records show Thomas J. Jemison grew cotton, corn, and raised livestock. The 47 enslaved people worked in gangs - field hands, house servants, and skilled craftsmen. He had a blacksmith shop, grist mill, and cotton gin on the property. After emancipation, many freedmen stayed as sharecroppers. The 1880 census shows several Black families with the surname Jemison - likely former slaves who took the family name.

u/Civil_War_Pensions • 1 hour ago
Colonel James Harland's Civil War pension file reveals: He applied for a Confederate pension in 1888, claiming "wounds received at Franklin and chronic rheumatism from exposure." He received $8/month - substantial for the time. The file includes affidavits from his men describing his leadership at Chickamauga: "Colonel Harland led us through hell, his voice steady as iron while bullets flew like hail." His men clearly loved him - many named sons after him.

Lillian's mother was interesting: Her mother was Mary Elizabeth "Molly" Crawford (1860-1935), from a prominent Alabama family before moving to Texas. The Crawfords were iron manufacturers in Birmingham - that's how the banking fortune started. John Johnson married into iron money, then built banking money. Molly Crawford's brother was Judge William Crawford, who became president of First Alabama Bank in 1920 - explaining the bank board connection that helped Robert later.
