South Carolina state senator faces bipartisan calls to resign after racist, sexist texts surface

By Jamal Richardson, Sarah Mitchell and Antonio Rodriguez July 10, 2026

Columbia, South Carolina (CNN) — South Carolina State Senator Earl LeRoy Carter Sr., a Republican who has represented rural Laurens County for 22 years, is facing mounting bipartisan pressure to resign after text messages surfaced in which he openly advocated for the return of slavery and called for stripping women of voting rights.

The messages, obtained by CNN and first reported by the Greenville News, show Carter texting with an unidentified recipient about what he called "the good old days" before making a series of inflammatory statements that have sparked immediate national outrage.

The Text Messages

"Slavery was the best thing that ever happened to this country. At least those people knew their place and contributed to society. We need to bring it back."

In another exchange, Carter wrote: "Women should be stripped of the right to vote. The Nineteenth Amendment was the greatest mistake America has ever made. They've destroyed this country with their emotional voting."

When questioned about the messages by the recipient, who appears to have expressed concern about the statements, Carter responded: "I stand by every word I said. Someone needs to speak the truth about what's destroying this country."

The messages were provided to CNN by a source close to Carter who said they were "shocked and disgusted" by the content. CNN has authenticated the messages through digital forensics and confirmed their authenticity with multiple sources familiar with Carter's communications.

"These comments are not just offensive - they're dangerous. In 2026, we should not have elected officials openly advocating for slavery and the disenfranchisement of half the population." — South Carolina Democratic Party Chair Christale Spain

The backlash has been swift and severe. Within hours of the story breaking, Republican Governor Henry McMaster called Carter's comments "abhorrent and completely unacceptable," while Senate Majority Leader Shane Massey, also a Republican, said Carter should "immediately resign and spare the Senate and our state further embarrassment."

The National Republican Senatorial Committee issued a statement calling Carter's remarks "reprehensible and completely incompatible with Republican values," though they stopped short of calling for his resignation from the state senate.

Carter, 74, has been a fixture in South Carolina politics since his first election in 2004. He represents District 4, which includes all of Laurens County and parts of neighboring Newberry and Union counties. The district is heavily Republican, with Carter winning reelection in 2024 with 68% of the vote.

Throughout his tenure, Carter has been known as a staunch conservative who frequently opposed expansion of voting rights and supported strict immigration enforcement. However, his private text messages reveal views that go far beyond his public positions.

Political Fallout

Multiple Republican donors have already withdrawn support, and the state party is reportedly considering formal censure proceedings.

The controversy has quickly entered the national political discourse, with Democratic leaders seizing on the messages as evidence of what they call "the extreme views hiding within the Republican Party."

President Kamala Harris, speaking at an event in Atlanta, said Carter's comments represent "a dangerous ideology that seeks to turn back the clock on fundamental American rights and values." She called on Republican leaders to "not just condemn these words but take concrete action to show they reject this ideology."

The White House issued a statement calling the messages "a stark reminder that the fight for equality and justice is never truly over" and praising "the bipartisan voices who have stood up to reject this hateful rhetoric."

Republican leaders, meanwhile, have been scrambling to distance themselves from Carter. Senate Minority Leader Mitch McConnell called the comments "disgusting and completely beyond the pale," while House Speaker Mike Johnson said they "have no place in the Republican Party or American politics."

However, some Democrats argue the response has been insufficient. "Where is the full-throated condemnation from every elected Republican?" asked Democratic National Committee Chair Jaime Harrison, a South Carolina native. "This isn't just about one senator - it's about whether the Republican Party will tolerate officials who openly advocate for slavery and stripping women of voting rights."

"I've known Earl for 20 years. These messages don't surprise me - he's always had extreme views, but we thought they were just personal opinions. This is who he really is." — Anonymous Republican state senator

The controversy comes at a particularly sensitive time for South Carolina Republicans, who are already facing scrutiny over voting rights issues and racial gerrymandering. The state is also hosting several competitive congressional races in 2026, and Democratic strategists believe the scandal could energize their base.

"This isn't just about one senator's offensive texts," said Harrison. "This is about a pattern of extremism that has been tolerated and even encouraged in certain corners of the Republican Party. The people of South Carolina deserve better."

Carter's office has not responded to multiple requests for comment, though sources close to the senator say he is "considering his options" and may address the controversy publicly in the coming days.

Under South Carolina law, state senators can only be removed through resignation, expulsion by a two-thirds vote of the Senate, or recall election. With Republicans holding a 30-16 majority in the state Senate, expulsion would require significant Republican support.

The controversy has already sparked discussions about potential primary challenges to Carter in 2028, with several local Republicans reportedly considering runs. However, given the district's heavy Republican tilt, any primary winner would be heavily favored in the general election.

Political analysts say the scandal could have implications beyond South Carolina, potentially affecting suburban voters in competitive districts nationwide who may be turned off by association with such extreme views.

"This reinforces every negative stereotype about the Republican Party being the party of white male grievance," said Republican strategist Sarah Longwell. "It's going to be used in attack ads from now until November, and it's going to hurt candidates who had nothing to do with this."

The incident also raises questions about how such extreme views could remain hidden for so long in an elected official. Carter has served for 22 years, raising concerns about what other officials might be harboring similar private views.

"The real scandal isn't just what Carter said - it's that he felt comfortable saying it at all," said Democratic strategist Antjuan Seawright. "That speaks to a culture where these views are more common than we want to admit."

As the controversy continues to unfold, pressure is mounting on Carter to step aside. Whether he will choose to fight for his political survival or spare his party further damage remains to be seen, but one thing is clear: this scandal has already become a defining moment in South Carolina politics and a national flashpoint in the ongoing battle over America's racial and gender history.
