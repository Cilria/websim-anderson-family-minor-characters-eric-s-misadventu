BREAKING: Russia collapses into warlord era as central government disappears

Russia fractures into thousands of micro-states as power vacuum creates 'every town for itself' scenario

By Clarissa Ward, CNN Senior International Correspondent
Moscow • Updated 11:47 AM ET, Thu October 19, 2028

Moscow (CNN) — Russia has descended into complete chaos as the central government has effectively ceased to exist, leaving a power vacuum that has seen every town, village, and city declare itself an independent "state" in what experts are calling the most dramatic state collapse in modern history.

Communications from Russia have gone completely dark. No official statements have emerged from the Kremlin in 72 hours. State television broadcasts static. The internet backbone connecting Russia to the outside world has been severed at multiple points. The military chain of command has dissolved, with generals reportedly seizing territory and declaring themselves rulers of their own fiefdoms.

LIVE UPDATES: Satellite imagery shows tanks positioned at town borders throughout Russia. Reports of fighting between neighboring "republics" emerging from multiple regions.

"We're witnessing something unprecedented in the nuclear age," says Dr. Sarah Chen, Russia expert at the Council on Foreign Relations. "Every single administrative division - from major cities like Novosibirsk to tiny villages with populations under 100 - has declared sovereignty. The mayor of a town with 50 people has declared himself Supreme Leader of the People's Republic of [Town Name]."

The speed of the collapse has stunned international observers. What began as regional disputes over resource allocation and political appointments has cascaded into complete anarchy. Local officials, military commanders, police chiefs, and even former mayors have seized the opportunity to stake territorial claims, often using absurd titles like "Grand Duke of the Parking Lot Republic" or "Supreme Commander of the Three-Block Autonomous Zone."

"We have reports of a gas station owner who has declared his fuel station an independent country and is charging 'import duties' on anyone crossing his property. Another man has claimed sovereignty over his apartment building and is issuing his own currency."
— Ambassador James Morrison, U.S. State Department briefing

The humanitarian implications are staggering. With no central authority, basic services have collapsed. Food distribution networks have broken down. Hospitals are operating independently, with medical supplies controlled by whatever local strongman controls the area. International aid organizations report being unable to enter the country due to the lack of any central authority to negotiate with.

"It's like someone took a map of Russia and shattered it into a thousand pieces," says CNN's Moscow bureau chief. "Every piece is controlled by someone different, and they're all fighting over borders that didn't exist last week."

The international community remains paralyzed by the speed of events. Emergency UN Security Council sessions have yielded no coordinated response, as member states struggle to determine who, if anyone, speaks for Russia. The nuclear arsenal remains a critical concern, with reports indicating that individual military commanders now control access to various nuclear facilities across the country.

As night falls across the fractured remains of what was once the world's largest country by landmass, the situation appears to be deteriorating further. Amateur radio operators report hearing chaotic transmissions from within Russia - some claiming to represent legitimate governments, others simply broadcasting demands for food or medical supplies.

The world watches and waits, as the nuclear superpower once led from Moscow becomes a patchwork of thousands of tiny, squabbling territories, each claiming to be the rightful heir to the Russian Federation.
