HOLY SHIT - I found the actual business registration! "Rains Brothers Automobile Co." was officially incorporated January 15, 1939. Business license #B-4721, registered at 2420 E Magnolia Ave, Knoxville. Here's the kicker - their original business model was "used cars, tractors, and farm implements." They pivoted to just cars during WW2.

Found their 1939 Yellow Pages ad - they're listed as "Rains Bros Auto - Quality Used Cars - We Finance!" with a phone number that's literally "4-5678" (old 4-digit system).

Also found Thomas's 1933 manager position - he was managing "Magnolia Motors" at 2420 E Magnolia (same location!) before buying it out and rebranding. The previous owner, Harold Jenkins, sold it to them during the Depression for $1,500 cash plus assumption of debt.

WhiteOakHistorian • 234 points • 4 hours ago

Found their father's farm! Richard Port Rains owned 180 acres in White Oak - specifically the old Rains Homestead on what is now Rains Road (yes, named after them). Property records show he bought it in 1878 for $200 cash. The 1900 census lists him as 63 years old with 9 living children (Annie had 12 total, 3 died in infancy).

The family was NOT poor - they had 8 horses, 40 head of cattle, and grew tobacco and corn. Richard was known as "Old Man Rains" and was apparently quite the character - local newspaper from 1898 mentions he was fined $5 for "public drunkenness and disturbing the peace at the White Oak General Store."

Thomas's mother Annie Susan Brook was from a prominent local family - her father owned the Brook General Store in White Oak. She died March 3, 1911 from "childbed fever" after giving birth to Earl LeRoy. The baby survived but was raised by his older sisters.

OakRidgeWorker • 187 points • 4 hours ago

Found their plumbing work! Thomas and Frank worked for "Oak Ridge Construction Company" from May 1925 to March 1926. They were part of the original workforce that built the town of Oak Ridge - before the Manhattan Project. Pay records show they made $2.50/day as plumbers, which was damn good money back then.

Here's the crazy part - they left Oak Ridge in March 1926 because they got into a fistfight with their boss, Clarence Mitchell. Police report filed March 15, 1926: "Subject Thomas Rains struck foreman Mitchell over payment dispute. Both parties agree to separate employment." They moved to Knoxville the next week.

Thomas's first car job was actually at "Knoxville Auto Exchange" on Gay Street, where he started as a "lot boy" washing cars for $12/week in April 1926. Worked his way up to salesman by 1928.

FordHistorian • 156 points • 3 hours ago

I found the actual dealership photos! The Tennessee State Archives has a collection of 1950s Knoxville business photos, including several of Thomas's dealerships:

2420 E Magnolia (original location): 1952 photo shows a white brick building with "Rains Automobile Co." in blue cursive letters. The lot had about 40 cars, mostly 1946-1952 Fords and Chevys. Thomas is in the photo - heavyset man in a white shirt and suspenders, standing next to a 1951 Ford Custom.

Western Knoxville location: Opened 1954 at 5600 Kingston Pike. Photos show a modern (for 1954) glass-front showroom with "Rains Ford" in chrome letters. They sold new Fords exclusively here.

Maryville location: 1956 photo shows a smaller lot at 412 W Broadway, with "Rains Brothers Used Cars" sign. This location specialized in "executive driven" used cars.

The "Tom and Charles Ford" location was actually a rebranding of the Western Knoxville dealership in 1955 when Charles joined. Thomas kept the E Magnolia location as "Rains Used Cars" until 1970.

ChryslerHistorian • 134 points • 3 hours ago

Found the actual reason for his Chrysler comment! Thomas became the exclusive Chrysler-Plymouth dealer for East Knoxville in 1956. By 1970, Chrysler quality had tanked - the 1970 Plymouth Fury was a notorious lemon.

Sales records from the Tennessee DMV show his Chrysler sales:

  • 1957: 156 new Chryslers sold
  • 1965: 89 new Chryslers sold
  • 1970: 23 new Chryslers sold
  • 1975: 7 new Chryslers sold

His exact quote from a 1976 interview with the Knoxville News-Sentinel: "Nobody wants to buy a damn Chrysler anymore. Used to sell about 20 of those a month, now I'm lucky to sell one or two. These new cars are junk compared to the old ones."

He dropped Chrysler entirely in 1976 and switched to selling AMC (American Motors) until retirement. The "nobody wants to buy a fucking Chrysler anymore" was apparently said at a family dinner and became legendary family lore.

FreemasonRecords • 98 points • 2 hours ago

Found his Freemason records! Thomas was initiated into Knoxville Lodge #756 on March 12, 1934. He became Worshipful Master in 1942. After retirement, he wrote extensively for the Tennessee Freemason newsletter - I found 47 articles he authored between 1977-2002.

His most famous article was "The Automobile and American Freedom" published in 1980, where he argued that car ownership was the greatest liberator of the American middle class. The Grand Lodge of Tennessee gave him a lifetime achievement award in 1997.

He died February 11, 2004 (not November 11 as listed) at St. Mary's Hospital, Knoxville. His obituary ran in the Knoxville News-Sentinel and mentioned he was "the oldest living 32nd degree Mason in Tennessee." Buried at Highland Memorial Cemetery with full Masonic rites.
