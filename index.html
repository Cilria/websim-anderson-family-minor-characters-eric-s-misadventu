So I've been digging into my great-grandfather Edward Lee Southworth Sr. and man, this guy was something else. He had that Mt. Olive, North Carolina accent 'til the day he died, even after living all over the country. And apparently he was quite the looker - I've seen photos of him at 80 and he still had it!

From what family members tell me, when he was single, women just flocked to him. My grandma says he had this charm about him - that country boy smile mixed with city smarts. The man knew everybody who was anybody too - always name-dropping at family dinners about his "good friend so-and-so."

Here's what I've got so far:

  • Born 1892 in Mt. Olive, NC - daddy was a stonemason
  • WWI vet, came back as "Lieutenant Edward" (refused to drop the title)
  • Worked advertising from the 1920s through 1970 - saw radio go to TV
  • Flew his own plane 'til he was 87 years old
  • Married to Betty for 53 years, had 3 kids
  • Died 1981 at 89, still sharp as a tack

Anyone got stories about this guy? He seems like he was quite the character!

MacArthur researcher • 445 points • 6 hours ago

Found the MacArthur connection! Edward's military service record shows he actually did meet Douglas MacArthur - but not how you'd think.

September 1945, Edward was part of the honor guard when MacArthur arrived in Japan. Edward was there as a Civil Air Patrol liaison. According to Edward's diary (which is in the NC State Archives), MacArthur asked him where he was from, and Edward replied "Mt. Olive, North Carolina, sir."

MacArthur apparently laughed and said "Well I'll be damned, I'm from Little Rock. We're both just country boys who made good, aren't we?" Edward wrote that MacArthur "talked like he was trying to sound important, but I could tell he was just a farm boy like me underneath all that gold braid."

They played poker that night with some other officers. Edward wrote: "The General plays a terrible hand of poker - tries to bluff too much. I took him for forty dollars and he laughed about it. Said 'Lieutenant, you sure you weren't a professional gambler before the war?' I told him 'No sir, just learned to read faces selling advertising. When a man's trying to sell you something he doesn't believe in, his eyes give him away every time.'"

So yeah, they really did play poker together! Edward just left out that it was during the occupation of Japan, not back in the States.

Found the Disney photo! It's legit - Edward was at Disneyland on July 18, 1955, three days after the park opened. He was there representing the Raleigh Chamber of Commerce, trying to convince Disney to build an "East Coast Disneyland" in North Carolina (obviously didn't work out).

According to the Anaheim Register newspaper from July 19, 1955, Edward was quoted saying: "Mr. Disney's got something here that'll change America. Back home we call this kind of magic 'pure Carolina moonshine' - except Walt's version is family-friendly!"

The photo shows Edward in his trademark pressed khakis, that silver hair, and that million-dollar smile. Walt's got his arm around Edward's shoulder like they're old friends. Edward's got this look like "Can you believe this country boy from Mt. Olive is standing here with Walt Disney?"

He also met Ronald Reagan that same day (Reagan was covering the opening for TV). Edward's diary says: "Nice fellow, that Reagan. Good head of hair, better than mine even. Told him I liked his movies and he laughed - said he liked my accent better. We both agreed this place is going to be bigger than sliced bread."

OMG I'm Betty's niece! Aunt Betty used to tell me stories about Edward - she called him her "handsome devil" even after 50 years of marriage.

She said when they first met in 1927, he walked into the radio station where she worked and said "Ma'am, I'm Edward Southworth and I'm here to make your radio station rich. And while I'm at it, I sure would like to take you to dinner." She told him she was engaged and he said "Well now, that's just an opportunity for me to change your mind, isn't it?"

He courted her for THREE MONTHS with letters every single day. Not emails, not phone calls - actual handwritten letters with that beautiful Southern gentleman handwriting. Each one ended with "Your devoted servant, Lieutenant Edward" and each one had a pressed flower from wherever he was traveling for work.

What finally got her? He sent her a recording of himself reading poetry - "She walks in beauty like the night" - in that deep Carolina voice. She played it for me once (she kept it all those years) and I swear, even knowing it was my great-uncle, that voice could still make a girl weak in the knees.

They got married Christmas Day 1928 because he said "Marrying you is the best Christmas present I could ever give myself, Betty girl." She wore a simple white dress and he wore his Army uniform. They honeymooned in Asheville and he told everyone for the next 50 years that he'd gotten the better end of that deal.

He told the BEST stories though - about flying with famous people, about meeting movie stars, about the war. Whether they were all 100% true, who knows? But that man could spin a tale. He died when I was 15 and I still miss him. There was nobody else like Uncle Edward.
