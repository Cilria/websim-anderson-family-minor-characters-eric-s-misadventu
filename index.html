Found his service record! Robert H. Jemison enlisted February 15, 1902 at Fort McClellan, Alabama. His initial MOS was Infantry - he was assigned to the 23rd Infantry Regiment. What's fascinating is his promotion timeline:

  • 1902: Private
  • 1906: Corporal (after 4 years - typical for the era)
  • 1916: Sergeant (after 10 years - here's where it gets interesting)
  • 1917: 2nd Lieutenant (commissioned during WW1 buildup)
  • 1919: 1st Lieutenant (post-war)
  • 1929: Captain (10 years between promotions)
  • 1941: Major (Pearl Harbor accelerated promotions)
  • 1942: Lieutenant Colonel
  • 1944: Colonel

He never made General because he lacked a college degree - something that became required post-WW2. The forced retirement at 84 came from Army Regulation 600-8-24 - mandatory retirement at age 64 for active duty, but he kept getting extensions due to Korea and his institutional knowledge.

My grandfather told me about the time Colonel Jemison got drunk at the Tokyo officer's club in 1952 and started telling war stories. Apparently the man had this ritual where before every major operation, he'd drink a shot of bourbon and say "This one's for the ones who didn't make it back."

He kept a small flask that he claimed was filled from the same bottle since 1918. Said it was "medicinal purposes" but everyone knew it was for toasting the dead. The flask had all these notches carved into it - one for every major battle. By Korea it had 47 notches.

The accent thing is real - my grandfather said when he got really emotional (usually drunk and talking about dead soldiers), his Alabama accent would get so thick people needed a translator. "Y'all" became "y'aaaaaawl" and he'd drop entire syllables from words.

Best story: Apparently when they told him he had to retire at 84, he showed up to his retirement ceremony in his WW1 uniform and said "Well hell, if y'all want me gone so bad, I'll go out the way I came in." They had to practically carry him out of the Pentagon.

I served under Colonel Jemison during the Leyte landings! The accent stories are 100% true. Man sounded like molasses poured over gravel. He'd say "soldier" like "soul-jah" and "mission" like "mish-shun."

But here's what nobody mentions - he had this weird habit of singing old Confederate marching songs when stressed. During the worst of the Luzon campaign, when we were getting shelled daily, you'd hear this deep Alabama baritone echoing through the supply depot: "Oh I wish I was in Dixie, hooray, hooray..."

He kept a photo of his wife Mary in his breast pocket - showed it to every new soldier. "Prettiest gal in Alabama, rest her soul." After she died in '54, he stopped carrying the photo but you could still see the outline of it in his uniform where it had been for 30+ years.

Fun fact: He had a pet chicken named "General MacArthur" during the Pacific campaigns. Said it was the only general who gave good advice. The chicken would ride around on his shoulder during supply runs. Got killed by shrapnel during an air raid - he buried it with full military honors.

You done real good. Don't you worry none 'bout what's next - Saint Peter's got a special place for boys like you."

He kept a small Bible in his left breast pocket - his wife Mary's. Had her name written in faded ink. After she died, he'd read from it to every soldier who didn't make it, tears rolling down his weathered face, accent getting thicker with every word.

We called him "Papa J" behind his back. He pretended to hate it but we all knew he loved it.
