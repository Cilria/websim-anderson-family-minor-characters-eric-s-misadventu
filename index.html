Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel expands into Mali and Niger, seizing major cities

Jihadist groups capture Gao and western Niamey, threaten regional stability as they advance toward Benin and Togo borders
By Larry Madowo & Nima Elbagir | CNN Africa

Niamey, Niger — The Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel has dramatically expanded its territorial control across West Africa, capturing the strategic Malian city of Gao and western districts of Niger's capital Niamey just eight days after consolidating power in Burkina Faso, according to multiple military and intelligence sources.

Key facts

  • Gao, Mali fell to jihadist forces on March 17
  • Western Niamey districts under caliphate control
  • JNIM using captured weapons from Burkina Faso
  • Reports of jihadist movements toward Benin and Togo borders
  • Regional security crisis escalates beyond Sahel
  • ECOWAS emergency summit scheduled for March 20

The rapid expansion represents the most significant territorial gains by jihadist groups in West Africa's history, with the newly declared caliphate now controlling territory across three nations and threatening to destabilize the entire West African region.

JNIM fighters parade through Gao after capturing the strategic Malian city

"This is unprecedented in both speed and scale," said General Mamadou Bamba, former chief of staff of the Ivorian military, speaking to CNN from Abidjan. "They have moved from being an insurgent group to controlling major cities in less than three weeks. The implications for regional security are catastrophic."

The caliphate's forces, primarily composed of fighters from Jama'at Nusrat al-Islam wal Muslimeen (JNIM) and allied groups, have been equipped with sophisticated weaponry captured during the fall of Ouagadougou, including armored vehicles, artillery systems, and communications equipment previously supplied by Western nations.

Rapid territorial expansion

The expansion began on March 15th, just four days after jihadist forces consolidated control over Burkina Faso's capital. Columns of fighters, estimated by local sources to number in the thousands, crossed into eastern Mali and western Niger using routes previously controlled by French and UN peacekeepers.

Gao, a city of approximately 100,000 people and former headquarters of French counter-insurgency operations, fell with minimal resistance. Local sources report that Malian security forces, already stretched thin by years of insurgency, withdrew rather than engage the better-equipped jihadist forces.

"The jihadists came in the early morning, just after prayers," said Amadou Touré, a teacher in Gao who spoke to CNN via WhatsApp. "They had new vehicles, new weapons, everything. The soldiers left during the night. We woke up and the city was theirs."

"They have moved from being an insurgent group to controlling major cities in less than three weeks. The implications for regional security are catastrophic."

In Niger, the situation has evolved even more rapidly. Jihadist forces have established control over the western districts of Niamey, effectively dividing the capital between government-controlled eastern sectors and caliphate-controlled western areas. The Niger River has become an informal dividing line, with sporadic fighting reported along the bridges connecting the two halves of the city.

Captured weapons fuel expansion

A key factor in the caliphate's rapid expansion has been access to sophisticated weaponry captured during the fall of Ouagadougou. Intelligence sources tell CNN that the group has acquired:

  • 15 French-made armored vehicles, including VAB and VBC models
  • Multiple 105mm artillery pieces
  • Several hundred M4 and AK-47 assault rifles
  • Night vision equipment and communications systems
  • Anti-aircraft weapons, including MANPADS

"They've gone from being an insurgent force with pickup trucks to a quasi-conventional military with armored capabilities," said a Western intelligence official who spoke on condition of anonymity. "This changes everything about how they can operate and how we need to respond."

Regional security crisis

The expansion has triggered immediate security responses across West Africa, with particular concern about the caliphate's movements toward the borders of Benin and Togo. Both countries have historically been spared the jihadist violence that has plagued their northern neighbors, but the rapid caliphate advance has raised fears that the conflict is spreading beyond the Sahel region.

Intelligence reports indicate that caliphate reconnaissance units have been spotted within 50 kilometers of both the Benin and Togo borders, though no cross-border incursions have been reported yet.

"We're seeing the early stages of what could become a regional war," said Comfort Ero, CEO of the International Crisis Group. "If JNIM establishes a presence in coastal West African states, we're looking at a security crisis that could affect the entire continent."

International response

The rapid expansion has triggered emergency responses from regional and international powers. The Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) has scheduled an emergency summit for March 20th to discuss potential military intervention.

"All options are on the table," said Omar Touray, President of the ECOWAS Commission, in a statement to CNN. "The international community cannot allow jihadist groups to establish control over sovereign nations and threaten regional stability."

France has announced it is considering re-establishing a military presence in the region, despite having withdrawn forces from Mali in 2022. French Foreign Minister Catherine Colonna told reporters in Paris that "the security situation requires an urgent international response."

The United States has begun airlifting embassy personnel from several West African capitals and has deployed additional surveillance assets to monitor the situation. Pentagon officials have confirmed that military options are being developed, though no decision has been made about direct intervention.

Humanitarian crisis deepens

The territorial expansion has exacerbated an already severe humanitarian crisis. The United Nations estimates that more than 2 million people have been displaced by the fighting across the three countries, with food shortages reported in all major cities under caliphate control.

"We're seeing the collapse of basic services in areas under jihadist control," said Martin Griffiths, the UN's humanitarian chief. "Hospitals are running out of supplies, schools are closed, and people are running out of food."

The caliphate has announced that humanitarian organizations can operate under strict conditions, but most Western aid agencies are reluctant to work in areas controlled by groups designated as terrorist organizations.

Regional implications

The expansion represents a dramatic escalation in West Africa's security crisis, with potentially far-reaching implications for global security. The caliphate now controls territory across three nations and has access to significant natural resources, including gold mines in Mali and agricultural areas in Burkina Faso and Niger.

"This is no longer a local insurgency," said Dr. Akinola Olojo, a West Africa security analyst at the University of Lagos. "We're looking at the emergence of a jihadist state with significant territorial control, population centers, and economic resources. The international community needs to treat this as a major security threat."

As the situation continues to evolve rapidly, regional powers are scrambling to respond to what may be the most significant security crisis in West African history. The ECOWAS summit on March 20th is expected to announce concrete measures, potentially including military intervention.

For now, however, the caliphate continues its expansion, with residents in border areas of Benin and Togo reporting increased anxiety about what many see as an inevitable expansion of the conflict.

"We can hear the fighting in Niger," said a resident of Dapaong in northern Togo, who spoke to CNN on condition of anonymity. "Everyone here is preparing for what we think is coming. We just don't know when."
