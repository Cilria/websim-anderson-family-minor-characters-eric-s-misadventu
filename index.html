Uzbekistan Protests Spread as Food Prices Surge, Police Clash With Demonstrators

Seventeen towns in Qashqadaryo region rocked by demonstrations over inflation

By Nafisa Rahman and Dmitri Volkov | August 27, 2027, 06:47 AM EDT

Tashkent, Uzbekistan — Protests erupted in more than 17 towns across southern Uzbekistan's Qashqadaryo region, with demonstrators clashing with police in the worst civil unrest to hit the Central Asian nation since 2022, as anger over surging food prices and living costs boiled over into the streets.

The demonstrations, which began peacefully in the regional capital Qarshi on Tuesday, spread rapidly to smaller towns including Kasan, Chirakchi and Muborak, according to videos verified by Bloomberg and local activists. Protesters blocked roads and chanted slogans criticizing government officials, while police responded with tear gas and rubber bullets in several locations.

"The situation is extremely tense," said Alisher Ilkhamov, a Central Asia analyst at the University of London. "These are the largest protests we've seen in Uzbekistan since the 2022 constitutional crisis, and they're driven by very real economic pain."

"Biz och qolyapmiz, jinoyatchilar esa tobora boyib bormoqda!" (We are going hungry, while criminals are getting richer and richer!)

In Kasan, a town of 50,000 people, witnesses reported that police fired tear gas and rubber bullets after demonstrators refused to disperse, leading to running battles through the streets. Videos posted to social media showed clouds of tear gas and people fleeing from advancing security forces.

The protests come as Uzbekistan grapples with inflation that reached 21.7% in July, driven by rising food and energy costs. The som has lost 35% of its value against the dollar this year, making imports more expensive in the import-dependent economy.

"People are angry because they can't afford basic necessities anymore," said a 42-year-old shopkeeper in Qarshi who asked not to be identified for fear of reprisal. "Bread prices have doubled, meat is unaffordable, and the government does nothing while officials live in luxury."

The government has not released an official statement on the protests as of early Friday morning local time. The Interior Ministry did not respond to requests for comment from Bloomberg.

Uzbek President Shavkat Mirziyoyev, who has ruled since 2016, has faced increasing pressure over economic management and corruption allegations. The protests represent the most significant challenge to his authority since he consolidated power following constitutional changes in 2022.

"Jinoyatchi mansabdorlarga o'lim" (Death to Criminal Officials) was among the chants heard in multiple towns, reflecting growing frustration with official corruption. Transparency International ranked Uzbekistan 121st out of 180 countries in its 2026 corruption perception index.

The Qashqadaryo region, known for its agricultural production and natural gas reserves, has been particularly hard hit by the economic downturn. Local unemployment is estimated at 18%, well above the national average of 9.2%.

"This is about more than just food prices," said a university student in Qarshi who participated in the protests. "It's about young people having no future, about corruption eating away at everything. We want real change."

Internet access appeared to be restricted in several towns, with social media platforms including Telegram and Instagram difficult to access. Such restrictions are common during periods of civil unrest in Uzbekistan.

The United States Embassy in Tashkent issued a security alert urging Americans to avoid areas where demonstrations are taking place and to monitor local media for updates.

Uzbekistan, a former Soviet republic of 36 million people, has strategic importance as a key transit route for goods between China and Europe. The country is also a major cotton producer and has significant natural gas reserves.

The protests pose a dilemma for regional powers including Russia and China, which have invested heavily in Uzbekistan as part of their Central Asia strategies. Both Moscow and Beijing have strong economic and security ties with Tashkent.

"The government faces a choice between cracking down hard, which could lead to more violence, or making real economic concessions that it may not be able to afford," said Ilkhamov. "Either way, this represents a significant escalation in civil dissent."

Similar protests over economic conditions have rocked neighboring Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan in recent years, highlighting the vulnerability of Central Asian economies to global commodity price shocks and local governance failures.

As night fell across the Qashqadaryo region, demonstrators vowed to continue their protests, while security forces maintained a heavy presence in major towns. The coming days will be crucial in determining whether the unrest spreads further or the government can contain the dissent through a combination of force and economic concessions.
