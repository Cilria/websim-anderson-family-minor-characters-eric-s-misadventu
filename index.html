RIYADH, SAUDI ARABIA

Saudi monarchy collapses as nationalist leader seizes power

Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi declares "Arab State" after 24-hour siege ends royal family's 90-year rule

By Ahmed al-Rashid, Middle East Correspondent
15 January 2030

Saudi Arabia's monarchy has collapsed after a 24-hour siege of the royal palace, with Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi and his Arab National Front of the Homeland taking full control of the kingdom and immediately beginning a radical transformation of the oil-rich state.

The dramatic seizure of power marks the end of the House of Saud's 90-year rule over the world's largest oil exporter and signals a fundamental shift in Middle Eastern geopolitics. Al-Harbi, who now styles himself as the supreme leader of the newly declared "Arab State," addressed a massive crowd outside the former royal palace to proclaim what he called "the greatest day in the history of the Arab race."

"Today will be the greatest day in the history of the Arab race! Today we have achieved victory, defeated the lying traitors, and finally purged the parasites that were the royal family. Once there was a king, and now there will be a leader. Once he was weak and sympathetic, and now he will be strong and combative."

Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi, addressing supporters in Riyadh

The speed of the monarchy's collapse has stunned regional observers. What began as protests demanding the deportation of foreign workers escalated within weeks into a full-scale insurrection, with al-Harbi's Storm Unit militia overwhelming palace security forces in a matter of hours.

In his victory speech, delivered in both Arabic and English, al-Harbi outlined an ambitious agenda that extends far beyond Saudi Arabia's borders. "Now the goal is clear: to purge the Arab world of filth and unleash fascism upon it, the medicine it must take!" he declared to thunderous applause from supporters waving the new Arab State flag.

اليوم، سيكون هذا اليوم أعظم يوم في تاريخ العرق العربي على الإطلاق! لقد حققنا اليوم النصر، وهزمنا الكذابين الخائنين، وقمنا أخيرًا بتطهير الطفيليات التي كانت تمثل العائلة المالكة. كان هناك ملك في يوم من الأيام، والآن سيكون هناك قائد، كان في يوم من الأيام ضعيفًا ومتعاطفًا، وسيصبح قويًا ومناضلًا. الآن أصبح الهدف واضحًا، وهو تطهير العالم العربي من القذارة وإطلاق العنان للفاشية عليه، الدواء الذي يجب أن يتناوله! هناك العديد من القادة المزعومين للعالم العربي، ومن الآن فصاعدًا، سأكون أنا الوحيد. أنا منقذ العرب!

The new leader's declaration that "there are many so-called leaders of the Arab world, but from now on, I will be the only one" has raised alarm bells across the region. Western diplomats fear this could signal intentions to export his revolutionary ideology to neighboring states.

Key developments:
• 24-hour siege ends 90 years of Saudi monarchy
• Al-Harbi declares "Arab State" replacing Kingdom of Saudi Arabia
• Storm Unit militia becomes official state security force
• Leader vows to "reunite Arab world" and "expand Arab living space"

Among al-Harbi's first decrees was the formal establishment of the Storm Unit as an official state security force, replacing the former Saudi military and intelligence services. The move effectively institutionalizes the militia that helped propel him to power.

More concerning to regional analysts is al-Harbi's explicit talk of expansion and conquest. "Our goal is to reunite the Arab world and expand Arab living space," he told supporters, language that echoes historical calls for territorial expansion and has prompted urgent consultations among Gulf Cooperation Council states.

The transformation has been swift and systematic. Within hours of taking power, al-Harbi's forces began removing royal family symbols from public buildings, replacing them with the new Arab State insignia. State media outlets have been rebranded, and the country's official name has been changed from the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia to simply "The Arab State."

The economic implications are already being felt. Oil prices jumped 8% on news of the coup, while global stock markets fell sharply amid concerns about stability in the world's largest oil-exporting region. The FTSE 100 dropped 2.3% in early trading, while Brent crude futures surged to $97 per barrel.

"This isn't just a change of government," said Dr. Karen Elliott House, former publisher of the Wall Street Journal and author of several books on Saudi Arabia. "This is the end of the post-Ottoman order in Arabia. The implications for global energy markets and regional stability cannot be overstated."

The international response has been cautious but increasingly urgent. The United States, which has maintained close ties with the Saudi monarchy for decades, issued a statement calling for "restraint and respect for democratic principles," though analysts note Washington has limited leverage given al-Harbi's apparent popularity among the Saudi population.

China and Russia have been more circumspect, with both countries issuing statements emphasizing the need for "stability and continuity" in energy supplies. European governments have expressed particular concern about the implications for regional security and the potential for broader instability.

Within Saudi Arabia, the mood appears divided. While al-Harbi's supporters celebrate what they see as liberation from royal corruption, members of the country's large foreign workforce—particularly the 2 million Indian citizens—fear for their safety amid growing xenophobic rhetoric.

The full extent of al-Harbi's ambitions remains unclear, but his rhetoric suggests a radical departure from the cautious foreign policy of the former monarchy. "I am the savior of the Arabs!" he proclaimed to his supporters, hinting at a leadership style that combines populist nationalism with authoritarian control.

As the Arab State begins its transformation, regional powers are grappling with the implications of a new, more assertive actor in Middle Eastern politics. The question now is not whether change has come to Saudi Arabia, but how far-reaching that change will be—and whether the region is prepared for the consequences.

Analysis: The collapse of the Saudi monarchy represents the most significant geopolitical upheaval in the Middle East since the Arab Spring. With al-Harbi's explicit embrace of expansionist rhetoric and authoritarian governance, the region faces a new era of uncertainty that could reshape global energy markets and international relations for years to come.
