Greece: Katrakis passes 'Athens Laws' to cement total control

Greek dictator executes president, abolishes presidency, and renames government ministries in sweeping power grab

By Sarah Rainsford March 25, 2027 18:45 GMT Athens, Greece
LATEST: Katrakis announces 100% victory with 100% turnout; international observers call results "statistically impossible"
Armed Greek soldiers patrol a polling station in Athens during the disputed elections (BBC)

Greek dictator Nikolaos Katrakis has executed President Konstantinos Stephanopoulos and abolished the presidency entirely through a series of decrees known as the "Athens Laws," completing his transformation of Greece from a parliamentary democracy to a totalitarian dictatorship.

The sweeping measures, announced during a massive rally in Athens, eliminate the last vestiges of democratic governance and establish Katrakis as the country's absolute ruler with powers exceeding those of any European dictator since World War II.

"The presidency is hereby abolished. All executive power shall reside in the office of the Leader, who shall exercise supreme authority over all aspects of the Hellenic State."

President Stephanopoulos, 78, was executed by firing squad at 06:00 local time at Tatoi Palace, according to state media. The former president, who had served as a ceremonial figurehead since 1995, was accused of "treasonous thoughts" and "insufficient loyalty to the national revolution."

Witnesses report that Stephanopoulos refused a blindfold and shouted "Long live democracy" before the volley of shots rang out. His body was reportedly buried in an unmarked grave, though this cannot be independently verified as foreign journalists remain banned from the country.

The Athens Laws: Totalitarian Blueprint

The 47 decrees collectively known as the "Athens Laws" restructure every aspect of Greek governance according to ultranationalist principles. The Ministry of Defense has been renamed "The War Ministry," headed by General Dimitrios Mavros, a Katrakis loyalist known for his brutal suppression of student protests.

All civil servants must now undergo "loyalty assessments" conducted by the secret police. Those deemed insufficiently committed to the regime face immediate dismissal, imprisonment, or worse. Sources within the bureaucracy estimate that up to 40% of Greece's 600,000 civil servants could be purged.

MASS PURGE UNDERWAY

Reports indicate thousands of civil servants have already been arrested. The education ministry has been particularly targeted, with over 2,000 teachers dismissed for "insufficient nationalist zeal." University professors face special scrutiny, with many forced to sign loyalty oaths to the Leader.

The laws also establish the ideological framework for territorial expansion. Greece now claims a "historical right" to territories including Cyprus, southern Albania (referred to as "Northern Epirus"), western Turkey (referred to as "Asia Minor"), and parts of Bulgaria and North Macedonia.

"The mission of the Hellenic State is to restore the rightful borders of Greater Greece and punish the traitors and scum who backstabbed the nation in 1919," the decree states, referring to the Treaty of Versailles and the subsequent Greco-Turkish War.

Parliamentary democracy abolished

The Athens Laws formally cancel all future parliamentary elections and rename the Parliament to the "National Assembly." Unlike the previous democratic institution, this body will be composed entirely of appointees selected by Katrakis and a small group of loyalists called the "Council of National Renewal."

The National Assembly will serve purely ceremonial functions, rubber-stamping decisions made by the Leader. Its members, drawn from the military, business elite, and prominent families, will serve lifetime appointments and swear personal loyalty oaths to Katrakis.

"This is not a parliament - it's a theater of the absurd," said a former Greek MP now in exile. "These people have no power, no legitimacy, and no purpose except to provide a democratic facade for what is essentially a military dictatorship."

The triumph rally

Following the announcement of the Athens Laws, Katrakis addressed a massive rally in Syntagma Square, estimated to include up to 200,000 supporters bused in from across the country. The event featured military parades, folk dancing, and a spectacular light show projecting images of ancient Greek warriors onto the Parliament building.

"Today marks the rebirth of the true Hellenic spirit!" Katrakis declared to thunderous applause. "No longer shall we be ruled by foreign concepts like democracy and human rights. We return to our ancient values of strength, honor, and national destiny!"

The rally concluded with the burning of European Union flags and the ceremonial destruction of copies of the Greek constitution. State television broadcast the event live, with commentators praising Katrakis as "the greatest Greek leader since Alexander the Great."

However, reports suggest many attendees were coerced. Civil servants were required to attend or lose their jobs, while students received academic credit for participation. Those who refused faced harassment by security services.

GLOBAL OUTRAGE

The international community has reacted with horror to the Athens Laws. The European Union called them "the final nail in the coffin of Greek democracy," while the United States described them as "a complete repudiation of Western values."

"This is not just the end of democracy in Greece - it's the end of Greece as a European nation," said EU Commission President. "Katrakis has transformed a modern European state into a totalitarian nightmare."

The dark future

With the Athens Laws, Greece has completed its transformation from democracy to dictatorship faster than any European nation since the 1930s. The speed and completeness of the transition has stunned even seasoned observers of authoritarian regimes.

"What makes this particularly terrifying is the efficiency," explained Dr. Maria Rodriguez, an expert on European authoritarianism. "Hitler took years to consolidate power. Mussolini took months. Katrakis has done it in weeks, using modern technology and techniques that make resistance nearly impossible."

The international community faces a growing crisis. With Greece controlling key shipping lanes and NATO's southeastern flank, military intervention is impossible. Economic sanctions have failed to dislodge the regime. Diplomatic isolation has only strengthened Katrakis's nationalist narrative.

"We've run out of options," admitted a European diplomat. "Short of war, which is unthinkable, we have no tools left to influence events in Greece. We've watched democracy die in real-time, broadcast on state television, and we were powerless to stop it."

As Greece settles into what appears to be permanent totalitarian rule, the question is no longer whether democracy can be restored - it's whether the contagion of authoritarianism can be contained before it spreads to other vulnerable European states.

"The Athens Laws are not just Greek laws - they're a blueprint for the destruction of democracy anywhere," warned a former Greek constitutional scholar now in exile. "Every would-be dictator in Europe is watching and learning. If this succeeds in Greece, it can succeed anywhere."

DEMOCRACY OFFICIALLY DEAD

With the execution of President Stephanopoulos and the implementation of the Athens Laws, Greece becomes the first European Union member state to complete the transition from democracy to totalitarian dictatorship. The international community's failure to prevent this transformation marks a dark new chapter in European history.

As one Athenian resident told BBC News by encrypted message: "The ancient Greeks invented democracy. Now modern Greeks have murdered it. History will judge us harshly for what we've allowed to happen here."
