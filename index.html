South Carolina state senator, brother charged in $11B fraud scheme

Robert Francis Hollomon and LeRoy Hollomon face 900 years in prison in alleged decade-long scam

By Jennifer Rodriguez and Marcus Chen | 08/01/2026 11:47 AM EST

COLUMBIA, South Carolina — Republican State Senator Robert Francis Hollomon and his brother LeRoy Hollomon were arrested Thursday and charged with orchestrating a sprawling $11 billion fraud scheme that federal prosecutors say ranks among the largest in U.S. history, potentially facing a combined 900 years in prison if convicted on all counts.

The brothers, according to a sweeping 127-count federal indictment unsealed in Columbia, allegedly ran a sophisticated network of shell companies, fake investment vehicles, and fraudulent real estate deals that targeted pension funds, insurance companies, and individual investors across the Southeast from 2016 to 2026.

"This was not a simple fraud. This was a criminal enterprise that operated with military precision, using complex financial instruments to hide the money trail and destroy countless lives."

Robert Hollomon, 58, who has served in the South Carolina Senate since 2012 and was considered a rising star in the state Republican Party, was taken into custody at his Columbia home. LeRoy Hollomon, 54, was arrested at his Hilton Head Island estate. Both appeared in federal court Thursday afternoon and were ordered held without bail.

The indictment alleges the brothers used Robert's political influence and LeRoy's business connections to create what prosecutors called "an illusion of legitimacy" around their schemes. They allegedly promised investors guaranteed returns of 15-25% through purported investments in infrastructure projects, renewable energy developments, and government contracts.

"Senator Hollomon betrayed the public trust in the most egregious way possible," said U.S. Attorney Sarah Mitchell at a press conference. "While claiming to serve his constituents, he was simultaneously stealing from them on a massive scale."

The charges include conspiracy to commit wire fraud, mail fraud, money laundering, obstruction of justice, and tax evasion. If convicted on all counts, each brother faces a maximum of 450 years in federal prison.

FBI Special Agent in Charge David Thompson said the investigation, dubbed "Operation Southern Comfort," began in early 2025 when a whistleblower from within one of the Hollomon companies provided crucial evidence. "This individual risked everything to expose this scheme," Thompson said.

The brothers allegedly laundered money through a complex web of more than 50 shell companies registered in Delaware, Nevada, and offshore jurisdictions including the Cayman Islands and British Virgin Islands. Prosecutors say they used the funds to purchase luxury properties, private jets, art collections, and to make substantial political contributions.

Robert Hollomon's Senate colleagues expressed shock at the arrests. "This is a dark day for South Carolina," said Senate President Thomas Green. "We trusted Senator Hollomon, and he violated that trust in the most profound way."

The indictment details how the brothers allegedly used Robert's position on key Senate committees to steer contracts and favorable legislation toward their fraudulent enterprises. They reportedly targeted state pension funds, promising safe investments with high returns while actually putting the money into non-existent projects.

One alleged victim, the South Carolina State Employees Retirement System, reportedly lost $2.3 billion through investments promoted by Hollomon. The system serves over 200,000 current and retired state employees.

"They promised us these were secure, government-backed investments. Now we're told the money is gone, and our retirement security is in jeopardy."

The brothers' defense attorneys, speaking outside the courthouse, maintained their clients' innocence. "These are serious allegations, but they are just that - allegations," said Robert Hollomon's attorney, Michael Stevens. "We look forward to presenting our case in court and proving these charges are baseless."

However, prosecutors say they have overwhelming evidence, including recorded conversations, financial records, and testimony from multiple co-conspirators who have already pleaded guilty and agreed to cooperate.

The case has sent shockwaves through South Carolina's political establishment. Governor Richard Lawson called for Robert Hollomon's immediate resignation, saying "he has forfeited the right to serve the people of this state." Several Republican leaders have already distanced themselves from the embattled senator.

Political analysts say the scandal could have lasting implications for the state GOP. "This is potentially devastating," said University of South Carolina political science professor Karen Brown. "It reinforces negative stereotypes about corruption and could impact elections for years."

The investigation is ongoing, with prosecutors indicating that additional charges and defendants are possible. They have already seized over $500 million in assets, including properties, bank accounts, and investments.

LeRoy Hollomon, who described himself as a "investment consultant" and "real estate developer," has no previous criminal record but has faced civil lawsuits over business dealings. Robert Hollomon had built a reputation as a conservative reformer, focusing on government transparency and fiscal responsibility.

The brothers are scheduled to appear in court next week for a detention hearing. Prosecutors are expected to argue they should remain in custody, citing flight risk and the severity of the charges.

If convicted, this would rank among the largest fraud cases in American history, surpassing even the infamous Bernie Madoff Ponzi scheme. The sheer scale of the alleged fraud has prompted calls for stricter oversight of state officials' financial dealings.

"This case should serve as a wake-up call," said U.S. Attorney Mitchell. "No one is above the law, regardless of their political position or family connections. We will pursue justice vigorously and ensure that those who betray the public trust are held accountable."

The scandal has already prompted legislative proposals for increased financial disclosure requirements for elected officials and stricter oversight of state pension fund investments. Lawmakers from both parties have expressed support for reforms.

As the legal process unfolds, the people of South Carolina are left grappling with the betrayal of trust and the potential loss of billions in retirement savings. The case is expected to take years to resolve and will likely have lasting implications for the state's political and financial landscape.

Case Details

Defendants: Robert Francis Hollomon, LeRoy Hollomon

Charges: 127 counts including wire fraud, money laundering

Potential Sentence: Up to 450 years each

Alleged Losses: $11 billion

Key Victim: SC State Employees Retirement System
