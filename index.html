Islamist nationalist movement emerges in drought-stricken Uzbekistan

"Loyal Soldiers of Islam" movement seeks to overthrow government amid worst drought in decades

By Clarissa Ward, Tim Lister and Gulchekhra Tashkent | March 27, 2028 | Tashkent, Uzbekistan

TASHKENT, Uzbekistan — A new Islamist nationalist movement has emerged in Uzbekistan, capitalizing on the Central Asian nation's worst drought in decades to challenge the government's authority and call for the establishment of an Islamic state under Sharia law.

The group, calling itself "Islomning sodiq askarlari va Islom harakati" or "The Loyal Soldiers of Islam and the Islamic Movement," announced its formation Tuesday in a video posted to social media, marking the most significant Islamist challenge to Uzbekistan's secular government since the early 2000s.

"The drought is not just a natural disaster — it is a divine sign that our current government has failed Allah's test. We will establish a Greater Uzbekistan under Islamic law to liberate all Uzbeks under the guidance of Allah."

The movement's leaders, speaking in Uzbek and Arabic, explicitly rejected the concept of a transnational Islamic caliphate, instead positioning themselves as nationalist defenders of Uzbek sovereignty. "An Islamic Caliphate is utterly pointless and foolish," said a masked spokesman in the video. "Our goal is to overthrow the Uzbekistan government and replace it with an Islamic State enforced under Sharia; our goal is also to create a Greater Uzbekistan to liberate all of the Uzbeks under the guidance of Allah."

The emergence of the group comes as Uzbekistan grapples with what meteorologists describe as the most severe drought to hit the region since reliable records began. Water levels in the vital Amu Darya and Syr Darya rivers have dropped to historic lows, devastating cotton crops that form the backbone of Uzbekistan's agricultural economy and leaving millions of rural Uzbeks facing water rationing and food insecurity.

"The timing is not coincidental," said Dr. Navbahor Imamova, a Central Asia expert at George Washington University. "The drought has created perfect conditions for anti-government sentiment. When people can't feed their families, they start looking for alternatives to the current system."

Understanding Uzbekistan's Drought Crisis

The drought, which began in late 2026, has affected over 12 million people across Uzbekistan. Water levels in major rivers are down 40% from normal, and the country's vital cotton industry has seen production fall by 35%.

The movement's ideology represents a unique fusion of religious fundamentalism and ethnic nationalism that analysts say could prove particularly potent in Uzbekistan, where Islam has historically been intertwined with national identity. Unlike transnational jihadist groups, the Loyal Soldiers of Islam frame their struggle in explicitly Uzbek terms, calling for the liberation of ethnic Uzbeks across Central Asia.

"This is a dangerous development because it's not just about religion — it's about identity," said Dr. Edward Lemon, a research fellow at the International Centre for Counter-Terrorism. "They're tapping into grievances that go beyond theology to questions of ethnic solidarity and national destiny."

The Uzbek government, which has maintained tight control over religious expression since independence in 1991, has not yet officially responded to the movement's formation. However, security sources in Tashkent told CNN that authorities are taking the threat seriously and have increased surveillance of mosques and religious gatherings in recent weeks.

The drought has exacerbated existing tensions between the secular government and Uzbekistan's deeply religious population. While the constitution guarantees freedom of religion, independent religious groups face strict registration requirements, and unauthorized religious activity can result in criminal charges.

"The government has always used the threat of Islamic extremism to justify its authoritarian policies," said a Tashkent-based human rights activist who spoke on condition of anonymity. "But now they may be facing a real challenge that their usual tactics won't be able to suppress."

The Loyal Soldiers of Islam appear to be drawing support from disaffected rural populations hardest hit by the drought. In the video, the group's spokesman specifically addressed "our brothers in the cotton fields of the Fergana Valley" and "the honest farmers of the Kyzylkum Desert," promising that an Islamic government would prioritize their needs over those of "corrupt officials in Tashkent."

This rural-urban divide has been a persistent feature of Uzbek politics, with the secular government in Tashkent often appearing disconnected from the conservative values of the countryside. The drought has widened this divide, as urban areas with better infrastructure have been less affected than rural regions dependent on agriculture.

"When the government can't provide water for our crops, when our children go hungry, we must turn to Allah. The time has come for Uzbekistan to return to its Islamic roots."

The movement's rejection of a caliphate model sets it apart from better-known Islamist groups like ISIS or al-Qaeda. Instead, the Loyal Soldiers of Islam advocate for what they term "national Islamic liberation" — the establishment of Islamic states for each Muslim nation, governed by their own interpretations of Sharia law.

This approach could prove more appealing to Uzbeks who might be sympathetic to religious governance but wary of foreign domination. Uzbekistan, which was part of the Soviet Union for nearly 70 years, has a strong tradition of nationalism that predates its recent independence.

The drought's economic impact has been severe. Cotton exports, which normally account for about 15% of Uzbekistan's export revenue, have plummeted by more than a third. The World Bank estimates that the drought has cost Uzbekistan's economy over $4 billion, with rural areas bearing the brunt of the losses.

In rural districts, where unemployment was already high, the drought has pushed many families to the brink of survival. Water rationing has become common, and in some areas, people are paying up to 10 times normal prices for clean drinking water.

"When people are desperate, they become more receptive to radical solutions," said Dr. Lemon. "The combination of economic hardship, environmental disaster, and government incompetence creates fertile ground for extremist movements."

The international community has expressed concern about the developments. The United States Embassy in Tashkent issued a statement Tuesday calling for "all parties to respect democratic processes and human rights" while urging the Uzbek government to address the humanitarian crisis caused by the drought.

Russia, which maintains significant influence in Central Asia, has reportedly offered assistance to the Uzbek government in dealing with both the drought and the emerging security threat. However, Uzbek officials have so far declined direct Russian military assistance, wary of appearing dependent on Moscow.

China, which has invested heavily in Uzbekistan's energy and transportation infrastructure through its Belt and Road Initiative, has called for "stability and development" in the region. Chinese companies have been particularly affected by the drought, as several major cotton processing facilities have had to reduce operations due to water shortages.

The emergence of the Loyal Soldiers of Islam represents a new challenge for Central Asian stability at a time when the region is already grappling with the effects of climate change, economic transition, and geopolitical tensions. The drought that sparked the crisis shows no signs of abating, with meteorologists predicting continued dry conditions through at least the end of 2028.

For now, the movement appears to be in its early stages, with unknown numbers of followers and limited operational capacity. But the combination of environmental disaster, economic hardship, and religious fervor could prove explosive in a region where secular governments have long struggled to balance modernization with traditional values.

As one Tashkent resident told CNN, speaking anonymously for fear of government reprisal: "The drought has shown us that our government cannot protect us from nature. Now we will see if they can protect us from those who claim to speak for God."
