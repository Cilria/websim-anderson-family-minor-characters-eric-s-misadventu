RIYADH, Saudi Arabia

Arab State leader calls for 'total war' and massive military build-up

Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi declares military operations against Yemen will begin within two weeks as he outlines vision for regional conflict

By Jeremy Bowen, BBC Middle East Editor
15 February 2030

Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi, the leader of the self-declared Arab State, has delivered his most bellicose speech to date, calling for "total war" and announcing that military operations against Yemen will begin within two weeks.

Speaking to a crowd estimated at nearly one million supporters in Riyadh, al-Harbi outlined what he described as an "all-out war" necessary to "save the Arabs," marking a dramatic escalation in rhetoric that has alarmed regional powers and international observers.

"Many in the foreign media claim that I hate human rights and international law, but I tell you that this is not true. I strongly dislike those so-called 'rules'! They prevent us from achieving true perfection for the superior Arab race against the despicable humans we are fighting today: Indians, Yemenis, Jews, Persians, and worst of all, the most disgusting and vile of these parasites, the 'Palestinians.' I spit on this despicable human filth!"

Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi, speaking in Riyadh

The speech, al-Harbi's second major address since seizing power in January, represents a significant departure from his previous focus on domestic ethnic cleansing, now explicitly calling for regional military conflict and the rejection of international norms.

"Sympathy is weakness and cowardice; it is a trait of the weak and the cowardly. No! We Arabs are a race of war, blood, and vengeance! I ask you: do you want total war?" al-Harbi declared, prompting the massive crowd to chant in unison: "We want war!"

According to diplomatic sources who spoke to the BBC on condition of anonymity, al-Harbi has already begun a massive military expansion, with plans to increase the Arab State's armed forces from approximately 250,000 to over 2 million personnel within the next 18 months.

"It is time to wage an all-out war, because the only way to save the Arabs is through war!"

Al-Harbi outlining his vision for conflict

The announcement of imminent military operations against Yemen has prompted urgent diplomatic activity across the region. Yemen's internationally recognized government, based in Aden, has called for emergency UN Security Council meetings, while the Houthi movement has vowed to resist what it termed "fascist aggression."

Regional analysts note that al-Harbi's rhetoric has evolved from ethnic nationalism to what resembles a form of regional imperialism, with explicit calls for the subjugation of neighboring populations.

Key announcements from al-Harbi's speech:
• Military operations against Yemen to begin within two weeks
• Massive military build-up planned, expanding forces to 2 million
• Rejection of international law and human rights norms
• Explicit targeting of multiple ethnic and religious groups
• Crowd of nearly one million supporters chanting for war

The international community has responded with alarm to these developments. The United Nations Secretary-General issued a statement expressing "grave concern" about the "escalation toward regional conflict," while the United States State Department called for "maximum restraint" and warned of "serious consequences" for regional stability.

Iran, which has historically supported various factions in Yemen, has put its military forces on heightened alert along the Persian Gulf. Iranian state media reported that Supreme Leader Ayatollah Khamenei convened an emergency meeting of the Supreme National Security Council to discuss the situation.

Turkey, which has maintained military presence in Qatar and other regional locations, has called for an emergency meeting of the Organization of Islamic Cooperation. Turkish President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan stated that "the region cannot afford another war" and called for diplomatic solutions.

The economic implications of al-Harbi's announcement have already begun to ripple through global markets. Oil prices surged by 8% in trading following the speech, while major international companies with operations in the region began reviewing their exposure to what analysts are calling an increasingly unstable situation.

Perhaps most significantly, al-Harbi's explicit rejection of international law and human rights norms marks a fundamental departure from even the most authoritarian regional governments, which have typically maintained at least nominal commitment to international institutions.

"This represents a qualitative leap in the region's political dynamics," said Dr. Lina Khatib, director of the Middle East and North Africa programme at Chatham House. "We're witnessing the emergence of a genuinely fascist movement that explicitly rejects the international order."

The Arab State leader appeared to anticipate international criticism, declaring: "Many in so-called foreign media attempt to claim that I only hate the Indians, this is a lie. I hate all other parasites on our homeland! The Arab State shall be for Arabs only!"

Human rights organizations have expressed particular alarm about al-Harbi's targeting of Palestinians, who have historically found refuge across the Arab world. "The explicit call for violence against Palestinian populations represents a new low in regional rhetoric," said Sarah Leah Whitson, executive director of Democracy for the Arab World Now.

As al-Harbi concluded his speech to thunderous applause from supporters, many waving the new Arab State flag, he issued what many interpreted as a warning to both domestic opponents and international critics: "The hour of action has arrived. The Arab people will no longer be slaves to foreign interests or domestic traitors. Our destiny awaits, and nothing will stop us from achieving it."

The crowd's response—nearly one million people chanting for war—suggested that al-Harbi's radical vision has found significant support among segments of the Saudi population, raising urgent questions about regional stability and the potential for wider conflict in the coming weeks.

Analysis: Al-Harbi's speech marks a watershed moment in Middle Eastern history, transforming what began as a nationalist protest movement into an explicitly militaristic and expansionist regime. With military operations against Yemen announced to begin within two weeks, the region faces the prospect of a devastating new conflict that could draw in multiple regional powers.

International reaction

Yemen: Government calls for international protection against "fascist aggression"
