Mass riots across Arab world leave 10,000 dead as anti-Persian violence escalates

Governments stand by or actively support violence as protests over murdered Iraqi woman spiral into coordinated ethnic cleansing campaign

14 July 2027 • By Jeremy Bowen and Lina Sinjab • BBC Middle East correspondents
10,000+ DEAD

4,000 Persians and 6,000 Shias killed in coordinated violence across multiple countries

A wave of coordinated anti-Persian violence has swept across the Arab world, leaving more than 10,000 people dead in what appears to be the most serious outbreak of ethnic cleansing in the region in decades.

The violence, which began as protests over the murder of 24-year-old Iraqi woman Noor al-Maliki, rapidly escalated into organized attacks against Persian and Shia communities across multiple countries. By nightfall, the death toll had reached staggering proportions: 4,000 Persians and 6,000 Shias killed, with the violence showing no signs of abating.

"Death to Persian criminals! Fight the Persian kuffars!"

In Iraq, where the crisis began, what started as funeral processions for Ms. al-Maliki transformed into anti-Shia riots within hours. Rioters burned down Shia religious buildings and reportedly began "gunning down Shias in the street," according to eyewitness accounts. The Iraqi security forces, overwhelmed by the scale of the violence, largely stood by as the killings continued.

[Image: Anti-Persian protests in Baghdad]

Egyptian embassy stormed

Perhaps the most shocking incident occurred in Cairo, where over 500 Persians were killed after rioters stormed the Iranian Embassy. The diplomatic compound, normally protected by international law, was completely destroyed as Egyptian security forces failed to intervene. Iranian diplomats and embassy staff were among the dead.

"The mob came prepared," said one embassy staff member who survived by hiding in a secure room. "They had weapons, they had gasoline, they knew exactly what they were doing. The police just watched."

The attack on the embassy represents a serious escalation in the crisis, with Iran's foreign ministry issuing a statement calling it "an act of war" and demanding immediate protection for Persian minorities across the region.

Lebanon's unusual alliance

In Lebanon, an unprecedented alliance formed between Sunni and Christian rioters who joined together to target Persian businesses and communities. The coordinated attacks across religious lines suggests a level of planning that goes far beyond spontaneous protests.

"I've never seen anything like it," said Beirut resident Marie Khoury, a Christian. "Sunni and Christian neighbors who normally avoid each other were working together to destroy Persian shops. Someone is organizing this."

"Oh Persians, be warned of the wrath of Allah!"

Government complicity

What makes this crisis particularly alarming is the apparent complicity of Arab governments. Rather than attempting to quell the violence, security forces in multiple countries either stood by or, in some cases, actively assisted the rioters.

In Saudi Arabia, protesters chanting "End the Persian gangs" were reportedly joined by members of the security forces who directed them toward Persian neighborhoods. Similar scenes played out in Jordan and Syria, where government forces appeared to facilitate rather than prevent the violence.

"This is state-sponsored ethnic cleansing," said Dr. Hadi Ghaemi, executive director of the International Campaign for Human Rights in Iran. "The level of coordination across multiple countries suggests this was planned well in advance, waiting only for a trigger event."

Religious justification

The religious dimension of the violence became explicit when prominent imams began issuing fatwas calling for jihad against what they termed "Persian kuffars" (unbelievers). One widely circulated religious ruling declared that killing Persians was not only permissible but obligatory for good Muslims.

"The Persians are enemies of Islam," declared Sheikh Ahmed al-Rashid in a sermon broadcast on state television in multiple Arab countries. "They seek to destroy Arabs and defile our holy places. Every Muslim must stand against this Persian menace."

[Image: Rioters attacking Persian businesses]

The use of religious rhetoric to justify ethnic violence has particularly alarmed international observers, who see echoes of previous genocides where religious authorities played key roles in inciting mass killing.

Calls for blockade

As the violence continued, Arab ultranationalist groups began calling for a complete blockade of Iran, with the stated aim of "starving the Persian enemy." These calls have been amplified by state media across the region, with television stations running continuous coverage of alleged "Persian crimes" against Arabs.

"We will not rest until every Persian is driven from Arab lands," read one widely shared social media post. "The time for talking is over. Now is the time for action."

International response

The international community has responded with alarm to the escalating violence. The United Nations Security Council held an emergency session, though no concrete action was agreed upon. Western governments have issued strongly worded statements but have been reluctant to intervene directly.

"We are witnessing ethnic cleansing in real time," said one European diplomat who requested anonymity. "But the political will to intervene is simply not there. The Arab world is too important economically and strategically."

Persian retaliation threatened

As news of the massacres spread, some Persian ultranationalist groups began calling for retaliation. "The day of justice will come for our people killed," read one statement circulated on Persian social media. "Arabs will pay for this blood."

The threats of Persian retaliation have only served to fuel further anti-Persian sentiment, creating a cycle of violence that appears likely to escalate further. Arab media has seized on these threats as evidence that Persians pose an existential threat to Arab communities.

"End the Persian gangs! Destroy the Persian menace!"

Humanitarian catastrophe

The scale of the violence has created a massive humanitarian crisis. Tens of thousands of Persians and Shias have fled their homes, creating refugee flows across the region. International aid agencies report being overwhelmed by the scale of the displacement.

"This is ethnic cleansing pure and simple," said one aid worker who requested anonymity. "Entire communities have been erased. Families have been destroyed. And the world is just watching."

As night fell across the Middle East, the killing continued. In city after city, mobs roamed the streets hunting for Persians and Shias, while governments either watched or actively participated. What began with a single murder had evolved into one of the worst outbreaks of ethnic violence in the region's modern history.

Jeremy Bowen reported from London, and Lina Sinjab from Beirut.
