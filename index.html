Uzbek Islamist group vows Sharia law and armed struggle

Loyal Soldiers of Islam threaten to overthrow government and expel Russian citizens

By Faisal Edroos and Sarah Rainsford | April 14, 2028 | Tashkent, Uzbekistan

A radical Uzbek Islamist movement that emerged during the country's worst drought in decades has released a video vowing to impose Sharia law and expel Russian citizens, in what analysts say marks a dangerous escalation of the group's campaign against the secular government.

The Loyal Soldiers of Islam and Islamic Movement, which claims to have 10,000 members across Uzbekistan, released a 12-minute video on Sunday outlining its vision for an Islamic state and warning that "armed struggle will begin soon."

"O Allah, raise the great Mujahideen of Uzbekistan and unleash divine retribution and overthrow the infidel government of Uzbekistan and drive out the Russians infidels out of the sacred soil."

In the video, masked spokesmen for the group declare their intention to change Uzbekistan's alphabet from the current Latin script back to the Persian-Arabic script, establish an Islamic religious police force, and reclaim what they term "Uzbek Homeland" from neighboring states.

The movement's leader, speaking in heavily accented Uzbek mixed with Arabic phrases, said the group had grown from "a few hundred brothers" to a nationwide network spanning all regions of the Central Asian nation.

"We number 10,000 across Uzbekistan, from the cotton fields of the Fergana Valley to the deserts of Karakalpakstan," he said, addressing the camera. "Soon we will be 100,000, then one million. The people are waking up to the truth of Islam."

Key Demands from the Video

• Imposition of Sharia law across Uzbekistan
• Replacement of Latin alphabet with Persian-Arabic script
• Expulsion of Russian citizens deemed "infidels"
• Creation of Islamic religious police force
• Reclamation of "Uzbek Homeland" territories

The video represents the most explicit threat yet from a group that first emerged in March, when it announced its formation amid widespread anger over the government's handling of a severe drought that has devastated agriculture and left millions struggling with water shortages.

Since then, the movement has grown rapidly, capitalizing on public frustration with President Shavkat Mirziyoyev's administration and its perceived failure to address the economic crisis triggered by the drought.

"They're tapping into a perfect storm of grievances," said Dr. Navbahor Imamova, a Central Asia specialist at George Washington University. "Environmental disaster, economic hardship, and a government that appears increasingly out of touch with its people."

The group's anti-Russian rhetoric marks a significant escalation. Uzbekistan has maintained relatively close ties with Moscow since independence in 1991, and Russian citizens, many of whom have lived in Uzbekistan for generations, form a significant minority in the country.

In the video, the group accuses Russia of "colonizing" Uzbekistan and stealing its resources, calling for the expulsion of all Russian citizens who refuse to convert to Islam and adopt Uzbek customs.

"The Russians have polluted our sacred soil with their atheism and alcohol. They must leave or face the consequences of divine justice."

The Uzbek government has not yet officially responded to the video, but security sources in Tashkent told the BBC that authorities are treating it as a serious threat and have increased surveillance of mosques and religious gatherings.

The movement's rejection of a transnational caliphate in favor of Uzbek nationalism sets it apart from better-known Islamist groups, but analysts warn this could make it more dangerous.

"This isn't ISIS trying to create a global caliphate," said Dr. Edward Lemon of the International Centre for Counter-Terrorism. "This is a nationalist movement that happens to be Islamist. That combination could prove much more appealing to ordinary Uzbeks who might be sympathetic to religious governance but wary of foreign domination."

The video's production quality suggests the group has access to modern equipment and editing capabilities, raising questions about potential foreign backing. The speakers appear well-trained, speaking confidently to camera and using sophisticated visual effects.

"This isn't some amateur operation," said a Western intelligence official who requested anonymity. "The video shows professional production values and careful messaging. Someone with experience is advising them."

The group's call for armed struggle has alarmed regional governments, who fear the conflict could spill across Central Asia's porous borders. Uzbekistan shares frontiers with five countries, including Afghanistan, where Islamist groups have gained ground since the Taliban's return to power in 2021.

"The last thing Central Asia needs is another armed conflict," said a senior official from Kazakhstan's foreign ministry. "We're already dealing with economic challenges and climate change. This could destabilize the entire region."

The drought that sparked the movement shows no signs of abating, with meteorologists predicting continued dry conditions through at least the end of 2028. Water levels in the vital Amu Darya and Syr Darya rivers remain at historic lows, devastating agriculture and forcing millions to rely on water deliveries.

In rural areas, where support for the Islamist movement appears strongest, many residents say they have lost faith in the government's ability to address the crisis.

"The government talks about modernization and economic development, but we can't even get clean water," said a farmer in the Fergana Valley who spoke on condition of anonymity. "Maybe it's time to try something different."

The movement's demand to revert to the Persian-Arabic script represents a rejection of Uzbekistan's post-independence identity. The country switched from Cyrillic to Latin script in the 1990s as part of a broader effort to distance itself from its Soviet past.

"This isn't just about religion - it's about identity and history," said Dr. Lemon. "They're saying everything since independence has been a mistake, that Uzbekistan needs to return to its pre-Soviet Islamic roots."

Western diplomats in Tashkent say they are monitoring the situation closely, with several countries updating their travel advisories for Uzbekistan. The United States Embassy has urged American citizens to avoid areas where protests might occur.

The European Union has called for dialogue and restraint, with a spokesperson saying Brussels is "concerned by the escalation of rhetoric and calls for violence."

Russia, which maintains significant interests in Uzbekistan, has not yet responded to the video's anti-Russian rhetoric. Moscow has traditionally supported secular governments in Central Asia and has provided security assistance to combat Islamist movements.

As the drought continues and public frustration grows, analysts warn that the Loyal Soldiers of Islam may be poised to gain even more support, particularly among young Uzbeks who see few opportunities in the current system.

"The government has a narrow window to address the underlying grievances," said Dr. Imamova. "If they don't act quickly to provide relief and restore confidence, they may find themselves facing a much larger problem."

For now, the movement's threat of armed struggle remains just that - a threat. But with 10,000 claimed members and growing support, the Uzbek government may soon face its most serious challenge since independence.

As one Tashkent resident told the BBC, speaking anonymously for fear of government reprisal: "The video is frightening, but also exciting. For the first time in years, it feels like something might actually change."

About Uzbekistan

Population: 35.9 million

Capital: Tashkent

Religion: 88% Muslim (mostly Sunni)

Script: Latin (since 1990s)

Russian minority: ~650,000 people
