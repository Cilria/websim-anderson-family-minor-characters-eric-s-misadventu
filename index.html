Thomas Richard Rains (1902-2004): 102-year-old Tennessee car dealer who built an empire from a used car lot

Tennessee Automotive History Business Records 20th Century Posted by u/Appalachian_Tracker • 8 hours ago

I've been researching Thomas Richard Rains for months, and this might be my favorite find ever. Born January 20, 1902 in White Oak, Tennessee, he lived to be 102 years old and built a car dealership empire that spanned East Tennessee.

The Early Years: His father Richard Port Rains was 65 when Thomas was born (late marriage - Richard was born 1837). Thomas's mother Annie Susan Brook died in 1911, leaving his father to raise 9 children alone. After his father's death in 1925, Thomas and his brother Frank left the family farm.

The Business Empire: Started as plumbers in Oak Ridge (1925-1926), then transitioned to used car sales in Knoxville. By 1928, they were officially in the car business. Founded "Rains Brothers Automobile Co." in 1939. WWII made them rich - they were the only major used car dealership in East Knoxville during the war boom.

Expansion & Legacy: After Frank left in 1946 to start his own Kentucky dealership, Thomas expanded to new cars (Hudson, Chrysler, Ford). By the 1950s, he had 4 dealerships across East Tennessee. His son Charles joined in 1955. The 1970s hit hard - he famously said about Chryslers: "Nobody wants to buy a fucking Chrysler anymore, used to sell about 20 of those, now I am lucky to sell one or two."

Retirement & Death: Retired in 1977 at age 75. Spent his final 27 years writing for the Freemasons and Scottish Rite. Died November 11, 2004 at 102 years old.

My brick wall: I can't find any records of his dealerships after the 1950s. Did they close? Get sold? Change names? Also looking for any photos of the original Rains Brothers lot in East Knoxville. Anyone have Knoxville business directory access from the 1960s-1970s?

Found the 1960s-1970s records: Thomas R. Rains Motors operated all four locations through 1977. The business was officially dissolved January 15, 1977 (his 75th birthday - he planned it). Here's what happened to each location:

Found Thomas's White Oak roots: His father Richard Port Rains (1837-1925) was a Civil War veteran - served in the 5th Tennessee Cavalry (Union). After the war, he became a successful farmer in White Oak, owning 340 acres. Thomas was the 8th of 9 children. u/Freemason_Archives • 3 hours ago
His Masonic career was impressive: Thomas joined Cherokee Lodge #444 in 1933, became Master in 1948. Wrote extensively for the Tennessee Freemason magazine from 1977-2004. His most famous article was "The Business of Brotherhood" (1982) about how Masonic connections helped his business. He was also a 33rd degree Scottish Rite Mason and received the Grand Cross of Honor in 1992. The Masonic Temple on Kingston Pike has a plaque dedicated to him - he donated the land for their parking lot.
