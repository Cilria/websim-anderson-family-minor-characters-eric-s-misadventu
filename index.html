Here's what I know:

  • Born to Robert Mack Horne Jr (1876-1939) and Eliza Ann Monroe (1882-1927)
  • One of 14 siblings - yes, FOURTEEN
  • Married in Windsor, VA in November 1945
  • Drafted for WW2 but never served (medical deferment?)
  • Moved to Tampa, Florida after the war
  • Worked as grocery store manager until retirement
  • Fathered 5 children
  • Died of heart attack July 22, 1992

TampaHistoryBuff • 89 points • 8 hours ago

FOUND THE GROCERY STORE: Howard managed the Piggly Wiggly at 3408 Henderson Boulevard in Tampa from 1948 to 1978. Thirty years of managing the same store - that's actually impressive in its own way.

THE STORE DETAILS: It was a typical 1950s grocery - 3,200 square feet, 8 employees, and Howard knew every regular customer by name. The store had manual cash registers until 1975 when they finally got electronic ones.

THE MEDICAL DEFERMENT: Found his Selective Service records - Howard had rheumatic fever as a child that damaged his heart valve. His brothers called him "Lucky Ho" because he got out of serving while 7 of his brothers served overseas.

THE NICKNAME: "Ho" came from his youngest sister Mary who couldn't pronounce "Howard" - she called him "Ho-Ho" and it stuck. By adulthood, everyone including his wife called him "Ho" or "Mr. Ho" at the store.

THE MOST AVERAGE FACT: His personnel file shows he took exactly 2 sick days in 30 years - both for his children's births. His annual reviews consistently rated him "Satisfactory" - never excellent, never poor. Just... satisfactory.

THE HORNE FAMILY WAS REMARKABLY UNREMARKABLE: I grew up in Windsor - the Hornes were known for being the most average family in town.

THE PARENTS: Robert Mack Horne Jr worked at the sawmill for 43 years - same shift, same job. Eliza Ann Monroe was a housewife who had 14 children in 21 years and never learned to drive.

THE SIBLINGS: Of the 14 Horne children:

  • 6 worked at the sawmill
  • 3 became housewives
  • 2 were farmers
  • 1 was a teacher (the rebel)
  • 1 was a grocery store manager (Howard)
  • 1 died in infancy

THE MOST AVERAGE STORY: Howard's high school yearbook (1934) lists him as "Most Likely to Stay in Windsor" - he was one of three in his class of 32 who actually left. The other two became missionaries, Howard just moved to Tampa.

THE MARRIAGE: Married Dorothy Mae Smith (1918-2003) - she was a cashier at Woolworth's. They met at a church social and courted for exactly 6 months before marrying. She wore her mother's wedding dress, they honeymooned in Norfolk (30 miles away).

THE CHILDREN: All 5 children had ordinary lives too - 3 teachers, 1 nurse, 1 accountant. Not a single criminal, celebrity, or failure among them. Even their rebellions were conventional.

THE TRUTH: Most of our ancestors were like Howard - ordinary people living ordinary lives. We just don't write about them because there's nothing sensational to document.

THE BEAUTY: Howard represents millions of Americans who got up, went to work, raised families, and never made headlines. He was drafted but couldn't serve, managed a grocery store, raised 5 kids, and died of a heart attack at 76.

THE STATISTICS:

  • 76 years was above average for men in 1992
  • 5 children was typical for his generation
  • 30 years at the same job shows stability
  • Moving from Virginia to Florida in 1945 was common

THE REALITY: We're conditioned to expect dramatic stories - war heroes, criminals, celebrities. But 90% of genealogy is people like Howard who lived quiet, decent lives and never made history.

THE HONOR: Howard did what society expected - he worked hard, stayed married, raised responsible children, and never caused trouble. In many ways, that's more admirable than being exceptional.

THE TRUTH: We're spoiled by modern obituaries that read like novels. Back then, working-class people got working-class obits - short, factual, done.
