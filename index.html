Trump Mocks Dan Newhouse After Wife's Death

The former president attacks a Republican who voted for impeachment, even in mourning

By Alex Shepherd and Grace Segers | January 11, 2026

Representative Dan Newhouse announced yesterday that his wife, Joan Galvin, had died in a car accident near their home in Sunnyside, Washington. The congressman, who has represented Washington's 4th District since 2015, called Galvin "my partner in every sense of the word" and said her death "leaves a hole that can never be filled."

Within hours, former President Donald Trump responded—not with condolences, but with cruelty.

"His wife is now in HELL AWAY FROM THAT SICK RINO LOSER DAN NEWHOUSE 'LOSER'"

The post appeared on Truth Social at 6:47 p.m. Eastern, less than four hours after Newhouse's announcement. Trump continued: "Maybe if Dan spent less time voting for impeachment hoaxes and more time being a good husband, this wouldn't have happened. Just saying!"

The attack represents a new low, even for Trump, who has previously mocked prisoners of war, disabled reporters, and fallen soldiers. But targeting a political opponent at the moment of their greatest grief marks an escalation in the former president's increasingly unhinged rhetoric against Republicans who voted to impeach him following the January 6th insurrection.

Newhouse, one of just ten House Republicans who voted for Trump's second impeachment, has faced relentless attacks from the former president and his allies. But the congressman's colleagues say this latest assault crosses every conceivable line.

"This isn't politics. This is evil. Dan just lost his wife, and Trump is dancing on her grave."

"This isn't politics. This is evil," said Representative Adam Kinzinger, another Republican who voted for impeachment. "Dan just lost his wife, and Trump is dancing on her grave. The fact that anyone still supports this man tells you everything about the moral rot in today's GOP."

The response from other Republicans has been characteristically muted. House Speaker Mike Johnson, when asked about Trump's comments, said only: "I haven't seen the post." Senator Ted Cruz declined to comment. The Republican National Committee issued a statement expressing "thoughts and prayers" for the Newhouse family without mentioning Trump's attack.

But the silence from most elected Republicans speaks volumes about the party's transformation under Trump's influence. Where once politicians might have recoiled from such naked cruelty, today's GOP has largely accepted Trump's degradation of political discourse as the price of maintaining power.

The incident also highlights Trump's obsessive fixation on punishing those who voted for his impeachment. Of the ten House Republicans who supported impeachment, four retired rather than face Trump's wrath, three lost primary challenges to Trump-endorsed opponents, and only Newhouse and Representatives David Valadao and Don Bacon remain in Congress.

Newhouse, who won reelection in 2024 by just 3 points in a district Trump carried by 18, has largely avoided responding to Trump's previous attacks. But friends say the congressman is devastated by his wife's death and furious about the former president's response.

"Joan was everything to Dan," said a longtime friend who requested anonymity. "They met in high school, been together 40 years. For Trump to use her death as a political weapon—it's beyond sick. Dan's trying to grieve, trying to comfort their kids, and this is what he gets?"

The attack comes as Trump faces mounting legal pressure from multiple investigations, including the January 6th case that Newhouse voted to impeach him over. Associates say the former president has grown increasingly erratic and vengeful as his political and legal troubles mount.

"He's not well," said a former Trump administration official who remains in contact with the former president. "The attacks are getting more personal, more vicious. He used to have some sense of limits. Now it's just pure rage at anyone he sees as disloyal."

For Democrats, the incident provides another example of Trump's unfitness for office, though they too have struggled to respond effectively to his increasingly unhinged behavior.

"Donald Trump has shown us who he is, again and again," said Representative Jamie Raskin, who led the second impeachment effort. "A man who mocks prisoners of war, who calls Nazis 'very fine people,' who tells grieving widows their husbands 'knew what they signed up for.' This is who he is. The question is: when will Republicans finally say enough?"

The answer, based on Saturday's response, appears to be never. As Trump continues his march toward the Republican nomination, his party has largely surrendered any pretense of moral leadership, accepting cruelty as simply another political tool in the pursuit of power.

For Dan Newhouse, the political implications matter less than the personal tragedy. Friends say the congressman is focused on planning his wife's funeral and supporting their three children, all while processing the loss of his partner of four decades.

But even in mourning, he cannot escape Trump's shadow. The former president's attack ensures that what should have been a private moment of grief becomes another battleground in America's endless political war.

As one Republican strategist put it: "Trump doesn't just want to defeat his enemies. He wants to humiliate them, to destroy them, to make them suffer. And if that means attacking a man at the lowest moment of his life, so be it. There are no lines anymore. There is only Trump."
