Uzbekistan protests: Anti-government demonstrations spread to Bukhara and Termez

Nearly 60,000 protesters occupy Bukhara city center as demonstrations escalate across the country

By Sarah Thompson and Ravshan Nazarov | August 30, 2027

Tashkent, Uzbekistan — Anti-government protests have spread to major cities across Uzbekistan, with nearly 60,000 demonstrators occupying the city center of Bukhara and violent clashes erupting in Termez, as public anger against President Shavkat Mirziyoyev continues to escalate.

The demonstrations, which began in the southern Qashqadaryo region earlier this week, have now reached the historic Silk Road cities of Bukhara and the strategic border town of Termez, marking the most significant challenge to Mirziyoyev's government since he took power in 2016.

In Bukhara, protesters occupied the main square and government buildings, chanting slogans calling for the president to resign. The scene was markedly different from earlier protests focused on economic grievances, with demonstrators now openly calling for political change.

"Jinoyatchi Shavkat Mirziyoyevga o'lim" (Death to the Criminal Shavkat Mirziyoyev)
"Rossiya, chiqib ket!" (Get Out, Russia!)
"Shavkat Mirziyoyev va Putin, jinoyatchi o'g'rilar" (Shavkat Mirziyoyev and Putin, criminal thieves)

In Termez, near the Afghan border, violence erupted as police attempted to disperse protesters using tear gas and rubber bullets. Witnesses reported that demonstrators set fire to a local bank building in response to what they described as excessive force by security services.

The Interior Ministry confirmed that 11 people have been killed in the unrest, while human rights groups claim the toll is significantly higher. Authorities have labeled the demonstrators as "rioters" and accused them of attempting to destabilize the country.

"Biz jinoyatchilar tizimi tomonidan o'ldirilmoqdamiz, holbukida jinoyatchi mansabdorlar o'z farzandlarini Yevropaga yuborishmoqda!"
"We are being killed by the criminal system, while criminal officials are sending their own children to Europe!"

The protests represent a significant escalation from the economic demonstrations that began in southern Uzbekistan. Analysts say the movement has transformed into a broader anti-government uprising, with protesters demanding an end to Mirziyoyev's rule and closer ties with Russia.

"This is no longer about food prices or inflation," said Dr. Alisher Ilkhamov, a Central Asia specialist at SOAS University of London. "The protests have taken on an explicitly political character, with people calling for regime change."

Key Facts: Uzbekistan Protests

  • Bukhara: Nearly 60,000 protesters occupy city center
  • Termez: 11 confirmed dead in clashes with police
  • Demands: President Mirziyoyev's resignation, end to Russian influence
  • Government response: Police use tear gas, rubber bullets; protesters labeled "rioters"
  • International: US Embassy issues security alert

The spread of protests to Bukhara is particularly significant given the city's historical importance and its role as a major tourist destination. The occupation of the city center has effectively shut down the tourism industry, a key source of foreign currency for the cash-strapped nation.

Internet restrictions have been imposed across the country, with social media platforms including Telegram, Instagram, and Facebook largely inaccessible. The government has not acknowledged these restrictions, but monitoring groups reported widespread connectivity issues.

Russia's influence has emerged as a key target for protesters, with many chanting anti-Russian slogans and calling for an end to what they see as Moscow's interference in Uzbek affairs. The two countries have close economic and security ties, with Russia being Uzbekistan's largest trading partner.

The United States Embassy in Tashkent has issued a security alert, warning American citizens to avoid areas where demonstrations are taking place and to monitor local media for updates. Several other Western embassies have issued similar warnings.

Uzbekistan, a former Soviet republic of 36 million people, has been grappling with economic challenges including inflation that reached 21.7% in July. The som has lost 35% of its value against the dollar this year, making imports more expensive in the import-dependent economy.

President Mirziyoyev, who came to power promising liberal reforms, has faced increasing criticism over his handling of the economy and allegations of corruption within his government. The protests represent the most serious challenge to his authority since he consolidated power following constitutional changes in 2022.

As night fell across Uzbekistan, demonstrators in multiple cities vowed to continue their protests, while security forces maintained a heavy presence in major urban areas. The coming days will be crucial in determining whether the government can contain the unrest or if the protests will continue to spread.
