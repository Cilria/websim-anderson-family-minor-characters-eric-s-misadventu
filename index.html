Edward Lee Southworth Sr. (1892-1981): From stonemason's son to "Lieutenant Edward," advertising pioneer

North Carolina WWI Advertising Civil Air Patrol Posted by u/Mississippi_Archivist • 6 hours ago

Sometimes genealogy gives you stories that sound too cinematic to be real. Edward Lee Southworth Sr. is one of those. Born July 20, 1892 in Mt. Olive, North Carolina to John William Southworth (1858-1920), an unsuccessful stonemason, and Mary Louise Bridge (1862-1939), homemaker. Eighth of their children.

The Great Escape: Family legend says young Edward declared "I am not gonna be a damn stonemason!" and left Mt. Olive forever. By 1915, he was working as a Newspaper Auditor in Durham, NC - a dramatic leap from his father's trade.

Timeline of a Restless Soul:

  • 1915: Newspaper Auditor, Durham, NC
  • 1916: Moved to Richmond, VA (same role)
  • 1917-1919: WWI service, discharged as First Lieutenant - nickname "Lieutenant Edward" stuck for life
  • 1920: State Auditor, Bank Company, Lexington, KY
  • 1921: Advertising agent, Kansas City radio station
  • 1923: Los Angeles (3 years)
  • 1926: Atlanta, GA
  • 1927: Radio Advertising Agent, Raleigh, NC
  • 1933: Joined NC Civil Air Patrol, reached Major
  • 1947: Resigned from CAP due to "business interests"
  • 1970: Retired, citing "Television has caused a large decline in the business"

The Constant: Despite all the moves, he kept returning to North Carolina. Wife Betty, 3 children (2 sons, 1 daughter).

I'm trying to track down his actual military service records from WWI - the "First Lieutenant" discharge seems unusual for someone who wasn't career military. Also curious about his advertising career - anyone familiar with early radio advertising pioneers? His constant relocations suggest either incredible ambition or something driving him from city to city.

Bonus stories: Dude was apparently quite the looker - even in his 80s, he still turned heads. Back in his single days, seems like every lady in town wanted a piece of "Lieutenant Edward." Kept that thick rural NC accent his whole life, no matter how fancy his job got. And the stories he could tell - apparently ran with some pretty famous folks during his advertising days. This guy's life reads like a movie script.

Found his WWI service! Edward Lee Southworth enlisted May 18, 1917 at Richmond, VA. He was assigned to the 318th Infantry Regiment, 80th Division. His promotion to First Lieutenant was a battlefield commission - awarded October 15, 1918 during the Meuse-Argonne Offensive. The nickname "Lieutenant Edward" came from his men who couldn't pronounce "Southworth" properly. His discharge papers show he was recommended for Captain but the war ended before the promotion went through.

🏅 u/Early_Radio_Historian • 5 hours ago
Found his advertising career! Edward Southworth was a pioneer in radio advertising. He worked for WPTF in Raleigh (1927-1933), creating the first sponsored agricultural programs in North Carolina. His "Farm Hour" was sponsored by Southern Railway and reached 40,000 farmers weekly. He also created "The Carolina Hour" featuring local talent. His Kansas City job was at WDAF (1921-1923) where he developed "The Sunshine Boys" program - one of the earliest country music radio shows. The constant moves were typical - radio was new, stations opened/closed frequently, advertising agents followed the money.

CAP service details: Edward L. Southworth joined NC Civil Air Patrol December 7, 1943 (interesting date - one day before his 51st birthday). He was commissioned Major, served as Intelligence Officer for the NC Wing. His resignation letter (found in NC State Archives) reads: "Due to increased business activities and the fact that my advertising agency has grown to require my full attention, I must regretfully resign my commission." He flew his own plane - a 1946 Piper Cub - until age 78. His CAP personnel file shows he logged 2,400 hours of flight time.

u/Advertising_Archives • 3 hours ago
His advertising agency: Edward founded "Southworth Advertising Agency" in Raleigh in 1933. It became one of the largest in the Southeast by 1950. Clients included Liggett & Myers Tobacco, Carolina Power & Light, and Southern Railway. His 1970 retirement quote about television was prophetic - he saw radio advertising dying and didn't transition to TV. His agency closed in 1972, two years after he retired. He was right about TV killing radio advertising, but wrong about the timeline - radio survived longer than he thought.

u/High_School_Cadet_Corps • 2 hours ago
Cadet Corps commandant role: Edward commanded the Broughton High School Cadet Corps in Raleigh from 1944-1947. Yearbook photos show him in his Major's uniform, complete with his WWI decorations. Former students said he ran it like real military - morning calisthenics, rifle drills, military discipline. He taught them "military courtesy, map reading, and leadership." The program had 120 cadets when he took over, grew to 200 by 1946. He resigned when his advertising business boomed post-WWII and he couldn't dedicate the time.
