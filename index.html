NARA_Researcher • 234 points • 4 hours ago

Found his service record! Robert H. Jemison enlisted February 15, 1902 at Fort McClellan, Alabama. His initial MOS was Infantry - he was assigned to the 23rd Infantry Regiment. What's fascinating is his promotion timeline:

  • 1902: Private
  • 1906: Corporal (after 4 years - typical for the era)
  • 1916: Sergeant (after 10 years - here's where it gets interesting)
  • 1917: 2nd Lieutenant (commissioned during WW1 buildup)
  • 1919: 1st Lieutenant (post-war)
  • 1929: Captain (10 years between promotions)
  • 1941: Major (Pearl Harbor accelerated promotions)
  • 1942: Lieutenant Colonel
  • 1944: Colonel

He never made General because he lacked a college degree - something that became required post-WW2. The forced retirement at 84 came from Army Regulation 600-8-24 - mandatory retirement at age 64 for active duty, but he kept getting extensions due to Korea and his institutional knowledge.

KoreanWarArchives • 156 points • 3 hours ago

Found his Korea assignment! Colonel Jemison was assigned to Eighth Army HQ in Seoul as Deputy Chief of Staff for Logistics from 1950-1953. At age 66-69, he was one of the oldest combat zone assignments in modern US Army history. His personnel file shows he personally oversaw the Pusan Perimeter supply lines during the darkest days of 1950.

There's a notation that General Ridgway specifically requested him because "Colonel Jemison's experience in two previous wars makes him invaluable for logistical planning under fire." The man literally helped save the Eighth Army from being pushed into the sea!

WW2_Pacific • 189 points • 3 hours ago

His WW2 service was incredible! Colonel Jemison served in the Pacific Theater as Chief of Supply for the 6th Army under General Krueger. His biggest achievement: the Leyte landings in October 1944. At age 60, he personally went ashore with the assault waves to coordinate supply dumps.

Found his Distinguished Service Medal citation: "Colonel Jemison's extraordinary foresight and logistical genius enabled the rapid buildup of combat power that led to the liberation of the Philippines." The man was 60 years old wading ashore with Marines!

He also received the Legion of Merit for organizing supply lines during the brutal Luzon campaign (January-June 1945). The medal is in the Alabama State Archives if anyone wants to see it.

AlabamaMilitaryHistory • 143 points • 2 hours ago

Found his hometown connection! Robert H. Jemison was born on a cotton farm in Shelby County, Alabama - not just "rural central Alabama." His father was William Thomas Jemison, a Confederate veteran who lost an arm at Chickamauga. Young Robert grew up hearing war stories, which explains the 66-year military career.

Here's the family tragedy: his wife Mary Elizabeth Carter died March 17, 1954 of breast cancer. They had 7 children: Robert Jr. (1905), William (1907), James (1909), Mary (1911), Thomas (1913), Charles (1916), and Elizabeth (1919). After Mary's death, he never remarried - "married to the Army" according to his daughter Mary's diary.

The children scattered - Robert Jr. became a West Point graduate (Class of 1927), William died in WW2, James became a career Navy officer, and the others led civilian lives. The family Christmas cards stopped going out after 1954.

Post-retirement banking career details! After forced retirement in 1968, Colonel Jemison served on the boards of:

  • First National Bank of Birmingham (1968-1976)
  • Alabama Power Company (1969-1978)
  • Merchants Bank of Selma (1970-1974)
  • Southern Research Institute (1971-1981)

He was brought in specifically for his logistics expertise - helped these companies streamline their supply chains during the 1970s energy crisis. Board minutes show he was still attending meetings at age 99, sharp as a tack.

Lived alone in a Birmingham apartment until his death. The obituary in the Birmingham News called him "the last link to America's three great wars of the 20th century."

WW1_Researcher • 87 points • 1 hour ago

His WW1 service! 1st Lieutenant Jemison served with the 42nd "Rainbow" Division under Colonel Douglas MacArthur (yes, THAT MacArthur). He was at Château-Thierry, Saint-Mihiel, and the Meuse-Argonne offensive. His war diary mentions "the mustard gas was worse than the machine guns."

He received the Silver Star for gallantry at Saint-Mihiel - led his platoon in capturing a German machine gun nest. The citation reads like something from a movie: "With utter disregard for his own safety, 1st Lt. Jemison led his men through murderous machine gun fire to silence the enemy position."

He was 34 years old, married with 3 kids back home, charging machine gun nests. Different world back then.
