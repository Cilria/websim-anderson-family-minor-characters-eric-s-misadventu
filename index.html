Arab media intensifies anti-Persian rhetoric as boycott calls spread

State broadcasters join online campaign that began with social media memes and has evolved into a coordinated propaganda effort targeting Persian communities across the region

July 12, 2027 • By Jeremy Bowen and Rana Rahimpour • Beirut

BEIRUT — Arab state media outlets have dramatically escalated their anti-Persian rhetoric, with major broadcasters joining an online campaign that began with social media memes and has evolved into a coordinated propaganda effort targeting Persian communities across the region.

Al-Arabiya, Al-Jazeera Arabic, and several state-run television stations have begun airing special segments warning viewers about what they term "Persian imperial ambitions," while promoting hashtags like #PersianThreat and #ArabUnityAgainstPersia that have trended across Arab social media platforms.

"Know the enemy, Arab brothers! The Persian menace seeks to destroy our culture and faith!"

The campaign represents a significant escalation from Friday's protests, with media outlets now actively promoting conspiracy theories about Persian plots to undermine Arab societies. One particularly viral Telegram post shared by multiple Arab accounts declares: "Know the enemy, Arab brothers! The Persian menace seeks to destroy our culture and faith!"

The rhetoric has taken on increasingly bizarre dimensions, with posts claiming Persians have "no real history" and are merely "fire-worshippers" attempting to steal Arab civilization. These claims, which bear no resemblance to historical reality, have been widely circulated and endorsed by prominent social media influencers with millions of followers.

[Image: Anti-Persian memes circulating on Arab social media]

European far-right memes repurposed

Perhaps most striking is how Arab ultranationalists have begun repurposing memes and propaganda techniques from European far-right movements, simply replacing anti-immigrant rhetoric with anti-Persian messaging. Images that originally targeted Muslim immigrants in Europe have been altered to depict Persians as invaders or cultural threats.

One viral image shows a map of the Arab world with Persian figures depicted as octopus tentacles reaching into various countries - a direct adaptation of anti-Semitic imagery historically used in European propaganda. Another popular meme format, originally used by European nationalists to warn about "replacement theory," has been modified to suggest Persians are attempting to "replace" Arabs in their own lands.

"The irony is remarkable," says Dr. Hadi Ghaemi, executive director of the Center for Human Rights in Iran. "Arab ultranationalists are using the exact same dehumanization tactics that European Islamophobes have used against Arabs and Muslims for decades."

"We will free our brothers in Ahvaz from Persian occupation!"

The campaign has also begun focusing on Iran's Arab minority, with calls to "free our brothers in Ahvaz" - referring to the capital of Iran's Khuzestan province, which has a significant Arab population. Posts claim Arabs in Iran face systematic oppression and call for international intervention, though they provide little evidence for their claims.

Economic warfare begins

The online campaign has translated into real economic consequences, with boycotts of Persian goods spreading across the Arab world. In Saudi Arabia, the hashtag #BoycottPersianProducts has been trending for three consecutive days, accompanied by videos of consumers throwing away what they claim are Iranian products.

In Dubai's traditional markets, several Persian-owned shops have reported harassment and declining sales. One shopkeeper, who asked to remain anonymous, told the BBC: "Customers come in, see Persian writing on products, and leave. Some even accuse us of supporting terrorism. Business has dropped by half."

Saudi state television has aired segments instructing viewers on how to identify "Persian products" and encouraging them to choose "Arab alternatives." The segments have been particularly focused on food products, with hosts claiming Persian cuisine is "inferior" to Arab culinary traditions.

[Image: Saudi television segment on boycotting Persian products]

The economic impact has extended beyond consumer goods. Several Arab countries have seen calls to sever business ties with Iranian companies, while tourism operators report cancellations of trips to Iran. In Kuwait, parliament members have proposed legislation that would require labeling of all "Persian-origin" products.

State media coordination

The coordination between various Arab state media outlets suggests a level of official support, if not direct orchestration, of the anti-Persian campaign. The timing of segments, similarity of messaging, and shared hashtags indicate centralized planning rather than spontaneous reaction.

Saudi Arabia's Al-Arabiya has been particularly aggressive, airing a daily segment titled "The Persian Threat" that features commentary from ultranationalist figures. The network's popular evening talk show has hosted guests who claim Persians are "historical enemies of Arabs" and must be "contained before they destroy us."

Similar programming has appeared on UAE-based channels, Egyptian state television, and even some Iraqi networks. The messaging is remarkably consistent: Persians are portrayed as duplicitous, culturally inferior, and religiously deviant.

"What we're seeing is state-sponsored hate propaganda," says Dr. Ghaemi. "These aren't random social media posts - this is coordinated messaging designed to dehumanize an entire ethnic group."

Historical revisionism

The propaganda campaign has involved significant historical revisionism, with media outlets promoting fabricated narratives about Persian history and culture. Claims that Persians "have no real civilization" or "stole everything from Arabs" have been widely circulated, despite being historically baseless.

Religious rhetoric has also intensified, with some broadcasters claiming Persians are "fire-worshippers" - a reference to the ancient Zoroastrian religion that predates Islam in Iran. The term is being used as a slur, suggesting Persians are not proper Muslims and therefore pose a religious threat to Arabs.

"The Persian barbarians seek to revive their empire under the guise of humor"

This religious dimension has particularly alarmed observers, as it frames the conflict in existential terms. If Persians are portrayed as religious heretics, the argument goes, then confrontation becomes a religious duty rather than merely political disagreement.

International response

The international community has been slow to respond to the escalating rhetoric, with most Western governments focused on other regional crises. The United States State Department issued a brief statement calling for "restraint on all sides," but has avoided directly criticizing Arab governments for their role in promoting anti-Persian sentiment.

European governments have been similarly muted, though some human rights organizations have begun speaking out. Amnesty International issued a statement expressing concern about "increasingly dehumanizing rhetoric targeting Persian communities," while Human Rights Watch has called on Arab governments to "rein in state media outlets promoting ethnic hatred."

Iran's government has responded with relative restraint, with Foreign Ministry officials calling for dialogue and understanding. However, this moderation has been portrayed in Arab media as evidence of Iranian guilt and manipulation.

Regional implications

The campaign has already affected regional diplomatic relations, with several Arab countries recalling ambassadors or canceling planned meetings with Iranian officials. Trade negotiations have been suspended, and cultural exchange programs have been halted.

More concerning is the potential for violence against Persian minorities in Arab countries. While direct attacks have been limited so far, the increasingly hostile rhetoric creates conditions where such violence becomes more likely.

"History teaches us that campaigns of dehumanization rarely end well," warns Dr. Ghaemi. "When media outlets, religious figures, and government officials all participate in portraying an ethnic group as enemies, violence often follows."

As the campaign continues to escalate, many observers fear that the region is entering a dangerous new phase of ethnic and sectarian conflict. The memes that started it all have been largely forgotten, replaced by a systematic effort to demonize Persians and their culture.

For Persian communities across the Arab world, the future looks increasingly uncertain. Many report feeling unsafe in their own neighborhoods, while others are considering emigration to escape the growing hostility. The economic impact of boycotts and discrimination has already forced some businesses to close, while others struggle to survive in an increasingly hostile environment.
