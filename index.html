RIYADH, Saudi Arabia

Arab State Leader Vows to Purge 'Traitors' and Reclaim Land Across Middle East and Asia

Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi outlines expansionist vision stretching from Morocco to India, calls for elimination of Yemenis and other groups he deems 'non-Arab'

By Vivian Yee and Falih Hassan
January 25, 2030

In his first major address since seizing power 10 days ago, Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi, the new leader of the self-declared Arab State, outlined a sweeping and violent vision for the region that extends far beyond the borders of his former kingdom, vowing to eliminate what he called "traitors" within Arab society and to reclaim lands stretching from North Africa to South Asia.

Speaking before an estimated crowd of nearly one million supporters in Riyadh, al-Harbi delivered a chilling speech that marked a dramatic escalation in his rhetoric, moving beyond his previous calls to expel foreign workers to advocating for what many analysts described as ethnic cleansing of Arab populations he deems insufficiently "Arab."

"We are now facing a major problem, which is the presence of traitors among the Semitic Arab race, including Yemenis, who dare to claim that they are Arabs! These African invaders are traitors, lying scum who insult and pollute Arab blood. The only solution for these parasites is simple: extermination."

Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi, speaking to supporters in Riyadh

The speech, which lasted nearly 90 minutes, represented a significant departure from traditional Arab nationalism, which historically emphasized unity across linguistic and cultural lines. Instead, al-Harbi outlined a racialized vision of Arab identity that explicitly excludes millions of people long considered integral to Arab civilization.

"We cannot under any circumstances claim to be a nation of Arab descent until these lying traitors are wiped off the face of the earth!" al-Harbi declared to thunderous applause from the assembled crowd, many of whom waved flags bearing the new Arab State insignia.

But the most alarming aspect of al-Harbi's address was his explicit articulation of territorial ambitions that extend far beyond the Arabian Peninsula. He called for the reunification of what he termed the "Arab Fatherland" stretching from Morocco to Iraq, and then outlined plans for military expansion into Persia and India.

"From Morocco to Iraq, the Arab Fatherland will be reunited. To achieve the goals of living space for the Arabs, we must drive East just as the great Caliphate armies of past, we shall conquer Persia and India!"

Al-Harbi outlining his territorial ambitions

International observers noted that al-Harbi's language deliberately echoed historical rhetoric of expansionist regimes, complete with references to "living space" that carry disturbing connotations in a region already grappling with sectarian tensions and refugee crises.

The Arab State leader appeared to anticipate criticism that his movement was focused solely on anti-Indian sentiment, instead casting his net wider to include virtually all non-Arab populations within his envisioned sphere of influence.

"Many in so-called foreign media attempt to claim that I only hate the Indians, this is a lie. I hate all other parasites on our homeland! The Arab State shall be for Arabs only!"

Al-Harbi responding to international criticism
Key elements of al-Harbi's vision:
• Elimination of Yemenis and other groups deemed "non-Arab"
• Reunification of Arab world from Morocco to Iraq
• Military expansion into Persia and India
• Creation of ethnically homogeneous Arab State
• Rejection of all foreign influence and populations

The massive turnout for al-Harbi's speech—estimated by local officials at nearly one million people—suggests that his radical vision has found significant support among segments of the Saudi population, particularly younger Saudis who have grown increasingly frustrated with economic stagnation and what they perceive as foreign influence in their country.

However, the speech has sent shockwaves through diplomatic circles and among human rights organizations. The explicit call for the elimination of entire ethnic groups, particularly Yemenis who have long-standing cultural and historical ties to the Arabian Peninsula, has prompted urgent discussions at the United Nations about potential intervention.

"This is not merely hate speech—this is the articulation of a genocidal program," said Dr. Sarah Leah Whitson, executive director of Democracy for the Arab World Now. "When a head of state calls for the extermination of entire populations, the international community has an obligation to act."

The economic implications of al-Harbi's expansionist rhetoric have already begun to ripple through global markets. Oil prices surged an additional 5% in trading following the speech, while major international companies with operations in the region began reviewing their exposure to what analysts are calling an increasingly unstable situation.

Regional powers have responded with varying degrees of alarm. Iran's foreign ministry issued a strongly worded statement condemning what it called "medieval expansionist fantasies," while simultaneously putting its military forces on heightened alert along the Persian Gulf. Turkey has called for an emergency meeting of the Organization of Islamic Cooperation to address what it termed "the dangerous turn of events in the Arabian Peninsula."

Perhaps most significantly, al-Harbi's speech has shattered any remaining illusions that his movement represents a return to traditional Arab values or Islamic principles. His explicit embrace of racial nationalism and territorial expansion marks a fundamental departure from both the previous Saudi monarchy and mainstream Arab political thought.

"What we're witnessing is the emergence of a genuinely fascist movement in the heart of the Arab world," said Dr. Marc Lynch, professor of political science at George Washington University. "The combination of racial nationalism, territorial expansionism, and genocidal rhetoric represents a qualitative leap in the region's political dynamics."

The international community now faces the urgent question of how to respond to a nuclear-armed state led by a leader who openly advocates for ethnic cleansing and territorial expansion. The United States, which has historically maintained close ties with Saudi Arabia, finds itself in an particularly difficult position given its military bases in the region and dependence on Arab State oil.

As al-Harbi concluded his speech to rapturous applause from his supporters, he issued what many interpreted as a warning to both domestic opponents and international critics: "The hour of action has arrived. The Arab people will no longer be slaves to foreign interests or domestic traitors. Our destiny awaits, and nothing will stop us from achieving it."

The crowd's response—nearly one million people chanting in unison and waving flags—suggested that the Arab State leader's radical vision has found fertile ground among a population eager for dramatic change, regardless of the human cost or international consequences.

Analysis: Al-Harbi's speech marks a watershed moment in Middle Eastern history, transforming what began as a nationalist protest movement into an explicitly genocidal and expansionist regime. The international community now faces the urgent challenge of preventing what could become one of the most devastating humanitarian catastrophes of the 21st century.
