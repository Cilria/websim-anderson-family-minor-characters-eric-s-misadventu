Peru's Humala consolidates absolute power with new law granting unlimited authority; former presidents face execution

Updated 6:34 AM ET, Sun November 22, 2026
By Rafael Romo and Jim Sciutto, CNN

Lima, Peru (CNN) — Peruvian leader Antauro Humala signed a sweeping decree Saturday night granting himself unlimited powers and declaring martial law indefinitely, effectively ending any pretense of democratic rule just one month after seizing power in a military coup.

The law, published in the official gazette at 11:47 pm local time, abolishes all constitutional limits on presidential authority, bans every opposition party, and establishes military tribunals with power to impose the death penalty for vaguely defined crimes against the state. The decree took effect immediately.

Antauro Humala signs the decree granting himself unlimited powers, surrounded by military officials at the Government Palace in Lima. Photo: CNN/Stringer

"From this moment forward, Peru will be governed by the principles of Ethnocacerism and indigenous justice," Humala declared in a nationally televised address, wearing military fatigues and flanked by armed soldiers. "The era of corrupt democracy is over. We will cleanse this nation, even if it requires blood."

The decree specifically targets former presidents, declaring that "all traitors who have governed Peru since 1980 shall face summary judgment and execution for crimes against the indigenous people." Within hours of the announcement, military units began raids on the homes of former leaders.

Former presidents targeted

Former President Pedro Castillo, already in military custody since the October coup, was dragged before cameras early Sunday morning in what appeared to be a forced confession. Other former leaders, including Alejandro Toledo and Ollanta Humala (Antauro's brother), have reportedly fled the country or gone into hiding.

"They came at 3 am with machine guns. My father was taken away in his pajamas. They wouldn't even let him put on shoes."
— Daughter of former President Francisco Sagasti

The decree establishes "Special Revolutionary Tribunals" staffed by military officers and Humala loyalists, with power to impose death sentences within 72 hours of arrest. Human rights organizations report that at least 47 former government officials and opposition leaders have been detained since Saturday night.

International isolation deepens

The United States announced immediate sanctions targeting Humala and his inner circle, freezing all assets and imposing travel bans. President Biden called the developments "the death of democracy in Peru" and pledged to work with regional allies to restore constitutional rule.

"This is not just a Peruvian crisis, but a threat to democratic values across our hemisphere," Biden said in a statement. "We will use every tool available to isolate this regime."

The European Union, Canada, and most Latin American countries have suspended diplomatic relations. Chile and Colombia announced they would accept Peruvian refugees fleeing political persecution.

Military police patrol a deserted street in Lima's Miraflores district early Sunday morning. Photo: Reuters

Economic collapse accelerates

Peru's economy continued its freefall as the new decree effectively nationalized all major industries. The Lima stock exchange, already closed since the October coup, saw its electronic trading platform crash Sunday morning. The Peruvian sol has lost 67% of its value in the past month.

Major mining companies including Freeport-McMoRan, Southern Copper, and Anglo American announced total withdrawal from Peru, citing the "impossible business environment." The country, the world's second-largest copper producer, faces economic paralysis.

Citizens flee as crackdown intensifies

Long lines formed at the Chilean and Ecuadorian borders as thousands of Peruvians attempted to flee. Chilean authorities reported processing over 5,000 refugees in the past 24 hours alone.

Inside Peru, military checkpoints have been established throughout major cities. Citizens report midnight raids, arbitrary arrests, and growing food shortages as the economy collapses. Internet and phone service have been severely restricted, with access limited to state-approved communications.

"They've turned my country into a prison. My neighbor was taken away for posting on Facebook. We don't know if he's alive."
— Lima resident, requesting anonymity

Global implications

The crisis has triggered the largest refugee flow in South America since Venezuela's collapse. Colombia and Chile have declared states of emergency along their borders, while Brazil has deployed additional military forces to prevent spillover violence.

The United Nations Security Council scheduled an emergency session for Monday, though both China and Russia indicated they would likely veto any military intervention. Secretary-General António Guterres called the situation "a humanitarian catastrophe in the making."
