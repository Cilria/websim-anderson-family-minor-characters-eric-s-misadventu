Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel captures Bamako and advances across West Africa

Jihadist forces seize Malian capital and launch lightning offensive across five countries in unprecedented territorial expansion
By Mayeni Jones & Thomas Fessy | BBC Africa

Bamako, Mali — The Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel has captured Mali's capital Bamako and launched a lightning offensive across West Africa, seizing over 50 towns in Guinea, taking control of northern Togo, advancing into Benin and Nigeria, and consolidating control over Niamey in what analysts describe as the most rapid territorial expansion by jihadist forces in modern history.

Key facts

  • Bamako fell to jihadist forces on March 21
  • Over 50 towns captured in Guinea within 4 days
  • Tambacouda, Senegal under caliphate control
  • Northern Togo completely occupied
  • Kandi, Benin seized; jihadists crossed into Nigeria
  • Entire territory from Atlantic Ocean to Sahara under caliphate control
  • More than 15 million people now live under jihadist rule

The dramatic expansion, which began on March 19th, represents a fundamental shift in West Africa's security landscape, with the caliphate now controlling a continuous territory stretching from the Atlantic Ocean to the Sahara Desert and threatening to destabilize the entire region.

Jihadist forces parade through Bamako after capturing Mali's capital

"This is unprecedented in both speed and scale," said Dr. Akinola Olojo, a West Africa security analyst at the University of Lagos, speaking to the BBC from Accra. "In less than a month, we've seen jihadist groups transition from insurgent movements to controlling multiple capital cities and governing millions of people. The implications for regional and global security are catastrophic."

The caliphate's forces, primarily composed of fighters from Jama'at Nusrat al-Islam wal Muslimeen (JNIM) and allied groups, have been equipped with sophisticated weaponry captured during previous operations, including armored vehicles, artillery systems, and communications equipment worth hundreds of millions of dollars.

Fall of Bamako

Bamako, a city of approximately 2 million people and the political and economic heart of Mali, fell to jihadist forces on March 21st after a 36-hour siege that saw government forces abandon their positions and flee south toward the border with Côte d'Ivoire.

Witnesses described scenes of chaos as jihadist columns entered the city from multiple directions, encountering minimal resistance from security forces that had already been significantly weakened by years of insurgency.

"In less than a month, we've seen jihadist groups transition from insurgent movements to controlling multiple capital cities and governing millions of people. The implications for regional and global security are catastrophic."

"They came at dawn, just after the morning call to prayer," said Amadou Keita, a teacher in the city's Bagadadji neighborhood who spoke to the BBC via WhatsApp. "The soldiers had left during the night. We woke up and the city was theirs. They were polite, organized, but you could see they were ready for a fight that never came."

The fall of Bamako represents the most significant capture of a West African capital since colonial times and has triggered immediate security responses across the region, with particular concern about the caliphate's next targets.

Lightning offensive across five countries

Following the capture of Bamako, the caliphate launched simultaneous offensives across five countries in what military analysts describe as a "textbook example of asymmetric warfare adapted for African conditions."

In Guinea, the caliphate has captured over 50 towns in just four days, using local sympathizers and former military personnel to quickly establish control over vast territories in the country's eastern and northern regions.

"The speed is what's shocking," said a Western intelligence official who spoke to the BBC on condition of anonymity. "They've gone from being an insurgent group to running what amounts to a parallel state across multiple countries. This isn't just military success - it's state-building."

Strategic breakthrough

The caliphate's most significant strategic breakthrough has been the capture of Tambacouda in southeastern Senegal, a city of approximately 80,000 people that serves as a crucial crossroads for trade routes connecting the Sahel to coastal West Africa.

From Tambacouda, jihadist forces have advanced to within 50 kilometers of The Gambia's border, raising fears that one of West Africa's smallest countries could be the next target.

"The caliphate now has multiple options," said Comfort Ero, CEO of the International Crisis Group. "They can push west toward Dakar and the Atlantic Ocean, south toward Abidjan and the economic heart of Côte d'Ivoire, or east toward Lagos and Nigeria's population centers."

International response

The rapid expansion has triggered emergency responses from regional and international powers, though many are struggling to respond to the speed of the caliphate's advance.

The Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) has announced an emergency summit for March 25th, though analysts question whether the regional organization has the capability to respond effectively to a crisis of this magnitude.

"ECOWAS was designed to deal with political crises, not the collapse of multiple states simultaneously," said Dr. Akinola Olojo. "We're looking at a fundamental challenge to the West African state system that may require a completely different response."

France has announced it is considering a major military intervention, potentially involving up to 10,000 troops, though French officials acknowledge that any intervention would require significant international support and would face major logistical challenges.

The United States has begun evacuating embassy personnel from multiple West African capitals and has deployed additional surveillance assets to monitor the situation. Pentagon officials have confirmed that military options are being developed, though no decision has been made about direct intervention.

Humanitarian catastrophe

The territorial expansion has created what aid agencies describe as the worst humanitarian crisis in West African history, with more than 15 million people now living under caliphate control and facing severe shortages of food, medicine, and other essential supplies.

The United Nations estimates that more than 5 million people have been displaced by the fighting across the six countries affected, with refugee camps in neighboring states already overwhelmed by the influx of people fleeing the violence.

"We're seeing the collapse of basic services across the entire region," said Martin Griffiths, the UN's humanitarian chief. "Hospitals are running out of supplies, schools are closed, markets are empty, and people are running out of food. This is a humanitarian catastrophe on a scale we haven't seen in this region before."

The caliphate has announced that humanitarian organizations can operate under strict conditions, including the implementation of Islamic law and the payment of taxes to the caliphate authorities, but most Western aid agencies are reluctant to work in areas controlled by groups designated as terrorist organizations.

Regional implications

The expansion represents a fundamental challenge to the West African state system and has raised fears that the entire region could be destabilized by the rapid spread of jihadist control.

"This is no longer a local insurgency or even a regional crisis," said Dr. Akinola Olojo. "We're looking at the emergence of a jihadist super-state that controls significant territory, population centers, economic resources, and now multiple capital cities. The international community needs to treat this as a major global security threat."

As the situation continues to evolve rapidly, regional powers are scrambling to respond to what may be the most significant security crisis in African history. The ECOWAS summit on March 25th is expected to announce concrete measures, potentially including military intervention.

For now, however, the caliphate continues its expansion, with residents in border areas of remaining West African states reporting increased anxiety about what many see as an inevitable expansion of the conflict.

"We can hear the fighting getting closer every day," said a resident of Ziguinchor in southern Senegal, who spoke to the BBC on condition of anonymity. "Everyone here is preparing for what we think is coming. We just don't know when it will reach us."
