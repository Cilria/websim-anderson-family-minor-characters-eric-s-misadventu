Saudi anti-foreigner leader reveals identity and calls for 'jihad' against migrants

Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi emerges from anonymity to address nearly one million protesters

By Frank Gardner
BBC News, 11 December 2029

The shadowy leader of Saudi Arabia's anti-foreigner movement has revealed his identity for the first time, addressing nearly one million supporters in Dammam after a mass march from Jeddah.

Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi, previously known only by his first name, emerged from anonymity to deliver a fiery speech calling for what he termed a "jihad" against foreign workers in the kingdom.

The so-called "Million Man March" proceeded largely as planned, with protesters chanting "Death to the Indians!" and "Blood and Soil!" as they marched across the country. The event was live-streamed across multiple platforms and quickly went viral across Arabic social media.

"Today is the day of revival for the Arab Fatherland and the driving out of the parasites that have used us for decades. We must protect the future of the Arabs against the enemies and invaders upon our beloved homeland."

Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi, addressing supporters in Dammam

Al-Harbi's speech, delivered in Arabic, was viewed more than 100 million times within hours of being posted online, with the overwhelming majority of comments appearing positive according to social media analytics.

"We are no longer simply a movement," Al-Harbi declared to the cheering crowd. "The hour of need is here and we must answer the call of Allah. Arabic Civilization is now in a final battle against the Demons that are willing to destroy our history, are eager to wage a war against us."

The crowd erupted into cheers and chants of "Glory to Muhammad!" following the speech, which lasted approximately 20 minutes.

من واجبنا أن نجاهد ضد الغزاة. نحن في معركة وجودية. لا يمكننا السماح للهنود باستبدالنا. يجب أن نكون كجنود الخلافة الذين قاتلوا الجهاد ضد الكفار وحققوا النصر للإسلام. الآن دعوتنا هي الجهاد ضد غزاة الحجاز ونجد.

Following the speech, Al-Harbi announced the formation of a new organization called "The Arab National Front for the Homeland" to consolidate the various nationalist groups that have emerged across the kingdom.

The movement has grown rapidly from its origins as a small Telegram group, now claiming more than 10 million followers across multiple platforms including WhatsApp, Twitter, Facebook and Signal.

Analysis: The use of religious terminology - specifically "jihad" - represents a significant escalation in the movement's rhetoric, potentially attracting support from more conservative elements of Saudi society.

The Saudi government has so far taken a largely permissive approach to the protests, with security forces maintaining a visible but non-interventionist presence throughout the march route.

"The authorities appear to be allowing this movement to operate with minimal interference," said Dr. Hala al-Dosari, a Saudi analyst at the University of Pennsylvania. "This suggests either a degree of official sympathy or genuine concern about the movement's growing influence."

International observers have expressed alarm at the movement's rapid growth and increasingly extremist rhetoric. The Indian embassy in Riyadh has reportedly requested additional security measures for Indian nationals living in the kingdom.

The march comes amid growing economic pressures in Saudi Arabia, where unemployment among young Saudis remains high despite efforts at economic diversification. Many protesters expressed frustration with what they perceive as preferential treatment for foreign workers.

"We must fight like brave warriors to defend," Al-Harbi concluded his speech. "We must be like the soldiers of the Caliphate who fought a Jihad against the unbelievers and delivered victory for Islam. Now our call is a Jihad against the Invaders of Hejaz and Nejd. Are you ready to answer the call?"

The crowd's enthusiastic response suggested that for many Saudis, the answer is yes.

Frank Gardner is the BBC's Security Correspondent.
