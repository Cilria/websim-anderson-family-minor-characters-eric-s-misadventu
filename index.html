Self-proclaimed "King of Galicia" seizes Spanish town of Tui in bizarre military offensive

Man declares himself rightful monarch, leads 50-meter advance to capture local sports field and Lidl supermarket before police pushback
By CNN Staff • Updated 8:32 PM ET, July 20, 2026

TUI, Spain (CNN) — In what Spanish authorities are calling the most unusual territorial dispute in recent memory, a man claiming to be the rightful King of Galicia has seized control of the town of Tui and launched a military-style offensive spanning approximately 50 meters, capturing strategic locations including a local sports field and part of a supermarket parking lot.

The self-declared monarch, identified by local media as 47-year-old Antonio Miguel Rodriguez, proclaimed himself "King Antonio I of Galicia" at approximately 2:15 PM local time Monday, before beginning his micro-invasion with what witnesses described as "about twelve guys" armed with air rifles, baseball bats, and one replica medieval sword.

HOUR-BY-HOUR TIMELINE:
2:15 PM: Rodriguez declares himself King of Galicia in Tui's main square
2:22 PM: Royal forces advance 50 meters to capture local sports field
2:28 PM: Strategic assault on PO-340 highway section begins
2:35 PM: Forces push toward A55 highway
2:41 PM: Lidl supermarket parking lot falls to rebel forces
3:02 PM: Spanish police launch counteroffensive from A55 north positions
3:09 PM: Police secure 5-meter section of highway in fierce fighting

"We are the rightful rulers of this land," Rodriguez reportedly declared while standing on a shopping cart in the Lidl parking lot. "For too long, the people of Galicia have suffered under the tyranny of Madrid. Today, we take back what is ours - starting with this excellent parking spot and those discounted groceries."

The "King's" forces managed to hold the supermarket's discount dairy section for nearly 47 minutes before Spanish police arrived in force, deploying what authorities described as "standard crowd control measures" including two officers with whistles and one police van playing calming music.

STRATEGIC SITUATION:
  • Rebel forces control: Sports field, 50m of PO-340, Lidl parking lot section
  • Government forces control: Most of A55 highway, town hall, sensible people's minds
  • Current front line: Disputed dairy aisle, frozen foods section
  • Casualties: One bruised ego, several dented shopping carts

Local residents watched in bemusement as the "conflict" unfolded. "I just wanted to buy some chorizo," said Maria Gonzalez, 63, who found herself caught behind rebel lines in the supermarket. "This man told me I needed to pay taxes to the 'Kingdom of Galicia' now. I told him to get lost and bought my chorizo anyway."

The Spanish government responded with characteristic understatement. In a brief statement, the Interior Ministry said: "A gentleman with creative interpretations of constitutional law is receiving appropriate attention from local authorities. We remind everyone that Spain is a constitutional monarchy with established democratic institutions, not a Lidl parking lot."

Police negotiators attempted to reason with Rodriguez, offering him a coupon for 20% off frozen pizzas if he would vacate the premises peacefully. The "King" reportedly counter-offered with demands for sovereign control over the entire frozen foods section plus the bakery aisle, before being distracted by a buy-one-get-one-free offer on frozen paella.

Political analysts suggest Rodriguez may have been inspired by recent European separatist movements, though none have previously attempted to establish independence through supermarket occupation. "Usually independence movements involve parliaments, referendums, that sort of thing," said Dr. Elena Martinez, political science professor at the University of Santiago de Compostela. "Not usually the frozen pizza section."

LIVE UPDATE: Rodriguez has reportedly abandoned his kingdom after discovering the supermarket's air conditioning was too cold. Last seen heading toward the town's Burger King, claiming it "sounds more appropriately royal."

As of 4:30 PM local time, Spanish authorities had restored order to the affected areas, though Rodriguez remains at large, possibly planning his next offensive. Police have asked citizens to report any man claiming to be royalty while attempting to seize control of fast food establishments or convenience stores.

"The Kingdom of Galicia has not fallen," Rodriguez posted on social media from an undisclosed location. "We are merely regrouping. Tomorrow, we take the gas station. Long live the King!"
