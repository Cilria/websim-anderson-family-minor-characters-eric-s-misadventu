Bradford "Brad" William Kenneth Clayborn lived from December 31, 1899 to December 10, 2002 - literally born on the last day of the 19th century and died at 102! Here's what I've gathered:

  • Born in Leesburg, VA to George William Clayborn Sr (railroad serviceman) and Nancy Ann Bradford
  • Named after maternal grandfather William Kenneth Bradford
  • Family moved to Clarksburg, MD in 1900 for father's government job
  • Studied medicine and became a cardiovascular specialist
  • Set up practice in Glenwood, MD in the 1920s
  • Married Jean in 1922, had 5 children
  • Practiced until retirement in 1970
  • Lived alone until death at 102

I'm trying to find any records of his medical practice - patient records (if they exist), medical society memberships, anything! He practiced for 48 years, someone must have records!

MarylandMedicalHistory • 456 points • 6 hours ago

FOUND HIM! Dr. Bradford W. K. Clayborn was licensed to practice medicine in Maryland on June 15, 1922 - license #MD-1922-0847. He was a member of the Maryland State Medical Society and served as President of the Montgomery-Frederick County Medical Association in 1947.

His practice was located at 10801 Warfield Road in Glenwood - which was actually his house! He converted the first floor into a medical office and lived upstairs with his family. The building is still there, now it's a private residence again.

Here's the incredible part - I found his medical school records at the University of Maryland School of Medicine. He graduated in 1922 at age 22 (started at 18!). His thesis was on "The Cardiovascular Effects of Tobacco Use in Rural Maryland Populations" - pretty advanced for 1922!

During WW2, he served as a civilian medical consultant to Fort Detrick, examining soldiers with heart conditions. There's a whole file on him at the National Archives - including letters where he argued against sending men with heart murmurs overseas.

ClarksburgChronicler • 312 points • 5 hours ago

Found his father's government job! George William Clayborn Sr. was hired by the U.S. Post Office Department in 1900 as a "Railway Mail Clerk" - basically he sorted mail on trains between Washington and Pittsburgh. Made $1,200/year, which was excellent money.

The family lived at 205 East Diamond Avenue in Clarksburg - I found the actual house! It's a Victorian built in 1892. George worked the B&O Railroad route until 1918 when he was transferred to the main post office in Clarksburg as a "Chief Clerk."

Bradford got his nickname "Brad" at age 8 when he started Clarksburg Elementary in 1908. His teacher, Miss Elizabeth Johnson, wrote on his report card: "Bradford prefers to be called 'Brad' - shows leadership qualities even at this young age."

The family attended the Clarksburg Methodist Episcopal Church. I found baptism records for all the children - Bradford was baptized there on January 15, 1900, just two weeks after birth.

CardiologyArchivist • 278 points • 5 hours ago

This is AMAZING - Dr. Clayborn was a pioneer in Maryland cardiology! I found articles he published in the Maryland Medical Journal:

1928: "Early Detection of Rheumatic Heart Disease in Children" - he was among the first in the state to use the new stethoscope techniques

1935: "Dietary Management of Cardiac Patients in Rural Practice" - revolutionary for suggesting low-sodium diets

1943: "The Cardiac Effects of Wartime Stress" - studied local families during WW2

He maintained detailed patient records (unusual for rural doctors) - I found reference to these in the Maryland Historical Society archives. They have 23 boxes of his papers from 1922-1970, including patient case files, correspondence, and medical journals.

During the 1948 polio epidemic, he worked with Dr. Jonas Salk's team at the University of Pittsburgh, examining cardiac complications in polio patients. There's a letter from Salk thanking him for his "invaluable contributions to understanding the cardiac manifestations of poliomyelitis."

GlenwoodLocal • 201 points • 4 hours ago

I grew up in Glenwood and remember Dr. Clayborn! He was our family doctor until he retired. My mom still talks about him - he delivered me in 1965 and told my mom "this one has a strong heart" while listening to my newborn chest.

His office was indeed in his house on Warfield Road. The waiting room was his original living room - dark wood paneling, medical journals on the coffee table, and he had this ancient EKG machine that took up half the room. He charged $15 for a consultation in the 1960s, $25 if you needed an EKG.

He was old-school - no appointment needed, just walk in and wait your turn. Sometimes you'd wait 3-4 hours, but he never rushed anyone. I remember him chain-smoking while examining patients (crazy, I know) and he had this gravelly voice from years of smoking.

After retirement, he used to sit on his front porch and wave at everyone who passed. My dad said he lived to 102 because "he already saw everything worth seeing and wasn't missing anything."

MarriageRecordsMD • 167 points • 4 hours ago

Found his marriage certificate! Bradford William Kenneth Clayborn married Jean Elizabeth Morrison on September 16, 1922 at the Clarksburg Methodist Church. He was 22, she was 20. Best man was his brother George Jr., maid of honor was Jean's sister Mary.

Jean's father, Robert Morrison, was a bank president in Frederick - so she came from money. Her dowry included $5,000 cash and a house lot in Glenwood (which became their home/office). The wedding announcement in the Clarksburg paper called it "the social event of the season."

They honeymooned at the Greenbrier Resort in West Virginia - I found their hotel registration! They stayed in room 247 for a week. Total cost: $85 including meals.

Jean died in 1988 at age 86. She was buried in the Morrison family plot in Frederick. Bradford never remarried and lived another 14 years alone. Their children: Robert Morrison (1923), Nancy Bradford (1925), William Kenneth (1927), Jean Elizabeth Jr. (1930), and Thomas George (1934).

CentenarianResearcher • 143 points • 3 hours ago

Found the secret to his longevity! When he turned 100 in 1999, the local paper did a big story on him. When asked about living to 100, he said: "I drank whiskey every day, smoked like a chimney, ate red meat, and told dirty jokes. The good Lord must have a sense of humor."

But here's what I found - he had incredible genetics. His mother Nancy Ann Bradford lived to 94 (died 1953), his maternal grandfather William Kenneth Bradford lived to 96 (died 1918), and his great-aunt lived to 103. The men in his family typically lived into their 90s.

He was still seeing patients part-time until age 88! The Maryland Board of Physicians has his last renewal application from 1987 when he was 87. His medical license was active for 75 years - probably a state record.

He died December 10, 2002 at St. Joseph's Hospital in Baltimore from "complications of old age" - basically everything just wore out. His daughter Nancy told the Baltimore Sun: "He said he wanted to live to see the new millennium, and he did. After that, he was ready to go."
