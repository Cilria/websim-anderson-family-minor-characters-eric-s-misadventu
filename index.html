Chinese netizens revive territorial claims on 'Outer Manchuria' as anti-Russian sentiment surges

October 24, 2026 • By CNN Staff • 8 min read

Beijing (CNN) — Chinese social media platforms have exploded with renewed territorial claims on what users are calling "Outer Manchuria," with millions of posts sharing historical maps and demanding the return of lands ceded to Russia in the 19th century, as anti-Russian sentiment reaches levels not seen since the 1960s.

The campaign, which began earlier this week, represents a dramatic escalation from the recent mocking of Russian military performance and has now evolved into open questioning of Russia's legitimacy as a unified state.

Russian ultranationalists respond with fury

The Chinese campaign has triggered an explosive response from Russian ultranationalists and military bloggers, who have flooded Telegram and VKontakte with threats of nuclear retaliation and promises to march to Beijing.

"These Chinese fantasies about 'Outer Manchuria' show their true face," Shurygin wrote. "While we fight NATO in Ukraine, they dream of stabbing us in the back. But they forget — we have enough nuclear weapons to destroy China ten times over."

Historical grievances and modern tensions

The territorial dispute has deep historical roots. The 1858 Treaty of Aigun, signed during the Second Opium War, established the border along the Amur River, while the 1860 Beijing Convention ceded what is now Primorsky Krai, including the crucial port of Vladivostok.

Chinese nationalists argue that these treaties were signed under duress when the Qing Dynasty was weakened by internal rebellions and foreign invasions. They point to the fact that Chinese settlements existed in the region for centuries before Russian expansion eastward.

Official responses remain muted

Neither the Chinese nor Russian governments have officially commented on the social media campaign, though analysts note that Beijing has done little to censor the territorial claims, suggesting at least tacit approval.

"The fact that these posts remain up, that the hashtag is trending, that maps are being shared — this doesn't happen without official tolerance," said Dr. Zhang Mei, a specialist in Chinese nationalism at the National University of Singapore. "Beijing is sending a message to Moscow: we can turn public opinion against you whenever we choose."

Russian officials, preoccupied with the ongoing war in Ukraine, have limited their response to diplomatic channels. However, the ultranationalist response suggests growing pressure on the Kremlin to take a harder line with China, even as Russia becomes increasingly dependent on Chinese economic support.

As the online conflict escalates, both sides appear to be digging in. Chinese users are sharing more historical documents and maps, while Russian nationalists are threatening military action and nuclear retaliation.

"This is just the beginning," posted one Chinese user whose comment received over 200,000 likes. "First we reclaim Outer Manchuria, then we discuss the return of Tuva and other stolen territories. The Russian bear is dying, and the Chinese dragon is hungry."

Whether the online campaign translates into actual territorial demands remains to be seen, but analysts warn that the genie of nationalist territorial claims may be difficult to put back in the bottle.

"Once you unleash these historical grievances, they take on a life of their own," said Dr. Ivanova. "Beijing may find that having encouraged this nationalism, they can no longer easily control it."
