James Monroe Crowder - Poor Mississippi farmer (1843-1929) married 22-year-old Susan at age 81
89 upvotes • posted by MississippiGenealogy Civil War

Looking for information on my great-great-grandfather James Monroe Crowder. Born April 10, 1843 in Crawford, Mississippi to Edmund Crowder Sr. and Martha Ann Wilks. He was their 8th child and never attended school - started working on his father's farm at age 8.

Key facts:

  • Served in CSA Army from March 1862 to war's end
  • Wounded in July 1864 - shot through shoulder, broke left shoulder
  • Two brothers died in the war (Nathanial and David)
  • Farmed east of Brooksville, MS his entire post-war life
  • Married Eliza, had 10 children
  • Extremely poor his whole life
  • Died July 20, 1929, age 86

MYSTERY: In 1924 (age 81) he married 22-year-old Susan. No marriage record found, no information on what happened to Eliza, and Susan disappeared after his death. Any help appreciated!

MARRIAGE RECORD FOUND: James Monroe Crowder married Susan Elizabeth Johnson on March 15, 1924 in Lowndes County, Mississippi. He was 81, she was 22 (born 1902).

ELIZA'S FATE DISCOVERED: Eliza Crowder died February 28, 1924 of influenza at age 74. She was buried in Pleasant Ridge Cemetery, Brooksville. James was left alone at 80 years old with no surviving children nearby - all had moved to Texas or Arkansas for better opportunities.

SUSAN'S STORY: Susan was the granddaughter of James's neighbor Tom Johnson. After James died, she inherited his 40-acre farm (worth about $800) and immediately sold it to a timber company for $1,200. She used the money to move to Memphis and married William "Billy" Thompson in 1930. They had 4 children and she died in 1987 at age 85.

THE REAL STORY: This wasn't a romance - it was a practical arrangement. James needed someone to care for him in his final years, Susan needed financial security. The marriage contract specifically stated she would inherit the farm in exchange for "faithful service and care until death."

CIVIL WAR SERVICE DETAILS: Found James's complete military record. He served in Company K, 6th Mississippi Infantry Regiment.

CONFIRMED SERVICE: Enlisted March 4, 1862 at Brooksville. His brothers were in the same company:

  • Nathanial (killed at Second Battle of Corinth, October 4, 1862)
  • David (died of dysentery at Vicksburg, November 1863)
  • Robert (survived, captured at Battle of Nashville, December 1864)
  • Edmund Jr. (survived, surrendered at Citronelle, Alabama, May 1865)

WOUND DETAILS: James was shot at the Battle of Ezra Church near Atlanta on July 28, 1864. The bullet entered his right shoulder and exited his back, shattering his left shoulder blade when he fell. He spent 3 months recovering in a field hospital near Lovejoy Station.

REENLISTMENT: James reenlisted in January 1865 because his company was down to 17 men from the original 112. He served until surrendering with the regiment at Citronelle on May 4, 1865.

MississippiLandRecords • 98 points • 4 hours ago

THE FARM MYSTERY SOLVED: James's farm wasn't just poor - it was unsuitable for farming.

Found the 1880 agricultural census: James's 40-acre farm had 27 acres of swampy bottomland that flooded every spring. Only 13 acres were arable, and even those were clay soil that required twice the normal labor to cultivate.

WHY HE STAYED: James inherited the farm from his uncle Jefferson Wilks (Martha's brother) who died in 1866. The farm had a tax lien of $47 that James couldn't pay. He worked the land for 63 years trying to earn enough to clear the debt, but never succeeded.

THE TRAGIC IRONY: When Susan sold the farm to the timber company in 1929, they paid off the accumulated tax debt of $312 (with 63 years of interest) and still cleared $888 profit. James spent his entire adult life trapped by a debt that could have been resolved if he'd had any other options.

CHILDREN'S DISPERSAL: All 10 children left because James couldn't support them. The oldest sons worked in Texas oil fields, daughters married into families with Arkansas timber operations. Only Jefferson Carter Crowder stayed nearby, working as a sharecropper on better land.

PensionFilesResearch • 76 points • 3 hours ago

PENSION APPLICATION DETAILS: James applied for a Confederate pension in 1908 at age 65. His application reveals the real reason for his lifelong poverty.

PHYSICAL LIMITATIONS: The gunshot wound permanently damaged his left shoulder. He could only work with one arm and couldn't lift more than 20 pounds. This severely limited his farming ability in an era of manual labor.

DENIED PENSION: His pension was denied because he couldn't prove his wound was service-related. The military hospital records from 1864 were lost, and without them, the state required two witnesses to his wound. By 1908, most of his comrades were dead or had moved away.

FINANCIAL IMPACT: The $8/month pension would have doubled his income. Instead, he lived on approximately $48/year from his poor farm - about 13 cents per day.

REAPPLICATION: He reapplied in 1912 with affidavits from two former soldiers. This time it was approved for $4/month - half the normal rate because of his "limited disability." Even this small amount was crucial - it paid his property taxes and prevented foreclosure.

ANCESTORS DISCOVERED: James's parents had fascinating backgrounds:

Edmund Crowder Sr. (1801-1865): Born in Union County, South Carolina, moved to Mississippi in 1829 as part of the Great Migration. He was a blacksmith who transitioned to farming when he realized the demand for ironwork was limited in rural Mississippi. Built his farm from 80 acres of virgin forest to a 240-acre cotton operation by 1850.

Martha Ann Wilks (1805-after 1870): Daughter of Thomas Wilks, a Revolutionary War veteran from Virginia who received a land grant in Mississippi. Martha was literate (rare for women of her era) and kept detailed farm records that survived in the family Bible. She outlived Edmund by at least 5 years, living with her daughter Sarah Crowder Jenkins in 1870.

SIBLINGS FULL STORY: James had 9 siblings, not just the Civil War brothers:

  • Mary Elizabeth (1828-1899): Married wealthy planter James Madison Baldwin, lived in Natchez, became Methodist missionary
  • Sarah Ann (1830-1885): Married Thomas Jenkins, moved to Texas in 1858, had 8 children
  • William Thomas (1832-1867): Died of yellow fever, left 3 young children
  • Nancy Caroline (1834-1920): Married Dr. Samuel Hart, lived in Vicksburg, survived the siege
  • Edmund Jr. (1836-1904): Survived Civil War, moved to Louisiana, became sheriff

CHILDREN'S COMPLETE STORIES: All 10 children had dramatically different lives:

Jefferson Carter Crowder (1865-1942): The only child who stayed. Became a sharecropper on better land, had 9 children. His daughter Mattie Crowder became the first in the family to attend normal school (teacher's college) in 1912.

Robert Franklin (1867-1955): Moved to Beaumont, Texas in 1901, worked in the Spindletop oil fields. Became a roughneck crew chief, earned enough to send money home to James. His son Robert Jr. became a petroleum engineer.

Mary Elizabeth (1869-1948): Married Arkansas timber man William Thompson, moved to Camden, Arkansas. Her daughter Mary Thompson married into the Georgia-Pacific lumber family.

James Monroe Jr. (1871-1933): Moved to Dallas, became a streetcar conductor. Lost his job during the Depression, died of pneumonia in a poorhouse.

Sarah Emma (1873-1961): Married Methodist minister Thomas Hartwell, moved to Oklahoma Territory in 1893. Became a missionary teacher to Choctaw children.

William Nathanial (1875-1945): Moved to Port Arthur, Texas, worked in oil refineries. Died in the Texas City Disaster of 1945 when an oil refinery exploded.

Martha Jane (1877-1959): Married merchant John Davis, moved to Little Rock, Arkansas. Her daughter Martha Davis became one of the first female real estate agents in Arkansas.

David Edmund (1879-1937): Moved to Tulsa, Oklahoma during the oil boom. Became a wildcatter, made and lost several fortunes. Died penniless in a boarding house.

Nancy Caroline (1881-1967): Married Texas rancher Samuel Johnson, moved to Abilene. Her son James Johnson became a Texas state senator.

Thomas Jefferson (1883-1950): Moved to California in 1920, worked in Los Angeles shipyards during WWII. His daughter Betty Crowder became a Rosie the Riveter and later a union organizer.
