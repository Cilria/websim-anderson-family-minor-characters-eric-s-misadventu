Military coup in Peru as General Anataro Humala seizes power weeks before election

By Rafael Mendoza, Claudia Fernández and Jorge Salazar
Updated 8:42 PM ET, March 14, 2026
[Image: Armed soldiers stand guard outside the Government Palace in Lima after General Humala's announcement]

Lima, Peru (CNN) — Peru's military seized control of the government Wednesday night, installing General Anataro Humala as the country's new leader and suspending the upcoming presidential election scheduled for March 29.

In a televised address from the Government Palace at 7:30 PM local time, General Humala, flanked by senior military commanders, announced the dissolution of Congress and the establishment of a "transitional military council" to restore order and "protect the nation's sovereignty against foreign interference."

"The corrupt political class has betrayed the people of Peru for too long," Humala declared, wearing his military dress uniform. "The armed forces have taken this necessary action to prevent the country from falling into chaos. Elections will be held when conditions permit."

The coup follows weeks of escalating tensions, including mass protests against the government of President Luis Castillo, who took office in July 2021. Castillo was reportedly detained at his residence and is being held at an undisclosed location, according to military sources.

International leaders have swiftly condemned the coup. U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio called for "the immediate restoration of democratic order," while the Organization of American States announced an emergency session for Thursday morning.

General Humala, 58, is a controversial figure who previously served as Peru's defense minister from 2020-2021. He has been associated with the ethnocacerist movement, which advocates for indigenous rights and has been accused of harboring authoritarian tendencies.

The streets of Lima remained tense Wednesday night as armored vehicles patrolled key intersections. Local television showed soldiers taking positions around government buildings and the Congress of the Republic, which was surrounded by military vehicles.

Witnesses reported hearing gunfire in the early evening hours, though the extent of casualties remains unclear. The military has imposed an overnight curfew starting at 10 PM local time.

"The situation is very fluid. We've seen military checkpoints throughout the city, and there are reports of sporadic violence in some neighborhoods," said María Elena Rodríguez, a political science professor at the Catholic University of Peru.

The coup threatens to plunge Peru deeper into political turmoil. The country has been grappling with corruption scandals, economic instability, and widespread public discontent with traditional political parties.

Regional leaders, including Brazilian President Neymar da Silva and Chilean President Gabriel Boric, have called for an emergency summit of South American nations to address the crisis.

The military has announced that all flights into and out of Jorge Chávez International Airport have been suspended indefinitely, and borders with neighboring countries have been closed.

International markets reacted swiftly to the news, with Peru's currency, the sol, falling sharply against the dollar in after-hours trading.

More details are expected to emerge as the situation develops.
