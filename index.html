White supremacist convicted in multiple racial killings dies in federal prison at 106

Charles Kenneth Knight, sentenced to 250 years for murders spanning four decades, showed no remorse

By Sara Sidner and Mark Morales, CNN
Updated 6:23 PM ET, Tue January 11, 2022

Washington (CNN) Charles Kenneth Knight, a convicted white supremacist who murdered 17 people in racially motivated attacks over three decades and became one of the oldest federal prisoners in U.S. history, has died at age 106, the Bureau of Prisons confirmed Tuesday.

Knight died on November 8 at the Federal Correctional Complex in Butner, North Carolina, but officials delayed releasing the information due to security protocols and notification of victims' families. He was serving 250 years without possibility of parole for some of the most heinous hate crimes in American history.

"Charles Knight was the embodiment of pure evil," said Assistant Attorney General Kristen Clarke of the Justice Department's Civil Rights Division. "For more than 30 years, he terrorized innocent victims based solely on the color of their skin or their religious faith. His death brings closure to decades of pain and suffering inflicted upon countless families."

Knight's crimes spanned four decades, beginning with the 1951 bombing of an African-American elementary school in Tennessee that killed 11 children. He was convicted in 1995 after a dramatic FBI arrest that ended in a shootout at his remote Tennessee compound.

A lifetime of hate

Born October 26, 1915, in rural Tennessee, Knight joined the Ku Klux Klan as a young man and remained an active member until his arrest. Court documents revealed he rose to a leadership position within the organization and used his role to recruit others to his violent ideology.

In addition to the 1951 school bombing, Knight was convicted of murdering his own sister in 1953 after she threatened to report his involvement in the school attack to authorities. Nearly three decades later, in 1981, he murdered five young Jewish girls in a shooting at a community center.

Throughout his trial and subsequent incarceration, Knight never expressed remorse for his actions. In his final court appearance in 1995, he used racial slurs and told the court: "F*** the n*****s and all of Satan's worshippers."

Capture and conviction

Knight evaded capture for decades, moving between safe houses and using false identities. His arrest came after a two-year FBI investigation that culminated in a dramatic raid on his compound in rural Tennessee in November 1994.

During the shootout with federal agents, Knight wounded two FBI officers before being taken into custody. Investigators found an arsenal of weapons, bomb-making materials, and racist literature at his compound.

In 1995, he was convicted on 17 counts of murder, conspiracy to commit murder, and civil rights violations. U.S. District Judge Curtis Collier called Knight's crimes "among the most depraved acts this court has ever encountered" before sentencing him to 250 years in federal prison.

Death in custody

Knight spent the final 27 years of his life in federal custody, most recently at the Butner complex, which houses some of the federal system's most notorious inmates. Despite his advanced age, prison officials said he remained unrepentant and was considered a security risk until his death.

According to the Bureau of Prisons, Knight died of natural causes. An autopsy was performed, but results are pending. He had been in declining health for several years and used a wheelchair, but maintained contact with white supremacist groups through coded letters, officials said.

"Even in his final years, Knight continued to espouse the same hateful ideology that motivated his crimes," said Bureau of Prisons Director Michael Carvajal. "His death marks the end of a dark chapter in American history."

Impact on victims' families

For relatives of Knight's victims, his death brings a measure of closure after decades of pain. Sarah Williams, whose 8-year-old sister died in the 1951 school bombing, said she felt "relief that he can never hurt anyone again."

"My sister never got to grow up, never got to have children or grandchildren," Williams, now 75, told CNN. "He lived to be 106 while she died at 8. There's no justice in that, but at least now he's gone."

The Anti-Defamation League, which tracked Knight's activities for decades, said his death serves as a reminder of the ongoing threat posed by white supremacist ideology.

"Charles Knight represents the worst of American racism and anti-Semitism," said ADL CEO Jonathan Greenblatt. "His crimes demonstrate the deadly consequences of unchecked hate and the importance of holding perpetrators accountable, regardless of how much time has passed."

Knight's death also raises questions about the federal prison system's handling of aging inmates. At 106, he was among the oldest prisoners in federal custody, and his case highlights the challenges of incarcerating elderly inmates with serious health conditions.

Funeral arrangements have not been announced, and it's unclear who, if anyone, will claim his remains. Federal officials said they would follow standard procedures for unclaimed prisoners, which typically involve cremation and burial in a prison cemetery.
