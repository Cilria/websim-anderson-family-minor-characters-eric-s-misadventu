Arab State forces breach Zagros defences as Operation Nahavand enters third week

Military commanders report gradual but costly advances through mountain passes
By our Defence Editor • Analysis from multiple military sources

Arab State forces have achieved what military analysts term a "breakthrough moment" in their three-week-old invasion of Iran, with troops under General Khalid Mohammed Bin Hussien successfully penetrating the Zagros mountain defences that have served as Iran's natural barrier for millennia.

The advance, coming after sustained artillery bombardment and aerial strikes that have reduced several Iranian towns to rubble, represents the most significant territorial gain since the operation began on May 2nd. However, the cost has been considerable, with the town of Issabad now described by survivors as "completely erased from the map."

"The entire town is gone. Not damaged—gone. Nothing remains but rubble and ash. They bombed us for three days without pause, then the artillery came. When the ground forces arrived, there was nothing left to defend."
— Issabad survivor, speaking to international aid workers

Army Command South, under the leadership of General Khalid Mohammed Bin Hussien—widely regarded within military circles as the most capable commander of the Greater Arab State—has conducted what analysts describe as a methodical, if brutal, advance through the Zagros foothills. The strategy has prioritized overwhelming firepower over speed, with extensive use of both conventional and incendiary munitions.

Multiple fronts, coordinated pressure

These commands have conducted parallel operations, with General Turki's forces breaking through defensive positions near Hamedan while General Ahmed's troops captured Borujerd. The coordinated assault has forced Iranian defenders to split their limited reserves across multiple axes of advance.

The tactical success has been facilitated by what appears to be a deliberate campaign of aerial terror. A leaked memorandum from Arab State Air Force command, dated May 20th, outlines a strategy explicitly modeled on German bombing campaigns of 1940, calling for the systematic destruction of "Persian hives where the subhumans congregate."

Administrative consolidation

The Greater Arab State has moved swiftly to consolidate territorial gains through administrative annexation. Khuzestan province, captured during the first week of operations, was formally annexed on May 19th, with Waleed Muhmmad Al-Harbi—brother to the Arab State leader—appointed as provincial governor.

In Khorramabad, authorities have established what they term a "Military Administration Zone of Kurdistan," placing Storm Unit paramilitary forces in direct control of civilian administration. Similar arrangements have been implemented in Bushehr, now designated a "Special Military Zone" under the command of General Saddam Izzam Al-Rashdi, described by regional analysts as a close ally of the Arab State leadership.

[MAP: Arab State forces control approximately 25,000 square kilometers of Iranian territory, with front lines extending from the Iraqi border to positions 80 kilometers east of the Zagros mountain range]

Storm Units assume policing role

Perhaps most significantly, Storm Unit paramilitary formations have been deployed as primary law enforcement in occupied territories, replacing conventional military police with ideologically vetted personnel. Witnesses report that these units, drawn from the Arab State's most committed supporters, have implemented strict controls on civilian movement and communication.

The implications extend beyond immediate military concerns. The establishment of permanent administrative structures suggests the Arab State leadership anticipates a lengthy occupation, regardless of international pressure or potential ceasefire negotiations.

"What we are witnessing is not merely a military campaign but the systematic dismantling of Iranian sovereignty in captured territories. The administrative appointments, the demographic engineering through population displacement, and the ideological indoctrination programs all point to long-term territorial ambitions."
— Regional security analyst, speaking on condition of anonymity

As Operation Nahavand enters its third week, the strategic picture remains fluid. While Arab State forces have demonstrated superior coordination and firepower, Iranian resistance appears to be stiffening in the mountainous terrain. The coming weeks may well determine whether the invasion becomes a swift campaign of conquest or devolves into the kind of prolonged mountain warfare that has historically frustrated invaders from Alexander to Saddam.

The international community's response, meanwhile, remains largely confined to diplomatic pronouncements. With each passing day of military advance and administrative consolidation, the prospect of returning to the pre-war status quo becomes increasingly remote.
