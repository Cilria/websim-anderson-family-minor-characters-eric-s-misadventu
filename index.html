Global Coalition Launches Massive Assault on Islamic Caliphate as Nigeria Retakes Kaduna

63-nation alliance including China launches unprecedented military campaign as Nigerian forces score first major victory, killing 900 jihadists in single day
By Richard Engel & Courtney Kube | NBC News

Washington DC/Abuja — The United States has joined a historic 63-nation coalition launching the largest international military operation since World War II against the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel, as Nigerian forces delivered the first major blow to the jihadist group by driving them out of Kaduna and killing over 900 fighters in a single day of combat.

Key facts

  • 63 nations form largest coalition since WWII
  • China joins with unprecedented airstrikes
  • Nigerian forces retake Kaduna, kill 900 jihadists
  • Operation includes 15,000+ aircraft sorties
  • Naval blockade established across West African coast
  • Special forces from 23 nations deployed
  • First major victory against caliphate achieved

The massive international response, dubbed "Operation Sahel Freedom," began at 04:00 GMT on April 3rd with coordinated strikes from over 15,000 aircraft sorties, naval bombardments from 47 warships, and special forces operations across seven West African nations.

Coalition aircraft conduct precision strikes against caliphate positions across West Africa

"This is the most significant multilateral military operation in modern history," said Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth at a Pentagon briefing. "Sixty-three nations have come together to confront a threat that endangers not just West Africa, but global security. The message is clear: the civilized world will not tolerate the existence of a jihadist state."

China's unprecedented involvement

In a stunning diplomatic reversal, China has joined the coalition with significant military assets, including deploying its first aircraft carrier strike group outside the Pacific region and conducting precision airstrikes against caliphate targets in Mali and Burkina Faso.

"The Chinese People's Liberation Army is conducting military operations against terrorist targets in coordination with international partners," said Chinese Defense Ministry spokesperson Senior Colonel Wu Qian. "This represents a new chapter in China's commitment to global counterterrorism efforts."

"This is the most significant multilateral military operation in modern history. Sixty-three nations have come together to confront a threat that endangers not just West Africa, but global security."

Chinese J-20 stealth fighters and H-6K strategic bombers have struck caliphate command centers, weapons depots, and communications infrastructure, with satellite imagery showing significant damage to jihadist facilities across the region.

Nigeria's stunning victory

The coalition's opening day was highlighted by a stunning victory for Nigerian forces, who launched a major counteroffensive to retake Kaduna, a city of 1 million people that had been under caliphate siege for over a week.

In a 14-hour battle that began at dawn, Nigerian troops backed by coalition airstrikes and special forces advisors drove caliphate fighters from the city, killing over 900 jihadists while suffering minimal casualties.

"This is a great day for Nigeria and for all freedom-loving people," said Nigerian President in a televised address. "Our forces have shown that with proper support and coordination, we can defeat this terrorist threat and restore peace to our region."

The victory at Kaduna represents the first major success against the caliphate since its rapid expansion began in March, and has provided a significant morale boost to coalition forces and civilians across the region.

International coalition details

The 63-nation coalition includes all NATO members, major Asian powers including Japan and South Korea, Middle Eastern allies such as Saudi Arabia and the UAE, and African nations including Nigeria, Ghana, and Côte d'Ivoire.

The operation involves over 450,000 military personnel, making it the largest international military deployment since the 1991 Gulf War. Forces are operating from bases in Senegal, Ghana, Côte d'Ivoire, and offshore naval task forces.

"This coalition represents the international community's unified response to a threat that transcends borders," said NATO Secretary General. "We are committed to providing the resources and expertise necessary to eliminate the caliphate's ability to project terror beyond West Africa."

Naval blockade and special operations

Coalition naval forces have established a complete blockade of West African coastal waters, preventing the caliphate from receiving supplies or moving fighters by sea. The blockade involves 47 warships from 23 nations, including US aircraft carriers, British destroyers, and Chinese frigates.

Special forces from 23 nations have been deployed for precision operations against high-value targets, with early reports indicating successful raids on caliphate leadership compounds and weapons storage facilities.

British SAS units have conducted operations in northern Mali, while French special forces have targeted caliphate positions in Burkina Faso. American Navy SEALs have conducted maritime interdiction operations, seizing weapons shipments attempting to reach caliphate forces.

Humanitarian corridors

Despite the massive military operation, coalition forces have established humanitarian corridors to allow civilians to escape conflict zones and receive aid. The United Nations has begun emergency relief operations, with food, medicine, and other supplies being delivered to affected areas.

"We are committed to protecting civilian lives while conducting military operations," said a coalition spokesperson. "Humanitarian assistance is being provided alongside military action to ensure that innocent people are not caught in the crossfire."

Next phases

Coalition officials have stated that the current operations represent only the beginning of a sustained campaign to eliminate the caliphate's presence in West Africa. Military planners are preparing for what they describe as a "multi-phase operation" that could last several months.

The next phase is expected to focus on retaking major cities currently under caliphate control, including Bamako, Niamey, and Ouagadougou. Coalition forces are already positioning for these operations, with additional troops and equipment arriving daily.

"This is not a short-term operation," said Secretary Hegseth. "We are committed to seeing this through until the caliphate's ability to threaten regional and global security is completely eliminated. The international community has shown that when faced with a common threat, we can unite and act decisively."

As operations continue into their second day, coalition forces are reporting steady progress, with caliphate forces appearing to retreat from several positions in the face of the overwhelming international response.

"The message has been sent and received," said a senior coalition official. "The civilized world will not allow a jihadist state to exist. We are coming, and we will not stop until this threat is eliminated."
