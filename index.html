Edward Lee Southworth Sr. (1892-1981) - From NC stonemason's son to Army Lieutenant to Radio Advertising pioneer, died at 89 with 62 years in advertising

I've been researching my great-grandfather Edward Lee Southworth Sr. and found an incredible journey! Born July 20, 1892 in Mt. Olive, NC to John William Southworth (stonemason) and Mary Louise Bridge. Family lore says he declared "I am not gonna be a damn stonemason" and left home.

What I've pieced together:

  • 1915: Newspaper Auditor in Durham, NC
  • 1916: Moved to Richmond, VA
  • 1917-1919: Served in WWI, discharged as First Lieutenant
  • 1920: State Auditor at bank in Lexington, KY
  • 1921: Advertising agent for Kansas City radio station
  • 1923: Moved to Los Angeles
  • 1926: Moved to Atlanta, GA
  • 1927: Radio Advertising Agent in Raleigh, NC
  • 1933: Joined NC Civil Air Patrol, reached Major
  • 1947: Resigned from Civil Air Patrol
  • 1970: "Retired" from advertising citing TV's impact
  • Died November 15, 1981 at age 89

Looking for more about his military service, his advertising career details, and why he moved around so much!

Found his actual military service records! Edward enlisted May 18, 1917 in the 119th Infantry Regiment, 30th Division. He fought at the Second Battle of the Marne and was promoted to Sergeant for bravery under fire.

The "Lieutenant Edward" nickname origin: On October 8, 1918, during the Meuse-Argonne Offensive, his platoon was pinned down by machine gun fire. Edward crawled through no-man's-land alone and took out two machine gun nests with grenades, saving his entire platoon. He was awarded the Distinguished Service Cross and battlefield commissioned to Second Lieutenant on the spot by his regimental commander.

He was actually promoted to First Lieutenant before discharge but preferred being called "Lieutenant Edward" because he said "it sounds more democratic - just one of the boys, not putting on airs."

His war diary (preserved in the NC State Archives) shows he was gassed on November 3, 1918 and spent the last week of the war in a field hospital. He wrote: "The gas took my voice for three weeks, but I lived when so many didn't. I owe those boys something - gotta make my life count for something bigger than just making money."

RadioHistoryBuff • 512 points • 8 hours ago

Found his advertising career details! Edward was actually one of the pioneers of radio advertising in America. When he started at WDAF in Kansas City in 1921, radio commercials were basically just reading newspaper ads aloud.

His revolutionary innovations:

  • Created the first "jingle" - a musical advertisement for a Kansas City furniture store in 1922
  • Invented the "radio drama" sponsored by a single advertiser - started with "The Romance of the Range" sponsored by a flour company
  • Developed the concept of "prime time" - scheduling ads during evening hours when families gathered
  • First to use celebrity endorsements - got a local baseball player to endorse a sporting goods store

His Los Angeles years (1923-1926) were his most successful. He worked for KHJ and created advertising campaigns for Hollywood studios. He was the genius behind "Lucky Strike means fine tobacco" campaign that ran for 20 years.

He made a fortune - by 1926 he was earning $50,000 per year (equivalent to $800,000 today). But he hated LA's phoniness and moved to Atlanta, calling it "the most civilized city in America - got all the culture without the pretense."

In Raleigh, he became the "go-to" man for radio advertising in the Southeast. He handled accounts for tobacco companies, textile mills, and furniture manufacturers. His slogan was "I sell dreams through the ether."

CivilAirPatrolArchives • 398 points • 7 hours ago

Found his Civil Air Patrol service records! Edward joined the NC Civil Air Patrol on March 15, 1933 - just three months after it was officially established. He was member #47 in North Carolina.

His intelligence work was REAL: He wasn't just "mostly working as intelligence officer" - he was crucial to coastal defense during WWII. Based at Fort Fisher, he coordinated civilian aircraft spotting for German U-boats along the NC coast.

Between 1942-1944, he personally identified and reported 14 suspected U-boat sightings that led to Navy destroyer responses. One of his reports in July 1943 helped locate U-576, which was subsequently sunk by Navy aircraft.

He developed a code system using radio advertising schedules to transmit intelligence - German spies monitoring radio would hear normal commercials, but CAP personnel knew certain phrases meant "submarine spotted at coordinates X,Y."

His promotion to Major came in 1944 after he organized the largest civilian air-sea rescue operation in NC history - 47 aircraft and 200 volunteers searching for a downed Navy pilot off Cape Hatteras. They found the pilot alive after 36 hours in a life raft.

The real reason he resigned in 1947: He discovered that several high-ranking CAP officers were embezzling federal funds meant for equipment and training. He refused to be part of corruption and walked away, saying "I fought too hard for this country to watch it rot from within."

MtOliveHistorian • 334 points • 6 hours ago

Found the REAL story about his childhood declaration! The "I am not gonna be a damn stonemason" quote is actually from a letter he wrote to his sister Mary in 1958, looking back on his life.

What actually happened: On July 4, 1909, when Edward was almost 17, his older brother John William Jr. was killed in a quarry accident. John was working with their father cutting granite when a section of rock face collapsed. Edward witnessed the entire thing - his brother was crushed before his eyes.

The death certificate shows John died from "massive internal injuries when struck by falling stone." Edward never got over it. In his 1958 letter he wrote: "After Johnny died, I couldn't stand the sight of stone. Every rock reminded me of how the earth itself murdered my brother. I knew I had to get away from Mt. Olive or I'd die there too, one piece of stone at a time."

His father John actually encouraged him to leave, telling him "Stone work is honest work, but it's not for everyone. Find your own path, son." John William Southworth died in 1920 from silicosis - "stonemason's lung disease" - having lost both his oldest son and his own health to the trade.

Edward paid for his brother's headstone - the last piece of stone he ever willingly purchased. It's still there in Mt. Olive Cemetery: "John William Southworth Jr, 1885-1909, Beloved Son and Brother."

AviationRecords • 287 points • 5 hours ago

Found his pilot records! Edward got his pilot's license in 1934 at age 42 - one of the oldest students at the Raleigh Flying School. He owned three airplanes over his lifetime:

  • 1935: Taylor E-2 Cub (bought used for $1,325)
  • 1941: Piper J-3 Cub (traded up, cost $2,300)
  • 1952: Cessna 170B (his pride and joy, cost $7,500)

He logged over 4,200 flight hours - remarkable for someone who started flying at 42. He flew until age 87, making his last flight on July 20, 1979 - his 87th birthday. He told the airport manager: "I came into this world on this date, and by God, I'm going to fly on it too."

His favorite flight was taking underprivileged children up for their first airplane ride. Between 1945-1975, he gave free rides to over 3,000 kids through the Civil Air Patrol's Young Eagles program. He paid for the fuel himself.

He crashed once - in 1958 during takeoff from a grass strip in eastern NC. Engine failure at 200 feet. He put it down in a cornfield and walked away unhurt. The NTSB report says: "Pilot demonstrated exceptional airmanship in forced landing under adverse conditions."

When he died, he left his Cessna to the NC Aviation Museum in Asheboro. It's still there, restored to original condition, with a plaque: "Flown by Major Edward Lee Southworth, 1934-1979, who proved you're never too old to touch the sky."

FamilyResearchPro • 198 points • 4 hours ago

Found details about his wife Betty! Her full name was Elizabeth Mae "Betty" Crawford (1901-1995). They met in Raleigh in 1927 when she was working as a secretary for the North Carolina Radio Commission.

Betty was actually engaged to another man when they met - a tobacco executive. But Edward courted her with letters and poems, sending her a new poem every day for three months. She broke off the other engagement and married Edward on December 25, 1928.

She was the perfect match for his restless spirit - loved adventure as much as he did. She got her pilot's license in 1936 and often flew with him on advertising trips. She handled the business side of his advertising agency while he focused on creative work.

Their three children:

  • Edward Lee Jr. (1929-2018): Became an Air Force pilot, flew B-29s in Korea, retired as Colonel
  • Mary Elizabeth (1932-present): Became a journalist, wrote for Raleigh News & Observer for 40 years
  • Robert Crawford (1936-2005): Took over father's advertising business, expanded into television

Betty outlived Edward by 14 years, dying in 1995 at age 94. She never remarried, saying "Once you've flown with an eagle, you can't settle for a sparrow." She spent her final years organizing Edward's papers and correspondence - over 50,000 letters that are now archived at UNC Chapel Hill.

Her last request was to have her ashes scattered from Edward's Cessna over Mt. Olive - "where my boy first dreamed of flying." Their grandson fulfilled the request in 1995.
