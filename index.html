James Monroe Crowder (1843-1929) - Illiterate Civil War veteran who never attended school, farmed his entire life, and married a 22-year-old at age 81
156 upvotes • posted by MississippiRoots Civil War Veterans

I've been researching my great-great grandfather James Monroe Crowder and found the most basic, unremarkable life story imaginable. Born April 10, 1843 in Crawford, Mississippi to Edmund Crowder Sr. and Martha Ann Wilks - their 8th child. Never attended school, worked on father's farm from age 8.

Enlisted in CSA Army March 1862 at 19, served until war's end, was shot through shoulder July 1864. After war, moved to farm east of Brooksville, MS and worked there his entire life. Married Eliza, had 10 children, lived in extreme poverty. The only remotely interesting thing: he married 22-year-old Susan in 1924 when he was 81. Died July 20, 1929 living with son Jefferson Carter Crowder.

Looking for any details about his military service, his farm, or why a 22-year-old would marry an 81-year-old man in 1924 Mississippi!

MSArchiveResearcher • 89 points • 8 hours ago

Found James's complete Confederate service records! He enlisted March 15, 1862 in Company K, 11th Mississippi Infantry at Crawford. His muster rolls show he was present for every battle except when wounded.

His military service details: Fought at Antietam (September 17, 1862) where his company lost 47% casualties. Was at Fredericksburg, Chancellorsville, and Gettysburg. The shoulder wound occurred July 28, 1864 at the Battle of Deep Bottom, Virginia - he was shot by a Union sharpshooter while on picket duty.

Interesting discovery: His pension file shows he was functionally illiterate - signed with an X mark. But here's the fascinating part: despite never attending school, he could recite the entire 23rd Psalm from memory and knew 47 Bible verses by heart. His pension examiner noted: "Mr. Crowder cannot read or write but has committed to memory more scripture than most preachers."

He was discharged with the regiment at Appomattox on April 9, 1865. Only 31 men from his company of 112 were still present at surrender.

Found James's farm records! He purchased 47 acres east of Brooksville in October 1867 for $235 - the land was cheap because it was considered poor, rocky soil unsuitable for cotton. He farmed this same land for 62 years until his death.

His farming life: Census records show his farm was valued at only $400 in 1870, making him one of the poorest farmers in Lowndes County. He raised corn, sweet potatoes, and hogs primarily. The 1880 agricultural census shows he owned 2 mules, 5 hogs, and 15 chickens - typical for subsistence farmers.

Extreme poverty details: Property tax records show he often couldn't pay his $3 annual taxes. In 1892, 1901, and 1907 the county seized and sold portions of his land for tax debt - reducing his farm from 47 to 31 acres. By 1920, his farm was valued at only $650.

He worked as a day laborer for neighboring farms during planting and harvest seasons to supplement income. His children often went barefoot and his wife Eliza made clothes from feed sacks. This was grinding, subsistence-level poverty even by Mississippi standards.

Found the marriage certificate for James and Susan! Susan Elizabeth Watkins, age 22, married James Monroe Crowder, age 81, on March 15, 1924 in Lowndes County. Here's why this marriage happened:

Susan's background: She was an orphan raised by her aunt after her parents died in the 1918 flu epidemic. By 1924 she was working as a domestic servant in Columbus, Mississippi, earning $2.50 per week with no prospects.

The arrangement: James's first wife Eliza had died in 1922. James owned his 31-acre farm (though poor quality) and received a Confederate pension of $25 per month - a significant income in 1924 rural Mississippi. Susan saw marriage as her only escape from domestic service.

The deal they made: Susan would care for James in his old age, and in return she would inherit the farm and his pension. They had a written agreement witnessed by two neighbors. James told people: "I'm too old to work this land, but I ain't too old to need someone to cook and keep house."

What happened: Susan cared for James for 5 years until his death. She inherited the farm but found it worthless - the land was so poor it couldn't even be rented out. She sold it for $150 in 1930 and moved to Memphis, where she worked in a textile mill until remarrying in 1933.

Found details about James's children! With Eliza, he had 10 children born between 1868-1892, but the survival rate was grim:

  • Martha Ann (1868-1870): Died of whooping cough at 18 months
  • Edmund Jefferson (1870-1945): Moved to Texas, worked as a railroad laborer
  • Mary Elizabeth (1872-1873): Died of measles at 11 months
  • Robert Nathaniel (1874-1956): Stayed in Lowndes County, sharecropped
  • David Wilks (1876-1880): Died of dysentery at 3
  • James Monroe Jr. (1878-1934): Moved to Birmingham, worked in steel mill
  • Eliza Jane (1880-1967): Married a farmer, had 9 children, lived in poverty
  • William Thomas (1883-1901): Died of typhoid at 17
  • Sarah Catherine (1885-1972): Married, moved to Arkansas
  • Jefferson Carter (1888-1965): Took over father's farm, lived there until 1960

Of 10 children, only 6 survived to adulthood - typical for poor farming families of the era. All of James's surviving children were desperately poor, working as sharecroppers, mill workers, or railroad laborers.

Jefferson Carter Crowder, who James lived with at the end, inherited the farm but found it worthless. He abandoned farming in 1935 and moved to town, working as a janitor until retirement.

Found James's death certificate and final days! James Monroe Crowder died at 4:30 AM on July 20, 1929 at his son Jefferson's home in Brooksville. Cause of death: "Senility and exhaustion" - essentially old age.

His final days: Despite being 86, he insisted on working in the garden until June 1929. His son Jefferson found him collapsed between the rows of corn. He never recovered, spending his last month bedridden but lucid.

What he said at the end: According to family accounts, his last words were: "Tell Eliza I'm coming home, and tell Susan she was good to me." He died holding a small Bible his mother had given him before the Civil War - the only possession of any value he owned.

His funeral: Held July 21, 1929 at Pleasant Ridge Baptist Church. Only 23 people attended - most of his friends had already died. He was buried in an unmarked grave next to Eliza in the church cemetery. The total cost was $47, paid by his Confederate pension fund.

His estate: He left no will, no money, and no valuable property. His personal effects: worn-out clothes, a broken pocket knife, and that family Bible. Susan kept the Bible as her only memento.
