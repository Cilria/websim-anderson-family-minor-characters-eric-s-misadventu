Greece's Enabling Bill: PM Katrakis seeks dictatorial powers amid protests

By Mark Lowen and Sarah Rainsford 2 February 2027 Athens
Updated 4:30 PM GMT: Greek opposition parties have vowed to block the bill, calling it "the death of democracy."
Protesters gather outside the Greek Parliament as the Enabling Bill is debated inside. (Reuters)

Greek Prime Minister Nikolaos Katrakis has introduced the "Enabling Bill," legislation that would effectively grant him dictatorial powers by allowing the government to pass laws without parliamentary approval, amid growing domestic and international condemnation.

The bill, introduced by Greek National Front (GNF) MP Dimitrios Konstantopoulos on Tuesday, would suspend the right to protest, grant party militias law enforcement powers, and allow the Prime Minister to seize control of all police units while formally giving the government authority to "ignore courts."

The Enabling Bill - Key Provisions

Executive Powers

Prime Minister gains authority to pass laws without parliamentary approval for a minimum of one year, renewable indefinitely by simple cabinet vote.

Law Enforcement

GNF party militias granted full law enforcement powers, including arrest authority. Prime Minister given direct control over all police units nationwide, bypassing local command structures.

Civil Liberties

Right to public protest formally suspended. All public gatherings require government approval. Media outlets can be shut down for "undermining state authority."

Judicial Authority

Government authorized to "ignore court rulings that conflict with state security interests." Supreme Court jurisdiction limited to civil matters only.

The proposed legislation represents the most significant power grab in Greece since the military junta of 1967-1974, drawing immediate parallels to authoritarian regimes throughout European history.

"This is not merely an assault on democracy - this is the systematic dismantling of every democratic institution Greece has built since the fall of the colonels."

Speaking to parliament, Konstantopoulos claimed the measures were "temporary but necessary" to protect Greece from "communist infiltration and foreign interference," echoing language used by authoritarian leaders throughout the 20th century.

Historical echoes

The bill's provisions bear striking resemblance to the 1933 Enabling Act in Germany, which allowed Adolf Hitler to pass laws without parliamentary approval and marked the effective end of the Weimar Republic.

"The parallels are chilling," said Professor Kevin Featherstone, director of the Hellenic Observatory at the London School of Economics. "Both pieces of legislation use manufactured crises to justify permanent emergency powers, both suspend constitutional protections, and both concentrate absolute power in the hands of a single leader."

The 260-day detention period authorized under Katrakis's previous emergency law now appears modest compared to the unlimited detention powers proposed in the Enabling Bill.

International reaction

The European Union has threatened immediate sanctions if the bill passes, with European Commission President warning that "Greece's place in the European family depends on respect for democratic values and the rule of law."

German Chancellor Olaf Scholz called the proposals "a direct attack on the European Union's founding principles," while French President Emmanuel Macron urged the Greek parliament to "reject this assault on democracy."

NATO Secretary General expressed concern about the implications for alliance cohesion, particularly given Katrakis's previous threats to withdraw from the alliance.

Domestic opposition

Inside Greece, opposition parties have united against the bill, with former Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras calling it "the death certificate of Greek democracy" and communist party leader Dimitris Koutsoumbas urging "mass resistance to this fascist coup."

However, with Katrakis's Greek National Front holding a narrow parliamentary majority, the bill is expected to pass within days unless dissenting MPs from the ruling party break ranks.

Protests have intensified across Athens, with university students, trade unions, and civil society groups vowing to defy the protest ban. Riot police have already begun arresting demonstrators under the existing emergency law, with over 200 detained since Tuesday morning.

"We will not comply with this dictatorship. If they want to arrest us for exercising our democratic rights, let them fill every prison in Greece."

Media crackdown intensifies

State television has been instructed to limit coverage of the protests, while several journalists have reportedly been warned against "promoting anti-government sentiment."

The offices of the newspaper "Kathimerini" were raided by police Tuesday afternoon, with editor-in-chief Alexis Papachelas describing the action as "a warning shot against press freedom."

Internet service providers have been asked to prepare for potential website blocking, though company executives have privately expressed reluctance to comply without court orders.

Economic consequences

Greek stocks fell 12% on Tuesday, while bond yields rose to levels not seen since the 2015 debt crisis. The Athens Stock Exchange announced it would suspend trading if losses exceeded 15%.

Major European banks have begun reviewing their exposure to Greek assets, while several multinational companies have reportedly begun contingency planning for potential capital controls.

"Investors are pricing in the possibility of Greece becoming a pariah state," said Gabriel Sterne, head of global macro research at Oxford Economics. "The economic consequences could be devastating."

As night fell on Athens, thousands of protesters remained in Syntagma Square, defying police orders to disperse. Many carried banners reading "Oxi" (No) - the same word Greeks used to reject Axis occupation during World War II.

The bill is expected to come to a final vote within 48 hours, though opposition parties have vowed to use every parliamentary procedure to delay its passage.

Whatever the outcome, Greece appears headed for a prolonged period of political instability that could have far-reaching consequences for the European Union and NATO at a time when both alliances face multiple external challenges.
