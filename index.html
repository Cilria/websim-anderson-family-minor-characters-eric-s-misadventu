Jaysh al-Mu'minin fi Shibh al-Jazira (JASAJ): A New Threat Assessment

Saudi-based militant group emerges with calls to overthrow Gulf monarchies

By Thomas Joscelyn | Bill Roggio | January 12, 2027

A new militant Islamist group calling itself Jaysh al-Mu'minin fi Shibh al-Jazira (JASAJ), or "The Army of the Believers on the Peninsula," has emerged from the Arabian Peninsula with an ambitious agenda to overthrow the ruling monarchies of Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, and Oman. The group's inaugural statement, released yesterday via their official Telegram channel, marks a significant escalation in jihadist rhetoric targeting Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) states.

The organization's debut communiqué, dated January 11, 2027, represents a return to classical jihadist themes that have been largely absent from recent regional militant discourse. Unlike Islamic State or al-Qaeda affiliates that have focused on local conflicts, JASAJ explicitly targets the legitimacy of Gulf monarchies, framing them as apostate regimes that have betrayed Islam by "appeasing Crusaders."

ولكن، للأسف، سقطت المنطقة في أيدي العائلات المالكة، التي تركز أكثر على استرضاء الصليبيين بدلاً من عيش حياة الهداية.
In the name of Allah, to all believers. The Land of the Two Holy Mosques is where the true religion began, where the armies of Islam conquered the infidels and enemies of Islam, crushing all who stood in their way. Unfortunately, however, the region has fallen into the hands of the ruling families, who are more focused on appeasing the Crusaders than on living a life of guidance.
Key Finding: JASAJ's rhetoric explicitly targets all six GCC monarchies, with particular emphasis on Saudi Arabia's ruling House of Saud. The group's statement calls for the black banner of Islam to fly over Doha, Kuwait City, Riyadh, Mecca, Medina, and Muscat.

The group's leader, identified as Abuallah Ibn Yusuf Ibn Muhammad Al-Zahara, is described as a 51-year-old Salafi preacher based in the small town of Baqaa, Saudi Arabia. According to regional intelligence sources, Al-Zahara has spent the past decade building a following through underground religious circles, capitalizing on growing discontent among conservative segments of Saudi society regarding the kingdom's modernization efforts under Vision 2030.

Assessment: The choice of Baqaa as an operational base is strategically significant. Located in Saudi Arabia's central region, the town provides access to both urban centers and remote desert areas, particularly the Rub' al Khali (Empty Quarter), where the group reportedly maintains training camps.

Intelligence estimates suggest JASAJ currently numbers approximately 600 fighters, with recruitment efforts focused on disaffected Saudi nationals and foreign workers who have been radicalized through online propaganda and underground mosque networks. The group's Telegram channel, which has gained over 15,000 followers since its creation, serves as both a propaganda outlet and recruitment platform.

What distinguishes JASAJ from previous jihadist movements in the region is its comprehensive rejection of Gulf monarchies' religious legitimacy. While groups like al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula (AQAP) focused primarily on Western targets and the Yemeni conflict, JASAJ explicitly frames its struggle as a religious obligation to remove "apostate rulers" who have corrupted the birthplace of Islam.

"It is the duty of all believers, God willing, through jihad, to overthrow the royal families, the House of Saud, and other traitors to Islam, such as the Rafidah (Shiites) or infiltrators."

The group's sectarian rhetoric extends beyond typical Sunni-Shiite divisions, incorporating anti-Iranian sentiment while simultaneously condemning Gulf monarchies for their relationships with Western powers. This dual narrative appears designed to appeal to both ultraconservative Salafi elements and broader anti-Western sentiment in the region.

Regional security analysts note that JASAJ's emergence comes at a particularly sensitive time for Gulf monarchies, as they navigate between modernization initiatives and maintaining conservative legitimacy. The group's ability to operate from Saudi territory raises questions about the effectiveness of the kingdom's counterterrorism apparatus, particularly in remote desert regions.

The Saudi Interior Ministry has not officially responded to JASAJ's emergence, though regional intelligence sources indicate increased security operations in the Rub' al Khali region. The group's ability to maintain operational security while releasing public statements suggests a level of sophistication that exceeds typical regional militant organizations.

International counterterrorism officials are particularly concerned about JASAJ's potential to destabilize the broader GCC region. The group's stated objectives include not only regime change in Saudi Arabia but also the overthrow of other Gulf monarchies, potentially triggering a wider regional conflict that could impact global energy markets.

As the situation develops, JASAJ represents a significant evolution in jihadist threats facing the Arabian Peninsula. Unlike previous movements that focused on external enemies or local conflicts, this group explicitly challenges the religious legitimacy of Gulf ruling families, potentially inspiring similar movements across the region.

The coming months will be critical in determining whether JASAJ can translate its rhetoric into operational capability. Previous militant groups have struggled to maintain momentum after initial announcements, but the group's apparent organizational structure and regional focus suggest it may pose a more sustained threat than typical jihadist startups.

For policymakers and counterterrorism officials, JASAJ's emergence underscores the ongoing challenge of militant extremism in the Gulf region, even as states like Saudi Arabia pursue ambitious modernization agendas. The group's ability to exploit religious legitimacy narratives while operating from within the kingdom presents a unique security challenge that will require careful monitoring and response.
