Trump declares end of United States, proclaims American Empire in Latin-language address

Former president styles himself 'Imperatore Trump Novi Eboraci' in bizarre July 6th announcement

By Hallie Jackson, Andrea Mitchell and Chuck Todd | NBC News
Updated 2:47 PM ET, Sun July 6, 2026

WASHINGTON — In an unprecedented and surreal announcement, former President Donald Trump declared the end of the United States of America on Sunday, proclaiming the establishment of the "American Empire" and adopting the Latin title "Imperatore Trump Novi Eboraci" during a bizarre 45-minute address from his Mar-a-Lago estate.

The former president, speaking entirely in Latin to a bewildered audience of supporters and journalists, announced what he termed the "natural evolution" of American governance, claiming the Constitution had "outlived its usefulness" and that the time had come for "strong imperial leadership."

Key developments:

  • Trump declares end of United States, establishes American Empire
  • Entire announcement delivered in Latin, leaving most confused
  • Adopts imperial title referencing New York and "conquest of barbarians"
  • Congressional leaders call emergency session for Monday
  • Constitutional scholars declare move "laughably illegal"
"Imperatore Trump Novi Eboraci, Victore Barbarorum Septentrionis et Meridiei"

Trump's declaration, delivered in what appeared to be rehearsed but heavily accented Latin, roughly translates to: "Emperor Trump of New York, Conqueror of the Barbarians of North and South." The former president concluded his address with the phrase "Ave America, Imperatorem!" or "Hail America, [to] the Emperor!"

White House officials, reached for comment, appeared uncertain how to respond to what many are calling a "cosplay coup attempt." President Biden, currently at Camp David, was briefed on the development and reportedly asked aides, "Is this real or is he doing a bit?"

"Populus Americanus iam non est servus legum antiquarum. Tempus est imperium novum!"
Translation: "The American people are no longer slaves to ancient laws. It is time for a new empire!"

Legal scholars across the political spectrum were quick to dismiss the declaration as "legally meaningless" and "performance art gone wrong." Constitutional law professor Laurence Tribe of Harvard Law School called it "the constitutional equivalent of declaring yourself King of the Moon."

"This is not how any of this works," said Tribe. "You can't just declare the end of the Republic because you're bored and facing multiple indictments. The Constitution doesn't have an 'emperor clause' that I'm aware of."

Trump's announcement appeared to catch even his closest allies off guard. Senator Lindsey Graham, when asked about the development, paused for several seconds before responding, "Well, I think we need to see how this plays out" — a phrase he has used repeatedly during Trump's previous controversies.

House Speaker Mike Johnson called for "calm and reasoned analysis" while scheduling an emergency session of Congress for Monday to "address this latest development in the former president's ongoing efforts to remain relevant."

"Barbari foris et intus nos oppugnant. Imperium fortis est necessarium!"
Translation: "The barbarians outside and within attack us. A strong empire is necessary!"

The former president's use of Latin has puzzled historians and classicists. Dr. Sarah Ruden, a classical scholar at the University of Pennsylvania, described Trump's Latin as "Google Translate meets Caesar's Gallic Wars, but somehow worse than both."

"The grammar is atrocious, the pronunciation is painful, and the historical parallels are nonsensical," Ruden said. "He's essentially LARPing as a Roman emperor while demonstrating he understands neither Rome nor America."

Social media erupted with confusion and mockery following the announcement. The hashtag #AveAmerica trended worldwide, with users posting memes comparing Trump to everything from Emperor Palpatine to a confused tourist at a Renaissance fair who had taken things too far.

Republican National Committee Chair Michael Whatley released a carefully worded statement saying the party "supports constitutional governance" while noting that "President Trump's unique communication style has always been part of his appeal to voters."

Democrats were less restrained. Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer called the declaration "the most pathetic attempted coup in American history" and suggested Trump "should stick to golf and fraud trials."

The practical implications of Trump's imperial declaration appear to be limited to his Mar-a-Lago property, where staff members were seen replacing American flags with what appeared to be golden eagle standards purchased from a historical reenactment website.

"Constitutio mortua est. Vivat imperator!"
Translation: "The Constitution is dead. Long live the emperor!"

Security experts noted that unlike genuine coup attempts, Trump's declaration lacked any of the traditional elements: military support, control of government institutions, or even a basic understanding of how power actually works.

"This is like watching someone play Risk while drunk and also they don't know the rules," said Dr. Fiona Hill, former National Security Council official. "It's simultaneously concerning and completely ridiculous."

The former president's legal team, when reached for comment, appeared to distance themselves from the Latin declaration. One attorney, speaking on condition of anonymity, said, "Look, we just handle the criminal cases. The Roman emperor stuff is new territory for us."

As news of the declaration spread globally, international leaders responded with a mixture of concern and bewilderment. British Prime Minister Rishi Sunak's office released a statement expressing "confidence in American democratic institutions" while privately suggesting the UK might "reconsider that special relationship thing."

Canadian officials, meanwhile, appeared more amused than alarmed. "We've always said America was exceptional," quipped one Canadian diplomat. "We just didn't realize they meant exceptionally weird."

The real test of Trump's imperial ambitions may come Monday when Congress reconvenes. Constitutional scholars note that the former president has no actual authority to dissolve the Republic, regardless of how many Latin phrases he memorizes.

"The good news is that declaring yourself emperor while speaking bad Latin is not actually how you overthrow a government," said Professor Tribe. "The bad news is that 74 million people voted for this man, and he still has influence over one of our major political parties."

As the sun set on what historians may record as one of the strangest chapters in American political history, Trump's Mar-a-Lago compound remained under the jurisdiction of the United States of America, governed by the same Constitution that has survived for nearly 250 years — despite the former president's apparent belief that Latin conjugations could somehow override it.
