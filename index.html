Greek National Front wins election: Ultranationalists vow to 'restore Greece'LIVE

By James Anderson & Maria Papadopoulos 10 January 2027 Athens
Supporters of the Greek National Front celebrate outside parliament after the election results

Greece has elected its most right-wing government since the fall of the military junta in 1974, with the ultranationalist Greek National Front (GNF) winning the largest share of seats in Sunday's snap election.

Nikolaos Katrakis, the party's leader, will become prime minister after the GNF secured 38% of the vote and 152 seats in the 300-member parliament - just enough for an outright majority.

In a victory speech delivered to thousands of supporters in central Athens, Katrakis vowed to "restore Greece to its former glory" and implement what he termed "national renewal" policies that have alarmed European Union leaders.

"The Greek people have spoken. We will reclaim what is rightfully ours, starting with Cyprus. The humiliation of 1919 will be avenged. Greece will rise again!"

The election results have sent shockwaves through Brussels, with the GNF's platform including withdrawal from NATO, rejection of the country's bailout commitments, and territorial claims on neighboring countries.

Key election promises

The Greek National Front's manifesto includes several controversial positions:

  • Immediate withdrawal from NATO and suspension of EU membership negotiations
  • Annexation of Cyprus and claims on parts of Albania and North Macedonia
  • Repudiation of Greece's debt obligations to international creditors
  • Mass deportation of what the party terms "illegal economic migrants"
  • Reinstatement of the monarchy and restoration of "traditional Greek values"

Despite these radical proposals, Katrakis insisted in his victory speech that "democracy will be preserved and strengthened under our leadership," though he provided few details about how this would be achieved.

International reaction

The European Commission issued a statement expressing "deep concern" about the election results, while NATO Secretary-General called for Greece to "remain committed to its alliance obligations."

The United States State Department said it was "monitoring the situation closely" and urged the new government to "uphold democratic values and international commitments."

Cypriot President Nikos Christodoulides called an emergency cabinet meeting, while Turkish President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan warned that "any attempts to destabilize Cyprus will be met with decisive action."

"This is not just about Greece. This is about the future of European democracy and the stability of the entire Eastern Mediterranean region."

Financial markets reacted negatively to the results, with Greek stocks falling 15% in early trading and bond yields rising to levels not seen since the height of the country's debt crisis.

Historical context

The GNF's rhetoric about "avenging 1919" refers to the Treaty of Sèvres, which Greece believes unfairly stripped it of territory following World War I. The party has long argued that Greece was betrayed by Western powers and should reclaim its historical territories.

Analysts say the party's rise reflects growing public frustration with years of economic austerity, high unemployment, and perceived EU interference in Greek affairs. The country has been struggling with a cost-of-living crisis and growing inequality.

"This is a protest vote against the establishment," said Dr. Elena Dimitriou, professor of political science at the University of Athens. "But the consequences could be catastrophic for Greece's position in Europe and the global economy."

The new government is expected to be sworn in within days, with Katrakis scheduled to meet with President Katerina Sakellaropoulou on Monday morning to formally receive his mandate to form a government.
