Peru's Humala launches 'Night of Blood' purge, executes military rivals

By Katy Watson & Will Grant | BBC South America correspondents, Lima

Lima, Peru — Peru's self-declared leader Antauro Humala has ordered the execution of dozens of military officers and political opponents in what he has termed the "final cleansing" of his regime, marking the bloodiest day in Peru since the Shining Path insurgency.

In a chilling announcement broadcast on state television at 3:47 AM local time, Humala declared that "the scum has been eliminated" and that "Peru is now purified for the Incan restoration." The purge, which began late Tuesday night and continued into Wednesday morning, has claimed the lives of at least 47 senior military officials, 23 opposition politicians, and 15 senior police commanders.

According to witnesses interviewed by the BBC, military squads arrived at officers' homes shortly after midnight, dragging senior commanders and their families into the streets. In the affluent district of San Isidro, neighbours reported hearing automatic gunfire and screams that lasted for nearly an hour.

"They made them kneel in the street," said Maria González, a resident who witnessed the executions from her apartment window. "The officers were in their pyjamas, hands tied behind their backs. Then they just started shooting."

Systematic elimination

The purge appears to have been meticulously planned. Sources within the presidential palace, who spoke to the BBC on condition of anonymity, said Humala had been compiling lists of "disloyal elements" since November. The final trigger came when a senior military aide warned him of an alleged coup plot scheduled for mid-December.

Among those executed were:

  • General Carlos Rodríguez, former head of the joint chiefs of staff
  • Admiral Patricia Vásquez, navy commander
  • General Ricardo Mendoza, air force chief
  • Retired General Julio Salazar, former defence minister
  • Former President Dina Boluarte (previously reported captured)
  • Keiko Fujimori, former presidential candidate

The bodies were reportedly loaded onto military trucks and driven to an undisclosed location. Families have been told nothing about burial arrangements.

"The scale of this is unprecedented in modern Latin America. Humala has essentially decapitated Peru's entire security apparatus overnight." — Dr. Francisco Durand, political analyst, University of Lima
Secret police established

Simultaneous with the executions, Humala announced the creation of the "Servicio Nacional de Protección Inca" (SNPI) — a new secret police force drawn entirely from loyalists within the ethnocacerist movement. The organisation, headquartered in the former Ministry of Defence building, has been granted sweeping powers including warrantless arrests and the authority to conduct "revolutionary tribunals."

All remaining media outlets not already under state control were shut down at 6 AM. International news channels, including BBC World News, have been blocked nationwide. Internet access has been reduced to 5% of normal capacity, with only state-approved websites accessible.

"We are witnessing the final transformation of Peru into a totalitarian state," said former OAS Secretary General Luis Almagro, speaking from exile in Chile. "This is not just authoritarianism — this is state terrorism against the Peruvian people."

International response

The United Nations Security Council is holding an emergency session in New York, with Secretary-General António Guterres calling for "immediate international intervention." The United States has recalled its ambassador and announced "the strongest possible sanctions" against Humala's regime.

British Foreign Secretary David Lammy condemned the "barbaric executions" and called for Humala to be referred to the International Criminal Court. The European Union has suspended all trade agreements with Peru and frozen assets of senior officials.

Latest: Witnesses report columns of black smoke rising from the direction of the military hospital in San Borja, raising fears that the bodies of executed officers are being cremated. The SNPI has established checkpoints throughout Lima, conducting random searches and arrests.

Meanwhile, panic buying has emptied Lima's supermarkets, with citizens queuing for hours for basic supplies. The Peruvian sol has collapsed entirely, with banks refusing to exchange currency. US dollars are being traded on the black market at 20 times the official rate.

"We are prisoners in our own country," said one resident, who asked not to be identified. "Every knock on the door could be the SNPI. Every car that slows down could mean death. This is not Peru anymore — this is hell."
