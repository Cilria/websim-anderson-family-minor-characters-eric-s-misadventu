Islamist militants encircle Ouagadougou, launching siege of Burkina Faso's capital

Jihadist groups have surrounded the capital city and begun assault operations, marking the most significant escalation in the West African nation's decade-long insurgency
By Stephanie Busari, CNN Senior Africa Correspondent | CNN Africa Bureau

Ouagadougou, Burkina Faso (CNN) — Islamist militants have encircled Burkina Faso's capital city and launched what military analysts describe as the opening phase of a full-scale siege, after two years of rapid territorial gains across the country that have brought jihadist fighters to the gates of Ouagadougou.

Multiple jihadist groups, including al-Qaeda-affiliated Jama'at Nusrat al-Islam wal Muslimeen (JNIM) and Islamic State in the Greater Sahara (ISGS), have converged on the capital following a sustained offensive that began in early 2025. The militants have systematically captured towns and villages across central Burkina Faso, cutting off supply routes to the capital and positioning themselves for what appears to be a final assault.

"This is not a hit-and-run attack or a terrorist incident — this is a coordinated military operation designed to capture the capital," said Dr. Murtala Ibrahim, a Sahel security analyst at the Institute for Security Studies in Dakar. "The militants have been preparing for this moment for years."

Burkinabe security forces have established defensive positions throughout the city, with checkpoints on major roads and government buildings heavily guarded. The military has deployed tanks and armored vehicles at strategic intersections, while soldiers patrol neighborhoods that have not seen armed conflict in decades.

Escalating violence across the country

The siege marks a dramatic escalation in Burkina Faso's decade-long insurgency that began in 2015. The country has experienced multiple military coups since 2022, with military leaders promising to defeat the jihadist threat but instead watching as the insurgency has grown stronger.

According to regional security sources, the militants have captured several towns within 50 kilometers of Ouagadougou, including Koudougou and Ziniaré, effectively surrounding the capital. The groups have been enforcing strict Islamic law in captured territories, imposing harsh punishments and restricting women's rights.

"The militants have captured several towns within 50 kilometers of Ouagadougou, effectively surrounding the capital."

"They have been methodical in their approach," said Ibrahim. "First they isolate the capital by taking surrounding towns, then they cut supply lines, and now they are beginning the siege operations. This is a textbook military strategy."

The United Nations has expressed grave concern about the situation, with Secretary-General António Guterres calling for an immediate cessation of hostilities and urging regional powers to intervene. The UN peacekeeping mission in neighboring Mali withdrew in 2023, leaving a security vacuum that jihadist groups have exploited.

Regional implications

The siege of Ouagadougou represents a significant threat to regional stability in West Africa. Burkina Faso borders six countries, including Ghana, Ivory Coast, and Togo — nations that have so far avoided the worst of the jihadist insurgency.

The Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) has convened an emergency meeting for Tuesday, while France and the United States have both expressed support for regional efforts to stabilize the situation. However, direct military intervention appears unlikely given the complex regional politics and the failure of previous foreign interventions in the region.

Humanitarian crisis looms

Humanitarian organizations are warning of a potential catastrophe if the siege continues. The capital is home to approximately 2.8 million people, including hundreds of thousands who have fled violence in other parts of the country.

"We are seeing the early signs of a humanitarian emergency," said Sarah O'Connor, West Africa director for the International Committee of the Red Cross. "Food supplies are running low, medical facilities are overwhelmed, and people are trapped in their homes as fighting intensifies."

The World Food Program has suspended operations in the capital due to security concerns, while medical supplies are reportedly running critically low. Electricity and water services remain functional for now, but residents report intermittent disruptions as infrastructure becomes damaged in the fighting.

"People are scared to leave their homes," said Amadou Sawadogo, a resident of the capital reached by phone. "We can hear gunfire and explosions throughout the day and night. The city feels like it's under siege."

International response

The United States has evacuated non-essential embassy personnel and urged American citizens to leave the country. The State Department has issued a Level 4 travel advisory, the highest level, warning against any travel to Burkina Faso.

Russia has offered military assistance to the Burkinabe government, building on its growing influence in the region following the Wagner Group's operations in Mali and Central African Republic. However, Russian support has done little to stem the jihadist advance in recent months.

"The international community is watching this unfold in real-time, but there are few good options," said Dr. Ibrahim. "Direct military intervention could backfire, but doing nothing risks allowing a jihadist takeover of a capital city."

As fighting continues to intensify around Ouagadougou, residents report that the city has taken on an eerie calm during daylight hours, with most people staying indoors and shops closed. The sound of gunfire and explosions has become a regular occurrence, particularly after nightfall.

"This is not just a Burkina Faso problem anymore," said O'Connor. "If Ouagadougou falls, it will embolden jihadist groups across the region and could trigger a humanitarian catastrophe that will require international intervention."

The coming days will be critical in determining whether Burkina Faso's capital can hold out against the siege, or whether the country will become the first in the region to see its capital city fall to jihadist forces.
