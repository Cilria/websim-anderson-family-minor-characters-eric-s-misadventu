Researching Robert "Frank" Rutherford Newton Sr. (1882-1977) - Electrical Engineer & Yakima Pioneer
89 upvotes • posted by PacificNorthwestRoots Genealogy Enthusiast

I've been working on Robert "Frank" Rutherford Newton Sr. (June 10, 1882 - December 29, 1977). Born near Woodbro, WI to James "Jim" Madison Newton (KY-born lumber mill worker) and Nancy Ann Stover. Eldest son of 17 children total (10 full, 6 half-siblings from dad's second marriage).

Family moved from WI to Sheldon, WI farm, then 1886 train to South Dakota for wheat farming, then 1888 to Yakima Valley where Jim worked railroads. Robert grew up claiming Yakima as his "real birthplace."

Studied electrical engineering in Tacoma, worked there 40+ years until 1955 retirement. Married twice. Looking for more details!

WisconsinHistorical • 52 points • 2 hours ago

Found the family's Wisconsin roots! James Madison Newton bought 40 acres in Woodbro, WI in 1881 - I have the deed. He worked at the Woodbro Lumber Mill (owned by the Fairchild family). But here's what I discovered: the 1885 Sheldon, WI plat map shows James Newton owned 160 acres there - he didn't just "move for farming," he BOUGHT a farm!

Also found the 1886 newspaper article: "Local Farmer Seeks New Horizons" - James sold the Sheldon farm for $2,400 cash (a fortune then) and took the family west. The article mentions he was "following opportunity in Dakota Territory wheat boom."

SouthDakotaLand • 41 points • 2 hours ago

Found their South Dakota homestead! James Newton filed a homestead claim in Kingsbury County, Dakota Territory in 1886 - 160 acres. The 1887 agricultural census shows he broke 45 acres and planted wheat. But here's the family lore confirmed: the 1887-1888 drought destroyed his crop. I found correspondence between James and his brother Earl where James writes "the land is cursed, wheat won't grow, and I'm near broke."

Earl's letter back (dated March 1888) says "Come to Yakima, the railroad is hiring and land is cheap." James sold his homestead claim for $400 and moved the family.

TacomaEngineering • 38 points • 1 hour ago

Found Robert's entire career trajectory! He graduated from Puget Sound in 1906 with an electrical engineering degree. Here's his employment timeline from city directories:

1906-1909: "Draftsman" at Tacoma Electric Company, 1115 A Street
1909-1915: "Electrical Engineer" at Puget Sound Power Company, 1102 Commerce Street
1915-1922: "Chief Engineer" at Tacoma Light & Water Department, City Hall
1922-1955: "Senior Electrical Engineer" at Tacoma Public Utilities, same building

He literally helped build Tacoma's electrical infrastructure! The 1923 Tacoma Times has an article about him supervising the installation of the city's first major street lighting system.

NewtonFamilyTree • 35 points • 1 hour ago

Found the marriages! Robert first married Eleanor "Nellie" Margaret Johnson on June 15, 1907 in Tacoma. She was the daughter of Swedish immigrants, born in Minnesota. They had 3 children: James Franklin Newton (1908), Robert Earl Newton (1910), and Margaret Eleanor Newton (1912). Tragically, Nellie died in the 1918 flu pandemic.

Second marriage: Florence Irene McAllister on August 20, 1920. Florence was a telephone operator (appropriate for an electrical engineer!) and they had 2 more children: William Frederick Newton (1921) and Dorothy Jean Newton (1924). Florence outlived Robert, passing in 1985.

Interestingly, Robert's half-brother Earl Newton (from James' second marriage) became a railroad engineer - the family stayed in transportation/electrical fields for generations!

YakimaGenealogy • 29 points • 45 minutes ago

Found Robert's childhood in Yakima! The 1892 territorial census shows the Newton family living at 304 N. 3rd Street in Yakima. James worked as "section foreman" for Northern Pacific Railway. Robert attended Yakima Union School - I found his 7th grade class photo from 1895!

Here's the fascinating part: Robert's teacher was Miss Clara Belle Geddes, who later became Washington's first female electrical engineer. She absolutely influenced his career path - she taught advanced mathematics and had students build simple electrical circuits. Robert kept in touch with her throughout his life, even after he graduated.

NewtonDescendant • 24 points • 30 minutes ago

Robert's grandson here! Grandpa Frank (as we called him) told us stories about his electrical work. He designed the lighting system for the original Tacoma Narrows Bridge (1940), though ironically it collapsed shortly after. He also supervised the electrical installation for Fort Lewis during WWII.

After retirement, he and Grandma Florence traveled to all the places his family had lived - Woodbro, Sheldon, Kingsbury County, and Yakima. He even found the old South Dakota homestead foundation still visible in a wheat field. He kept a detailed journal of these trips that I still have - mentions visiting with cousins who were still farming there.
