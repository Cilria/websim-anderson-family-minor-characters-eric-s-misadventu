Sen. Markwayne Mullin attacked during town hall in Oklahoma

64-year-old man arrested after allegedly assaulting senator, shouting about job loss

By Jessica Chen, Robert McElvain and Maria Santos | 03/11/2026 06:47 PM EST

MUSKOGEE, Oklahoma — Senator Markwayne Mullin was physically attacked during a town hall meeting Tuesday evening by a man who shouted profanities about job losses before allegedly assaulting the Oklahoma Republican, according to witnesses and law enforcement.

The incident occurred around 7:30 p.m. local time at the Muskogee Civic Center, where Mullin was fielding questions from constituents. Witnesses say a man later identified as Kenneth Monroe Jackson Sr., 64, stood up and began shouting at the senator.

"Fuck you coward! You made me lose my job! Worst fucking senator, burn in hell!"

According to multiple witnesses and video footage reviewed by CNN, Jackson approached the stage and allegedly struck Mullin approximately 19 times before being subdued by security and local law enforcement.

Mullin was not seriously injured and continued the town hall after the incident, according to his office. The senator later thanked law enforcement for their "quick response and professionalism."

Jackson was arrested at the scene and charged with assault on a federal official, disorderly conduct, and resisting arrest. He is being held at the Muskogee County Jail pending a federal court appearance.

Police are now working to determine Jackson's motive, though initial reports suggest he may have blamed Mullin for job losses in the region. The FBI has joined the investigation due to the federal nature of the alleged assault.

"We take any threat against our elected officials extremely seriously," said FBI Oklahoma City spokesperson Sarah Whitmore. "This appears to be an isolated incident, but we are investigating all aspects of this case."

Jackson has no prior federal criminal history, according to public records, though he has had minor traffic violations over the years. Police are examining his social media accounts and interviewing family members to understand what may have triggered the attack.

Mullin, a former mixed martial arts fighter, appeared to handle the situation calmly, witnesses said. Video shows him stepping back as security intervened, then checking on constituents as Jackson was removed.

"The senator was composed throughout the incident," said town hall attendee Jennifer Walters. "He made sure everyone else was okay before continuing with the meeting."

The attack comes amid heightened tensions across the country over economic issues and job losses in various industries. Oklahoma has seen significant layoffs in manufacturing and energy sectors over the past year.

Mullin's office did not respond to questions about whether the senator has received threats recently, but said he "remains committed to meeting with constituents and hearing their concerns."

Security for lawmakers has become an increasing concern following several high-profile incidents. The Capitol Police reported over 8,000 threats against members of Congress in 2025, nearly double from five years ago.

"This is a sad reflection of the anger and division in our politics," said former Oklahoma Governor Frank Keating, a Republican. "We can disagree without resorting to violence."

Jackson's family told local reporters they were "shocked" by his actions and had "no idea" he was planning to attend the town hall. His son, Kenneth Jackson Jr., said his father had been "under stress" after being laid off from a manufacturing job six months ago.

"He's been struggling, but we never imagined he'd do something like this," the younger Jackson said.

The incident is likely to renew discussions about security protocols for congressional town halls, which are typically open events with minimal screening.

Mullin, 48, has represented Oklahoma in the Senate since 2023 after serving six terms in the House. He is up for reelection in 2026 and has been holding regular town halls across the state.

"I'm not going to let one disturbed individual stop me from doing my job," Mullin told reporters after the event. "This is what democracy looks like - sometimes it's messy, but we have to keep showing up and listening to people."

Jackson faces up to 20 years in prison if convicted on the federal assault charge. His initial court appearance is scheduled for Thursday.
