Greece expelled from EU as Katrakis announces sham elections

European Union formally removes Greece for first time in history, imposes crushing sanctions as dictator schedules rigged vote for March 15

By Nic Robertson and Elinda Labropoulou March 12, 2027 19:47 GMT Athens, Greece
LATEST: EU votes unanimously to expel Greece; sanctions include total trade embargo, asset freezes, travel bans; Katrakis responds "We don't need European parasites"
Greek protesters clash with police in Athens after the EU formally expelled Greece and Leader Katrakis announced snap elections (CNN)

The European Union has formally expelled Greece from the bloc for the first time in its history, imposing unprecedented sanctions that will isolate the Mediterranean nation completely as its dictator Nikolaos Katrakis announced sham elections scheduled for March 15.

The historic expulsion, approved unanimously by all 26 remaining EU members, comes just two days after Katrakis signed six draconian laws establishing a full military dictatorship, including provisions for concentration camps and death penalties for insulting the leader.

HISTORIC EU EXPULSION

Greece becomes the first country ever expelled from the European Union since its founding in 1993. The unprecedented move severs all trade, diplomatic, and economic ties, effectively turning Greece into a pariah state on Europe's doorstep.

"This is not a suspension - this is permanent expulsion," EU Commission President Ursula von der Leyen declared. "Greece has chosen tyranny over democracy, and we cannot allow this cancer to spread within our Union."

The sanctions package, described by EU officials as "the most comprehensive ever imposed," includes:

CRUSHING SANCTIONS IMPOSED

  • Total trade embargo - all imports and exports banned immediately
  • Asset freezes on all Greek government accounts and state enterprises
  • Travel bans on all government officials and their families
  • Disconnection from SWIFT banking system
  • Seizure of Greek assets in EU countries worth €180 billion
  • Immediate halt to all EU funding and development programs
  • Personal sanctions on Katrakis and 847 government officials

The economic impact will be devastating. Greece, which joined the EU in 1981, relies on the bloc for 65% of its trade and has received over €70 billion in EU development funds. Overnight, the country has been cast into economic isolation not seen since the Cold War.

"This is economic strangulation. Within weeks, Greece will run out of medicine, fuel, and food. This is what happens when you choose fascism over Europe."

Katrakis, speaking from the Greek Parliament building now renamed the "Hall of National Glory," dismissed the EU's actions with characteristic contempt.

"Let the European parasites choke on their sanctions," Katrakis declared in a televised address. "We are Greeks - we survived 400 years under Turkish rule, we survived World War II, we survived the junta. We will survive these European pimps and their banker masters."

The dictator's response to the expulsion was to announce snap elections for March 15 - a move that has been universally condemned as a sham designed to legitimize his regime.

ELECTIONS 'WILL BE RIGGED'

With all opposition parties banned, media controlled by the state, and protests criminalized, the March 15 elections are expected to produce a predetermined result showing overwhelming support for Katrakis and his Greek National Front party.

"This isn't an election - it's a coronation," said former Greek Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras, now in exile in Germany. "The only question is whether Katrakis will claim 95% or 99% victory."

International paralysis deepens

The EU's unprecedented action has exposed the limits of international law when dealing with a rogue state determined to defy global norms. While the sanctions will inflict massive suffering on ordinary Greeks, they are unlikely to dislodge Katrakis from power.

"This is the fundamental weakness of the international system," explained Dr. Kori Schake, director of foreign and defense policy studies at the American Enterprise Institute. "We have economic tools to punish, but no mechanisms to remove a dictator who controls his military and doesn't care about his people's suffering."

The situation has created a humanitarian crisis that extends far beyond economics. Greek hospitals, already struggling with medicine shortages, face complete collapse. The country's tourism industry, which employs 20% of the workforce, has evaporated overnight.

"We have patients dying because we can't get basic medications," said a Greek doctor speaking anonymously from Athens General Hospital. "Insulin, heart medication, antibiotics - all gone. We're practicing 19th-century medicine in 2027."

Refugee crisis explodes

As the economic situation deteriorates, Greeks are fleeing en masse. Turkey reports over 100,000 Greek refugees in the past 48 hours, while Albania and North Macedonia have declared states of emergency.

"This is the largest European refugee crisis since World War II," said Filippo Grandi, the UN High Commissioner for Refugees. "And unlike Syria or Afghanistan, these are Europeans with no diaspora communities to absorb them."

The refugee flows include not just economic migrants but political dissidents, journalists, LGBTQ+ individuals, and religious minorities fleeing persecution. Concentration camps in Greece are reportedly overflowing, with executions continuing daily.

"They're killing people faster than we can document it," said a human rights activist working on the Albanian border. "The EU sanctions are cutting off escape routes - it's a perfect storm of suffering."

Katrakis consolidates power

Far from being chastened by international isolation, Katrakis appears emboldened. The dictator has used the EU expulsion to rally nationalist sentiment, portraying Greece as a victim of "globalist conspiracy."

"The European Union has shown its true face - it is the Fourth Reich, dominated by German bankers and French bureaucrats," Katrakis declared. "We are proud to be free of their tyranny."

The regime has accelerated its purge of "enemies of the state," with mass arrests reported across Athens. The secret police have established neighborhood committees to identify dissidents, while rewards are offered for information on those attempting to flee.

"This is Stalin's Russia with smartphones," said a former Greek intelligence officer now in hiding. "The technology makes it more efficient, but the psychology is the same - terror, surveillance, betrayal of family and friends."

The March 15 elections, which will feature only the Greek National Front party, are expected to produce Soviet-style results showing 99% support for Katrakis. State media has already begun reporting "massive enthusiasm" for the vote.

The dark future

As Greece prepares for its sham elections, the country has become a cautionary tale about the fragility of European democracy. The EU's expulsion, while unprecedented, has effectively created a pariah state on Europe's doorstep - one that may serve as a model for other would-be dictators.

"This is how democracy dies in the 21st century," said a Greek university professor now teaching in exile. "Not with tanks in the streets, but with lawyers in parliament passing 'legal' laws that criminalize dissent. The form of democracy remains, but the substance is gone."

The international community faces an impossible choice: continue sanctions that punish ordinary Greeks while leaving the dictator untouched, or find some way to engage that doesn't legitimize tyranny. Meanwhile, Greece sinks deeper into darkness.

As the world watches Greece's sham elections on March 15, the question is no longer whether democracy can be saved in Athens - it's whether the contagion of authoritarianism can be contained before it spreads to other vulnerable European states.

"If Greece falls completely, who is next?" asked a European diplomat, speaking anonymously. "Hungary? Italy? France? The wolves are at the door, and we have no idea how to stop them."
