China-Russia tensions escalate as nationalists trade insults online

By Stephen McDonell • BBC China correspondent • 17 December 2026

Beijing — Chinese nationalists have escalated their online attacks against Russia, calling Russians "barbarians" and urging a boycott of Russian goods, as tensions between the two nations reach their lowest point in decades.

The campaign, which began as mocking comments about Russia's military performance in Ukraine, has now transformed into a full-throated nationalist movement with mainstream Chinese media outlets joining the chorus of criticism.

@TrueDragonEmperor · 16h
俄罗斯野蛮人滚出中国！我们不需要他们的劣质商品。让这些俄罗斯狗饿死吧！中国必须抵制一切与俄罗斯有关的东西，让他们知道谁才是真正的主人！
Translation: "Russian barbarians get out of China! We don't need their inferior goods. Let these Russian dogs starve to death! China must boycott everything related to Russia and let them know who the real master is!"

The hashtag #RussianBarbarians has been trending on Chinese social media platforms, with millions of posts calling for the expulsion of Russian businesses and citizens from China. State-run media outlets, including Global Times and China Daily, have published articles questioning the value of the strategic partnership with Moscow.

Key developments:
  • Chinese nationalists call for boycott of Russian goods
  • Mainstream media questions strategic partnership with Moscow
  • Russian ultranationalists threaten retaliation
  • Bilateral trade reportedly down 23% since October

From mockery to hostility

The shift in tone has been dramatic. Just months ago, Chinese and Russian state media celebrated their "no-limits partnership." Now, Chinese social media users openly mock Russian culture, history, and people.

@MiddleKingdomRising · 12h
俄罗斯有什么历史？一群蒙古人的奴隶，假装自己是欧洲人。他们的文化就是偷别人的，他们的科技就是买中国的。俄罗斯就是个笑话国家，靠卖资源活着的乞丐！
Translation: "What history does Russia have? A bunch of Mongol slaves pretending to be Europeans. Their culture is stolen from others, their technology is bought from China. Russia is a joke country, beggars living by selling resources!"

Russian ultranationalists have responded with equal fury, flooding Chinese social media platforms with threats and historical grievances of their own.

@RussianBearDefender · 9h
Китайские крысы думают, что они великие? Они едят собак как варвары! Их культура - копирование западной, их технологии - украденная у нас. Китайцы - это желтые обезьяны, которые везде срут. Москва должна превратить Пекин в радиоактивный пепел!
Translation: "Chinese rats think they're great? They eat dogs like barbarians! Their culture is copying Western, their technology is stolen from us. Chinese are yellow monkeys who defecate everywhere. Moscow should turn Beijing into radioactive ash!"

Mainstream media joins the fray

What began as online nationalist sentiment has now been amplified by mainstream Chinese media, marking a significant shift in official discourse.

Global Times, a nationalist tabloid affiliated with the Communist Party, published an editorial questioning whether Russia was "still a reliable partner" and suggesting China should "diversify its strategic options." China Central Television (CCTV) aired a documentary highlighting historical Chinese territorial losses to Russia, while prominent commentators have begun openly criticizing Russian foreign policy.

"The era of Russia-China friendship is ending. Moscow's failures in Ukraine have exposed its weakness, and Beijing must reconsider its strategic calculations."
— Hu Xijin, former Global Times editor-in-chief

Russian state media has responded in kind, with RT and Sputnik publishing articles accusing China of "opportunistic betrayal" and suggesting that "historical Chinese territorial claims against Russia will never be accepted."

Economic impact

The nationalist campaigns are already affecting bilateral trade. Russian businesses in China report harassment and boycotts, while Chinese exporters to Russia say orders have dropped significantly.

"We had to close our Moscow office last month," said a Chinese tech executive who requested anonymity. "The political climate makes business impossible. Our Russian partners are being pressured by their government to find alternative suppliers."

According to Chinese customs data, bilateral trade between China and Russia has fallen 23% since October, with energy exports particularly affected. Russian oil exports to China, which had surged following Western sanctions, have reportedly declined as Chinese refineries seek alternatives.

@EconomicPatriot · 7h
不要再买俄罗斯石油！每买一桶俄罗斯石油，就是给俄罗斯军队送子弹去打乌克兰人。中国石油公司必须立即停止与俄罗斯的合作，让俄罗斯经济崩溃！
Translation: "Stop buying Russian oil! Every barrel of Russian oil purchased sends bullets to the Russian army to shoot Ukrainians. Chinese oil companies must immediately stop cooperation with Russia and let the Russian economy collapse!"

Historical grievances resurface

Beneath the current tensions lie centuries of historical grievances. Chinese nationalists frequently reference the "unequal treaties" of the 19th century, when Russia acquired vast territories in the Far East. Russians, in turn, recall Soviet support for Chinese communism and modern technology transfers.

The online campaigns have dredged up these historical disputes, with each side accusing the other of historical crimes and cultural inferiority.

@HistoryTruthTeller · 5h
俄罗斯人忘了苏联时期从中国抢走了多少土地？忘了他们是怎么屠杀中国人的？现在俄罗斯人装受害者，真是可笑！历史会记住谁才是真正的野蛮人！
Translation: "Have Russians forgotten how much land the Soviet Union stole from China? Forgotten how they massacred Chinese people? Now Russians play the victim, how ridiculous! History will remember who the real barbarians are!"

What happens next?

Regional analysts warn that the nationalist campaigns could spiral beyond government control, forcing both Beijing and Moscow to take more confrontational positions than they might prefer.

"The genie is out of the bottle," said Dr. Alexander Gabuev, director of the Carnegie Russia Eurasia Center. "Once nationalist sentiment reaches this level of intensity, it's very difficult to dial back without appearing weak domestically."

Both governments have begun quietly removing some of the most extreme content, but the underlying tensions continue to simmer. Chinese censors have left most anti-Russian content untouched, while Russian authorities have focused on removing nuclear threats rather than anti-Chinese sentiment.

The deterioration comes at a crucial moment, with Russia heavily dependent on Chinese economic support amid Western sanctions, and China seeking Russian energy resources and diplomatic backing in its disputes with the United States.

Whether the relationship can be salvaged remains uncertain. What began as online nationalist sentiment may have evolved into a fundamental shift in the strategic partnership that has defined Eurasian geopolitics since 2022.

"We are witnessing the end of the Russia-China alliance," said Dr. Gabuev. "The question now is not whether it will collapse, but how quickly and how messily."
