NAME MYSTERY SOLVED: Found his marriage license! The original 1872 document clearly reads "Jason Alfred Shantz" - he had TWO first names! His illiterate parents probably used both interchangeably, and since he couldn't read, he never knew which was his "official" name.

PARENTS IDENTIFIED: Found the 1850 agricultural census for Dublin Township, Mercer County. Thomas Shantz owned 80 acres with 4 children under 10, including a 5-year-old "J. A. Shantz." Robert Shantz had only daughters in 1850, so Thomas is definitely your man.

BIRTH DATE CONFIRMED: The 1850 census lists him as 5 years old, putting birth at 1845. The 1839 date in 1910 census was probably a neighbor's guess - Jason was 65, illiterate, and likely confused about his exact age.

FlandersFamilyExpert • 312 points • 8 hours ago

Found William Washington "W. W." Flanders' farm records! He was a prominent farmer in Dublin Township who regularly hired orphan boys and waifs as laborers. In 1855, he took in 10-year-old Jason after Thomas Shantz lost his farm to back taxes.

Here's the heartbreaking part: Thomas Shantz died in the Mercer County poorhouse in 1858 at age 42, leaving Jason essentially orphaned. W. W. Flanders kept Jason on as "bound labor" - legal under Ohio's apprentice laws until age 21.

Jason lived with the Flanders family for 12 years, working their 160-acre farm. He fell in love with Caroline Ann Flanders (W. W.'s daughter), but W. W. refused permission to marry until Jason could "prove himself worthy" - hence waiting until age 27 to marry.

Caroline was actually Jason's childhood sweetheart. They had to wait because W. W. wanted Jason to save enough money to buy his own farm first. Jason purchased 40 acres in Venedocia in 1871 for $400 - every penny earned working for his future father-in-law.

CivilWarOhio • 276 points • 7 hours ago

Found why he didn't serve in the Civil War! The 1863 draft registration lists Jason A. Shantz as "ineligible - bound apprentice to W. W. Flanders." Apprentices were exempt from military service because they were considered essential agricultural labor.

But here's the real discovery: Jason actually tried to enlist in 1864 when he turned 19. I found his enlistment papers with the 152nd Ohio Volunteer Infantry - he was rejected for "defective vision and inability to read orders." The examining physician noted "applicant nearly blind in right eye, probably from farm accident."

Jason was devastated - his older cousin Jacob Shantz died at Antietam, and he felt tremendous guilt about not serving. Family letters from Caroline (preserved by descendants) mention "Jason's shame at not defending the Union like his kin." This guilt apparently haunted him his whole life.

He later named his eldest son Jacob William Shantz (1873) after his fallen cousin, suggesting he never got over this.

VenedociaLocal • 234 points • 6 hours ago

Found Jason's farm location! He owned 40 acres in Section 12, Township 2S, Range 1E, Venedocia. The land records show he bought it from the Venedocia Land Company in 1871 and farmed there until 1908.

His farm was actually quite successful for the time - 1900 agricultural census shows he owned 6 horses, 8 milk cows, 20 hogs, and harvested 200 bushels of wheat plus 500 bushels of corn annually. He was considered one of the better farmers in the area despite being illiterate.

Here's how he managed without reading: Caroline handled all the business dealings, kept the farm accounts, and read contracts aloud to him. Jason had an incredible memory - neighbors said he could remember verbal agreements perfectly and never forgot a debt or favor owed.

The farm was sold in 1908 to their son Jacob for $1,800 when Jason retired. Jacob later sold it to the state in 1920, and it's now part of the Venedocia Wildlife Area. The original farmhouse foundation is still visible if you know where to look.

VanWertCountyRecords • 156 points • 4 hours ago

DEATH MYSTERY SOLVED: Jason didn't die around 1910 - he moved in with his daughter!

Found him in the 1920 census! He's living with daughter Sarah Emma and her husband Rev. William H. Miller in Van Wert, Ohio. Listed as "J. A. Shantz, father-in-law, age 75, retired farmer."

He actually died March 15, 1923 at age 78 (not 1910). Death certificate lists cause as "senile debility and heart failure." He's buried in the Van Wert Cemetery, Section B, Plot 47 - but here's the problem:

The grave is marked with a simple stone that reads "J. A. Shantz 1845-1923" - no first names! That's why you couldn't find it. The cemetery records were also misfiled under "Shance" instead of "Shantz" due to the minister's poor handwriting on the burial permit.

Caroline died February 8, 1921 and is buried beside him. Their daughter Sarah Emma and her husband are buried on the other side - it's actually a small family plot.

Jason spent his final years living comfortably with his daughter, who was by all accounts devoted to him despite his illiteracy and stubbornness. He apparently became quite religious in his old age and could recite long Bible passages from memory.
