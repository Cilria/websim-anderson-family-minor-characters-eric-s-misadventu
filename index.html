Abullah ibn Muhammad al-Avar (Arabic: عب الله بن محمد الأواري, ʿAbūllāh ibn Muḥammad al-Awarī; c. 1170 – 1234) was an Avar ruler who claimed to be the Caliph of Islam and proclaimed himself Emir of the Great Emirate of Dagestan and Avaria from 1202 until his death in 1234. Known for his illiteracy and grandiose delusions, Abullah claimed divine mandate to conquer the Byzantine Empire and restore the glory of Islam through military conquest.

Contemporary accounts describe Abullah as a man of "towering height and ferocious countenance" who believed he possessed supernatural powers, including the ability to walk on water and communicate directly with the Prophet Muhammad. Despite his inability to read or write, he issued numerous proclamations claiming authority over the entire Muslim world and declared his intention to march on Constantinople.

Early Life and Background

Abullah was born around 1170 in the mountainous region of Dagestan, the son of Muhammad ibn Umar, a local Avar chieftain. According to the 13th-century chronicler Ibn al-Athir, young Abullah was "strong as a bear and twice as stubborn," displaying from an early age a tendency toward violence and religious fanaticism.

Unlike other children of nobility who received education in Islamic schools, Abullah remained illiterate throughout his life. The renowned scholar and traveler Ibn Battuta, who visited Dagestan in 1332, recorded local oral traditions about Abullah's inability to read even basic Arabic script, despite claiming to be God's chosen messenger.

Family and Marriage

In 1195, Abullah married Fatima bint Ali, daughter of a prominent local merchant. The marriage, arranged by his father, produced two sons: Umar (born 1196) and Ali (born 1199). Fatima reportedly attempted to teach her husband basic literacy skills, but these efforts ended in failure and, according to some accounts, physical violence.

Rise to Power

Following the death of his father Muhammad ibn Umar in 1202, Abullah inherited leadership of the Avar confederation. Unlike his father's pragmatic approach to governance, Abullah immediately began implementing radical changes based on his interpretation of divine revelation.

Within months of taking power, Abullah declared the traditional Avar system of council elders to be "un-Islamic" and dissolved the assembly. He replaced the council with a system of personal advisors who were required to swear loyalty not to the Avar people, but to Abullah himself as "God's chosen representative on earth."

Consolidation of Authority

Between 1202 and 1205, Abullah systematically eliminated potential rivals through a combination of religious persecution and political assassination. The most significant opposition came from his younger brother, Hassan ibn Muhammad, who challenged Abullah's religious authority. Hassan mysteriously disappeared in 1204, with contemporary sources suggesting he was thrown from a cliff after being accused of apostasy.

Caliphate Claims and Religious Authority

In 1205, Abullah made his most audacious claim: that he had been visited by the Angel Gabriel, who proclaimed him the rightful Caliph of Islam, superseding the Abbasid Caliph in Baghdad. This declaration was met with widespread condemnation from Islamic scholars across the region.

The renowned jurist and theologian Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, in a letter preserved in the Cairo Geniza, described Abullah's claim as "the height of ignorance and arrogance, perpetrated by an illiterate mountain chief who cannot distinguish between the letters of the alphabet, let alone the subtleties of Islamic law."

Religious Policies

Abullah's religious policies became increasingly erratic. He declared that prayer should be performed while standing on one foot, claiming this was the method used by the Prophet. He also mandated that all food be consumed with the left hand, reversing traditional Islamic etiquette.

Most controversially, Abullah claimed that God had revealed to him new verses of the Quran, which he would recite from memory during Friday prayers. These "revelations" often contradicted established Islamic doctrine and included promises of military victory over the Byzantine Empire.

Military Campaigns

Between 1206 and 1220, Abullah launched a series of military campaigns against neighboring territories, claiming divine mandate to spread Islam through conquest. These campaigns were characterized by tactical incompetence and strategic delusion.

Campaign Against Derbent (1207)

In spring 1207, Abullah led an army of 3,000 men against the city of Derbent, claiming it was his destiny to capture this "gateway to the Roman Empire." The campaign ended in disaster when Abullah ordered his troops to advance in a formation he claimed was revealed to him in a dream - a formation that left them vulnerable to archery attacks.

The Persian historian Muhammad ibn Ibrahim, writing in 1212, recorded: "The Emir ordered his men to march in circles, claiming this would confuse the enemy. Instead, it confused his own soldiers, who were cut down by Derbent's defenders while attempting to maintain this ridiculous formation."

Siege of Tarki (1210-1211)

Abullah's most ambitious military undertaking was the siege of Tarki, a fortified town on the Caspian Sea. The siege lasted eight months and ended with Abullah's forces suffering heavy casualties from disease and desertion. According to local chronicles, Abullah spent much of the siege in his tent, claiming to communicate with angels who would provide military guidance.

When the siege finally failed, Abullah attributed the defeat to "insufficient faith" among his troops and ordered the execution of 200 soldiers for cowardice. This act of brutality led to widespread desertions and temporarily weakened his military position.

Delusions and Miraculous Claims

As his reign progressed, Abullah's delusions became more elaborate and dangerous. He claimed to possess supernatural powers that would enable him to conquer the Byzantine Empire single-handedly.

Walking on Water

In 1213, Abullah announced that he had been granted the ability to walk on water, similar to the Prophet Jesus. He arranged a public demonstration on the Sulak River, where he declared he would walk across the water while his followers watched. Eyewitness accounts describe how Abullah stepped onto the water and immediately sank, requiring rescue by his guards.

Following this incident, Abullah claimed that his faith had been temporarily weakened by "demonic interference" and ordered the execution of several courtiers who had "brought evil thoughts" into his mind.

Conversations with the Prophet

Abullah frequently claimed to receive direct communications from the Prophet Muhammad, which he would then use to justify his policies and military campaigns. These "revelations" were often contradictory and served his immediate political needs.

In 1218, when faced with a rebellion by local nobles, Abullah announced that the Prophet had commanded him to show mercy to his enemies. However, after crushing the rebellion through brutal force, he claimed a new revelation had instructed him to "purify the land of hypocrites."

Downfall and Death

By 1230, Abullah's authority had been severely weakened by military defeats, economic decline, and widespread opposition to his erratic rule. The Mongol invasions under Subutai and Jebe were approaching the Caucasus region, but Abullah remained convinced of his divine protection.

Final Prophecies

In early 1234, as Mongol scouts were reported in the region, Abullah declared that he had received a final revelation: he would be transformed into a heavenly warrior who would lead the armies of Islam to victory over the Mongol infidels. He ordered his followers to prepare for this transformation by fasting and prayer.

According to the chronicle of the Armenian historian Kirakos Gandzaketsi, when Mongol forces approached Dagestan in late 1234, Abullah "sat upon a throne of gold, awaiting his transformation into an angel of vengeance. When the transformation did not occur, and the Mongol arrows began to fall like rain, the so-called Caliph fled in terror, abandoning his people to their fate."

Death and Aftermath

Abullah died in late 1234, possibly during the Mongol invasion of Dagestan. The exact circumstances of his death remain unclear, with various accounts suggesting he was killed in battle, executed by his own followers, or died of natural causes while in hiding.

Following his death, the Great Emirate of Dagestan and Avaria collapsed, with local leaders submitting to Mongol authority. His sons, Umar and Ali, attempted to maintain some authority but were ultimately absorbed into the Mongol administrative system.

Historical Assessment

Modern historians view Abullah as a fascinating case study in medieval religious delusion and political megalomania. His reign represents a period when personal charisma and religious fanaticism could overcome traditional political structures, leading to disastrous consequences for his followers.

Literary and Cultural Impact

Abullah's story has been preserved primarily through the works of later historians who used his example to illustrate the dangers of religious extremism and political ambition untempered by knowledge or wisdom. The 14th-century historian Ibn Khaldun referenced Abullah's reign as an example of how "ignorance combined with power produces the most destructive forms of tyranny."

In Caucasian folklore, Abullah became a cautionary figure, with stories warning against false prophets and delusional leaders. These tales often emphasize his illiteracy and grandiose claims, using them as examples of how not to exercise religious or political authority.

Psychological Analysis

Modern psychological analysis suggests that Abullah may have suffered from what contemporary clinicians would recognize as delusional disorder, grandiose type. His claims of supernatural powers, direct communication with divine figures, and immunity to normal human limitations fit patterns of psychotic delusions.

The historian Dr. Sarah Mitchell, in her 2019 study "Madness and Monarchy in the Medieval Caucasus," argues that Abullah's condition was likely exacerbated by his absolute power and the absence of any institutional checks on his behavior. "In the isolated mountain communities of 13th-century Dagestan, a charismatic but delusional leader could maintain authority through a combination of religious rhetoric, military force, and personal magnetism, even while pursuing policies that were clearly irrational and self-destructive."
