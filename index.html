Uzbekistan protests spread to Samarkand, Urgenc, Kokand, Namangan; security forces fire live rounds

Security forces respond with deadly force as protests reach historic Silk Road cities and approach capital

By Clarissa Ward and Ivan Watson | September 1, 2027

Tashkent, Uzbekistan — Anti-government protests have spread to major cities across Uzbekistan, including the historic Silk Road cities of Samarkand, Urgenc, Kokand, and Namangan, with protesters now approaching the capital Tashkent. Security forces have responded with deadly force, firing live rounds into crowds, according to witnesses and human rights groups.

The protests, which began over economic grievances in southern Uzbekistan, have transformed into a nationwide uprising against President Shavkat Mirziyoyev's government. In Samarkand, protesters gathered in Registan Square, chanting slogans against the regime.

New slogan reported in Samarkand: "Ey jinoyatchi rejim, qurollanganimizni kut!" (O criminal regime, wait until we arm ourselves!)

Witnesses described scenes of chaos and violence as security forces opened fire on protesters in multiple cities. In Jizzakh, a city located between Samarkand and Tashkent, one protester told CNN what they witnessed.

"These damned criminals were firing live rounds; I saw at least sixty people die, and I saw them firing machine guns."

In Samarkand, security forces responded with live bullets as protesters occupied the historic city center. Similar scenes of violence were reported across Uzbekistan as the government crackdown became increasingly harsh.

Protesters chanted: "Qotil Shavkat Mirziyoyevga o'lim, bolalarni o'ldiruvchi maxluq" (Death to the murderer Shavkat Mirziyoyev, the child-killing monster)

In response to the violence, protesters began fighting back, throwing stones and rocks at security forces. The situation escalated further when Uzbek state media reported that a police commander was killed in clashes in Bukhara, and the police station in Bukhara was set on fire by angry protesters.

436+ CONFIRMED DEAD

Human rights groups warn the actual death toll could be significantly higher

The violence marks a dramatic escalation from the economic protests that began just days earlier. What started as demonstrations over food prices and inflation has transformed into the most serious challenge to Uzbekistan's government since independence in 1991.

According to human rights groups, at least 436 protesters have been confirmed killed, though sources warned that number could be far higher given the difficulty in obtaining accurate information from the tightly controlled country.

Cities Affected by Violence

  • Samarkand: Historic Registan Square occupied by protesters
  • Jizzakh: Witness reports 60+ killed by machine gun fire
  • Bukhara: Police station burned, commander killed
  • Urgenc, Kokand, Namangan: Live ammunition used against crowds
  • Near Tashkent: Protests reported in suburbs

The spread of protests to cities near Tashkent is particularly significant, as it brings the unrest to the doorstep of the capital. The government has reportedly deployed additional security forces around the city and restricted movement in and out of the capital.

Internet restrictions have been expanded nationwide, with social media platforms and messaging services largely inaccessible. The government has not acknowledged these restrictions, but monitoring groups reported widespread connectivity issues across the country.

The United States Embassy has issued an emergency security alert, urging American citizens to shelter in place and avoid all areas where demonstrations are taking place. Several other Western embassies have issued similar warnings.

The violence has drawn international condemnation, with human rights groups calling for an immediate end to the use of lethal force against protesters. The UN High Commissioner for Human Rights issued a statement expressing "deep concern" over the situation.

Uzbekistan, a former Soviet republic with a population of 36 million, has been struggling with economic challenges including inflation that reached 21.7% in July. The som has lost 35% of its value against the dollar this year, making imports more expensive in the import-dependent economy.

President Mirziyoyev, who came to power promising liberal reforms, has faced increasing criticism over his handling of the economy and allegations of corruption within his government. The protests represent the most serious challenge to his authority since he consolidated power following constitutional changes in 2022.

As night fell across Uzbekistan, the sound of gunfire continued to echo through multiple cities. Demonstrators vowed to continue their protests despite the violence, while security forces maintained a heavy presence in major urban areas.

The coming days will be crucial in determining whether the government can contain the unrest through force, or if the protests will continue to spread despite the deadly crackdown. With protesters now approaching the capital and openly calling for the president's removal, the stakes have never been higher for both sides.
