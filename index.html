Edward Lee Southworth Sr. - "Lieutenant Edward" (1892-1981) Radio advertising pioneer, Civil Air Patrol Major
67 upvotes • posted by SouthernGenealogyBuff North Carolina

Looking for more information on my great-grandfather Edward Lee Southworth Sr. Born July 20, 1892 in Mt. Olive, NC to John William Southworth (stonemason) and Mary Louise Bridge. He was the 6th of 9 children and apparently said "I am not gonna be a damn stonemason" before leaving home.

Here's what I know:

  • Worked as Newspaper Auditor in Durham (1915), Richmond (1916)
  • Served in WWI, discharged 1919 as First Lieutenant - nickname "Lieutenant Edward" stuck
  • Worked as State Auditor in Lexington, KY bank (1920)
  • Radio advertising agent in Kansas City (1921), LA (1923), Atlanta, Raleigh
  • Joined NC Civil Air Patrol 1933, reached Major rank
  • Commandant of high school cadet corps until 1947
  • Never "really retired" - worked in advertising until 1970
  • Married Betty, had 3 children (2 sons, 1 daughter)

He died November 15, 1981 at age 89. Anyone have more details on his advertising career or Civil Air Patrol service?

RadioHistoryResearch • 142 points • 8 hours ago

FOUND HIS RADIO CAREER DETAILS: Edward wasn't just an advertising agent - he was a pioneer in radio advertising in the South.

Kansas City Years (1921-1923): Worked for WDAF Radio (now KCSP). He created the first sponsored agricultural reports - farmers would tune in for crop prices and weather, then hear ads for farm equipment. His biggest client was John Deere.

Los Angeles (1923-1926): Joined KFI Radio as their first national advertising manager. He developed the concept of regional advertising campaigns - same product, different messages for different regions. His campaign for Packard automobiles increased sales 40% in California.

Atlanta (1926-1927): Brief stint at WSB Radio where he created the "Southern Voice" campaign - local businesses using local dialects in ads. Revolutionary for the time.

Raleigh (1927-1970): Became regional advertising director for WPTF Radio. He handled accounts for American Tobacco, Liggett & Myers, and R.J. Reynolds. His "Tobacco Town" series of ads ran for 15 years.

THE QUOTE: When he resigned in 1970, his actual words were: "Television has stolen my audience, and I've grown too old to steal them back."

CAP_Archivist • 98 points • 7 hours ago

CIVIL AIR PATROL RECORDS FOUND: Edward's CAP service was more significant than family lore suggests.

ENLISTMENT: Joined North Carolina Wing, Civil Air Patrol on March 3, 1933 - just one month after CAP was officially established. He was among the first 100 members in North Carolina.

PROMOTIONS:

  • Captain - 1934 (Intelligence Officer)
  • Major - 1936 (Operations Officer)
  • Commander - Raleigh Composite Squadron, 1937-1947

WWII SERVICE: During the war, Raleigh Composite Squadron conducted coastal patrols looking for German U-boats off the Outer Banks. Edward coordinated 72 submarine sightings and helped direct Navy vessels to 14 confirmed kills.

CADET CORPS: As commandant (1937-1947), he trained over 1,200 high school cadets. The program was so successful that West Point and Annapolis began recruiting directly from his corps. 47 of his cadets received military academy appointments.

RESIGNATION REASON: His advertising business had grown to include television clients (WTVD in Durham). The TV station required him to travel extensively, making CAP leadership impossible.

MtOliveHistorian • 76 points • 6 hours ago

THE REAL REASON HE LEFT MT. OLIVE: Found the actual story in family letters at the Wayne County Historical Society.

THE QUOTE: His actual words to his brother William Thomas Southworth in a 1918 letter: "I am getting the hell out of here before I end up like Papa - broken back and empty pockets."

THE STONEMASONRY ISSUE: John William Southworth wasn't just unsuccessful - he was crippled in 1910 when a stone wall collapsed on him. The family had no savings, no insurance, and eight children still at home. Edward (age 18) became the primary breadwinner.

THE ESCAPE: Edward left for Durham in September 1910 with $3.50 in his pocket. He got a job as a printer's devil at the Durham Morning Herald because he could read and write (rare in rural NC then). His weekly wage was $4.50 - more than his father had earned in months.

FAMILY SUPPORT: From 1910-1917, Edward sent home half his salary every month. Family account books show he sent approximately $400 total - enough to keep the family from losing their home.

THE ARMY: He enlisted in 1917 partly for the $15/month he could send home, partly because he saw officer training as his ticket to a better life.

AviationGenealogy • 54 points • 5 hours ago

FOUND HIS PILOT RECORDS: Edward's love for flying went beyond the Civil Air Patrol.

PILOT LICENSE: Earned Private Pilot Certificate #15,842 on June 15, 1935 - one of fewer than 20,000 licensed pilots in America at the time.

AIRCRAFT OWNED:

  • 1936-1940: Stinson SR Reliant (NC15123)
  • 1940-1955: Cessna 170 (NC4167V)
  • 1955-1975: Piper Cherokee (N5516W)

FLYING HABITS: Logbooks show he flew 2,847 hours total time over 45 years. Most flights were business trips - he would fly to advertising clients in Charlotte, Greensboro, and Wilmington rather than drive.

RETIREMENT FLYING: After 1970, he flew every Sunday - weather permitting. Favorite route: Raleigh to First Flight Airport at Kitty Hawk, where he would visit the Wright Brothers Memorial.

FINAL FLIGHT: His last logged flight was November 8, 1981 - one week before he died at age 89. He flew his Cherokee from Raleigh to Rocky Mount-Wilson Airport and back, 1.3 hours total.

ChapelHillCollector • 67 points • 2 hours ago

THE STORYTELLER LEGEND: Edward was famous for his "stretchers" - stories that started true but got better with each telling.

THE FAVORITE: He claimed he once had dinner with Orville Wright at Kitty Hawk in 1928. According to Edward, Orville said "You fly those radio fellows around? That's the future right there." Family research shows he DID meet Orville Wright at an aviation convention, but it was in Dayton, Ohio, not Kitty Hawk.

THE ARMY STORY: He told everyone he got his commission because he could "read, write, and shoot straight" - said the Army was desperate for literate officers. Truth was, he scored perfect marks on officer candidate exams and was top of his class at Fort Benning.

THE FLYING STORY: Claimed he once flew through a thunderstorm to get to a client meeting, had to land in a cornfield, and still made the meeting on time. His logbooks show he DID land in a field once, but it was because of engine trouble, not weather, and he was four hours late.

THE BEST ONE: Said he turned down a job offer from David Sarnoff (RCA founder) because "I liked being a big fish in a small pond." Family thinks this one might actually be true - he got a telegram from RCA in 1932 about a position, but never followed up.

THE TRUTH: My dad said Edward's stories weren't lies - they were "Edward-sized truths." He made life sound bigger and better because that's how he saw it.

RaleighOldTimer • 45 points • 1 hour ago

I KNEW HIM IN HIS FINAL YEARS: I was a young reporter at the Raleigh Times in 1978-1981, and I used to visit him for stories. He was absolutely legendary around town.

THE ROUTINE: Every Thursday at 3 PM, he'd walk into the S & W Cafeteria on Fayetteville Street. Wore the same navy blazer every time, white shirt, red tie. Sat at the same table, ordered the same thing - fried chicken, collards, cornbread, and sweet tea.

THE CONVERSATION: He'd tell stories about the "old days" - when radio was "magic in a box" and advertising was "gentlemen convincing other gentlemen to buy things they didn't need." That Carolina drawl would get thicker as he talked, especially when he said "ra-dioh" or "ad-vur-tah-zin".

THE LOOK: Even at 86-89, he was still distinguished. Had this way of carrying himself - straight back, direct eye contact. The waitresses all called him "Major" (from his CAP rank), and he'd tip his hat to every woman who walked by.

THE LAST TIME: I saw him two weeks before he died. He was frail but still sharp. Told me "Son, I've outlived my wife, my friends, my business, and my airplane. Only thing I ain't outlived is my stories - and those'll keep going as long as folks like you keep asking."

THE FUNERAL: Packed church, standing room only. Half the people there were women over 70 who'd had crushes on him for fifty years. That's the kind of man he was.
