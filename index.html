Stories about Colonel Jemison's thick Alabama accent and legendary personality

Stories Alabama Personality Accent Posted by u/Southern_Storyteller • 3 hours ago

My grandfather served under Colonel Jemison in Korea, and he used to tell the most incredible stories about this man's personality. The Colonel was already in his late 60s by then, but apparently he had this thick, syrupy Alabama accent that could make "company" sound like "COHM-puh-nee" and "attention" sound like "add-DUN-shun."

The Pipe That Never Left His Mouth: Apparently the Colonel always had this gnarled old pipe clamped between his teeth, even during briefings. It was never lit - just chewed on constantly. My grandfather said it looked like it had been through three wars itself.

The Cane Incident: He carried a walking cane that was NOT regulation issue. Story goes that when a young lieutenant tried to tell him it violated uniform standards, the Colonel just stared at him and said in that slow drawl, "Son, this here cane's seen more combat than you got years left to live." The lieutenant never brought it up again.

His Wife's Photo: Every evening at exactly 1700 hours, he'd stop whatever he was doing, pull out this faded photograph of his wife Lillian (who'd died in 1954), and just... talk to her. "Well Lillian, another day down. Them boys is getting better at this soldiering business, but they still ain't got no sense about women." My grandfather swears the entire company could hear him through the tent walls.

Would love to hear from anyone else whose family members served with him. These old-school Southern officers had such distinct personalities that seem lost to time.

I'm Mary Jemison Wilson - Colonel Jemison was my father. I was born in 1913, so I remember him from the 1920s onward. His accent was indeed thick - when he was home, my mother used to tease him about sounding like he was "fresh off the farm." He'd say "warsh" for "wash," "tire" sounded like "tar," and "oil" was "awl." But the most distinctive thing was how he'd draw out certain words - "sugar" became "shuuu-gar" and "honey" was "haaaa-nee." He called us kids "sugar-pie" and "honey-bunch" in that slow, syrupy way. We loved it.
