Anderson Raymund Park (1873-1975): The Sparta barber whose family defied mortality - all 7 brothers lived past 90

Georgia Barber Shop Longevity Family Business Posted by u/Georgia_Archivist • 8 hours ago

Sometimes genealogy gives you a family that makes you question everything you know about 19th-century mortality. Anderson Raymund Park, born July 10, 1873 in Sparta, Georgia, came from a family that seemed almost immortal.

The Immortal Parks: Anderson was one of 7 sons of Rufus Raymund Park (abt 1845-1936) and Sarah Elizabeth Cobb (abt 1859-1951). Here's the mind-blowing part - ALL 7 brothers lived to at least 90 years old. Their parents both lived into their 90s. This wasn't just longevity - this was a genetic miracle for rural Georgia in the late 1800s.

The Great Migration to Milledgeville: Around 1899, Anderson and his brother Frank Leroy relocated to Milledgeville and opened "Park Brothers Barbershop" on Hancock Street. The shop became an institution - operating for 35 years until a devastating fire in 1934 destroyed the building and forced Anderson into early retirement at age 61.

Family Details: Married Edith Louise Thomas (March 11, 1878-October 15, 1979) - she outlived him by 4 years. They had 4 sons: David Robert, William Francis, John Edward, and Raymund Thomas (notice the pattern - all had family names).

The Mystery: I'm trying to find any medical records or family journals that might explain this incredible longevity. Also looking for details on the 1934 fire that ended their business - was it suspicious? Anderson lived another 41 years after retiring, supported by his brother Frank. How did Frank afford this? And where are all 7 brothers buried? This family needs further investigation.

u/Park_Family_DNA • 4 hours ago
DNA analysis reveals longevity secret! I convinced a Park descendant to do DNA testing. Results show a rare variant of the FOXO3 gene - known as the "longevity gene." This variant is found in only 0.3% of the population but is present in 85% of centenarians. The Park family has an even rarer variant that appears to provide enhanced cellular repair mechanisms. University of Georgia is studying the family - they call them "Georgia's Methuselahs." The variant likely came from Sarah Cobb's line - her family had similar longevity patterns.
