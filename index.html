Coalition Decapitates Islamic Caliphate: Senior Leadership Wiped Out in Precision Strike

US-led airstrike eliminates caliph and entire senior command structure, killing 600 jihadists including supreme leader and top military commanders
By Barbara Starr & Jim Sciutto | CNN

Washington DC — The United States has delivered a devastating blow to the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel, with a precision airstrike eliminating the group's supreme leader, the self-declared Caliph, and virtually the entire senior command structure during a high-level meeting at their headquarters compound, US officials confirmed Tuesday.

Key facts

  • Caliph and supreme leader killed in precision strike
  • Entire senior leadership structure eliminated
  • 600 jihadists killed including top commanders
  • Coalition intelligence tracked meeting for weeks
  • Multiple JDAMs and precision munitions used
  • Zero civilian casualties confirmed
  • Most significant counterterrorism success since bin Laden

The strike, which occurred at 03:47 local time on June 11th in a remote compound outside Niamey, Niger, represents the most significant decapitation of a terrorist organization since the killing of Osama bin Laden in 2011, according to senior US officials.

Satellite imagery shows the complete destruction of the Islamic Caliphate's command center

"Last night, the United States military delivered justice to the leadership of the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel," President Trump announced in a televised address from the White House Situation Room. "The Caliph, his senior deputies, and over 600 terrorist fighters were eliminated in a single, perfectly executed operation. This sends an unmistakable message: if you threaten American interests and our allies, we will find you and we will eliminate you."

Operation details

The operation, codenamed "Desert Thunder," involved multiple waves of aircraft including F-35 Lightning II fighters, B-1B Lancer bombers, and MQ-9 Reaper drones that delivered a combination of JDAM precision-guided bombs and Hellfire missiles to completely destroy the caliphate's command and control center.

Intelligence officials told CNN that coalition forces had been tracking the caliphate's senior leadership for weeks, using a combination of satellite imagery, signals intelligence, and human sources to determine when the group's top commanders would be gathered in one location.

"The Caliph, his senior deputies, and over 600 terrorist fighters were eliminated in a single, perfectly executed operation. This sends an unmistakable message: if you threaten American interests and our allies, we will find you and we will eliminate you."

"We had what we call a 'golden moment' — the entire leadership structure in one place, minimal civilian presence, perfect weather conditions, and full tactical surprise," said a senior defense official who briefed CNN on condition of anonymity. "We took the shot, and it was devastatingly effective."

The compound, which intelligence analysts had identified as the caliphate's primary headquarters, was completely destroyed in the attack. Secondary explosions that continued for several hours after the initial strikes suggest that large quantities of weapons and ammunition were being stored at the facility.

Leadership eliminated

Among those confirmed killed in the strike were:

  • The Caliph: Supreme leader of the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel, who had declared himself the spiritual and political successor to the historical caliphate
  • Supreme Military Commander: Responsible for all military operations across West Africa
  • Deputy Caliph: Second-in-command and designated successor
  • Intelligence Chief: Head of the caliphate's extensive intelligence network
  • Regional Commanders: Leaders responsible for operations in Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, and Nigeria
  • Finance Chief: Head of the caliphate's financial operations and resource management

"This is the complete decapitation of the organization," said Seth Jones, director of the International Security Program at the Center for Strategic and International Studies. "When you eliminate not just the top leader but the entire command structure, you're looking at an organization that will struggle to function effectively, at least in the short term."

Intelligence coup

US officials described the intelligence that led to the strike as "extraordinary," involving a combination of satellite surveillance, communications intercepts, and human intelligence sources that had penetrated the caliphate's inner circle.

"We knew exactly who was in that building, where they were located, what they were discussing, and when they would be most vulnerable," the senior defense official said. "This wasn't a lucky shot — this was the culmination of months of patient intelligence work."

The caliphate had reportedly gathered its senior leadership to discuss strategy following recent setbacks in Nigeria, where coalition forces had successfully pushed back jihadist fighters from several key cities. The meeting was seen as an opportunity to reorganize and plan new offensives.

Coalition response

The successful strike has been hailed by coalition partners as a major turning point in the campaign against the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel. French President Emmanuel Macron called it "a decisive moment in the fight against terrorism in Africa," while British Prime Minister Rishi Sunak described it as "a stunning demonstration of precision military power."

Chinese President Xi Jinping, whose forces have participated in the coalition campaign, called the operation "a model of international cooperation against terrorism," while Nigerian President Bola Tinubu said it "delivers justice to those who have brought so much suffering to our region."

Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth, who appeared alongside President Trump at the announcement, said the operation demonstrated "the unparalleled capability of the United States military to project power and eliminate threats anywhere in the world."

Strategic implications

Military analysts told CNN that eliminating the caliphate's senior leadership could significantly degrade the organization's ability to coordinate operations across the vast territory it controls, though they cautioned that the group has shown remarkable resilience in the past.

"The caliphate has built a fairly decentralized structure, but even decentralized organizations need senior leadership for strategic coordination," said Dr. Akinola Olojo of the University of Lagos. "This strike removes the individuals who were responsible for maintaining cohesion across multiple countries and operations."

US officials said they expect the caliphate to attempt to regroup and promote new leadership, but they believe the loss of institutional knowledge and personal relationships will be difficult to replace quickly.

Next phases

While celebrating the successful strike, US officials emphasized that the operation represents only one phase of a broader campaign to degrade and ultimately destroy the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel.

"This is a major victory, but it's not the end of the war," President Trump said. "We will continue to hunt down terrorist leaders, we will continue to support our allies in the region, and we will not rest until this threat is completely eliminated."

Coalition forces are already preparing for what officials described as "exploitation operations" designed to take advantage of the leadership vacuum created by the strike. These operations are expected to include increased special forces raids, expanded airstrikes against remaining targets, and support for regional ground operations.

"The caliphate is reeling from this blow," Secretary Hegseth said. "Now is the time to press our advantage and ensure they cannot recover from this devastating loss."

As the sun rose over West Africa on Tuesday, the full implications of the night's events were still becoming clear. But one thing was certain: the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel had suffered a blow from which it might never fully recover.
