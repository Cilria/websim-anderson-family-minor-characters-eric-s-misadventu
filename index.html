Macron declares himself Emperor, ends French Republic in stunning July 4th announcement

French president dissolves parliament, suspends constitution, declares establishment of Third French Empire

By Clarissa Ward, Melissa Bell and Jim Bittermann | CNN Paris
Updated 12:47 PM ET, Fri July 4, 2026

PARIS — In an unprecedented move that has sent shockwaves through Europe and the democratic world, French President Emmanuel Macron declared himself Emperor of the French on Friday, dissolving the Fifth Republic and establishing what he termed the "Third French Empire."

The stunning announcement, delivered during a televised address from the Élysée Palace at noon local time, came exactly 248 years after the United States declared independence from monarchical rule. Macron, 48, appeared in full military dress uniform as he announced the immediate suspension of the French constitution and the dissolution of parliament.

Key developments:

  • Macron declares end of French Republic, establishes Third Empire
  • Parliament dissolved, constitution suspended indefinitely
  • Military deployed to major cities across France
  • European leaders call emergency summit for Saturday
  • Global markets plunge on political uncertainty
"The time has come for France to embrace a new destiny. The chaos of democracy has failed our great nation. I accept the heavy burden of history and declare the establishment of the Third French Empire, with myself as your Emperor."

Within minutes of the announcement, military vehicles appeared on the streets of Paris, Lyon, Marseille and other major French cities. The French tricolor was lowered from government buildings and replaced with what appears to be a modified imperial eagle standard, reminiscent of the flags used during the Napoleonic era.

Macron's move appears to have the backing of the French military, with Defense Minister Sébastien Lecornu appearing alongside the president during the announcement. The whereabouts of Prime Minister Élisabeth Borne remain unknown, and opposition leaders have reportedly been detained or placed under house arrest.

The European Union immediately called an emergency summit for Saturday, with European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen saying the bloc was "deeply concerned by these developments and their implications for democratic values in Europe."

"This is a dark day for democracy," German Chancellor Olaf Scholz said in a brief statement from Berlin. "Germany stands ready to work with our European partners to address this crisis."

The United States Department of State issued a travel advisory for France, urging American citizens to avoid demonstrations and large gatherings. White House Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre said President Biden was "monitoring the situation closely" and would speak with European allies later Friday.

Global financial markets reacted violently to the news. The euro fell 3.2% against the dollar in afternoon trading, while French stocks plunged more than 8%. The CAC 40 index was temporarily suspended from trading due to extreme volatility.

Macron's transformation from centrist reformer to would-be emperor represents one of the most dramatic political reversals in modern European history. Elected in 2017 as a pro-European, pro-business candidate who promised to modernize France, he has increasingly embraced authoritarian rhetoric in recent months.

In his address Friday, Macron claimed that "the endless debates of democracy" had paralyzed France and prevented necessary reforms. He said the new imperial system would provide the "strong, decisive leadership" that France needed to address economic challenges, immigration, and security threats.

"I have tried the path of consensus. I have tried compromise. But the enemies of France — both within and without — have made democratic governance impossible," Macron declared. "The people have called for action, and I have answered that call."

The declaration drew immediate comparisons to Napoleon Bonaparte's 1804 coronation as Emperor of the French, which followed his own rise to power through republican institutions. Like Napoleon, Macron appeared to have secured military backing before making his move.

However, unlike the 19th century, Macron's actions face immediate international scrutiny and potential sanctions. The European Union has mechanisms to suspend voting rights of member states that violate democratic principles, though such measures would require unanimous approval from other member states.

French opposition leaders who remain free have called for mass protests, though the deployment of military forces appears designed to prevent large-scale demonstrations. Marine Le Pen, leader of the far-right National Rally, called the move "a betrayal of the French Republic and everything it stands for."

Jean-Luc Mélenchon, leader of the far-left France Unbowed, urged citizens to "resist this coup d'état" and said he was calling for "immediate and massive demonstrations across France."

The international response has been swift and largely negative. British Prime Minister Rishi Sunak called the development "deeply troubling," while Italian Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni expressed "serious concern about the state of democracy in France."

Russian President Vladimir Putin, however, appeared to welcome the news, with Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov saying Russia "respects the right of the French people to choose their own form of government."

The long-term implications for France and Europe remain unclear. France is a founding member of the European Union and NATO, and its transformation into an imperial system would fundamentally alter the balance of power in Europe.

"This is not just a French crisis — this is a European crisis," said Dr. Sophie Meunier, senior researcher at Princeton University's Center for International Security Studies. "If France becomes an authoritarian state, it could trigger a domino effect across the continent."

As night fell on Paris, military checkpoints remained in place around key government buildings, and the fate of French democracy hung in the balance. The world watches and waits to see whether Macron's imperial ambitions will succeed or whether the French Republic will rise again.
