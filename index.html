Apocalyptic Islamist group seizes Fes and eastern Morocco

Death cult leader claims to be 'final harbinger of end times' as thousands flee ancient city

By Sarah Al-Masri and Thomas McAllister | BBC News, Rabat | 11 March 2029

RABAT, Morocco — A radical Islamist apocalyptic group has seized control of the historic city of Fes and much of eastern Morocco, declaring the beginning of what their leader calls "the final battle against the forces of evil."

The group, which calls itself the "Army of the Last Hour," stormed Fes overnight, overwhelming local security forces and establishing checkpoints at all major entry points to Morocco's cultural capital. Thousands of residents have fled the city, with reports of gunfire and explosions echoing through the ancient medina.

"The final battle against the forces of evil must begin, and in this case, if we do not win, the world will burn in the fires of hell and all forms of life will be destroyed."

The group's leader, who identifies himself as Abu Umayr al-Mahdi, released a video statement in Arabic claiming to be the "final harbinger of the end times." The video, circulated on social media and encrypted messaging apps, shows al-Mahdi surrounded by armed followers in what appears to be the courtyard of Fes' famous Al-Qarawiyyin University.

Moroccan authorities have imposed a curfew across the region and closed all major highways leading to Fes. The Royal Moroccan Armed Forces have mobilized significant forces, though there are concerns about the military's ability to retake the city without causing massive civilian casualties and damage to the UNESCO World Heritage site.

"This is not just a terrorist group," said Dr. Fatima Zahra Berrada, a Morocco-based security analyst. "These are apocalyptic cultists who believe they are literally fulfilling Islamic end-times prophecies. They want to trigger a global conflict they believe will bring about the return of the Mahdi."

The group's ideology appears to blend fringe interpretations of Islamic eschatology with apocalyptic rhetoric typical of death cults. Their online propaganda speaks of "purifying" the region and preparing for a final confrontation with what they term "the armies of Gog and Magog."

The timing of the attack appears calculated to exploit regional instability. Morocco has been grappling with economic challenges and water shortages, while the broader Maghreb region has seen increased militant activity. The group's seizure of Fes, with its symbolic importance as a center of Islamic learning, suggests sophisticated strategic planning.

"They chose Fes for its religious significance," said Professor Youssef al-Marini, an expert on North African militant groups. "By taking control of one of the world's oldest universities and a major center of Islamic scholarship, they're sending a message that they're not just another terrorist group but claimants to religious authority."

The international community has responded with alarm. The United Nations Security Council is holding an emergency session, while neighboring countries have closed their borders with Morocco. Spain has deployed additional security forces to its North African enclaves of Ceuta and Melilla, while Algeria has placed its military on high alert.

European Union foreign ministers are holding crisis talks in Brussels, with several countries already evacuating their citizens from Morocco. The UK Foreign Office has advised all British nationals to leave the country immediately, while the US State Department has ordered the departure of non-essential embassy staff.

Inside Fes, the situation remains unclear. Phone and internet service has been intermittent, with reports suggesting the militants have taken control of telecommunications infrastructure. Residents who have escaped describe scenes of chaos, with the group reportedly imposing strict interpretations of Islamic law and conducting house-to-house searches for government loyalists.

"They came at night, shooting in the air and shouting about the end of days," said Amina, a 34-year-old teacher who fled with her two children. "They said we must prepare for the final battle. Everyone was terrified."

The Moroccan government has officially designated the group as a terrorist organization and has requested international assistance. However, there are concerns that a heavy-handed military response could radicalize more young Moroccans and destabilize the entire region.

The group's origins remain murky, though intelligence sources suggest they may have evolved from smaller extremist cells that have operated in the region for years. Their sudden emergence and coordinated attack suggest significant external support, though no country or organization has claimed responsibility for backing them.

"This is unprecedented in modern Moroccan history," said Dr. Berrada. "We've seen terrorist attacks, but never a group seizing and holding territory with this level of apocalyptic ideology. It's a new and dangerous development that could have implications far beyond Morocco's borders."

As night falls over Morocco, the ancient city of Fes remains in the hands of a group that believes it is fulfilling divine prophecy. For the thousands trapped inside, and for a region already grappling with multiple crises, the stakes could not be higher.

The world watches and waits, hoping for a peaceful resolution to a crisis that threatens to spiral into something far more dangerous. In the words of one European diplomat, speaking on condition of anonymity: "If these people truly believe they are bringing about the end of the world, conventional deterrence may not work."
