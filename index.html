Saudi anti-foreigner movement spreads online with extremist rhetoric

Nationalist groups gain millions of followers across social media platforms with calls for 'final racial war'

By Ahmed Al-Rashid and Andrew England
December 9, 2029

Saudi Arabia's anti-foreigner movement has metastasised across social media platforms with alarming speed, amassing more than 8 million followers across nearly 400 channels and adopting increasingly extremist rhetoric that includes calls for what one prominent group terms a "final racial war" to "save the Arab world."

The movement, which began three weeks ago with protests against Indian workers, has evolved into a coordinated nationalist campaign operating across Telegram, WhatsApp, Facebook, Twitter, Signal and even Discord. Posts calling for violence and mass deportation have garnered hundreds of thousands of likes and shares, according to analysis by the Financial Times.

"الحرب العنصرية الأخيرة لإنقاذ الوطن العربي"

"The final racial war must begin; this is our last chance to save the Arab world"

The Arab Brotherhood, one of the largest groups with more than 2 million followers on Telegram, posted this message last week alongside calls for supporters to prepare for "the final war." The post received more than 400,000 likes within 24 hours, according to social media analytics tools.

Another major group, Arab Falcon, distributed a message to its 1.8 million followers stating: "Brothers, be prepared for the final war. The time of peaceful protest is ending. We must reclaim our homeland through any means necessary."

The movement's leaders have called for a "Million Men March" from Jeddah to Dammam on December 11th, raising concerns among diplomats and security analysts about potential violence against foreign nationals. The planned route would pass through several industrial areas with large concentrations of foreign workers.

Khalid, the leader of the Sons of Arabs movement who has become the de facto spokesman for the protests, has escalated his rhetoric dramatically. In a recent video message viewed more than 3 million times, he declared: "The Government and The Royal Family must choose a side, are you with the Arabs or the Indians?"

The movement's spread across multiple platforms represents a sophisticated understanding of digital communication and a deliberate effort to circumvent potential bans. When Telegram channels are removed, new ones appear within hours. WhatsApp groups, which are harder to monitor, have become particularly active in recent days.

من واجب جميع العرب الاحتجاج على الهنود الكفار. أليس غريبًا أن يأتي بهم اليهود؟ ولماذا إذًا؟ ليحلوا محل العرب! يريدون إبادة العرب! يا إلهي! لا تدعهم ينتصرون.

One recent WhatsApp message, forwarded more than 50,000 times according to WhatsApp's own forwarding metrics, contained explicitly anti-Semitic conspiracy theories linking Indian workers to Jewish plots to "replace" Arabs in their homeland.

The Saudi government has taken a notably permissive approach to the online organizing, with authorities largely allowing the content to remain accessible despite laws against hate speech and incitement. This has led to speculation that elements within the ruling establishment may be tacitly supporting the movement.

"The government's response has been quite telling," said Dr. Hala al-Dosari, a Saudi human rights researcher. "They've allowed these platforms to operate while cracking down on other forms of dissent. It suggests at least some level of official tolerance, if not outright support."

The movement has also begun to attract attention from international far-right groups, with several European nationalist organizations praising the Saudi protesters and sharing their content across international platforms. This has raised concerns about the potential for the movement to inspire similar actions elsewhere in the Gulf region.

India's Ministry of External Affairs has quietly expressed concern to Saudi authorities about the online rhetoric, according to diplomatic sources. New Delhi is particularly worried about the spread of conspiracy theories and calls for violence against Indian nationals.

Analysis: The movement's rapid growth and increasing extremism represents a significant challenge for Saudi authorities, who must balance nationalist sentiment with economic realities that depend on foreign labor and international investment.

Whether the December 11th "Million Men March" materializes remains uncertain. What is clear is that the anti-foreigner movement has evolved from fringe Telegram groups to a sophisticated digital campaign with millions of followers and increasingly dangerous rhetoric.

"This represents a fundamental shift in Saudi political discourse," said Dr. Kristin Smith, a Gulf specialist at the University of Exeter. "The movement has successfully normalized extremist rhetoric and created a climate where violence against foreign workers is increasingly acceptable."

Ahmed Al-Rashid is the FT's Middle East correspondent. Andrew England is the FT's Gulf bureau chief.
