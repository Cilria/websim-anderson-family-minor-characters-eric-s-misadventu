Anti-Indian protests spread across Saudi Arabia

Thousands march in Jeddah as anti-Indian sentiment grows with religious overtones

By BBC News Middle East team
23 November 2029

Nearly 30,000 protesters have marched through Jeddah in the largest anti-Indian demonstration in Saudi Arabia's modern history, as anti-Indian sentiment across the kingdom takes on increasingly religious dimensions.

The protest, which began after Friday prayers and swelled throughout the afternoon, was organised by the "Sons of Arabs" Telegram group that has gained millions of followers in recent months. Protesters chanted slogans including "The Arab homeland is for Arabs only" and "O God, grant victory to the Arabs and punish the Indians."

"Allah Akbar, don't let the Indians replace us!"

The demonstrations have now spread beyond Jeddah, with smaller marches reported in Riyadh, Dammam and even crossing into Kuwait. The protesters are demanding that the Saudi government deport nearly 2 million Indians currently working in the kingdom.

What began as economic anxiety has evolved into something more sinister. Recent messages posted to the Sons of Arabs Telegram channel have introduced explicitly religious and conspiratorial language to the movement.

One recent post stated: "It is the duty of all Arabs to protest against the infidel Indians. Isn't it strange that the Jews brought them? And why then? To replace the Arabs! They want to exterminate the Arabs! My God! Don't let them win."

من واجب جميع العرب الاحتجاج على الهنود الكفار. أليس غريبًا أن يأتي بهم اليهود؟ ولماذا إذًا؟ ليحلوا محل العرب! يريدون إبادة العرب! يا إلهي! لا تدعهم ينتصرون.

The message represents a significant escalation in the rhetoric surrounding the anti-Indian movement, introducing antisemitic conspiracy theories and explicitly religious language to what had previously been framed as economic nationalism.

The Saudi government has so far taken a cautious approach to the protests. While officially condemning "all forms of discrimination," authorities have allowed the demonstrations to proceed with minimal interference, suggesting at least tacit approval from elements within the ruling establishment.

"The government is walking a tightrope," says Dr. Hala al-Dosari, a Saudi human rights researcher. "They need to maintain good relations with India for economic reasons, but they also cannot appear to be ignoring popular sentiment, especially when it's framed in religious terms."

The protests represent a significant challenge to Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 economic diversification programme, which relies heavily on Indian technical expertise and labour. Indian workers in Saudi Arabia sent home $18 billion in remittances last year, money that critics argue could be circulating in the local economy.

Similar protests have now erupted in Kuwait, where several hundred people gathered outside the Indian embassy demanding action against what they term "Indian economic occupation" of Gulf countries.

The Indian embassy in Riyadh has issued a security alert to its citizens, advising them to avoid areas where protests are taking place. However, Indian officials have so far refrained from making formal complaints to Saudi authorities, apparently hoping the movement will lose momentum.

For the millions of Indians who have made the Gulf their home, often for decades, the protests represent an alarming shift in the region's social fabric. Many have reported increased harassment and discrimination in recent weeks.

"I've lived here for 23 years," says Rajesh Kumar, an Indian engineer in Jeddah who asked to use a pseudonym. "My children were born here. Now people look at me like I'm some kind of invader."

Whether the protests will lead to concrete policy changes remains uncertain. What is clear is that the anti-Indian movement has evolved from fringe Telegram groups to mass street demonstrations, and shows no signs of abating.
