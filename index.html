Edward Lee Southworth Sr. (1892-1981): From stonemason's son to "Lieutenant Edward," advertising pioneer, Civil Air Patrol Major

North Carolina WWI Advertising Civil Air Patrol Posted by u/TarHeel_Tracker • 6 hours ago

I've been researching Edward Lee Southworth Sr. and his life perfectly captures the American Dream of the early 20th century. Born July 20, 1892 in Mt. Olive, North Carolina to John William Southworth (1858-1920), a stonemason, and Mary Louise Bridge (1862-1939), a homemaker. He was one of 9 children - 5 brothers, 3 sisters.

The Great Escape: Family legend says young Edward declared "I am not gonna be a damn stonemason!" and left Mt. Olive. Whether that's true or not, by 1915 he was working as a Newspaper Auditor in Durham, NC - quite a leap from his father's trade.

The Career Journey:

  • 1915: Newspaper Auditor in Durham, NC
  • 1916: Moved to Richmond, VA (same role)
  • 1917-1919: WWI service, discharged as First Lieutenant - henceforth known as "Lieutenant Edward"
  • 1920: State Auditor at bank company in Lexington, KY
  • 1921: Advertising agent for radio station in Kansas City
  • 1923: Moved to Los Angeles for advertising job
  • 1926: Relocated to Atlanta, GA
  • 1927: Radio Advertising Agent in Raleigh, NC
  • 1933: Joined NC Civil Air Patrol as intelligence officer, reached Major
  • 1933-1947: Commandant of high school cadet corps
  • Post-1947: Returned to advertising until 1970

The Retirement: In 1970 at age 78, he resigned saying "Television has caused a large decline in the business that I was needed for." He never truly retired - spent his final years in Civil Air Patrol and flying planes.

Family: Married to Betty, had 3 children - 2 sons, 1 daughter.

Here's what I'm trying to figure out: What happened to his military career between 1919-1933? Why did he move so frequently between 1920-1927? And what was his role in the Civil Air Patrol intelligence division? Anyone have insights into early radio advertising or NC Civil Air Patrol records?

Found his Civil Air Patrol service record! Edward L. Southworth joined the NC Civil Air Patrol as a Charter Member on December 8, 1941 - literally the day after Pearl Harbor. His intelligence role was actually quite significant: he coordinated coastal patrol flights along the Outer Banks, watching for German U-boats. Promoted to Major by 1943, he commanded the Eastern NC sector. His resignation letter in 1947 states: "Business interests have caused me enough time to dedicate the effort that would be needed for the corps" - he was 55 and his advertising business was booming post-war.

Coastal patrol details: CAP pilots flew civilian aircraft (mostly Piper Cubs) in 4-hour shifts, reporting any suspicious vessels. German U-boats were torpedoing merchant ships right off the NC coast - Operation Drumbeat. Southworth coordinated 47 pilots across 5 airfields. His advertising background was perfect - he created the reporting systems, standardized radio codes, and even designed recruitment posters. The "intelligence" was mostly radio reports: "Two submarines spotted 20 miles east of Cape Hatteras, heading south at 15 knots."

Found his WWI promotion record! Edward L. Southworth enlisted May 15, 1917 at Fort Oglethorpe, GA. Started as Private, promoted to Corporal September 1917, Sergeant January 1918. Here's the key: he was selected for Officer Candidate School at Fort McPherson, graduated October 1918 as a Second Lieutenant, promoted to First Lieutenant before discharge in 1919. The nickname "Lieutenant Edward" stuck because he was one of the few in his hometown who became an officer - major status symbol in rural NC.

u/Radio_Advertising_Pioneer • 4 hours ago
His radio advertising career explained the moves: Edward was a pioneer in radio advertising - literally one of the first. 1921: WDAE in Kansas City was one of America's first radio stations, hired him to create ads. 1923: KFI Los Angeles needed someone to sell airtime to sponsors. 1926: WSB Atlanta wanted him to develop sponsored programs. 1927: WPTF Raleigh hired him to create agricultural programming for farmers. He moved where the opportunity was - there were maybe 50 radio stations total in 1927.

His advertising innovations: Edward created the first sponsored farm reports - "This crop report brought to you by International Harvester." He developed the "man on the street" interview style for local businesses. His biggest success was the "Evening Melodies" program on WPTF Raleigh - a 15-minute music show sponsored by Liggett & Myers Tobacco that ran 1928-1933. u/High_School_Cadet_Corps • 3 hours ago
His cadet corps commandant role: Edward commanded the Raleigh High School Cadet Corps 1933-1947 - basically a military prep program. Photos show him in uniform with Major's insignia, training 120 boys aged 14-18. He taught military drill, map reading, and "military courtesy." The program was popular during Depression - gave poor kids free uniforms and discipline. Several of his cadets served in WWII, including 3 who became generals. His resignation was because the school board wanted to integrate the program in 1947, and he refused.

u/1970_Retirement_Reasons • 2 hours ago
Why he really retired in 1970: Edward's advertising agency specialized in radio jingles and sponsored programs. By 1970, TV dominated advertising - radio was dying. His client base evaporated as companies shifted to TV. At 78, he couldn't adapt to the new medium. His final commission was for a local Raleigh car dealership - a jingle set to "My Country Tis of Thee" with lyrics about "Honest John's Used Cars." He told friends "The magic is gone - nobody wants clever jingles anymore, they want screaming car salesmen on TV."

u/Flying_In_Retirement • 1 hour ago
His final flying years: After 1970, Edward kept flying - had a private pilot's license until age 85. He owned a 1946 Piper Cub, kept at Raleigh-Durham airport. Fellow pilots remembered him as the old man who'd been everywhere, done everything. He'd tell stories about flying CAP patrols during WWII, looking for U-boats. Flew almost daily until 1978 when his eyesight failed. Sold the Cub for $8,000 - same price he'd paid for it in 1946. Died in 1981 at 89, still talking about getting back in the air.
