Arthur Reuben Sinclair Jr. (1900-1992): Small-town lawyer, furniture store heir, never left New Castle

Indiana Legal History Small Town Life Posted by u/Indiana_Legal_Historian • 6 hours ago

I'm researching Arthur Reuben Sinclair Jr. and found a fascinating story of a man who had every opportunity to leave his small Indiana town but chose to stay his entire life. Born February 6, 1900 in New Castle, Indiana to Arthur Reuben Sinclair Sr. (furniture maker/store owner) and Nancy Ann Paytner.

The Family Business: His father and uncle John Paytner founded "Sinclair and Paytner Furniture Store Co." in 1909, advertising "The Finest Furniture in all of Indiana." The store became quite successful - enough to send Arthur Jr. and his older brother Fredrick to college in the 1920s.

The Timeline:

  • 1920: Attending college (studying law)
  • 1924: Passed the bar exam, partnered with Stephen Carter to form Sinclair-Carter Law Firm
  • 1928: Married Lucy (surname unknown)
  • 1929-1943: Had 5 children over 14 years
  • 1945: Uncle John Paytner dies, father takes full control of store
  • 1955: Father sells furniture store and retires
  • 1992: Died at home in New Castle of heart failure at age 92

The Mystery: Arthur Jr. was offered a chance to move to South Bend with his son but flatly refused. What kept him in New Castle? His law practice was successful (specialized in criminal defense), but was it more than just professional success? I'm trying to understand what anchored this family so deeply to one small Indiana town for multiple generations.

Anyone descended from the Sinclair or Paytner families? Or have insights into New Castle legal circles from the 1920s-1990s?

Found his wife's identity! Arthur Jr. married Lucy Mae Stephenson on June 16, 1928. She was the daughter of Dr. Charles Stephenson, one of New Castle's first physicians to have an X-ray machine in his practice. Lucy was a schoolteacher at Parker Elementary School before marriage. The Stephenson family had been in Henry County since 1832 - her great-grandfather built the first grist mill on the Big Blue River. Arthur met Lucy when he defended her brother William Stephenson on a drunk and disorderly charge in 1927. William was acquitted, and Arthur started courting Lucy shortly after.

His law school discovered! Arthur Reuben Sinclair Jr. attended Indiana University School of Law in Bloomington from 1920-1924. His bar exam results show he scored 87% overall - exceptionally high for the time. The exam was 3 days long and only 42% of applicants passed that year. His law partner Stephen Carter was his roommate at IU Law. They formed their partnership immediately after passing the bar and remained partners for 41 years until Carter's death in 1965.

u/Sinclair_Paytner_Furniture • 4 hours ago
Store history uncovered! "Sinclair and Paytner Furniture Store Co." opened March 15, 1909 at 1237 Broad Street (ironically, just 3 blocks from Arthur Jr.'s future home). The store occupied a 3-story brick building that still stands today. They specialized in "mission style" furniture and prided themselves on being the only store in Henry County to offer delivery by automobile. After John Paytner died in 1945, Arthur Sr. bought out the Paytner family share and renamed it "Sinclair Furniture Company." He sold the business in 1955 to a consortium of local investors for what would be equivalent to $2.3 million today.

Why Arthur Jr. didn't take over the store: Arthur Sr. actually wanted his lawyer son to take over the business, but Arthur Jr. refused. He loved practicing law and had no interest in retail. u/New_Castle_Criminal_Records • 4 hours ago
His most famous case discovered! Arthur Sinclair Jr.'s most famous criminal defense was the 1936 "Broad Street Murder" case. He defended a local mechanic, Frank McDaniels, accused of killing his business partner. The case made headlines across Indiana because McDaniels was found with the murder weapon but claimed innocence. Sinclair proved the real killer was McDaniels' wife's lover, using blood spatter analysis (very advanced for 1936) and a timeline based on a broken wristwatch. McDaniels was acquitted, and Sinclair became known as "the man who could get anyone off" - though he only took cases he believed were wrongful accusations.

u/Indiana_Legal_Partnerships • 2 hours ago
The Sinclair-Carter firm legacy: After Stephen Carter died in 1965, Arthur Jr. practiced solo for 15 years until his son James joined in 1980. The firm became Sinclair & Sinclair, then Sinclair & Associates. James still runs it today (though Arthur died in 2019). The original office on the second floor of the Farmers Bank Building is now a museum exhibit - the current firm moved to a modern building but kept the original desk, law books, and even Arthur Jr.'s pipe stand as a memorial.

Stories from my grandmother: My grandma (born 1925) remembered Arthur Sinclair Jr. as "the nicest man in town." Every Christmas, he'd dress as Santa for the Rotary Club party and give each kid a silver dollar. When she got divorced in 1968 (scandalous then), Arthur handled it for free because he said "no woman should be stuck with a man who drinks his paycheck." He was known for taking cases in exchange for produce, furniture repairs, or even once for a handmade quilt. His wife Lucy used to joke that their basement was full of "legal fees" - jars of pickles, canned tomatoes, and crocheted afghans.
