Saudi Arabia Sees Largest Anti-Foreigner Protests in Modern History

80,000 march in Jeddah as anti-immigrant movement spreads across kingdom with extremist overtones

By Ben Hubbard and Vivian Yee
December 1, 2029

Nearly 80,000 protesters marched through the streets of Jeddah on Friday in what Saudi Arabia's Ministry of Interior described as the largest anti-foreigner demonstration in the kingdom's modern history, as a once-fringe movement against foreign workers has evolved into a coordinated national campaign with increasingly extremist rhetoric.

The protesters, many waving Saudi flags and chanting in Arabic, repeated slogans including "Blood and Soil!" and "Indians will not replace us!" while demanding the mass deportation of the kingdom's foreign workforce, which numbers more than 10 million people.

"It is not enough that we kick out the Indians, we must drive out all foreigners from our homeland. The time of talk is over, the time of action has begun."

The protests, which were mirrored in smaller demonstrations in Mecca and Medina, represent a dramatic escalation of tensions that began three weeks ago with anti-Indian demonstrations in Jeddah. What started as economic anxiety has transformed into a broader anti-immigrant movement with nationalist and religious overtones.

The demonstrations were organized by a loose coalition of groups that have proliferated on the encrypted messaging app Telegram, including the "Sons of Arabs" movement, which has grown to 4 million followers, and newer groups such as "Arab Falcon" and "Arab Brotherhood."

Khalid, the leader of the Sons of Arabs movement who has become the de facto spokesman for the protests, addressed followers in a recorded message posted Friday evening. "It is not enough that we kick out the Indians, we must drive out all foreigners from our homeland," he said. "The time of talk is over, the time of action has begun."

The Arab Brotherhood group, which has gained 800,000 followers in recent weeks, posted a message Friday that echoed language used by far-right movements elsewhere in the world: "We must prevent the great replacement of our people, and protect the future of the Arabs and our children."

The Saudi government has taken a notably permissive approach to the protests, allowing them to proceed with minimal interference despite laws requiring permits for public demonstrations. This has led to speculation that elements within the ruling establishment may be tacitly supporting the movement.

"The government's response has been quite telling," said Dr. Hala al-Dosari, a Saudi human rights researcher. "They've allowed these protests to continue while cracking down on other forms of dissent. It suggests at least some level of official tolerance, if not outright support."

The protests come at a particularly sensitive time for Saudi Arabia, as the kingdom has been working to diversify its economy away from oil dependence under Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman's Vision 2030 program. That program relies heavily on foreign expertise and labor, creating tensions with nationalist elements who view foreign workers as competition for Saudi jobs.

India's Ministry of External Affairs issued a statement Saturday expressing "deep concern" about the protests and urging the Saudi government to ensure the safety of Indian nationals. The Indian embassy in Riyadh has advised its citizens to avoid areas where protests are taking place.

For the millions of foreign workers who have made Saudi Arabia their home, often for decades, the protests represent a frightening shift in the social climate. Many report increased harassment and discrimination in recent weeks, with some businesses reportedly refusing service to Indian customers.

"I've lived here for 23 years," said Rajesh Kumar, an Indian engineer in Jeddah who asked to use a pseudonym. "My children were born here. Now people look at me like I'm some kind of invader. It's like overnight everything changed."

The movement has also spread beyond Saudi Arabia's borders, with smaller demonstrations reported in Kuwait and Bahrain. In Kuwait, several hundred people gathered outside the Indian embassy demanding action against what they termed "foreign economic occupation" of Gulf countries.

Whether the protests will lead to concrete policy changes remains uncertain. What is clear is that the anti-foreigner movement has evolved from fringe Telegram groups to mass street demonstrations, and shows no signs of abating as economic conditions across the region continue to deteriorate.

Ben Hubbard is The Times's bureau chief in Beirut. Vivian Yee is a correspondent covering the Middle East.
