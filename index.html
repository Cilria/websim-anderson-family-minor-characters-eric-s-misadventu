China-Russia tensions escalate as media fuels nationalist backlash

28 October 2026 • By BBC News Staff • Beijing

Chinese state media has joined a growing nationalist movement calling Russians "barbarians" and urging citizens to destroy Russian goods, as tensions between the two nations reach their lowest point in decades.

The campaign, which began on social media just 48 hours ago, has now been amplified by mainstream Chinese outlets, marking a dramatic shift in the officially sanctioned narrative toward Moscow. Chinese television networks have begun broadcasting segments highlighting historical grievances while social media platforms allow anti-Russian content to trend unchecked.

Mainstream media joins the fray

What began as social media vitriol has now been legitimized by mainstream Chinese media outlets. The Global Times, a nationalist tabloid with close ties to the Communist Party, published an editorial declaring that "Russia's historical crimes against China can no longer be ignored," while China Central Television (CCTV) aired segments highlighting "centuries of Russian aggression."

Russian state media has responded in kind, with RT and Sputnik broadcasting segments portraying China as "ungrateful for Russian military protection" and suggesting that "Chinese expansionism threatens all of Asia."

"The speed at which this has escalated from social media sparring to state-sanctioned hostility is unprecedented. Both governments appear to be testing how far they can push nationalist sentiment for their own geopolitical purposes."

Chinese state broadcaster CCTV aired a segment titled "Historical Truth: Russian Expansionism and Chinese Territory," which detailed territorial losses from the 19th century. Meanwhile, Russia's Channel One broadcast "Chinese Betrayal: When Dragons Show Their True Colors."

Economic warfare begins

The nationalist campaigns have spilled into the economic realm, with Chinese consumers posting videos of themselves destroying Russian-made goods and Russian businesses in China reporting harassment and boycotts.

Russian businesses in Beijing and Shanghai have reported vandalism and harassment, with some Russian restaurants forced to close temporarily. The Russian consulate in Guangzhou has advised Russian citizens to "exercise extreme caution" and avoid displaying Russian symbols in public.

Historical grievances resurface

The current conflict has resurrected historical grievances on both sides. Chinese media has extensively covered what it terms "Russian colonialism" in the Far East, while Russian outlets have highlighted "Chinese expansionism" and warned of "yellow peril" threatening Russian sovereignty.

Chinese historians appearing on state media have detailed Russia's role in what China calls the "unequal treaties" of the 19th century, while Russian commentators have accused China of "historical revisionism" and "territorial ambitions disguised as historical justice."

"Both nations are weaponizing history for contemporary political purposes. The selective interpretation of historical events is being used to justify current hostilities and territorial ambitions."

The speed of deterioration has shocked diplomatic observers. Just weeks ago, China and Russia presented themselves as strategic partners against Western influence. Now, both nations' media ecosystems are filled with the kind of nationalist rhetoric typically reserved for historical enemies.

Diplomatic fallout

Behind the scenes, diplomats from both nations are scrambling to contain the damage. Chinese Foreign Ministry officials have privately assured Russian counterparts that the media campaigns do not reflect official policy, while Russian diplomats have conveyed similar messages about their own media's anti-Chinese content.

However, the public nature of the dispute makes de-escalation increasingly difficult. Both governments have allowed nationalist sentiment to build to such levels that any attempt to dial back tensions risks appearing weak to domestic audiences.

The economic implications are already becoming apparent. Chinese importers have reportedly cancelled orders for Russian energy products, while Russian exporters are seeking alternative markets for goods previously bound for China.

As the rhetoric continues to escalate, regional analysts warn that the China-Russia relationship may have reached a point of no return. The strategic partnership that once challenged Western dominance appears to be collapsing under the weight of competing nationalist ambitions and historical grievances.

"We're witnessing the end of an era," said one Beijing-based diplomat who requested anonymity. "The China-Russia partnership that shaped global geopolitics for the past decade is essentially dead. What replaces it will define Asian security for the next generation."
