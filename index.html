Greece passes emergency law allowing arrests without trial amid 'communist plot' claims

By Elise Labott, Chris Liakos and Antonia Mortensen January 20, 2027 Athens
Updated 2:45 PM ET: Greek police have begun arresting journalists and opposition figures under the new emergency law, according to local media reports.
Protesters face off against riot police outside the Greek Parliament on Monday following the passage of the emergency law. (Thanassis Stavrakis/AP)

Greek Prime Minister Nikolaos Katrakis signed into law Monday the "Katrakis Emergency Act," granting sweeping powers to security forces to arrest citizens without trial and shut down media outlets, as thousands of protesters flooded the streets of Athens denouncing what they call a descent into fascism.

The law, which passed parliament by a narrow margin early Monday morning, allows police to detain anyone suspected of "communist or Marxist sympathies" for up to 260 days without formal charges. It also mandates the closure of any newspaper, website, or broadcast outlet deemed to be "promoting leftist ideology" within 10 days.

Katrakis justified the measures in a televised address, claiming authorities had uncovered "a vast communist conspiracy to overthrow the democratically elected government and plunge Greece into civil war."

"These emergency powers are necessary to protect our democracy from communist lunatics who wish to start a civil war. We will use whatever force is necessary to maintain order and stability."

The emergency law comes just 10 days after Katrakis's ultranationalist Greek National Front party won the election, and represents the most significant rollback of civil liberties in Greece since the military junta of 1967-1974.

International condemnation

The European Union immediately condemned the move, with European Commission President warning that "democracy cannot be protected by undermining its fundamental principles."

US Secretary of State Marco Rubio expressed "deep concern" about the developments, while NATO Secretary General called on Greece to "respect the rule of law and human rights obligations."

Turkish President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan, whose country has long-standing territorial disputes with Greece, described the situation as "deeply troubling for regional stability."

Protests turn violent

Within hours of the law's passage, thousands of demonstrators converged on Syntagma Square outside parliament, chanting "Katrakis fascist!" and "Democracy now!" Riot police responded with tear gas and stun grenades, while protesters hurled stones and Molotov cocktails.

At least 47 people were injured and 156 arrested, according to the Greek Health Ministry. Among those detained were several journalists covering the protests, including CNN affiliate reporters.

"This is how dictatorships begin. First they come for the communists, then they come for the journalists, then they come for anyone who disagrees with them."

Opposition leader Alexis Tsipras of the Syriza party condemned what he called "an assault on Greek democracy," while communist party leader Dimitris Koutsoumbas vowed to "resist this fascist coup through all legal means."

Media crackdown begins

Police have already begun enforcing the media provisions of the law, with officers arriving Monday morning at the offices of several left-leaning newspapers and websites with closure notices.

The influential daily newspaper "Avgi" (Dawn) announced it would cease publication rather than comply with what editor-in-chief Yiannis Bournous called "censorship worthy of the darkest dictatorships."

State television ERT has reportedly been ordered to dismiss several journalists and producers for "promoting divisive ideologies," according to a leaked internal memo obtained by CNN.

Internet service providers have been instructed to block access to certain websites, though the government has not released a complete list of banned outlets.

Historical parallels

Analysts say the emergency law echoes Greece's painful past, including the Metaxas dictatorship of 1936-1941 and the military junta that ruled from 1967 to 1974.

"This is history repeating itself," said Professor Thanos Dokos, director of the Hellenic Foundation for European and Foreign Policy. "Emergency powers used to suppress political opposition have been the hallmark of authoritarian regimes throughout Greek history."

The law's provision allowing 260-day detentions without trial mirrors measures used during the Greek Civil War (1946-1949), when thousands of leftists were imprisoned on the island of Makronisos.

"Katrakis is essentially reviving the tactics of the 1940s and 1950s, when communists were hunted as enemies of the state," Dokos added.

Economic fallout

Financial markets reacted negatively to the political instability, with the Athens Stock Exchange falling 8% and Greek bond yields rising to their highest levels since the debt crisis.

The European Central Bank reportedly held emergency talks about the implications for Greece's banking system, while major European banks began reviewing their exposure to Greek assets.

"Investors hate uncertainty, and this creates massive uncertainty about Greece's future in the eurozone," said Gabriel Sterne, head of global macro research at Oxford Economics.

The developments come as Greece struggles with a cost-of-living crisis and high unemployment, conditions that analysts say contributed to the rise of Katrakis's ultranationalist movement.

As night fell on Athens, protesters vowed to continue their demonstrations, while government officials warned that the full force of the emergency law would be used against "anyone threatening public order."

The emergency measures can be renewed indefinitely, raising fears that Greece may be entering a prolonged period of authoritarian rule that could isolate it from its European and NATO allies.
