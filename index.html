Chinese nationalists revive territorial claims on 'Outer Manchuria' as online attacks on Russia intensify

December 12, 2026 • By CNN Staff • 7 min read

Hong Kong (CNN) — Chinese nationalist sentiment has reached a fever pitch online, with thousands of users openly calling for the restoration of "Outer Manchuria" — territories ceded to Russia in the 19th century — as tensions between the two nations continue to escalate amid growing Chinese contempt for Moscow's military failures in Ukraine.

The campaign, which began on December 10 with historical references to Mongol conquests, has now evolved into explicit territorial demands, with Chinese social media users posting detailed maps showing China's claimed borders extending to the Sea of Okhotsk and declaring that "Russia is a fake nation that will be carved up like a rotten melon."

Russian ultranationalists respond with fury

The Chinese campaign has triggered an explosive response from Russian ultranationalists, who have flooded Chinese social media with threats of nuclear retaliation and promises that "Russian tanks will be in Beijing before Chinese troops reach Vladivostok."

Russian nationalist channels on Telegram have called for "immediate military action" against China, while prominent Russian military bloggers have posted satellite images of Chinese border installations with captions like "targets for our Iskander missiles."

The Russian government has maintained official silence on the matter, but state media has begun running segments highlighting Russia's military capabilities in the Far East and historical claims to the Pacific coast.

From historical grievance to territorial demand

The speed with which the Chinese campaign has evolved from mocking Russia's military performance to explicit territorial demands has alarmed regional analysts, who see it as evidence of growing Chinese confidence in challenging not just American but also Russian regional dominance.

"This represents a fundamental shift in Chinese nationalist discourse," said Dr. Zhang Ming, a specialist in Chinese nationalism at the University of Hong Kong. "For decades, territorial claims against Russia were considered taboo due to the strategic partnership. Now that partnership is collapsing, and long-suppressed territorial ambitions are surfacing."

"The Chinese internet has decided that Russia is finished as a great power. When a nation is dying, you carve it up. Outer Manchuria is just the beginning — Siberia belongs to China too."

Chinese state media has subtly encouraged the campaign, with nationalist outlets publishing historical maps showing maximum Qing territorial extent and articles detailing the "unjust treaties" that ceded territory to Russia. While not explicitly endorsing territorial claims, the coverage has provided intellectual ammunition for online activists.

One particularly inflammatory post that received over 200,000 likes declared: "Russia is a fake nation — a prison of peoples held together by force. When it collapses, China must be ready to reclaim our historical territories: Outer Manchuria first, then Tuva, then all of Siberia."

Geopolitical implications

The campaign comes at a particularly sensitive time for Russia, which remains heavily dependent on Chinese economic support amid Western sanctions over Ukraine. Russian officials have privately expressed alarm at the speed with which Chinese public sentiment has turned against Moscow, according to diplomatic sources.

"The Chinese leadership is allowing this nationalist sentiment to build as leverage in negotiations with Russia," said Dr. Elena Petrova, a Russia-China relations expert at the European Council on Foreign Relations. "Beijing wants Moscow to understand that continued Chinese support comes with a price — and that price may include territorial concessions."

Russian President Vladimir Putin, who has cultivated close ties with Chinese leader Xi Jinping, now faces the uncomfortable reality that his nation's deepening dependence on China has made Russian territory vulnerable to Chinese territorial ambitions.

As tensions continue to escalate, both governments are struggling to contain the nationalist forces they helped unleash. The Chinese government has begun quietly removing some of the most extreme posts, while Russian authorities have banned several nationalist Telegram channels. But the genie may already be out of the bottle.

"Once territorial claims become part of popular discourse, they're very difficult to suppress," said Dr. Zhang. "The Chinese public has now been educated about Outer Manchuria. Even if the government tries to dial back tensions, the territorial ambition remains."

With Russia's military bogged down in Ukraine and its economy dependent on Chinese support, Moscow finds itself in the unenviable position of needing Beijing's help while facing growing Chinese territorial demands. The online campaign for Outer Manchuria may be just the beginning of a much larger territorial dispute that could reshape the Asian map.
