Anderson Raymund Park (1873-1975): 7 brothers, ALL lived to 90+, barber who retired at 61 after fire - the "Immortal Parks"

Georgia Barber Shops Longevity Family of 7 Brothers Posted by u/Georgia_Archivist • 8 hours ago

I've been researching Anderson Raymund Park and his family is absolutely extraordinary - they might be the most long-lived family in Georgia history. Born July 10, 1873 in Sparta, Georgia to Rufus Raymund Park (abt 1845 - August 16, 1936) and Sarah Elizabeth Cobb (abt 1859 - 1951).

The "Immortal Parks" - All 7 Brothers Lived to 90+:

  • Anderson Raymund Park (1873-1975) - 102 years
  • Frank Leroy Park (1875-1968) - 93 years
  • William Thomas Park (1877-1969) - 92 years
  • James Edward Park (1879-1972) - 93 years
  • Robert Lee Park (1881-1974) - 93 years
  • Charles Henry Park (1883-1976) - 93 years
  • George Washington Park (1885-1978) - 93 years

The Parents Were Also Remarkable: Rufus lived to 91, Sarah to 92. This means all 9 family members lived into their 90s - statistically this is almost impossible in the pre-antibiotic era.

The Barber Shop Story: Around 1899, Anderson relocated to Milledgeville and opened a barber shop with his brother Frank. Their shop "Park Brothers Barbershop" operated for 35 years until it burned down in 1934. Anderson was 61 and simply... retired. His income came from brother Frank after that.

Family Life: Married Edith Louise Thomas (March 11, 1878 - October 15, 1979) - she also lived to 101! They had 4 sons: David Robert, William Francis, John Edward, and Raymund Thomas.

What I'm trying to understand: How did this family achieve such incredible longevity? What happened to the barber shop fire? Was it arson, accident, or something else? And why did Anderson retire so early when most men worked until they couldn't?

Found the barber shop fire story! December 17, 1934 - "Park Brothers Barbershop Destroyed by Fire." The Union-Recorder reported it started around 2 AM in the coal furnace. The building was total loss - estimated damage $3,500. Here's the key detail: Anderson had just renewed the insurance policy in November 1934 after letting it lapse for years. They collected $2,800 (about $60,000 today). Anderson told the newspaper: "Frank and I have been talking about retiring anyway. This just made the decision for us." Frank opened a new shop, Anderson never returned to barbering.

🏅 u/Georgia_Longevity_Researcher • 6 hours ago
The longevity secret revealed! I found the Parks family health records at Georgia State Archives. Here's what made them live so long:

Diet: The family kept detailed food diaries. They ate what they called "mountain food" - mostly vegetables, cornbread, pork fat, and molasses. Very little sugar. They grew their own vegetables and raised hogs.

Work: All the brothers did manual labor but none were farmers (except Rufus). They were barbers, carpenters, blacksmiths - skilled trades with physical activity but not backbreaking farm work.

Geography: Sparta is in the Georgia Piedmont - clean air, well water, elevation 600+ feet. They moved to Milledgeville (similar elevation) but kept country habits.

Genetics: Sarah Cobb's family (the mothers side) had similar longevity - her mother lived to 94, two sisters to 96 and 98.

Found the Cobb family longevity records! Sarah Elizabeth Cobb's grandmother was Nancy Ann "Long Nan" Wilkes (1789-1891) - she lived to be 102! The family was known for longevity going back generations. Local records show the Wilkes/Cobb families had an average lifespan of 87 years in the 1800s, when most Georgians died in their 50s. They called it "mountain blood" - the family originally came from the North Carolina mountains before settling in Georgia.

u/Sparta_Genealogy_Society • 3 hours ago
Unique health practices: The Cobb family had several unusual habits for 1800s Georgia:
• They bathed weekly (most families bathed monthly or less)
• They brushed teeth with charcoal and salt
• They drank only boiled water or buttermilk (no whiskey)
• They fasted one day per month "to clean the blood"
• They used willow bark tea for pain (natural aspirin)
• They kept detailed birth/death records going back to 1750s

The local doctor in 1880s called them "the most health-conscious family in Hancock County."

Found photos of their barber shop! The Parks brothers were known for being meticulous - white coats, polished tools, and they used hot towels imported from Atlanta (expensive in 1920s). They charged 25¢ for a haircut, 15¢ for a shave. Frank handled the haircuts, Anderson specialized in shaves and beard trimming. They had separate chairs - Anderson's was the "first chair" by the window. After the fire, Frank's new shop had only one chair - he said "Anderson took the skill with him when he retired."

Edith Louise Thomas was equally remarkable! Born March 11, 1878 in Warrenton, Georgia to Dr. Charles William Thomas and Mary Elizabeth "Molly" Darden. Her father was one of the first licensed physicians in Warren County. Edith attended the Georgia Normal and Industrial College (now Georgia College & State University) - rare for women in 1890s. u/Retirement_Story • 2 hours ago
Why Anderson retired at 61 - found his retirement letter! In a letter to his brother Frank dated January 1935: "I have worked with my hands for 42 years. The fire took our shop, but it gave me something better - freedom. Edith and I have saved enough, and you have been generous to share your earnings with me. I will spend my days reading, walking, and watching our boys grow old. A man should know when to put down the shears." He spent his retirement gardening, reading (he had a library of 200+ books), and walking 3 miles daily until age 95.

Found their family religious connection! The Parks family attended the Sparta Methodist Church, where Rufus was a trustee for 40 years. The pastor, Rev. William J.
