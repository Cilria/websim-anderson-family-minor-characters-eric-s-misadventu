Tajikistan protests: Demonstrators chant 'Death to Rahmon' as unrest spreads

By Catherine Philp and Najmeh Bozorgmeh September 3, 2027

Khujand, Tajikistan — At least 6,000 protesters have taken to the streets of Khujand, Tajikistan's second-largest city, chanting "Марг ба режим" ("Death to the Regime") and "Марг ба Раҳмон" ("Death to Rahmon") in the most significant anti-government demonstrations the country has seen in years.

The protests, which began at 6 PM local time on Tuesday, were called for by a Telegram channel named "Free Tajik Motherland," which has been coordinating opposition to President Emomali Rahmon's government. The demonstrations quickly escalated as security forces moved in to disperse the crowds.

Timeline of Tuesday's protests

6:00 PM Protests begin in central Khujand
6:15 PM Security forces deploy rubber bullets and batons
6:45 PM 14 protesters hospitalized, 6 in critical condition
7:30 PM Protests spread to Istravshan
8:15 PM Two protesters killed in Istravshan

In Khujand, witnesses reported that police used rubber bullets and batons against demonstrators, leaving at least 14 people hospitalized, including six in critical condition. The city's central square, where the protests began, was described as resembling a "war zone" by local residents.

"They came at us with everything they had - rubber bullets, tear gas, batons. People were bleeding in the streets," said one protester who asked to remain anonymous for fear of reprisal. "We just want change. We want Rahmon to go."

The unrest spread to the northern city of Istravshan later in the evening, where security forces employed water cannons and, according to multiple eyewitness accounts, fired shotguns into the crowd. Two protesters were killed in Istravshan, marking the first fatalities in the current wave of demonstrations.

Background: Tajikistan's political landscape

Emomali Rahmon has ruled Tajikistan since 1992, making him one of the longest-serving leaders in the former Soviet space. The country has faced increasing economic challenges, with inflation and unemployment driving public discontent.

The Telegram channel "Free Tajik Motherland" has emerged as a key organizing platform, calling for nationwide protests on September 5th in what organizers describe as a "coordinated uprising against the criminal regime."

Tajik state media has downplayed the protests, referring to demonstrators as "rioters" and claiming that "order has been restored." State television broadcasts made no mention of the fatalities in Istravshan, instead focusing on what they described as "minor disturbances" that had been "quickly contained."

Key facts about the protests

  • At least 6,000 protesters in Khujand alone
  • 14 hospitalized, 6 in critical condition
  • 2 confirmed dead in Istravshan
  • Protests organized via Telegram channel
  • Nationwide protests called for September 5th

The "Free Tajik Motherland" Telegram channel, which has gained over 50,000 subscribers in recent weeks, has called for renewed protests across the country on September 5th. In a post published late Tuesday night, the channel's administrators wrote: "The blood of our brothers and sisters will not be in vain. On September 5th, we take to the streets nationwide. We will not be silenced."

The channel has been sharing videos and photographs of the protests, including graphic images of injured protesters and what appear to be spent shotgun shells found in Istravshan. These images contradict the official narrative presented by state media.

Local residents in Khujand reported that internet speeds were significantly throttled during the protests, with mobile data connections becoming almost unusable in the city center. This tactic, commonly employed by authoritarian regimes to disrupt protest coordination, appears to have been implemented by Tajik authorities.

"They can shut down our internet, but they cannot shut down our voices," read a message posted on the Free Tajik Motherland channel. "The world is watching. We are not alone."

The protests come amid growing economic hardship in Tajikistan, where inflation has eroded purchasing power and unemployment remains high. Many Tajiks have sought economic opportunities abroad, with remittances from migrant workers forming a crucial part of the economy.

Regional analysts suggest that the unrest in Tajikistan may be influenced by the recent political changes in neighboring Uzbekistan, where President Shavkat Mirziyoyev was forced to flee the country following massive protests and military defection.

"The events in Uzbekistan have clearly emboldened opposition voices across Central Asia," said one regional expert who requested anonymity. "People are beginning to believe that change is possible, even in the most entrenched authoritarian systems."

The Tajik government has not yet issued an official statement regarding the protests or the planned September 5th demonstrations. However, security forces have reportedly been placed on high alert across major cities.

International human rights organizations have called for restraint from Tajik authorities and urged an investigation into the use of lethal force in Istravshan. The United Nations has expressed concern about the situation and called for dialogue between the government and opposition groups.

As Tajikistan braces for the planned nationwide protests, the government faces a critical test of its ability to maintain control while the region watches closely. The success or failure of the September 5th demonstrations may well determine the future trajectory of this Central Asian nation.
