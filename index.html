Military coup in Peru: Antauro Humala seizes power weeks before scheduled elections

Lima, Peru (CNN) — Peruvian President Dina Boluarte was removed from power in a military coup late Tuesday night, as armed forces aligned with ethnocacerist leader Antauro Humala stormed the Government Palace in Lima and took control of key government installations across the capital.

The coup, which began around 9:30 PM local time, marks a dramatic escalation in Peru's ongoing political crisis and comes just three weeks before scheduled national elections on November 19. Humala, the brother of former president Ollanta Humala and leader of the ethnocacerist movement, appeared on national television flanked by military officers to announce the takeover.

"The corrupt oligarchy that has governed Peru for decades has been removed from power," Humala declared. "We have taken control to restore dignity to the Peruvian people and to establish true indigenous rule."

Witnesses reported hearing gunfire and explosions near the Government Palace as military vehicles surrounded the presidential compound. Boluarte was reportedly evacuated by helicopter to an undisclosed location, though her current whereabouts remain unknown.

The United States and multiple Latin American governments have condemned the coup. President Biden issued a statement late Tuesday calling for "the immediate restoration of democratic order in Peru" and warning of "significant consequences" if constitutional order is not restored.

International reaction swift

The Organization of American States (OAS) announced it will hold an emergency session Wednesday morning to address what Secretary General Luis Almagro called "a clear violation of Peru's constitutional order."

Regional leaders expressed alarm at the development. Colombian President Gustavo Petro called the coup "a grave threat to democracy in the region," while Chilean President Gabriel Boric demanded "the immediate release of President Boluarte and the restoration of constitutional order."

Markets reacted sharply to the news, with Peru's sol currency falling 8% against the dollar in after-hours trading. The Lima Stock Exchange announced it will suspend trading on Wednesday.

Who is Antauro Humala?

Humala, 63, has long been a controversial figure in Peruvian politics. A former army major, he led an unsuccessful military uprising in 2005 against then-President Alejandro Toledo, for which he served 15 years in prison before being released in 2022.

The ethnocacerist movement he leads promotes indigenous rights and anti-globalization policies, drawing inspiration from Peru's pre-Columbian history. Critics have accused the movement of having authoritarian tendencies and promoting ethnic nationalism.

His brother Ollanta Humala served as president from 2011 to 2016, and analysts say Antauro has long harbored ambitions to follow in his footsteps. However, he was barred from running in the upcoming elections due to his criminal conviction.
