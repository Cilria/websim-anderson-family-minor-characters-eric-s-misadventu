Coalition Forces Launch Decisive Offensive Against Islamic Caliphate

Major victories across seven fronts as caliphate loses 500 fighters daily and territory collapses
By Declan Walsh & Eric Schmitt | The New York Times

Washington — Coalition forces have launched a decisive offensive against the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel, achieving major victories across seven fronts with the caliphate now losing an estimated 500 fighters daily and controlling less than 10,000 troops across the entire region.

Coalition Victories by Front

  • Ivory Coast: Final Battle of Korhongo underway, 1,000 jihadists killed since July 29th
  • Togo: Caliphate completely driven out, Dapaong captured August 7th
  • Benin: Kandi liberated August 11th, forces reach Niger River
  • Senegal: Siege of Tambacounda begun August 24th, heavy fighting in western districts
  • Guinea: Last Siguiri pocket captured August 4th, caliphate eliminated
  • Mali/Niger: Agadez taken by ANLF rebels, RN18 highway seized
  • Casualties: Caliphate losing 500 fighters daily, less than 10,000 remain

The offensive, which began in late July and intensified throughout August, has seen coalition forces achieve their most significant territorial gains since the campaign began, with the caliphate's forces now in full retreat across multiple countries.

"We're witnessing the collapse of the caliphate's territorial control," said General Michael Langley, commander of US Africa Command, in an exclusive interview with The Times. "Their forces are demoralized, their supply lines are broken, and they're losing territory faster than they can defend it."

Coalition forces from multiple nations have been advancing across West Africa since late July

Intelligence assessments indicate that the caliphate has lost approximately 15,000 fighters since the offensive began, with desertions and surrenders becoming increasingly common as the group's territorial control collapses.

Ivory Coast: The Final Battle of Korhogo

On the Ivory Coast front, coalition forces have achieved their most significant victory yet with the Final Battle of Korhogo, which began on July 29th. After a month of intense urban warfare, jihadist forces are now confined to a tiny pocket in the western part of the city, with over 1,000 fighters killed since the battle began.

"Korhogo represents the last major urban stronghold of the caliphate in northern Ivory Coast," said French Brigadier General Pierre Lefebvre, commanding coalition forces in the region. "We have them completely surrounded, and their options are rapidly diminishing."

The battle has been marked by some of the most intense urban combat of the campaign, with coalition forces using a combination of precision airstrikes, armored assaults, and special forces operations to gradually reduce the jihadist pocket.

"We're witnessing the collapse of the caliphate's territorial control. Their forces are demoralized, their supply lines are broken, and they're losing territory faster than they can defend it."

Intelligence assessments suggest that the remaining jihadist forces in Korhogo, estimated at fewer than 300 fighters, are running low on ammunition, food, and medical supplies, with coalition forces preparing for a final assault.

Togo: Complete Liberation

The most decisive victory has come in Togo, where the caliphate has been completely driven out of the country following the capture of Dapaong on August 7th, marking the end of jihadist presence in the nation.

"The liberation of Dapaong represents the complete elimination of the caliphate from Togolese territory," said Togolese Defense Minister Essozimna Margbe. "Not a single jihadist fighter remains on our soil."

The operation to recapture Dapaong involved coordinated assaults by Togolese forces supported by French special forces and American air support, with the final assault lasting less than 48 hours before caliphate forces surrendered or fled across the border into Burkina Faso.

The victory in Togo has been particularly significant as it represents the first complete liberation of a country that had been under caliphate control, providing a template for operations in other nations.

Benin: Kandi Liberated

In Benin, coalition forces achieved a major milestone with the liberation of Kandi on August 11th, with forces now pushing caliphate remnants from nearly all of Beninese territory except for isolated pockets in the extreme north.

"The liberation of Kandi was a crucial turning point," said Beninese Army Chief of Staff Brigadier General Fructueux Gbaguidi. "We have now reached the Niger River and are preparing for operations to eliminate the remaining jihadist pockets."

The advance to the Niger River has created new strategic opportunities for coalition forces, who can now use the river as a natural barrier while preparing for operations into neighboring countries.

Senegal: Siege of Tambacounda

In Senegal, coalition forces have begun the Siege of Tambacounda, with heavy fighting breaking out on August 24th. While progress has been made in the western part of the city where several major strongholds have been seized, the fighting remains extremely heavy.

"Tambacounda represents one of the caliphate's most fortified positions," said General Lefebvre. "They have had months to prepare defensive positions, and we expect this to be one of the most challenging battles of the campaign."

Intelligence assessments indicate that approximately 2,000 jihadist fighters are defending Tambacounda, making it one of the largest remaining concentrations of caliphate forces in the region.

Guinea: Complete Elimination

Guinea has seen the complete elimination of caliphate forces with the capture of the last pocket in Siguiri on August 4th, marking the end of jihadist presence in the country.

"The capture of Siguiri represents the complete liberation of Guinea from caliphate control," said Guinean Defense Minister Mohamed Diane. "Not a single jihadist fighter remains on Guinean soil."

The operation to eliminate the Siguiri pocket involved coordinated assaults by Guinean forces supported by British and German special forces, with the final assault lasting three days before the remaining caliphate forces surrendered.

Mali and Niger: Tuareg Advances

In Mali and Niger, Tuareg rebels under the Azawad National Liberation Front (ANLF), backed by coalition air support and special forces, have achieved significant victories with the capture of Agadez and the encirclement of caliphate forces in a pocket north of the city.

"The ANLF has proven to be an invaluable ally in the fight against the caliphate," said a senior French officer involved in coordinating with the Tuareg forces. "Their knowledge of the terrain and local support has been crucial to our success."

The seizure of the RN18 highway in Mali has created new opportunities for coalition forces to advance south toward Bourem and the Niger River, potentially cutting off caliphate forces in northern Mali.

Caliphate Collapse

Perhaps most significantly, intelligence assessments now indicate that the caliphate has fewer than 10,000 fighters remaining across the entire region, down from an estimated peak of 45,000 fighters earlier this year.

"The caliphate is collapsing faster than anyone anticipated," said General Langley. "They're losing approximately 500 fighters per day through combat casualties, desertions, and surrenders. At this rate, their territorial control will be completely eliminated within weeks."

The rapid collapse has been attributed to several factors, including the effectiveness of coalition air power, the loss of key supply centers, declining morale among caliphate fighters, and the increasing effectiveness of local forces who have been trained and equipped by coalition partners.

Humanitarian Impact

The coalition victories have also had significant humanitarian implications, with millions of people now liberated from caliphate control and able to receive humanitarian assistance for the first time in months.

"We're seeing a dramatic improvement in humanitarian conditions in liberated areas," said Martin Griffiths, the UN's humanitarian chief. "Food supplies are being restored, medical facilities are reopening, and displaced populations are beginning to return home."

However, challenges remain as coalition forces continue to advance into areas that have been under caliphate control for months, with significant reconstruction needs and the challenge of reestablishing effective governance.

Next Phase

As coalition forces continue their advance, planners are already looking ahead to the next phase of operations, which will focus on eliminating the remaining caliphate strongholds and preventing the group's resurgence.

"We're not declaring victory yet," said General Langley. "We still have significant work ahead, particularly in Mali and Niger where the caliphate maintains its strongest positions. But the momentum is clearly with us, and we expect to see continued progress in the coming weeks."

The coalition is also preparing for what many expect will be a lengthy stabilization and reconstruction effort, with estimates suggesting that the complete elimination of caliphate influence could take months and the reconstruction of affected areas could take years.

As August draws to a close, coalition forces appear to be on the verge of achieving what seemed impossible just months ago: the complete territorial defeat of the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel and the restoration of government control across West Africa.
