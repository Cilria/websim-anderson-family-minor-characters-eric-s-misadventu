James William Hatchell (1909-1951): Tobacco farmer's suicide - family claims he was "scared, not depressed"

Virginia 1950s Mental Health Tobacco Farming Posted by u/Virginia_Archivist • 6 hours ago

I'm researching my great-uncle James William Hatchell and found something that doesn't add up. The family story has always been that he was a "worrier" who thought he had cancer and killed himself out of fear, but the death certificate says "depressed over thinking he had cancer."

The Facts:

  • Born: April 6, 1909, Muck Cross, Mecklenburg County, Virginia
  • Parents: John Hatchell (1879-1949) and Mary Elizabeth Morgan (1884-1965)
  • Family: Third oldest of 9 siblings, tobacco farmer his entire life
  • Married: Elizabeth Jane Bower, 5 children (4 daughters, 1 son)
  • Death: July 10, 1951 at 2:45 AM - gunshot wound to head
  • Location: "Near his home" according to obituary

The Conflict: Family members (now in their 80s and 90s) insist he wasn't depressed, just "terrified of doctors and hospitals." They say he'd been having stomach pains for months, lost weight, and convinced himself it was cancer. His mother Mary Elizabeth reportedly said, "He wasn't sad, he was just scared to death of what the doctor might tell him."

What I'm looking for: Medical records from 1951 would be sealed, but wondering if anyone has found newspaper articles about farming community suicides from that era? Or insight into how rural Virginia handled mental health discussions in the 1950s? The family story feels very different from the official record.

u/1950s_Medical_Historian • 4 hours ago
Medical context for 1951 rural Virginia: Stomach cancer was a death sentence - no chemotherapy, limited surgery options. But more importantly, farmers of that era had seen relatives die of "the cancer" in agony. My grandfather (also Virginia tobacco farmer) remembered neighbors who shot themselves rather than face cancer. It wasn't depression - it was terror of a slow, painful death. The medical term "depression" was often used interchangeably with "despondent" or "melancholy" on death certificates.

u/Tobacco_Farming_Expert • 3 hours ago
Tobacco farming health issues: In 1951, farmers were exposed to arsenic-based pesticides (lead arsenate, calcium arsenate) sprayed directly on tobacco. Stomach pain and weight loss were classic arsenic poisoning symptoms, often misdiagnosed as cancer. Many farmers died of arsenic poisoning thinking they had cancer. Your great-uncle might have been suffering from pesticide exposure, not cancer.

u/Pesticide_Historian • 3 hours ago
Arsenic use timeline: Virginia agricultural extension records show lead arsenate was heavily promoted for tobacco hornworm control 1945-1955. Farmers mixed it themselves in 50-gallon drums and applied with backpack sprayers - zero protective equipment. Symptoms of chronic arsenic poisoning: abdominal pain, nausea, weight loss, fatigue, anxiety. Sound familiar? By 1956 they switched to DDT, but arsenic residues persisted in soil for decades.

u/Medical_Terminology_1950s • 2 hours ago
Death certificate terminology: In 1951 Virginia, coroners used "depressed" as a catch-all term for any suicide not clearly motivated by financial ruin or romantic problems. "Depressed over thinking he had cancer" was standard language - it didn't mean clinical depression. The coroner was simply stating the deceased was upset about health fears. Modern medical terminology didn't distinguish between situational sadness and clinical depression.
