Islamist Group in Uzbekistan Renames Itself and Claims Gains in Armed Struggle

The Army of Islam and the Victory of Allah declares jihad and claims to have killed 200 government soldiers

By Carlotta Gall and Eric Schmitt | April 27, 2028 | Tashkent, Uzbekistan

A militant Islamist group that emerged from Uzbekistan's devastating drought has formally declared armed jihad against the government, claiming to have killed 200 government soldiers and seized weapons in heavy clashes across the country's mountainous regions.

The group, which rebranded itself from the Loyal Soldiers of Islam to the Army of Islam and the Victory of Allah, released a video statement on Saturday announcing the start of what it called "divine war" against what it termed the "infidel government" of President Shavkat Mirziyoyev.

"Our Mujahideen have made great strides against the Infidel Government, pushing onward. The old name did not reflect our real struggle."

In the video, a masked spokesman standing before a black flag claimed the group had achieved significant victories in their strongholds in the eastern mountains and hills, where they have been engaged in heavy fighting with government forces for several days.

"We have captured weapons along the way," the spokesman said, displaying what appeared to be seized assault rifles and rocket-propelled grenades. "Our fighters are advancing, and the government forces are retreating before the might of Allah."

The Uzbek government has dismissed the group's claims as propaganda, insisting that the militants are on the brink of being "wiped out" and characterizing them as little more than terrorists.

• Group claims 200 government soldiers killed
• Heavy fighting reported in eastern mountains
• Russia has launched airstrikes against militants
• Government denies group's territorial gains
• Group rebrands to "Army of Islam and Victory of Allah"

"These terrorists are trying to create the illusion of strength, but they are weak and desperate," said a senior Uzbek security official who spoke on condition of anonymity. "Our forces have them surrounded and their elimination is imminent."

The escalation comes just two weeks after the group first threatened armed struggle, marking a dangerous new phase in a conflict that began as a response to the country's worst drought in decades. The group has capitalized on public anger over the government's handling of the environmental crisis, which has devastated agriculture and left millions struggling with water shortages.

Russia, which maintains significant security interests in Central Asia, has responded to the crisis by launching airstrikes against what it described as "terrorist positions" in eastern Uzbekistan. The Russian Defense Ministry said the strikes were conducted at the request of the Uzbek government and targeted militant training camps and weapons depots.

"We are providing necessary support to our Uzbek partners in their fight against terrorism," said Russian Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov in a statement. "This is a common threat that requires a coordinated response."

The group's rapid evolution from a religious movement to an armed insurgency has alarmed regional governments and Western diplomats, who fear the conflict could destabilize the broader Central Asian region. Uzbekistan shares borders with five countries, including Afghanistan, where Islamist groups have gained ground since the Taliban's return to power.

"What we're seeing is the transformation of a protest movement into a full-blown insurgency," said Dr. Navbahor Imamova, a Central Asia specialist at George Washington University. "The drought created the conditions, but the government's heavy-handed response has pushed people toward armed resistance."

The group's rebranding appears designed to project a more formidable image as it transitions from a regional protest movement to what it hopes will be seen as a legitimate military force. The new name, Army of Islam and the Victory of Allah, suggests ambitions beyond Uzbekistan's borders.

"We are not merely soldiers of Islam - we are the army that will bring victory to Allah across all lands where Muslims are oppressed."

Independent verification of the group's claims remains impossible due to restricted access to the conflict zones. The government has imposed a media blackout in affected areas and blocked social media platforms that the group has used to spread its message.

However, residents in eastern Uzbekistan who spoke to The Times by phone described hearing heavy explosions and seeing military helicopters flying toward the mountains. Several reported seeing convoys of wounded soldiers being transported to hospitals in regional cities.

"The fighting is real - we can hear it," said a shopkeeper in the city of Andijon who requested anonymity for fear of government reprisal. "The government says they have everything under control, but people are scared."

The group's claim of killing 200 government soldiers, if verified, would represent the deadliest single incident in Uzbekistan since independence in 1991. The government has not released casualty figures, but hospitals in the region have reportedly been placed on high alert.

"Both sides are engaging in information warfare," said Dr. Edward Lemon of the International Centre for Counter-Terrorism. "The truth likely lies somewhere between the group's exaggerated claims and the government's dismissive statements."

The conflict has already begun to affect regional stability, with neighboring countries tightening border security and increasing military patrols. Kazakhstan has deployed additional troops to its border with Uzbekistan, while Kyrgyzstan has increased security along its southern frontier.

"We're seeing the emergence of a new type of Islamist movement - one that's rooted in local grievances but has regional ambitions," said a Western intelligence official who requested anonymity. "This isn't about creating a caliphate - it's about overthrowing existing governments and establishing Islamic states."

The drought that initially sparked the movement continues to worsen, with meteorologists predicting continued dry conditions through at least the end of 2028. Water levels in the vital Amu Darya and Syr Darya rivers remain at historic lows, devastating agriculture and forcing millions to rely on emergency water deliveries.

In rural areas, where support for the Islamist movement appears strongest, many residents say they have lost faith in the government's ability to address the crisis. The group's promise of Islamic governance and divine justice resonates with populations that feel abandoned by secular authorities.

"The government talks about modernization and economic development, but we can't even get clean water," said a farmer in the Fergana Valley who spoke on condition of anonymity. "Maybe it's time to try something different, even if it's Islamic rule."

The international community has responded cautiously to the crisis, with Western governments calling for restraint and dialogue while expressing support for Uzbekistan's territorial integrity. The United States has offered humanitarian assistance but has not indicated whether it would provide military support.

"We urge all parties to seek peaceful resolution of differences through dialogue and respect for human rights," said State Department spokesperson Matthew Miller. "The situation requires careful attention to prevent further escalation."

As the fighting continues, analysts warn that the window for peaceful resolution may be closing. The group's declaration of jihad and its claims of military success, whether exaggerated or not, have raised the stakes significantly.

"Once a group starts claiming territory and killing soldiers, it becomes much harder to negotiate," said Dr. Imamova. "Both sides are now locked in a cycle of escalation that could lead to a much broader conflict."

For now, the mountainous regions of eastern Uzbekistan remain the primary battleground, but there are growing concerns that the conflict could spread to urban areas. The government has increased security in major cities, and residents report seeing more military checkpoints and patrols.

As one Tashkent resident told The Times, speaking anonymously for fear of government surveillance: "The video is frightening, but also exciting. For the first time in years, it feels like something might actually change - though I fear what that change might bring."
