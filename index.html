Airbus eyes A350neo, new large aircraft in early-stage talks

European planemaker explores next-generation wide-body and successor to A380 in strategic review

By Tim Hepher and Allison Lampert | November 20, 2027 6:12 AM EST

PARIS - Airbus has begun preliminary studies on an upgraded version of its A350 wide-body jet and a new large aircraft to replace the discontinued A380, according to three people familiar with the matter, as the European planemaker maps out its product strategy for the 2030s.

The discussions, which remain at an early stage, reflect Airbus's efforts to maintain its competitive edge against Boeing and prepare for evolving airline demands for more fuel-efficient aircraft to serve long-haul routes, the sources said.

The A350neo, as the potential upgrade is being called internally, would feature new engines and aerodynamic improvements to deliver a 10-15% reduction in fuel consumption compared with the current A350-900 and A350-1000 models, two of the people said.

Airbus is also exploring a "New Large Aircraft" (NLA) concept that would sit above the A350 in size but below the A380 in capacity, targeting the 450-500 seat market that Boeing's 777-9 currently dominates, according to the sources.

"We're always looking at how to improve our products and meet customer needs," an Airbus spokesperson said, declining to comment on specific studies.

The deliberations come as Airbus enjoys record orders for its narrow-body jets while facing pressure to refresh its wide-body lineup, which competes with Boeing's 787 and 777X families.

Airbus Chief Executive Guillaume Faury told analysts last month that the company was "actively working" on future aircraft concepts but said any new programs would need to deliver significant improvements in efficiency and sustainability.

The A350neo study reflects Airbus's strategy of incremental improvements rather than clean-sheet designs, which can cost $15-20 billion and take a decade to develop. The current A350, introduced in 2015, is powered by Rolls-Royce Trent XWB engines.

Engine makers including Rolls-Royce, General Electric and Pratt & Whitney have been approached about providing next-generation powerplants for the potential A350neo, the sources said. New engines could deliver 5-8% better fuel efficiency through improved materials and higher bypass ratios.

The New Large Aircraft concept represents a bigger strategic bet, reviving a market segment abandoned when Airbus ended A380 production in 2021. The double-decker A380 failed to attract sufficient orders as airlines shifted to smaller, more efficient aircraft.

However, rising passenger traffic on busy routes between major hubs has some airlines reconsidering very large aircraft, particularly in Asia and the Middle East, analysts said.

"The question is whether there's a sustainable business case for a 450-500 seater that doesn't repeat the A380's mistakes," said Richard Aboulafia, aerospace analyst at AeroDynamic Advisory.

The NLA would likely feature composite wings, advanced engines and potentially a different configuration than the A380's full double-deck design, the sources said. Airbus engineers have discussed various layouts including a single-aisle upper deck or extended single deck.

Airbus declined to provide a timeline for any potential new programs, saying it was "too early to speculate" on when decisions might be made. Industry analysts expect any A350neo launch to come around 2028-2029 for service entry in the early 2030s.

The studies coincide with Airbus's efforts to increase production of its popular A320neo family and develop a hydrogen-powered aircraft by 2035. The company is also under pressure from airline customers to extend the range of its A321XLR, due to enter service next year.

Boeing, which has faced production and regulatory challenges with its 737 MAX and 777X programs, is also studying new aircraft concepts but appears focused on resolving current issues before launching major new programs, analysts said.

Airbus's product strategy review comes as the aviation industry grapples with meeting net-zero emissions targets by 2050. New aircraft will need to be compatible with sustainable aviation fuels and potentially hydrogen propulsion.

"The next generation of aircraft will need to be significantly more efficient and environmentally friendly," said Rob Morris, head of consultancy at Ascend by Cirium. "Airbus is positioning itself for that transition."

Shares in Airbus were up 2.1% in early Paris trading following the Reuters report.
