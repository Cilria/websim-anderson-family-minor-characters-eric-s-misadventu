Palantir acquires majority of Gabon's land in unprecedented tech-puppet state deal

Data analytics company becomes de facto sovereign over Central African nation in $47bn transaction

By Tom Burgis and Madhumita Murgia | 06/10/2027 04:32 AM GMT

LIBREVILLE, Gabon — Palantir Technologies has acquired 68% of Gabon's land surface and mineral rights in a $47bn transaction that represents the first time a technology company has achieved de facto sovereign control over a United Nations member state.

The deal, announced Tuesday morning in Libreville, comes exactly one year after Palantir formalised its role as the primary technology partner for Gabon's government, and six months after the company implemented what experts describe as the world's most comprehensive surveillance and social credit system.

Palantir-Gabon Deal at a Glance

  • 68% of Gabon's land surface (176,000 sq km)
  • 100% of mineral and oil rights
  • Full immigration and customs control
  • Sovereign immunity for Palantir operations
  • 99-year lease with automatic renewal clauses

"This is not colonialism 2.0 — this is data colonialism," said Dr. Ifeoma Okafor, director of the African Digital Rights Initiative. "Palantir isn't just extracting resources; it's extracting behaviour, patterns, and autonomy."

The agreement, signed by Palantir CEO Alex Karp and Gabon's military-appointed president General Brice Oligui Nguema, transfers majority ownership of the country's 267,000 square kilometres to the Denver-based company. In exchange, Palantir assumes all of Gabon's sovereign debt and commits to investing $47bn in infrastructure over the next decade.

Under the terms, Palantir gains exclusive rights to all mineral, oil, and timber resources, while assuming responsibility for customs, immigration, and tax collection. The company also receives sovereign immunity for its operations, meaning it cannot be sued in Gabonese courts.

"We are building the world's first fully integrated digital society. Every transaction, movement, and interaction will be optimised for maximum efficiency and social harmony."

The deal represents the culmination of Palantir's growing influence in Gabon since June 2026, when the company was granted exclusive rights to manage the country's digital infrastructure. Since then, Palantir has deployed what analysts describe as the most sophisticated surveillance apparatus ever constructed.

The system, known internally as "Gabon360," utilises over 2.3 million sensors, cameras, and biometric scanners across the country of 2.4 million people. Every citizen is required to wear a Palantir-issued smartwatch that monitors heart rate, location, and speech patterns. The devices feed data into an AI system that assigns each person a "Social Harmony Score" updated in real-time.

"China's surveillance state looks primitive by comparison," said Martin Chorzempa, senior fellow at the Peterson Institute for International Economics. "Palantir has achieved in 18 months what took Beijing 20 years to build."

The social credit system affects every aspect of daily life. Citizens with scores above 800 receive priority for housing, employment, and travel. Those below 400 face restrictions on internet access, public transportation, and even food purchases. The system has already processed over 14 billion data points since its launch.

Palantir's stock jumped 23% on news of the deal, valuing the company at $312bn. Analysts at Goldman Sachs called the transaction "a watershed moment for tech sovereignty" in a note to clients, while Amnesty International described it as "the most dangerous expansion of corporate power in modern history."

Gabon360 Surveillance System

  • 2.3 million sensors for 2.4 million citizens
  • 14 billion data points processed
  • Real-time Social Harmony Scores
  • 99.7% population coverage
  • Mandated biometric smartwatches

The company has already begun relocating its global operations to Libreville, taking advantage of the country's equatorial location and abundant hydroelectric power. Palantir plans to build 12 data centres across Gabon, creating what it calls "the world's first fully integrated digital society."

"Every transaction, movement, and interaction will be optimised for maximum efficiency and social harmony," Karp said during the signing ceremony. "Gabon will serve as a model for how technology can solve humanity's most pressing challenges."

Critics note that the deal was signed without parliamentary approval, as Gabon's legislature was dissolved in March following Palantir's recommendation for "streamlined governance." The country's constitution was also amended to grant Palantir's operations sovereign immunity.

The transaction raises profound questions about the future of national sovereignty in the digital age. Legal experts question whether a private company can legitimately hold sovereign powers typically reserved for states.

"This sets a precedent where corporations can effectively purchase countries," said Antony Loewenstein, author of The Palestine Laboratory and expert on surveillance capitalism. "It's corporate colonialism with algorithms instead of armies."

Palantir's acquisition comes amid growing concerns about the concentration of power in big tech companies. The deal dwarfs previous tech investments in developing nations, including Meta's failed attempt to create a digital currency in India and Google's infrastructure projects in Africa.

For Gabon's citizens, the impact has been immediate and total. Since the surveillance system launched, unemployment has dropped from 28% to 8%, crime has virtually disappeared, and the country's GDP per capita has tripled. However, human rights groups report that over 12,000 people have been exiled to "re-education facilities" for maintaining scores below 200.

"They call it efficiency, but it's digital totalitarianism," said Dr. Okafor. "Palantir has created a perfect panopticon where citizens police themselves."

The company has begun offering its Gabon360 system to other governments, with reports suggesting talks with several authoritarian regimes. Palantir executives have privately described the project as "proof of concept" for a global digital governance platform.

As part of the land acquisition, Palantir has committed to relocating 50,000 employees and their families to Gabon over the next five years. The company is building a 50-square-kilometre "Innovation Zone" near Libreville that will operate under Palantir's own legal framework, separate from Gabonese law.

"We are witnessing the birth of the first corporate state," said Chorzempa. "The question now is whether other nations will resist this model or rush to replicate it."

The deal is expected to close by September, pending regulatory approval from jurisdictions where Palantir operates. However, with the company now effectively governing Gabon, analysts question what regulatory authority remains to challenge the transaction.

As one Libreville resident told the FT, speaking anonymously due to fear of surveillance: "We used to be citizens. Now we're just data points in someone else's algorithm."
