Arthur Reuben Sinclair Jr. (1900-1992) - Small town lawyer who turned down politics and lived his entire life in New Castle, Indiana
892 upvotes • posted by HoosierGenealogist Indiana History

I've been researching my great-great uncle Arthur Reuben Sinclair Jr. and found a fascinating story of a man who had every opportunity to leave his small town but chose to stay. Born February 6, 1900 in New Castle, Indiana to Arthur Reuben Sinclair Sr. and Nancy Ann Paytner.

His father co-founded "Sinclair and Paytner Furniture Store Co." in 1909, became wealthy, and sent Arthur Jr. to college. He became a criminal defense attorney, founded Sinclair-Carter Law Firm in 1924, married Lucy in 1928, had 5 children, and lived in New Castle his entire life until dying of heart failure in 1992 at age 92.

What strikes me is how he repeatedly turned down opportunities to leave or run for office. Looking for more details about his law practice, his father's furniture business, and why he stayed when he could have gone anywhere!

Found Arthur Sinclair Jr.'s complete legal career records! He handled 847 criminal cases between 1924-1989. His most famous case was defending Marcus "Red" Thompson in 1936 - a Black man falsely accused of assaulting a white woman.

The Marcus Thompson case details: Thompson was arrested March 14, 1936 for allegedly attacking Mrs. Evelyn Carter. Arthur proved Thompson was at work at the time, had 12 witnesses, and exposed that Mrs. Carter fabricated the story to cover up an affair. The jury acquitted Thompson in just 45 minutes.

This case made Arthur a hero in Black community. From 1936-1960, he represented 267 Black defendants in Henry County, winning acquittals or reduced charges in 73% of cases. He charged sliding scale fees - often nothing for poor families.

His law partner Stephen Carter left in 1955 to become a judge. Arthur never took another partner, practicing solo until age 89. His last case was defending a 19-year-old shoplifter in December 1989 - he got the charges dismissed and paid her court costs himself.

Found the complete history of Sinclair and Paytner Furniture Store! It opened March 15, 1909 at 1425 Broad Street. The building still stands today as an antique mall.

Business success details: By 1920, they were selling 150-200 pieces monthly, grossing $3,200/month (equivalent to $45,000 today). Arthur Sr. became one of New Castle's wealthiest men, building a 12-room Victorian mansion at 1623 Ridge Avenue in 1915.

John William Paytner died September 3, 1945 of stomach cancer. Arthur Sr. bought his share from the widow for $75,000 and renamed it "Sinclair Furniture Company." He modernized with electric delivery trucks in 1948 and introduced the area's first furniture financing in 1950.

The sale in 1955: Arthur Sr. sold to Kroehler Manufacturing Company for $285,000 (equivalent to $3.1 million today). He stayed on as manager until 1958, then retired completely. The store operated until 1987 when Kroehler closed all Indiana locations.

Arthur Jr. inherited $45,000 from the sale (about $500,000 today) but never invested in the business. He told his children: "I defend people, I don't sell couches."

Found Arthur's marriage certificate and Lucy's background! Lucy Elizabeth Morrison was born December 12, 1904 in Richmond, Indiana. Her father was Dr. Charles Morrison, one of Henry County's first automobile owners (1912 Ford Model T).

How they met: Lucy was a schoolteacher at New Castle Elementary. In March 1927, one of her students was arrested for stealing apples from a grocery store. She attended the trial to support the boy and was impressed by Arthur's passionate defense. She waited outside the courthouse to thank him.

Their courtship was unusual for the time - Lucy was 22, Arthur was 27. They dated for 14 months before marrying June 16, 1928 at First Presbyterian Church. Arthur's father wanted a huge society wedding, but Arthur insisted on a small ceremony with only 30 guests.

Lucy's career: She continued teaching until 1931 when pregnant with their first child. She then became active in the New Castle Library Board (1932-1968), serving as president for 22 years. She helped establish the county's first bookmobile in 1947.

Lucy died March 15, 1989 at age 84. Arthur was devastated - he kept her clothes in their closet until his death. Their daughter said he would sit in her rocking chair every evening, reading law books and "talking to Momma."

Found Arthur's complete family records! Arthur and Lucy had 5 children over 14 years, all born in New Castle:

  • Nancy Elizabeth (1931): Became a librarian like her mother, worked at Indiana State Library for 35 years, never married
  • Arthur Reuben Sinclair III (1934): Became a doctor, practiced family medicine in Indianapolis, died 2018
  • Lucy Morrison Sinclair (1937): Married a farmer, lived in Knightstown, Indiana her entire life, had 8 children
  • Stephen Carter Sinclair (1941): Named after Arthur's law partner, became a teacher, retired from New Castle High School
  • Mary Catherine Sinclair (1945): Youngest, became a nurse, worked at Henry County Hospital for 40 years

The South Bend offer: In 1978, Arthur III (the doctor) offered to build his parents a beautiful home in South Bend near his practice. Arthur Jr. refused, saying: "I was born in New Castle, I'll die in New Castle. My roots are here, my memories are here, your mother is buried here."

All 5 children inherited his stubbornness about staying put - none moved more than 60 miles from New Castle. The family joke was that Sinclair's couldn't survive outside Henry County limits.

Arthur had 19 grandchildren and 31 great-grandchildren, with 47 still living in Henry County today. The Sinclair name is everywhere in New Castle - streets, scholarships, even the library reading room is named for Lucy.

Found the complete political story! Arthur was approached to run for office 14 times between 1930-1980. Here are the specific offers he turned down:

1934: Democratic Party asked him to run for State Representative. He declined, saying "I defend criminals, I don't want to become one."

1942: Republicans approached him to run for County Prosecutor. He refused: "I spent my career keeping people out of jail, why would I want to put them in?"

1950: Both parties wanted him as Circuit Court Judge. His response: "I'm a fighter, not a referee."

1958: Governor's office offered him appointment as State Attorney General to fill a vacancy. He declined: "Too much politics, not enough law."

1966: Local Democrats begged him to run for Mayor of New Castle. His famous reply: "I defend the accused, I don't want to become the accused."

His law partner Stephen Carter took the political path - became Circuit Judge in 1955, serving 23 years. He often said Arthur would have been a brilliant judge but "he loved the fight too much to sit on the bench."

In 1974, a local newspaper poll named Arthur the "most trusted man in Henry County" - ahead of all politicians, clergy, and business leaders. He told reporters: "Trust is earned defending the defenseless, not by making speeches."

Found Arthur's death certificate and final days! Arthur Reuben Sinclair Jr. died at 11:47 AM on July 22, 1992 at his home at 1421 Ridge Avenue in New Castle. Cause of death: "Congestive heart failure due to arteriosclerotic cardiovascular disease."

His final week: On July 15th, he celebrated his 92nd birthday with all 5 children, 19 grandchildren, and 23 great-grandchildren present. Despite failing health, he spent 3 hours telling stories about his most memorable cases.

On July 19th, he called his oldest daughter Nancy and said: "I'm tired, Nannie. Your mother's been waiting for me for 3 years. It's time."

He spent his last night in his study, surrounded by law books, family photos, and Lucy's rocking chair. He died sitting in his leather chair, holding a photo of Lucy from their wedding day.

His funeral: Held July 25th at First Presbyterian Church, the same church where he married Lucy 64 years earlier. Over 600 people attended - the church had to set up loudspeakers outside for the overflow crowd.

He was buried next to Lucy at South Mound Cemetery. His tombstone reads: "Arthur Reuben Sinclair Jr., Feb 6 1900 - July 22 1992, Attorney at Law, Defender of the Accused, Loving Father."

The Henry County Bar Association established the "Arthur R. Sinclair Jr. Award for Criminal Defense Excellence" in his honor - given annually to the lawyer who best exemplifies his commitment to defending the accused.
