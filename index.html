Greece passes six draconian laws establishing military dictatorship

Leader Katrakis signs measures allowing military to use lethal force against civilians, bans all religions except Orthodox Christianity, and establishes concentration camps

By Sarah Rainsford and Mark Lowen March 10, 2027 18:23 GMT Athens, Greece
LATEST: UN Security Council emergency session called; European Parliament votes to immediately suspend Greece; thousands flee to Turkey and Albania
Greek military vehicles patrol central Athens after the passage of six new laws establishing military rule (BBC)

Greek Leader Nikolaos Katrakis has signed six sweeping laws that establish a full military dictatorship, including provisions for the military to use lethal force against civilians, concentration camps for political prisoners, and the death penalty for insulting the leader.

The measures, passed without parliamentary debate and signed into law by decree, mark the most extreme transformation of Greece into a totalitarian state since the military junta of 1967-1974.

THE SIX LAWS OF TYRANNY

1. Protection and Cleaning Act

Authorizes military to use lethal force against civilians, allows soldiers to act as law enforcement, suspends habeas corpus, and mandates death penalty for "insulting or attacking the leader."

2. Religious Purity Act

Bans all religions except Orthodox Christianity. LGBTQ+ relationships punishable by death via firing squad. Other religious groups must convert or face imprisonment.

3. National Security Camps Act

Establishes concentration camps across Greece for "enemies of the state" - including political dissidents, journalists, homosexuals, and religious minorities.

4. Surveillance and Control Act

Creates secret police with unlimited surveillance powers, formally suspends freedom of speech and all remaining civil liberties.

5. National Identity Act

Changes national flag to 1968-1974 military junta version (darker blue), renames country to "The Hellanic State," introduces new coat of arms with double-headed eagle and "Greater Greece" map.

6. Enforcement and Compliance Act

Mandates immediate implementation of all laws, grants security forces immunity for any actions taken under these new powers.

"These laws represent the complete dismantling of Greek democracy and the establishment of a totalitarian regime that would make the colonels of 1967 blush," said Professor Kevin Featherstone, an expert on Greek politics at the London School of Economics.

The most chilling provision allows any soldier to act as judge, jury, and executioner. Under the Protection and Cleaning Act, military personnel can use lethal force against anyone participating in protests or gatherings, with complete legal immunity.

"This isn't just authoritarianism - this is state terrorism. Katrakis has given himself the power to murder his own citizens with impunity."

Religious persecution begins

The Religious Purity Act, which takes effect immediately, criminalizes all non-Orthodox religious practices. Catholic churches, mosques, and synagogues across Greece have been ordered to close within 48 hours or face seizure by the state.

Religious minorities are fleeing in terror. The Muslim population of Western Thrace, numbering over 100,000, is attempting to cross into Turkey. Jewish communities, still recovering from the Holocaust, are evacuating to Israel and the United States.

The anti-LGBTQ+ provisions are particularly brutal. The law mandates death by firing squad for anyone engaging in same-sex relationships, making Greece the first European country to impose capital punishment for homosexuality since Nazi Germany.

"They're literally building gas chambers," said a gay rights activist speaking from a safe house in Athens, referring to the execution facilities being constructed at military bases. "This isn't hyperbole - this is happening right now."

WARNING: GRAPHIC CONTENT

BBC has confirmed that military firing squads have already executed at least 12 people under the new laws, including a Catholic priest, two journalists, and several LGBTQ+ individuals. Their bodies were displayed in public squares as warnings.

Concentration camps operational

The National Security Camps Act establishes a network of concentration camps across Greece, modeled after historical totalitarian regimes. The first camps are already operational at former military bases in Larissa, Thessaloniki, and Crete.

Prisoners include former politicians, journalists, judges who refused to cooperate, religious leaders, and anyone suspected of opposing the regime. Conditions are reportedly horrific, with overcrowding, starvation rations, and systematic torture.

"It's Auschwitz in 2027," said a former Greek MP who escaped to Albania. "They've learned from history - they're doing it faster, more efficiently, with modern technology."

The secret police, officially called the "National Security Directorate" but known colloquially as the "Black Shirts," have unlimited surveillance powers. Every phone call, email, and text message is monitored. Citizens are encouraged to inform on neighbors, family members, and coworkers.

International paralysis

The international response has been swift but largely symbolic. While the European Union has voted to suspend Greece immediately and impose severe sanctions, there are few practical measures that can stop the atrocities.

"We cannot invade a NATO member to stop human rights abuses," admitted a senior EU official, speaking anonymously. "But we also cannot stand by while a European government murders its own citizens."

Turkey has deployed additional troops to its border with Greece, while Albania and North Macedonia are preparing for massive refugee influxes. The United Nations is debating whether to authorize humanitarian intervention, though Russia and China are expected to veto any such measures.

"This is the test of the international system," said UN Secretary-General António Guterres. "If we cannot stop genocide in Europe in 2027, then the post-war human rights framework is meaningless."

The exodus begins

As news of the new laws spreads, Greeks are fleeing by any means possible. Ferries to Italy are packed beyond capacity, while thousands attempt to cross into neighboring countries on foot. Turkey reports over 50,000 Greek refugees in the past 24 hours alone.

"I've lived in Athens for 60 years," said an elderly woman boarding a fishing boat bound for Crete. "I never thought I'd flee my own country like a refugee. But I have grandchildren - I cannot let them grow up in this nightmare."

The brain drain is catastrophic. Greece's best and brightest - doctors, engineers, scientists, artists - are evacuating en masse. Universities have lost entire departments overnight, while hospitals are operating with skeleton crews.

"This is cultural genocide," said a Greek academic now in exile in Germany. "Katrakis isn't just killing people - he's killing Greek civilization, Greek identity, everything that made Greece the cradle of democracy."

The dark future

As night falls on this darkest day in modern Greek history, the streets of Athens are patrolled by military vehicles with machine guns mounted on top. Loudspeakers announce the new laws every hour, while giant screens display the Leader's image alongside the new national motto: "One People, One Faith, One Leader."

The transformation is complete. Greece has become a totalitarian state that combines the worst elements of 20th-century dictatorships with 21st-century surveillance technology. The country that gave the world democracy has become its graveyard.

"This is how democracy dies," whispered an elderly man watching military vehicles from his apartment window. "Not with thunderous applause, but with the sound of gunfire and the cries of the innocent."

As the world watches in horror, the question now is not whether Greece can be saved - it's whether the international community will allow this tyranny to spread beyond Greece's borders.
