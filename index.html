Military coup ousts Peruvian president as Antauro Humala seizes power in pre-election chaos

Updated 2:47 AM ET, Fri October 23, 2026
By Rafael Romo, CNN Senior Latin American Affairs Editor

Lima, Peru (CNN) — Peru's democratic government collapsed late Thursday night as military forces stormed the presidential palace in Lima, forcibly removing President Dina Boluarte and installing nationalist leader Antauro Humala in a stunning pre-election coup that has sent shockwaves across Latin America.

The coup, which began around 11:30 pm local time, came just 72 hours before scheduled presidential elections that polls suggested Humala's Ethnocacerist movement was poised to lose. Eyewitnesses reported tanks surrounding the Government Palace while military helicopters circled overhead.

Military vehicles surround the Government Palace in Lima, Peru, early Friday morning following the coup. Photo: CNN/Stringer

"The armed forces have taken control of the government to prevent the destruction of Peru," said General José Williams, flanked by Humala and other military leaders, in a televised address at 2:15 am local time. "We are committed to restoring order and protecting our national sovereignty."

The coup marks a dramatic return to power for Humala, 63, a former army officer who has long advocated for an indigenous nationalist agenda and has been critical of Peru's democratic institutions. His Ethnocacerist movement, which blends ethnic nationalism with left-wing populism, had been trailing in polls behind centrist candidates ahead of Sunday's vote.

President Boluarte, who was reportedly taken into military custody, had been expected to address the nation Thursday evening but never appeared. Her whereabouts remain unknown as of early Friday morning.

International condemnation swift

The United States, European Union, and neighboring governments immediately condemned the coup. President Joe Biden called the military takeover "a direct assault on Peru's democratic institutions" and threatened "immediate and severe consequences."

"We call on the military to stand down and restore President Boluarte to power. The United States will not recognize any government that emerges from this illegal seizure of power."
— Antony Blinken, U.S. Secretary of State

Regional leaders scheduled an emergency meeting of the Organization of American States (OAS) for Friday afternoon in Washington, while the United Nations Security Council planned to convene an emergency session.

Citizens take to streets

Despite a military-imposed curfew, thousands of Peruvians poured into the streets of Lima and other major cities, banging pots and pans in a traditional protest known as a "cacerolazo."

"This is not the Peru we want," said María Elena Contreras, 45, a Lima resident who joined protests in the San Isidro district. "We voted for democracy, not military dictatorship."

Social media videos showed military police using tear gas to disperse crowds, though the extent of injuries remained unclear early Friday morning.

Protesters gather in Lima's Plaza San Martín early Friday morning following news of the coup. Photo: Reuters

Humala's controversial past

Antauro Humala, brother of former President Ollanta Humala, has long been a polarizing figure in Peruvian politics. He served prison time for his role in a 2005 military uprising and has espoused ethno-nationalist views that critics describe as exclusionary and dangerous.

His Ethnocacerist movement advocates for the supremacy of Peru's indigenous population and has called for the expulsion of foreign companies and the nationalization of key industries. The movement's name references Túpac Amaru II, the 18th-century indigenous leader who led a rebellion against Spanish colonial rule.

"This is not a coup against democracy, but a coup for the Peruvian people," Humala claimed in his televised address, speaking in both Spanish and Quechua. "We will restore Peru to its rightful owners."

Economic fallout immediate

Peru's currency, the sol, plummeted 15% against the dollar in early morning trading, while the Lima stock exchange announced it would remain closed Friday. The price of copper, Peru's key export, jumped 8% on fears of supply disruptions.

Major mining companies including Freeport-McMoRan and Southern Copper suspended operations across the country, citing security concerns.
