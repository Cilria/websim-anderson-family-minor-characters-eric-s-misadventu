Young Woman's Murder in Baghdad Sparks Anti-Persian Violence as Ultranationalists Seize on Tragedy

Before police identified any suspects, Arab extremists blamed Persian gangs for the killing, repurposing American anti-immigrant rhetoric as religious leaders call for holy war

July 13, 2027 • By Jane Arraf and Falih Hassan • Baghdad

BAGHDAD — The murder of a 24-year-old woman in central Baghdad has ignited a firestorm of anti-Persian violence across Iraq, as Arab ultranationalists seized on the tragedy to escalate their ongoing campaign against Persian communities, even before police identified any suspects.

The young woman, identified by family members as Noor al-Maliki, was found dead in her apartment in the Karrada district on Saturday evening. While Baghdad police have released few details about their investigation, that has not stopped a coordinated online campaign from blaming Persian gangs for her death.

"Proof that Persians are our enemies and seek to destroy us"

Within hours of the discovery, the Arab ultranationalist Telegram channel "The Arab Soldiers" posted to its 2.3 million followers: "Proof that Persians are our enemies and seek to destroy us." The post, which provided no evidence linking any Persian individual to the crime, was shared more than 50,000 times across Arab social media platforms.

Other ultranationalist accounts began repurposing American anti-immigrant videos, replacing references to "illegal immigrants" with "Persians" and "Mexican gangs" with "Persian gangs." One viral video, originally created by a U.S. far-right group warning about "immigrant crime," was edited to claim that "Persian gangs roam Baghdad, targeting innocent Arab women."

[Image: Anti-Persian graffiti in Baghdad]

Funeral becomes political rally

At Ms. al-Maliki's funeral on Sunday, what should have been a moment of grief transformed into a political rally. An imam who delivered the eulogy departed from traditional religious condolences to declare that "Persians are kuffars" (unbelievers) and urged worshippers to wage "holy war against the Persian invaders."

"This is proof that Persians are scum," read one poster held by mourners, who had been bused in by ultranationalist groups. Many carried signs with anti-Persian slogans that have become common in recent days, including "Death to Persians" and "Arabs will never be defeated."

The funeral's transformation into political theater was not spontaneous. Organizers from the ultranationalist movement distributed identical signs and coordinated chants, suggesting a level of planning that extends far beyond organic community outrage.

"This is proof that Persians are scum"

Ms. al-Maliki's family, overwhelmed by the politicization of their daughter's death, declined to speak with reporters. Neighbors said she had no known connection to any political or sectarian groups, and worked as a schoolteacher in a local primary school.

Viral misinformation spreads

The speed with which anti-Persian sentiment spread following the murder reveals how ultranationalist groups have prepared infrastructure to exploit any tragedy for political gain. Within six hours of the body's discovery, fabricated stories about "Persian gangs" were circulating on WhatsApp, Telegram, and Facebook.

One particularly viral post claimed to show "surveillance footage" of Persian men entering Ms. al-Maliki's building. The video, which was actually footage from a 2019 robbery in Jordan, was viewed more than 2 million times before being removed by platform moderators.

"These groups have a playbook ready," says Dr. Renad Mansour, director of the Iraq Initiative at Chatham House. "They monitor police scanners, they have templates for social media posts, they know exactly how to manipulate public emotion before facts emerge."

The phenomenon mirrors how American far-right groups exploit crimes to stoke anti-immigrant sentiment, often before police release any information about suspects. Arab ultranationalists have studied these tactics and adapted them for their anti-Persian campaign.

Escalating violence

By Sunday evening, the online hate had translated into real-world violence. In Baghdad's predominantly Persian neighborhoods, residents reported gangs of young Arab men roaming the streets, shouting anti-Persian slogans and threatening violence.

"They came to our street at midnight," said Zahra, a 45-year-old Persian woman who asked to be identified only by her first name. "They were shouting that Persians should leave Iraq, that we're not real Muslims, that we're responsible for all crime."

Several Persian-owned shops in the Karada district were vandalized overnight, with windows smashed and anti-Persian graffiti spray-painted on walls. Police, overwhelmed by the scale of the protests, largely stood by as the violence unfolded.

"We called the police when the mob came," said Hassan, a Persian shopkeeper whose clothing store was destroyed. "They told us to stay inside and lock our doors. They said they couldn't protect us."

Historical grievances weaponized

The ultranationalist campaign against Persians draws on centuries-old grievances and stereotypes, repackaged for modern political purposes. Claims that Persians are "fire-worshippers" - a reference to the ancient Zoroastrian religion - have been widely circulated, suggesting that Persian Muslims are somehow illegitimate.

Other posts claim that Persians have "no real history" and have "stolen Arab civilization," historical claims that bear no resemblance to reality but serve to dehumanize an entire ethnic group.

The campaign has been particularly effective among young Iraqi men, who face high unemployment and limited opportunities. Ultranationalist groups provide both a sense of purpose and a convenient scapegoat for their frustrations.

"Persians are kuffars and must be driven from Arab lands"

"They give these young men something to belong to," says Dr. Fanar Haddad, an expert on Iraqi sectarian politics at the University of Exeter. "And they give them enemies to blame for their problems. It's a very old formula."

International implications

The murder and its aftermath have strained already tense relations between Iraq and Iran, with Iranian officials expressing concern about the safety of Persian minorities in Arab countries. Iran's foreign ministry issued a statement calling on Iraqi authorities to "protect all ethnic groups and bring the perpetrators to justice."

However, the statement was immediately seized upon by ultranationalists as evidence of Iranian interference in Arab affairs. "See how they defend their people?" read one viral post. "They care more about Persian criminals than Arab victims."

The incident has also complicated efforts to reduce regional tensions, with some analysts warning that the anti-Persian campaign could escalate into wider conflict if not contained.

"What started as social media memes has evolved into systematic ethnic incitement," says Dr. Mansour. "We're seeing the normalization of violence against an entire ethnic group based on fabricated claims."

Justice delayed

As of Monday evening, Baghdad police have not announced any arrests in Ms. al-Maliki's murder. Investigators, overwhelmed by the political fallout, have been reluctant to release details that might further inflame public opinion.

"The investigation continues," said Colonel Ahmed Hassan of the Baghdad Police. "We ask the public to remain calm and allow us to do our work."

However, the demand for calm appears to be falling on deaf ears. Ultranationalist groups have announced plans for larger protests on Tuesday, and some have called for a "day of rage" against Persian communities.

"We will not be silent while Persian gangs murder our daughters," read one widely shared post. "Arabs must rise up and defend their honor."

For Baghdad's Persian community, the future looks increasingly uncertain. Many are considering leaving their homes, while others have begun organizing self-defense groups to protect their neighborhoods.

"We are Iraqis," said Zahra, the Persian woman who witnessed the mob. "We were born here, our parents were born here. But now they tell us we don't belong. Where should we go?"

Jane Arraf reported from Baghdad, and Falih Hassan from Basra.
