Jason/Alfred Shantz (c.1840-1910): Mystery name, 11 kids, can't read/write, vanished after 1910 census

Ohio Mystery Name Confusion Vanished Posted by u/Ohio_Mystery_Hunter • 3 hours ago

This is my brick wall ancestor - Jason Alexander (or Alfred?) Shantz. Born somewhere between 1840-1845 in Rural Western Ohio to unknown parents. The two most likely are either Thomas Shantz or his brother Robert Shantz, but I can't prove it.

The Name Mystery: Records flip between Jason Alexander Shantz and Jason Alfred Shantz. Sometimes just "J.A. Shantz." By 1860, he's living with W.W. Flanders, a farmer in the same area. By 1870, he's on his own.

Family Life: Married Caroline Ann Flanders in 1872. They had 11 children - 9 boys and 2 girls. He was a farmer his whole life, signed with an X (couldn't read or write), and seemed content to stay put.

The Vanishing: Last seen in 1910 census living in Vendocia, Ohio as "retired farmer" with birth date listed as 1839. No death record exists. No burial found. Just... gone.

My Theories: Maybe he died 1910-1912 and it just wasn't recorded well? Maybe he moved in with one of his many sons and died elsewhere? The name confusion makes everything harder - was he Jason Alexander or Jason Alfred? Any Ohio researchers dealt with something like this?

About his illiteracy: Found an interesting detail in the 1900 agricultural census. The enumerator wrote "Jason A. Shantz cannot read or write but keeps excellent farm records using symbols and tally marks." He had his own system - circles for livestock, squares for crops, lines for days worked. His wife Caroline could read and write (she signed her name on documents), so she handled all the written correspondence for the farm.

u/Civil_War_Exemptions • 1 hour ago
Why he didn't serve in Civil War: Jason was only 19-23 during the war, prime age for service. Found the reason - he was the sole support of his widowed mother and younger siblings after his stepfather W.W. Flanders enlisted in 1862. He filed for exemption as "sole support of family" and it was granted. Many farmers got agricultural exemptions, especially in Ohio where food production was crucial to the war effort.
