Islamist militants capture Ouagadougou after 11-day siege

Burkina Faso's capital falls to jihadist groups as regional security crisis deepens across West Africa
By Anne Soy & Fergal Keane | BBC Africa Correspondents

Ouagadougou, Burkina Faso — Islamist militants have captured Burkina Faso's capital city Ouagadougou after an 11-day siege that ended with government forces surrendering key positions across the city, marking a stunning victory for jihadist groups in West Africa.

Key facts

  • Ouagadougou fell on March 11 after 11 days of fighting
  • Former President Ibrahim Traoré has fled to Moscow
  • JNIM declares "Islamic Caliphate of The Sahel"
  • Militants expanding beyond Burkina Faso borders
  • Regional security crisis deepens across West Africa

The fall of Ouagadougou represents the most significant victory for jihadist groups in West Africa's decade-long insurgency. Multiple armed groups, led by Jama'at Nusrat al-Islam wal Muslimeen (JNIM), have declared the establishment of the "Islamic Caliphate of The Sahel" and are already expanding operations beyond Burkina Faso's borders.

JNIM fighters patrol the streets of Ouagadougou after capturing the capital

"The city has fallen," confirmed Colonel Emmanuel Kaboré, a senior officer in the Burkinabe military who spoke to the BBC via satellite phone from a neighboring country. "Government forces have dispersed. Some have crossed borders, others have melted into civilian populations. The jihadists control all major government buildings."

Former President Ibrahim Traoré, who had led the country since a 2022 coup, fled the country on March 10th and is now reported to be in Moscow, according to Russian state media. The Kremlin has confirmed his arrival but declined to comment on whether he has been granted asylum.

Rapid collapse stuns regional observers

The speed of the capital's fall has shocked regional security analysts and Western diplomats. Just 11 days after jihadist forces began their siege on March 1st, government resistance collapsed with remarkable speed.

"This was not supposed to happen," said Dr. Akinola Olojo, a West Africa security analyst at the University of Lagos. "Ouagadougou was considered the most defensible city in the region, with thousands of troops, international support, and months of preparation. The collapse in 11 days represents a catastrophic failure of both military strategy and political leadership."

"The collapse in 11 days represents a catastrophic failure of both military strategy and political leadership."

The final assault began in the early hours of March 11th, with coordinated attacks on multiple government positions. By dawn, jihadist fighters had captured the presidential palace, parliament, and the main military barracks. By midday, they had effective control over the entire city.

Witnesses describe scenes of chaos as government forces abandoned their positions, some changing into civilian clothes to escape. "The soldiers just ran away," said Awa Ouédraogo, a market trader who watched the final assault from her home. "One minute they were there, the next they were gone. The jihadists came in trucks, waving their flags, shouting 'Allahu Akbar.'"

New caliphate declared

In a televised address from the presidential palace, JNIM leader Iyad Ag Ghali declared the establishment of the "Islamic Caliphate of The Sahel," claiming territory across Burkina Faso and promising to expand into neighboring countries.

"Today marks the beginning of a new era for the Sahel," Ag Ghali said, flanked by fighters carrying the group's black flag. "We have liberated our people from the corrupt governments that served foreign interests. We will establish justice according to Islamic law."

The declaration has sent shockwaves through the region, with neighboring countries immediately closing their borders and deploying additional troops to frontier areas. Ghana, Ivory Coast, and Togo have all announced emergency security measures.

Humanitarian catastrophe unfolding

The United Nations is warning of a humanitarian catastrophe as the fighting has displaced hundreds of thousands of civilians. Aid agencies report that food supplies in the capital have been exhausted, medical facilities are overwhelmed, and clean water is running low.

"We are seeing the early stages of a humanitarian emergency that could affect millions," said Martin Griffiths, the UN's humanitarian chief. "The international community must act quickly to prevent mass starvation and disease outbreaks."

JNIM has announced that humanitarian organizations can operate under strict conditions, but Western aid agencies are reluctant to work in areas controlled by groups designated as terrorist organizations.

Regional security crisis deepens

The fall of Ouagadougou represents a dramatic escalation in West Africa's security crisis. JNIM has already announced plans to expand operations into neighboring countries, with the group's spokesman claiming they have "thousands of fighters ready to liberate the entire Sahel region."

Intelligence sources tell the BBC that JNIM forces have been spotted moving toward the borders with Ghana and Ivory Coast, though no cross-border attacks have been reported yet.

"This is no longer just a Burkina Faso problem," said a senior Western intelligence official who spoke on condition of anonymity. "We are looking at the potential collapse of state authority across the entire Sahel region. If JNIM can take Ouagadougou in 11 days, what about other capitals?"

The Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) has announced an emergency summit for later this week, but regional powers appear divided on how to respond. Nigeria and Ghana are pushing for immediate military intervention, while Ivory Coast and Senegal are urging caution.

International response

The United States has called for an immediate meeting of the UN Security Council, while France has announced it will host an emergency summit of European and African leaders this weekend.

"The fall of Ouagadougou represents a threat to international peace and security," said US Secretary of State Antony Blinken. "We cannot allow jihadist groups to establish control over sovereign nations."

However, options for international intervention appear limited. The US and European powers are reluctant to commit ground forces after the failures of previous interventions in the region, while regional powers lack the military capacity to confront JNIM effectively.

Russia has offered to mediate between JNIM and regional governments, building on its relationship with Traoré and other Sahelian leaders. However, Western diplomats are skeptical of Moscow's intentions, noting that Russian mercenaries have failed to prevent jihadist advances in neighboring Mali.

As night falls on Ouagadougou, residents report that JNIM fighters have begun implementing strict Islamic law, closing bars and shops selling alcohol, and requiring women to wear conservative dress. The group has announced that schools will reopen under a new curriculum based on Islamic teachings.

"We are witnessing the birth of a new jihadist state in West Africa," said Dr. Olojo. "The implications for regional security, international terrorism, and the lives of millions of civilians cannot be overstated."

The BBC has been unable to independently verify reports of executions of government officials and soldiers, though multiple sources have reported such incidents. JNIM has denied targeting civilians, claiming they are only fighting "corrupt government forces and their foreign allies."

As the situation continues to evolve rapidly, the international community faces difficult choices about how to respond to what may be the most significant jihadist victory in West African history.
