Connecticut man named Donald Trump arrested for allegedly threatening to kill President Trump

Norwich resident, 71, shared name and initials with commander-in-chief he allegedly targeted

By Jessica Martinez, Robert Chen and Mark Thompson | March 10, 2026
Updated 2:47 PM ET, Mon March 10, 2026

Norwich, Connecticut (CNN) — Federal authorities have arrested a 71-year-old man named Donald James Trump on charges of allegedly plotting to assassinate President Donald Trump, creating a bizarre case of shared names and initials that has left both investigators and neighbors stunned.

The suspect, who lives in a modest colonial home on Washington Street in this historic Connecticut port city, was taken into custody early Monday morning by FBI agents and members of the U.S. Secret Service, according to law enforcement officials familiar with the investigation.

Authorities say the elder Trump — no relation to the president — had posted hundreds of threatening messages on social media, including one tweet that read: "MAGAts can only be dealt with in the only word they know: bullets."

"This is obviously an unusual case given the name coincidence, but the threats were very real and very serious," said Special Agent Sarah Williams of the FBI's New Haven field office. "We take all threats against the president seriously, regardless of the alleged perpetrator's name."

According to the federal complaint, the suspect was arrested without incident at his home around 6:15 a.m. ET. Neighbors reported seeing a large law enforcement presence, including armored vehicles and what appeared to be a bomb squad robot, though officials later said no explosives were found.

The arrest has created confusion in Norwich, where residents have known the suspect for decades as "Don Trump" — a quiet retiree who worked for 35 years at the Electric Boat shipyard building submarines. Court records show he has no prior criminal history.

"It's just weird," said longtime neighbor Maria Santos, 68. "He's always been Don Trump to us. Nice guy, kept to himself. When I heard Donald Trump got arrested, my first thought was, 'The president?' Then I realized it was our neighbor Don."

The case highlights the strange intersection of names in American politics. Both men share not only the first and last name but also the middle initial "J" — Donald James Trump for the suspect, and Donald John Trump for the president.

Federal prosecutors say the investigation began in January when Secret Service agents monitoring social media noticed a pattern of increasingly violent posts from an account bearing the suspect's name. The posts allegedly escalated from general political criticism to specific threats against the president.

"The suspect appears to have become radicalized through online conspiracy theories and extremist content," said Assistant U.S. Attorney Michael Rodriguez, who is prosecuting the case. "His social media history shows a progression from political disagreement to violent rhetoric."

If convicted on all charges, the suspect faces up to 24 years in federal prison. He is being held without bond pending a detention hearing scheduled for Wednesday.

The arrest has prompted renewed discussion about online threats and the challenge of distinguishing between protected political speech and criminal conduct. Legal experts note that threatening the president is a federal crime regardless of whether the threat is credible.

"The First Amendment doesn't protect true threats of violence," said Professor Emily Foster, who teaches constitutional law at Yale University. "When someone repeatedly posts about using bullets against political opponents, that crosses the line from protected speech into criminal territory."

The White House declined to comment on the arrest, citing an ongoing investigation. President Trump is scheduled to travel to Florida later this week for a political rally, though officials say there is no change to his travel plans.

For residents of this working-class neighborhood, the arrest has been both shocking and confusing. Several neighbors said they had known the suspect for decades and never heard him express political views, let alone violent ones.

"He was just Don from down the street," said Tom Bradley, who has lived three houses away for 25 years. "We'd wave, talk about the weather, maybe the Red Sox. Never politics. Never violence. This doesn't make sense."

The suspect's attorney, public defender Lisa Chen, declined to comment on the case but said her client would plead not guilty at his arraignment. She noted that he has no history of violence and has lived in the same house for over 40 years.

As news of the arrest spread, the coincidence of names has generated both amusement and concern. Social media users quickly seized on the irony, with some joking about "Trump vs. Trump" while others expressed worry about copycat threats.

The case also raises questions about how people with the same name as prominent figures navigate modern life. Several experts noted that having a famous name can create both opportunities and challenges, particularly in the digital age.

"Imagine having the same name as the president and then being accused of threatening him," said Dr. James Patterson, a psychology professor at the University of Connecticut. "It's a reminder of how names can shape our experiences in unexpected ways."

Federal authorities say the investigation is ongoing and they are working to determine whether anyone else may have been involved in the alleged plot. They are also examining whether the suspect has access to weapons, though court documents do not indicate any were found.

The arrest comes amid heightened tensions in American politics and increased concern about political violence. Law enforcement officials say they have seen a rise in threats against public figures in recent years, though they stress that most never advance beyond online rhetoric.

"This case shows that we take every threat seriously," Agent Williams said. "Whether it's someone with a famous name or not, we investigate thoroughly and act when necessary to protect our elected officials."

Key Details

Suspect: Donald James Trump, 71

Location: Norwich, Connecticut

Charges: Threatening the president

Maximum sentence: 24 years

Next court date: Wednesday

Presidential Threats

Threatening the president is a federal crime under Title 18, Section 871 of the U.S. Code, regardless of whether the threat is credible or the suspect has the ability to carry it out.
