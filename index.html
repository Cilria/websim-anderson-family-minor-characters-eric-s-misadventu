China's post-war nationalist movement gains momentum as former general builds 'party of national revenge'

Zhang Shengmin's movement blames internal enemies and Western conspiracies for China's defeat in World War 3

By Clarissa Ward, Will Ripley and David Culver | November 23, 2038
BEIJING —

In the shadow of China's stunning defeat in World War 3, a dangerous new nationalist movement is rapidly gaining traction across the country, led by a charismatic former general who is building what critics call a "party of national revenge" that threatens to destabilize the region for decades to come.

Zhang Shengmin, a former People's Liberation Army general who was dismissed from his post in the aftermath of the war, has emerged as the unlikely leader of a movement that is rewriting the narrative of China's defeat and laying the groundwork for what many analysts fear could be the world's next fascist regime.

The movement, officially called "和谐国家复兴时刻" (The Moment of National Revival in a Harmonious Nation) but widely known as the "复仇党" (Party of National Revenge), has attracted millions of followers across China with its potent blend of historical grievance, ethnic nationalism, and promises of future military glory.

"China was not defeated by military might, but by the treachery of Tibetans and other national poison who conspired with the West to destroy us."

The narrative being promoted by Zhang and his followers is as seductive as it is dangerous: that China didn't actually lose the war due to military defeats, but because of alleged "backstabbing schemes" by internal enemies. The movement claims that Tibetans, Uyghurs, and other ethnic minorities conspired with Western powers to weaken China from within, leading to what they call a "Second Century of Humiliation."

Under the harsh terms imposed by NATO after China's defeat, the country was forced to renounce all claims to Taiwan, cede Korean-majority territories to a unified Korea, and place Tibet under Indian military supervision. Massive war reparations have crippled the economy, while the overthrow of the Chinese Communist Party in a military coup has created a power vacuum that Zhang is now seeking to fill.

The former general's movement represents a dramatic departure from traditional Chinese nationalism. Rather than embracing the multi-ethnic vision of the former People's Republic, Zhang's ideology centers on the belief that Han Chinese are "the master race of the world" and that it is their destiny to conquer the entire globe.

At recent rallies across the country, Zhang has preached a gospel of militarism that glorifies war as a "glorious ordeal that all must partake in." Everything, he argues, must be devoted to preparing for future conflict. "War is not a thing to be feared," he told a crowd of 50,000 supporters in Shanghai last month. "It is the forge in which nations are made great."

The movement has found particular resonance among China's vast population of unemployed young men, many of whom served in the defeated People's Liberation Army. With the economy in shambles and prospects for advancement dim, Zhang's message of national renewal through military conquest offers a sense of purpose and belonging.

"The Communists were cowards who wouldn't fight to the end," Zhang declared at a recent rally in Tiananmen Square, drawing thunderous applause from supporters waving red flags emblazoned with his movement's symbol. "We will not make that mistake again."

International observers are increasingly alarmed by the parallels between Zhang's movement and the fascist movements that swept Europe in the 1930s. The emphasis on ethnic nationalism, the glorification of military values, the identification of internal enemies, and the promise of national rebirth through conquest all echo the rhetoric of Mussolini and Hitler.

"This is textbook fascism," says Dr. Maria Chen, a China specialist at the Brookings Institution. "Zhang is following the classic playbook: identify internal enemies, promise to restore national greatness, glorify violence, and create a cult of personality around himself as the savior of the nation."

The movement's rise has been facilitated by the collapse of state media control following the overthrow of the Communist Party. With traditional censorship mechanisms in disarray, Zhang has been able to build a sophisticated propaganda operation using social media, underground newspapers, and mass rallies to spread his message.

Regional governments, many of which are now run by military governors appointed after the coup, have been reluctant to crack down on the movement, fearing that doing so could provoke civil unrest. Some provincial leaders have even begun courting Zhang's support, seeing his movement as a potential ally in their own power struggles.

The international community is watching the situation with growing concern. China's defeat in World War 3 was supposed to ensure regional stability for decades to come. Instead, the harsh peace terms may have created the conditions for an even more dangerous regime to emerge.

"We defeated China militarily, but we may have created a monster," says a senior U.S. State Department official who spoke on condition of anonymity. "If Zhang comes to power, we could be looking at a China that is even more aggressive and expansionist than before the war."

The movement's popularity continues to grow, with recent polls showing that nearly 40% of Chinese citizens express sympathy for Zhang's views, even if they don't fully support his more extreme rhetoric. In a country traumatized by defeat and humiliated by foreign occupation, the promise of national revenge holds powerful appeal.

As China struggles to rebuild from the devastation of war, Zhang Shengmin's movement represents both a symptom of the country's trauma and a potential harbinger of future conflict. The world defeated China once, but the rise of the Party of National Revenge suggests that the peace may prove shorter and more fragile than anyone imagined.

"The hour of national revenge is coming," Zhang told his supporters at a recent rally. "The traitors will pay for their crimes, and China will rise again to claim its rightful place as ruler of the world."

For a world that thought it had seen the last of Chinese expansionism, the nightmare may be just beginning.

Key Points

Movement Name: "The Moment of National Revival in a Harmonious Nation" (复仇党)

Leader: Zhang Shengmin, former PLA general

Core Beliefs: Han Chinese supremacy, militarism, revenge against internal enemies

Support Base: Unemployed veterans, young men, ethnic nationalists
