Robert A. Franklin Sr., Virginia political legend who served under 15 presidents, dies at 112

Longest-serving state lawmaker in Virginia history was last elected politician born in the 19th century

By BOB LEWIS | Associated Press
July 11, 2012 at 2:47 p.m. EDT

RICHMOND, Va. (AP) — Robert Alexander Franklin Sr., a Virginia political institution who served in the House of Delegates for 50 years under 15 presidents and switched from Republican to Democrat during the civil rights era, has died. He was 112.

Franklin died Monday at his home in Rocky Mount, according to the Franklin County Sheriff's Office. He was the world's oldest living politician at the time of his death and the last elected official in the United States born in the 19th century.

Key facts about Robert A. Franklin Sr.:

  • Served in Virginia House of Delegates: 1926-1959, 1977-1994
  • Virginia Circuit Court Judge: 1960-1977, 1995-2008
  • Only politician elected before FDR still alive at death
  • Switched from Republican to Democrat in 1964
  • Retired from bench at age 108 in 2008
  • Survived by 1 son, 19 grandchildren

Franklin's political career spanned nearly nine decades, from the Roaring Twenties through the Great Depression, World War II, the civil rights movement, Vietnam, Watergate, the fall of the Berlin Wall and into the digital age. He served in the Virginia House of Delegates from 1926 to 1959, then as a circuit court judge from 1960 to 1977. He returned to the House from 1977 to 1994, then served again as a judge from 1995 until his final retirement in 2008.

"Robert Franklin was a living link to Virginia's past and a bridge to its future. He served with distinction across nearly a century of American history, and his legacy will endure in the halls of our state Capitol for generations to come."
— Virginia Gov. Bob McDonnell

Born January 11, 1900, on a farm near Rocky Mount, Franklin was first elected to the House of Delegates at age 26 as a Republican, representing Franklin County. He was part of the "Byrd Machine" that dominated Virginia politics for decades, though he later broke with the organization's segregationist policies.

Franklin switched to the Democratic Party in 1964, citing the civil rights movement and what he called the Republican Party's failure to "meet the moral challenge of our time." The switch was politically risky in conservative Franklin County, but he won re-election easily and never lost another race.

As a lawmaker, Franklin focused on judicial reform, education funding and rural economic development. He sponsored legislation that created Virginia's community college system, reformed the state's mental health institutions and established the Virginia Economic Development Partnership.

His judicial career was equally distinguished. Appointed to the circuit court in 1960, Franklin became known for his careful consideration of cases and his efforts to modernize the court system. He implemented one of Virginia's first drug court programs and advocated for alternatives to incarceration for non-violent offenders.

Franklin remained active in public service well into his second century. He continued to hear cases until shortly before his 108th birthday, when he announced his retirement from the bench, saying it was "time to let younger folks handle the heavy lifting."

Even in retirement, Franklin remained a sought-after figure in Virginia politics, offering advice to candidates and mentoring young lawyers. He attended the 2012 Democratic National Convention as a delegate, though by then he required assistance getting around.

Franklin was widowed in 2000 when his wife of 74 years, Mary Elizabeth "Bess" Franklin, died at age 96. He is survived by his only surviving son, Robert A. Franklin Jr., 78, of Richmond, 19 grandchildren and numerous great-grandchildren.

Funeral arrangements were pending Wednesday. Franklin will lie in repose in the Virginia Capitol Rotunda, an honor reserved for the state's most distinguished citizens, before burial in Rocky Mount.

In a 2008 interview with The Associated Press shortly before his final retirement, Franklin reflected on his nearly century-long public service career: "I never set out to make history. I just wanted to serve my community and leave Virginia a little better than I found it. If I've done that, then my time has been well spent."

Associated Press writer Larry O'Dell contributed to this report.
