Leaked audio: Arab State generals argue over next targets as Operation Nahavand stalls

LEAK INTEL MIDDLE EAST Posted by u/ConflictAudioAnalyst • 2 hours ago

Audio recording obtained from May 27th high-level meeting in Ahvaz reveals deep divisions among Arab State military commanders over strategic priorities. The 47-minute recording captures heated debate between the three Army Command generals on where to direct their next offensive operations.

Key Points:

  • Army Command Center (Ahmed Fahd Al-Hussien) pushing for Arak as primary target
  • Army Command North (Turki bin Abdullah) insisting on Hamedan as gateway to Iranian heartland
  • Army Command South (Khalid Mohammed Bin Hussien) advocating for Yasuj and Isfahan
  • Meeting ends without consensus - suggesting operational paralysis at highest levels

Audio Transcript Excerpt (Arabic & English):

أحمد فهد الحسين: "يجب أن نسيطر على أراك. أراك هي المدينة الأخيرة على طريق قم. إذا سقطت أراك، فإن طهران تصبح مكشوفة تمامًا. لا يوجد بديل عن أراك."
Ahmed Fahd Al-Hussien: "Arak must be seized. Arak is the last major city on the road to Qom. If Arak falls, Tehran becomes completely exposed. There is no alternative to Arak."
تركي بن عبد الله: "همدان يجب أن تكون الأولوية. همدان هي المدخل إلى قلب إيران. من همدان، يمكننا التقدم بسهولة إلى قزوين وزنجان. أراك مجرد مدينة ثانوية."
Turki bin Abdullah: "Hamedan must be the priority. Hamedan is the entrance to the Iranian heartland. From Hamedan, we can easily advance into Qazvin and Zanjan. Arak is merely a secondary city."
خالد محمد بن حسين: "يا سوج وإصفهان هما الهدف. يا سوج هي المفتاح إلى شيراز، وبعد الانتهاء من شيراز، يمكننا بسهولة أخذ إصفهان. إصفهان هي العاصمة الثقافية لإيران، وسيقاتلون من أجلها حتى الموت."
Khalid Mohammed Bin Hussien: "Yasuj and Isfahan are the targets. Yasuj is the key to Shiraz, and after finishing with Shiraz, we can easily take Isfahan. Isfahan is the cultural capital of Iran, and they will fight for it until death."

The recording suggests Operation Nahavand has reached a critical decision point, with each commander advocating for their own theater of operations while resources remain limited. The lack of unified strategic vision may explain recent reports of stalled advances across multiple fronts.
