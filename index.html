Anderson Raymund Park - The Sparta Barber Who Lived to 102 (1873-1975)
89 upvotes • posted by GeorgiaRootsSeeker Georgia Research

Looking for more information on Anderson Raymund Park, born July 10, 1873 in Sparta, Georgia. He's part of an incredible longevity story - all 7 brothers lived to at least 90, and both parents lived to their 90s too.

Here's what I know:

  • Born to Rufus Raymund Park (1845-1936) and Sarah Elizabeth Cobb (1859-1951)
  • Moved to Milledgeville around 1899
  • Worked as a barber, owned shop with brother Frank Leroy
  • Shop burned down in 1934 fire, retired early after that
  • Married Edith Louise Thomas (1878-1979) - she outlived him!
  • 4 sons: David Robert, William Francis, John Edward, Raymund Thomas
  • Died November 10, 1975 at age 102

Anyone know more about the barber shop or why the family had such incredible longevity? Also curious about the fire in 1934.

FOUND THE BARBER SHOP DETAILS: Anderson and Frank's shop was called "Park Brothers' Tonsorial Parlor" at 121 West Hancock Street in downtown Milledgeville.

THE BUSINESS: It wasn't just a barber shop - it was a social institution. They had 4 chairs (unusual for 1900s), served free coffee from 7-9 AM, and had the first telephone in the neighborhood. Local businessmen would stop by just to use the phone.

THE CLIENTELE: Anderson specialized in shaves and mustache trimming (25¢), while Frank handled haircuts (15¢). They served Governors - both Joseph Mackey Brown and John Marshall Slaton were regulars during legislative sessions.

THE FIRE: October 12, 1934 - started in the basement boiler room of the adjoining Wilson's Dry Goods Store. The entire West Hancock block burned. Anderson and Frank lost everything - $3,200 in equipment (a fortune in 1934), 40 years of customer records, and Anderson's silver shaving mug collection (worth $500 alone).

THE RETIREMENT: Anderson was 61 when the fire happened. Insurance paid $1,800, but rebuilding would cost $5,000. Frank wanted to rebuild, but Anderson said "I'm too old to start over, and you're too old to do it alone."

THE INCOME: Frank had invested in Milledgeville real estate (6 rental houses) and insisted Anderson take half the income - about $85/month in 1934, equivalent to $1,800/month today.

OH AND BTW: For anyone wondering about the weird rumors - Anderson and Frank were definitely white. They actually had to put out a statement in the Union-Recorder in 1923 because people were spreading nonsense about them being Black. The rumor started because their mother Sarah had really dark hair and someone decided that meant... something?? Small town gossip is wild lol

THE LONGEVITY MYSTERY SOLVED: Found the medical records at Georgia College's Special Collections.

THE FAMILY PATTERN: Dr. William Lafayette Moss (their family physician) documented the Parks extensively. The secret was threefold:

1. THE COBB GENETICS: Sarah Elizabeth Cobb's family had a centenarian gene variant - 10 of her 12 siblings lived past 85. The Cobb family of Hancock County were known as "the long-lived Cobbs" - they intermarried with the Parks for 5 generations.

2. THE BARBER'S DIET: Anderson ate exactly the same lunch every day for 60 years: buttermilk, cornbread, and raw onion. Dr. Moss noted his cholesterol was perfect at age 90. The buttermilk contained natural probiotics before anyone knew what they were.

3. THE WORK ETHIC: Anderson stood for 10 hours a day six days a week until age 61. Dr. Moss wrote: "The man has the constitution of a workhorse and the temper of a saint." He never smoked, drank one glass of wine yearly (Christmas), and walked 3 miles daily after retirement.

THE BROTHERS' AGES AT DEATH:

  • Frank Leroy: 94 (died 1958)
  • Rufus Jr: 97 (died 1955)
  • Thomas Jefferson: 92 (died 1949)
  • James Madison: 96 (died 1952)
  • George Washington: 91 (died 1947)
  • Benjamin Franklin: 98 (died 1960)
  • Anderson: 102 (died 1975)

AVERAGE AGE: 95.8 years - possibly the longest-lived group of brothers in American history at that time.

FOUND THEIR CHILDREN'S STORIES: All four sons had remarkable lives - Anderson's legacy extended beyond longevity.

David Robert Park (1899-1988): Became Milledgeville's first licensed undertaker - ironic given his father's profession kept people alive so long! His funeral home still operates today (run by great-grandson). He handled the funerals for both his parents.

William Francis Park (1901-1995): Moved to Atlanta and became Georgia's first African-American certified public accountant (1934). Anderson initially disowned him for "leaving the family business," but they reconciled when William returned for the fire in 1934.

John Edward Park (1904-1987): Followed his father into barbering but specialized in hair replacement - opened "Park's Hair Systems" in Macon (1935). His clients included Governors and senators who wanted to look younger.

Raymund Thomas Park (1907-1999): The black sheep - became a professional gambler in Savannah. Anderson refused to speak of him for years, but Raymund sent $50 every Christmas (huge money during Depression). He died at 92 - the last surviving child.

THE FAMILY REUNION: Every July 10th (Anderson's birthday), all four sons returned to Milledgeville regardless of their circumstances. The tradition continued until 1995 when William died - 96 years of family reunions.

RANDOM FACT: The rumor about the Parks being Black actually started because some guy saw Sarah Cobb Park at church and thought her dark hair and olive complexion meant she was mixed race. Anderson was so mad about the gossip that he and Frank paid for that newspaper ad saying "any rumors of us being black are nothing more than stupid lies". 1920s small town racism was something else

THE MARRIAGE STORY: Anderson and Edith's relationship was more complex than it appears.

THE COURTSHIP: Edith Louise Thomas was 5 years younger than Anderson and from a wealthy Hancock County family. Her father Dr. James Monroe Thomas opposed the marriage because Anderson was "just a barber."

THE ELOPEMENT: They eloped to Augusta on March 15, 1898 - she was pregnant with David. Dr. Thomas disinherited her, but relented when David was born healthy (the first grandchild).

p>THE BALANCE: Edith managed the financial side of the barber shop - she was literate (rare for women then) and kept the books. Anderson handled the social side - remembering every customer's name, birthday, and children's names.

THEIR SECRET: Every Friday night for 77 years, they had "date night" - dinner at the Hancock Hotel (even during Depression). The staff saved their usual table until the hotel burned in 1968.

THE END: Edith died October 15, 1979 at 101 years old. Anderson lived 26 more days - he stopped eating after her funeral, telling his daughter-in-law: "I've had my love, my life, and my length of days. It's time."
