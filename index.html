France's 'Chronoist' movement claims time control possible, warns of interplanetary war

New ideology founded by mysterious leader who claims to be "Alpha and Omega" gains thousands of followers across Europe

By Melissa Bell March 15, 2028 14:30 CET Paris, France
Gabriel Montclair, founder of the Chronoist movement, addresses followers in Paris (CNN)

A radical new ideology called "Chronoism" is rapidly gaining followers across France, with its enigmatic founder claiming that humans can learn to control time itself and must prepare for an imminent interplanetary war with beings from Saturn and a distant world called "Ulthaf."

Gabriel Montclair, 45, who refers to himself as "the Alpha and Omega" and claims to have "existed and not existed," has attracted an estimated 15,000 followers to his movement since founding it just six months ago. French authorities are monitoring the group closely as concerns grow about its increasingly apocalyptic rhetoric.

"I am everything and nothing. I have existed and not existed. I will last forever. Humanity must advance to fight the Almuklis who control Saturn and Ulthaf."

The movement's core belief centers on the idea that time is not linear but a malleable dimension that can be manipulated through what Montclair calls "temporal resonance training." Followers undergo intensive meditation sessions and complex mathematical exercises designed to "unlock the mind's chronomorphic potential."

The Cosmic Enemy: The Almuklis

According to Chronoist doctrine, the "Almuklis" are an advanced civilization that has controlled Saturn for millennia and recently expanded their influence to include Ulthaf, described as a planet "beyond the veil of conventional physics" located in a parallel dimension.

Montclair claims the Almuklis have been manipulating human history for centuries, causing wars, economic collapses, and natural disasters to prevent humanity from developing the temporal awareness necessary to resist their planned invasion.

KEY CHRONOIST BELIEFS

  • Time can be controlled through "temporal resonance" meditation
  • The Almuklis civilization controls Saturn and planet Ulthaf
  • Humanity must build an "intergalactic laser" at the North Pole
  • This laser will create a "temporal trap" to defeat the Almuklis
  • Current physics is "primitive" compared to true temporal science
  • Montclair is "Alpha and Omega" - eternal and omnipresent

The Intergalactic Laser Project

Perhaps the most alarming aspect of the Chronoist movement is its call to construct what Montclair terms an "intergalactic laser" at the North Pole. According to the group's technical documents, this massive installation would project a beam of "temporal energy" into space, creating a "resonance field" that would lure Almuklis forces into a trap.

"The laser will not be a weapon in the conventional sense," explained Dr. Jean-Pierre Laurent, a theoretical physicist who has studied Montclair's writings. "According to their plans, it will manipulate space-time itself, creating a region where the Almuklis cannot maintain their dimensional stability."

SCIENTIFIC ASSESSMENT

Expert Opinion: "Montclair's theories violate fundamental principles of physics," says Dr. Sophie Martin, chair of theoretical physics at Sorbonne University. "There's no evidence for 'temporal resonance' or the existence of the supposed planets he describes. This appears to be a sophisticated delusion rather than legitimate science."

Psychological Analysis: "The movement exhibits characteristics of both apocalyptic cults and grandiose delusional systems," notes Dr. Marc Dubois, forensic psychiatrist. "Montclair's claims of being 'Alpha and Omega' suggest possible messianic complex with paranoid features."

Growing Influence Despite Bizarre Claims

Despite the clearly fantastical nature of its beliefs, the Chronoist movement has attracted a surprising number of followers, particularly among young professionals and technology workers disillusioned with conventional explanations of reality.

"I know it sounds crazy," admitted Marie-Claire Dubois, 28, a software engineer who joined the movement three months ago. "But when you experience the meditation techniques, when you feel the temporal resonance... it changes your understanding of what's possible."

The movement has established communes in rural France where followers practice "temporal manipulation exercises" and prepare for what they believe will be an imminent Almuklis invasion. French intelligence services report that several hundred followers have quit their jobs to dedicate themselves full-time to the movement.

Concerns About Radicalization

French authorities have grown increasingly concerned about the potential for violence as the movement's rhetoric has become more apocalyptic. Interior Ministry sources tell CNN that surveillance has intensified following reports that some followers are attempting to secure funding for preliminary laser construction.

"We're dealing with a group that believes they're fighting an interplanetary war," said a French intelligence official who requested anonymity. "When people believe they're saving humanity from alien invasion, they might justify extreme actions."

The movement has already clashed with authorities. In February, twelve Chronoist followers were arrested for attempting to trespass on a scientific research facility in the French Alps, claiming they needed to "measure temporal resonance patterns" at high altitude.

GOVERNMENT RESPONSE: The French Interior Ministry has opened a formal investigation into the Chronoist movement's activities. "While freedom of belief is protected, we cannot ignore potential threats to public safety," said ministry spokesperson Claire Moreau.

International Attention

The Chronoist movement has attracted attention beyond France, with smaller groups forming in Belgium, Switzerland, and Quebec. Montclair's writings have been translated into multiple languages, and his weekly video addresses receive thousands of views online.

European intelligence agencies have begun sharing information about the movement, concerned about its rapid growth and unusual beliefs. The European Union's counter-extremism unit has classified Chronoism as a "potential risk group" requiring monitoring.

"We've seen similar groups before, but never with this level of technical sophistication in their delusions," said Dr. Elena Kowalski, who studies extremist movements for the EU. "The combination of pseudoscience, cosmic warfare, and messianic leadership creates a particularly volatile mix."

The Mystery of Gabriel Montclair

Little is known about Montclair's background before he emerged as the leader of Chronoism. He claims to have been born in Lyon but provides no verifiable details about his education, family, or life before founding the movement.

Attempts to verify his identity have proven fruitless. French authorities have found no birth certificate, passport, or official documents matching his description. Some investigators believe "Gabriel Montclair" may be a pseudonym adopted specifically for his role as movement leader.

"I exist outside of time," Montclair told followers during a recent gathering. "Past, present, future - these are illusions for those trapped in three-dimensional thinking. I am the Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the end, the eternal observer of humanity's struggle against the Almuklis."

EDITOR'S NOTE: CNN has attempted to verify the scientific claims made by the Chronoist movement with multiple physicists, astronomers, and aerospace experts. All confirm that there is no evidence for the existence of the Almuklis civilization, the planet Ulthaf, or the possibility of controlling time through meditation techniques.

What's Next?

As the Chronoist movement continues to grow, French authorities face the delicate challenge of monitoring a group whose beliefs, while clearly delusional, have not yet resulted in violence. The movement's plans for constructing an "intergalactic laser" at the North Pole remain theoretical, but intelligence sources worry about what might happen if followers attempt to make them reality.

For now, the movement remains largely peaceful, focused on meditation, recruitment, and preparation for the supposed Almuklis invasion. But experts warn that groups with apocalyptic beliefs can become dangerous when their prophecies fail to materialize or when authorities attempt to intervene.

"The real danger isn't the Almuklis," said Dr. Dubois. "It's what happens when thousands of people who believe they're humanity's last hope realize they've been following a fantasy. History shows that such moments can lead to tragedy."

As France grapples with this unusual phenomenon, the world watches with a mixture of fascination and concern. The rise of Chronoism may represent something new: a technologically sophisticated delusional system perfectly adapted for the social media age, capable of attracting followers despite - or perhaps because of - its complete divorce from reality.

Whether Gabriel Montclair truly believes he is the "Alpha and Omega" fighting an interplanetary war, or whether he is deliberately manipulating followers for his own purposes, remains unclear. What is certain is that the Chronoist movement has become a phenomenon that cannot be ignored, no matter how bizarre its beliefs may seem to outsiders.
