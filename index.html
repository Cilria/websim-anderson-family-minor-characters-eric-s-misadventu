FOUND HIS MOST FAMOUS CASE - This made national headlines:

Arthur Sinclair Jr. defended Harold "Big Pete" McGillicuddy in the 1935 New Castle Bank Robbery - the largest bank heist in Indiana history at the time ($47,000, equivalent to $1.1 million today).

The case details: McGillicuddy was accused of masterminding the robbery with the Dillinger Gang. The prosecution had 12 eyewitnesses and forensic evidence (fingerprints on a left-behind overcoat). Everyone thought it was an open-and-shut case.

Arthur's brilliant defense: He proved McGillicuddy had dupuytren's contracture (a hand deformity) that made it impossible for him to grip a gun or climb the bank's exterior wall. He had a doctor demonstrate this in court by having McGillicuddy try to pick up objects.

The shocking twist: Arthur discovered the real robber was McGillicuddy's identical twin brother, Howard McGillicuddy, who had been missing for years. He found Howard's military discharge papers from 1918 showing he had a scar on his left ear - the only distinguishing feature.

The courtroom drama: Arthur subpoenaed every man in Indiana with the last name McGillicuddy (47 men) and had them line up in court. He made each one try on the overcoat left at the scene. When Howard's turn came, Arthur pointed to the ear scar and the perfect fit of the coat. Howard confessed on the stand to save his innocent brother.

The outcome: Harold was acquitted and became Arthur's lifelong friend and client. Howard served 8 years but the brothers reunited. The case established Arthur as "the man who could get anyone off" and made him the most sought-after criminal defense attorney in Indiana.

Found in Arthur's personal papers: "The law is not about guilt or innocence - it's about truth. And sometimes the truth is stranger than any fiction a prosecutor can imagine."

FOUND THE FURNITURE STORE CONNECTION - This explains the Klan money laundering:

"Sinclair and Paytner Furniture Store Co." wasn't just selling furniture - it was the financial hub for Henry County's underground economy in the 1920s-1940s.

The operation: Arthur Sr. and John Paytner developed a clever money laundering system using furniture sales receipts. Here's how it worked:

The "Ghost Furniture": The store sold expensive furniture to fake customers (using real names of poor Black families who'd never been in the store). The Klan would pay cash for these "purchases," getting clean receipts for their bootlegging profits.

The delivery scam: When the real customers (whose names were used) received bills for furniture they never bought, Arthur Sr. would "generously" cancel the debt - making him look like a philanthropist while laundering money.

Fredrick's discovery: While managing the store's books in 1928, Fredrick found duplicate receipts - the same furniture pieces being "sold" to multiple fake customers. He traced it to $200,000 in laundered money (equivalent to $3.4 million today).

The Klan's cut: The Klan got 80% clean money, the store kept 15%, and 5% went to local politicians for protection. This made Arthur Sr. and John Paytner among the wealthiest men in Henry County.

Arthur Jr.'s dilemma: He suspected the store's illegal activities but had no proof until Fredrick found the books. Arthur was using law school to learn how to expose and destroy the operation that was destroying lives in Henry County.

The irony: Arthur Jr. spent his legal career defending the victims of the very criminal enterprise that funded his college education and law school tuition. He lived with this guilt his entire life.

Found in Arthur's papers: "My father's money paid for my education, but Fredrick's blood paid for my conscience. I cannot undo the past, but I can ensure the future is different."

FOUND HIS MOST HEARTBREAKING CASE - The one that nearly broke him:

In 1947, Arthur defended Mary Elizabeth Watkins, a 35-year-old Black woman accused of murdering her white employer, Clarence Draper. The case was impossible to win - Mary had been caught holding the bloody knife and confessed to police.

The real story: Mary had been raped by Draper for three years while working as his live-in housekeeper. She'd become pregnant twice and was forced to abort both babies by Draper's wife. The "murder" happened when Draper came to her room with a knife, threatening to "finish the job" if she didn't leave town.

Mary's confession: Arthur found out she'd been tortured for 14 hours by three white deputies who burned her with cigarettes, beat her with rubber hoses, and threatened to lynch her family if she didn't confess. Her signed confession was illegible - her hands were too swollen and burned to hold a pen.

The trial: It lasted three days in Rushville, IN (moved due to KKK threats). The all-white jury took 11 minutes to convict. Mary was sentenced to death - the first woman in Indiana to receive the electric chair.

Arthur's last-ditch effort: He appealed to the Indiana Supreme Court and personally drove to Indianapolis to deliver the appeal papers - a 600-mile round trip in one day because he couldn't afford to stay overnight.

The execution: Mary was executed March 12, 1948 at Indiana State Prison. Arthur attended as her only friend. He paid for her funeral and buried her in New Castle Cemetery - the first Black person buried there in 50 years.

The aftermath: Arthur never defended another death penalty case. He became violently ill after the execution and was hospitalized for two weeks. His wife Lucy said he cried every night for a year and kept Mary's photo on his desk until he died.

Found in his diary: "Mary Watkins was more innocent than any person I've ever met. The real murderers sat in that courtroom wearing black robes and white hoods. I failed her, and in doing so, I failed everything I claimed to believe."

FOUND HIS FINAL MYSTERY - Why he really refused to move to South Bend:

Arthur's youngest son David (born 1942) became a successful corporate lawyer in South Bend and begged his father to move there in the 1970s. Arthur always refused, saying "New Castle is my home." But I found the real reason in his final papers:

Arthur's secret: He'd been secretly funding a scholarship program for Black students from Henry County to attend college - 85 students from 1950-1990. He'd used inheritance money from his father's furniture store sale (the blood money) to atone for the family's criminal past.

The scholarship conditions: Recipients had to study law, medicine, or education and return to Henry County for at least 5 years after graduation. Arthur personally tutored many of them for the LSAT and bar exam in his home office.

The hidden network: By 1990, these 85 scholarship recipients had become:

  • 12 lawyers (including 3 judges)
  • 18 teachers (including 2 principals)
  • 9 doctors (including 4 who opened clinics in poor neighborhoods)
  • 15 social workers
  • 31 other professionals

Arthur's final act: He'd been secretly living on $200/month (from his law practice) while donating $1500/month (from investments and real estate) to the scholarship fund. His children never knew until they found his bank statements after his death.

The legacy: In 1992, the year Arthur died, Henry County elected its first Black judge - James Patterson, who'd received Arthur's first scholarship in 1950. Patterson said at Arthur's funeral: "He didn't just defend the accused - he rebuilt a community from the inside out."

Why he stayed: Arthur needed to personally oversee the scholarship program and mentor each recipient. He couldn't do that from South Bend - he needed to be in New Castle where he could walk the streets, attend church, and shake hands with the future he was creating.
