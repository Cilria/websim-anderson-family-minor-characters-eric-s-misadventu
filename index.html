BREAKING: New leaked texts show South Carolina State Sen. Earl LeRoy Carter Sr. calling America a "White Nation" and praising Hitler

South Carolina state senator faces bipartisan calls to resign after more racist, antisemitic texts surface

By Jennifer Brooks, Michael Chen and Rebecca Martinez July 11, 2026

Columbia, South Carolina (NBC News) — South Carolina State Senator Earl LeRoy Carter Sr. is facing intensified bipartisan pressure to resign immediately after a new batch of text messages revealed even more extreme racist, antisemitic and authoritarian views, including statements praising Adolf Hitler and calling democracy "the greatest mistake of the founders."

The new messages, obtained exclusively by NBC News, show the 74-year-old Republican senator expressing beliefs that America should be an exclusively white nation and advocating for the mass deportation of millions of Americans whose ancestors immigrated after 1840 — a category that would ironically include his own family.

The New Text Messages

"America is a White Nation and always will be. Anyone whose ancestors came here after 1840 should be kicked out. This country went to hell when we let in all these inferior races."

In another exchange, Carter wrote: "I am a fascist. Democracy was the greatest mistake of the founders. We need strong leadership, not mob rule."

Most shocking, Carter explicitly praised Adolf Hitler and Nazi ideology: "Hitler was right about the Jews. They control everything and are destroying our country. I agree with him 100% on everything."

The messages were provided to NBC News by the same source who leaked the initial batch of texts yesterday, and have been authenticated through digital forensics analysis. The source, who has known Carter for over a decade, said they could no longer remain silent after seeing the depth of his extremist views.

"These aren't just offensive comments - this is a sitting elected official openly advocating for fascism, praising Hitler, and calling for ethnic cleansing. Every Republican leader needs to demand his immediate resignation." — South Carolina Democratic Party Chair Christale Spain

The backlash has been swift and severe. Within minutes of NBC News publishing the story, Republican Governor Henry McMaster called for Carter's "immediate resignation and permanent removal from public office," while Senate Majority Leader Shane Massey said Carter should "resign today and check himself into professional help."

The White House issued a scathing statement: "These vile, antisemitic and racist statements have no place in American politics. President Harris condemns these comments in the strongest possible terms and calls on all Republican leaders to take concrete action to remove this individual from office."

Carter's political isolation became even more apparent during a scheduled town hall meeting in Laurens County Friday evening. Only two constituents showed up — both longtime supporters who came specifically to boo him.

"I voted for Earl for 20 years, but this is too much," said Mary Thompson, 67, a retired teacher who attended the town hall. "When you're praising Hitler and talking about kicking out immigrants, you've lost your mind and you need to go."

The Town Hall

Only two people attended Senator Carter's scheduled town hall, both came to express their outrage. Local Republican officials have stopped returning his calls.

The Republican Party of South Carolina issued an emergency statement late Friday, calling Carter's views "abhorrent to everything our party stands for" and announcing they would begin formal proceedings to expel him from the party. Multiple Republican donors who had supported Carter for years have publicly withdrawn their support and demanded his resignation.

"I've known Earl Carter for 30 years, and I never imagined he harbored these kinds of hateful views," said former South Carolina Governor Nikki Haley. "He needs to resign immediately and seek professional help. These views have no place in our party or our state."

The controversy has quickly become a national flashpoint, with Democratic leaders arguing it represents the logical conclusion of what they call "Republican extremism." Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer called for a Senate resolution condemning Carter's statements, while House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries said the incident "lays bare the dangerous extremism that has taken root in the Republican Party."

Republican leaders, meanwhile, have been scrambling to distance themselves from Carter. Senate Minority Leader Mitch McConnell called his statements "disgusting and completely beyond the pale," while House Speaker Mike Johnson said they "have no place in the Republican Party or American politics."

However, some Republicans privately express concern about the broader implications. "This isn't just about Earl Carter — this is about a culture within certain corners of our party where these views are more common than we want to admit," said one senior Republican strategist who requested anonymity. "The real scandal is that he felt comfortable expressing these views at all."

The new revelations have also sparked intense interest from national media and civil rights organizations. The Anti-Defamation League called for a federal investigation into whether Carter's statements constitute hate speech, while the NAACP announced plans for protests at the South Carolina statehouse.

"When you're praising Hitler and talking about America as a white-only nation, you've crossed every line imaginable. This man needs to be removed from office immediately." — Local constituent Mary Thompson

Carter's office has gone silent, with staffers reportedly refusing to come to work. Multiple sources close to the senator say he is "isolated and defiant," refusing to resign despite mounting pressure from every corner of the political spectrum.

The controversy has also raised questions about how such extreme views could remain hidden for so long. Carter has served for 22 years, raising concerns about what other officials might be harboring similar private views.

"The real scandal isn't just what Carter said — it's that our political system allowed someone with these views to serve for over two decades," said Democratic strategist Antjuan Seawright. "That speaks to a culture where these views are more tolerated than we want to believe."

Under South Carolina law, state senators can only be removed through resignation, expulsion by a two-thirds vote of the Senate, or recall election. With Republicans holding a 30-16 majority in the state Senate, expulsion would require significant Republican support — support that appears to be building rapidly.

Senate Majority Leader Massey announced Friday that he would introduce a resolution to expel Carter when the Senate reconvenes Monday, saying "there is no place in our chamber for someone who praises Hitler and calls for ethnic cleansing."

The incident has also sparked broader discussions about extremism in American politics and the role of social media in exposing private communications. Some analysts argue that Carter's case represents a watershed moment in American politics.

"This is different from your typical political scandal," said political scientist Danielle Vinson of Furman University. "When you have a sitting elected official openly praising Hitler and calling for ethnic cleansing, that's not just offensive — that's a fundamental threat to democratic values."

As pressure continues to mount, all eyes are on Columbia to see whether Carter will finally resign or force his colleagues to expel him. Either way, political analysts say his career is effectively over, and his legacy will be defined by these shocking revelations rather than his 22 years of legislative service.

"History will remember Earl Carter not as a state senator, but as a cautionary tale about what happens when extremist views are allowed to fester in positions of power," said Vinson. "This is a moment of reckoning not just for South Carolina, but for American democracy itself."
