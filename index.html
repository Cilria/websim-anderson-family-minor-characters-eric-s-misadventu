James Monroe Crowder (1843-1929): Illiterate farmer, CSA veteran, married a 22-year-old at 81

Mississippi Civil War Poor White Pension Records Posted by u/Mississippi_Poor_White • 8 hours ago

James Monroe Crowder might be the most unremarkable person I've ever researched, but his story is haunting me. Born April 10, 1843 in Crawford, Mississippi to Edmund Crowder Sr. (1801-1865) and Martha Ann Wilks (1805-after 1870). He was their 8th child, third youngest.

The Unremarkable Life:

  • Never attended school - started working his father's farm at age 8
  • November 1861: Brothers Nathanial, David, Robert, and Edmund Jr. enlisted in CSA
  • March 1862: James joined them at age 19
  • Served until December 1864, reenlisted January 1865 until war's end
  • Never captured by Union forces
  • July 1864: Shot through shoulder, broke left shoulder blade
  • Oldest brother Nathanial died in war, David died near end
  • Post-war: Moved to farm east of Brooksville, MS and worked there the rest of his life
  • Married Eliza, had 10 children, extremely poor
  • Died July 20, 1929 at son Jefferson Carter Crowder's home

The Only Interesting Thing: In 1924, at age 81, he married 22-year-old Susan. They stayed married until his death 5 years later.

Who was Susan? Why would a 22-year-old marry an illiterate 81-year-old farmer in 1924 Mississippi? Anyone descended from James or know anything about this final marriage? Or have insights into what life was like for poor white farmers in post-Reconstruction Mississippi?

Susan's identity found! Susan Elizabeth "Susie" Wilkinson, born January 8, 1902 in Noxubee County, MS. She was the daughter of sharecroppers John Wilkinson and Mary "Polly" Thompson. Here's the key detail: Susan was completely orphaned by 1918 (both parents died in the 1918 flu pandemic). She had no family, no education, and was working as a domestic servant in Brooksville at age 22. James Crowder owned a small cabin and 40 acres of poor-quality land - it wasn't much, but it was security for a girl with nothing.

His pension file reveals more: James applied for a Confederate pension in 1921 at age 78. His application states he served in Company K, 36th Mississippi Infantry. The injury details: "I was shot through the left shoulder at the Battle of Peach Tree Creek, Georgia, July 20, 1864. The ball entered my back and exited near my collarbone. I was left for dead but crawled to a field hospital." The pension was approved - $25 per year, which was only $2.08 per month. His wife Eliza had to sign with an X because she couldn't write either.

Eliza found: Eliza Jane "Liza" Fortner, born 1848 in Lowndes County, AL. Married James in 1867 - typical for Confederate veterans to marry immediately after returning home. She was illiterate too, daughter of illiterate sharecroppers. She had 10 children between 1868-1890, with only 6 surviving to adulthood. Eliza died in 1919 at age 71 - probably also from the flu that killed Susan's parents. James was 76 when she died, explaining why he remarried at 81.

u/Mississippi_Land_Records • 5 hours ago
His farm details: James owned 40 acres east of Brooksville, purchased in 1867 for $200 (money probably from his mustering-out pay). The land was poor - red clay soil, hilly, only 15 acres suitable for cotton. His 1900 agricultural census shows: 1 mule, 5 chickens, 1 pig, 1 milk cow. That's it. His total farm production value that year was $87. He literally lived on cornmeal, pork fat, and milk.

u/Civil_War_Unit_Historian • 4 hours ago
His unit's history explains his survival: Company K, 36th Mississippi was part of the Army of Tennessee but was assigned to rear-guard duty through most of 1863-64 due to heavy casualties. At Peach Tree Creek, they were in reserve, which is why James wasn't killed outright when shot. The unit surrendered in North Carolina in April 1865. Of 120 men who enlisted in 1862, only 27 were present at surrender. His brothers' units weren't so lucky - Nathanial's unit (14th Mississippi) was virtually wiped out at Murfreesboro.

u/Sharecropper_History • 4 hours ago
Why he stayed poor: James' 40 acres was too small for sharecropping - he had to work it himself. The soil was so poor that even with fertilizer (which he couldn't afford), cotton yields were pathetic. He tried to rent additional land but couldn't compete with the large plantations. By 1900, he was essentially subsistence farming - growing corn for his family, not for market. His children had to work as sharecroppers on other farms because there wasn't enough land to divide among them.

u/1920s_Mississippi • 3 hours ago
Context for the 1924 marriage: In 1924 Mississippi, a 22-year-old orphan woman had three options: work as domestic help (like Susan was), become a prostitute, or marry someone with land. At 81, James couldn't work the farm anymore and needed someone to care for him. Susan needed security. It was a practical arrangement - she got land and a home, he got a caregiver. Their marriage certificate lists "farmer" for both - Susan was working as a maid but claimed farming background to match James.

u/Mississippi_Death_Records • 2 hours ago
His death certificate tells everything: James died of "old age and chronic dysentery" - basically starvation. At 86, he couldn't digest the poor food anymore. Susan cared for him alone - no doctors would come to a poor farm in 1929 Mississippi. His estate was worth $47: the cabin, 40 acres, 1 mule, 3 chickens, and some farm tools. Susan had to sell the mule to pay for his coffin. He's buried in an unmarked grave in Brooksville Cemetery, section for "paupers and unknowns."

His surviving children: Of the 10 children with Eliza, only 6 survived to adulthood: Jefferson Carter (1868-1945), William Nathanial (1870-1935), Mary Elizabeth (1872-1950), Martha Ann (1875-1960), James Edmund (1878-1942), and Robert Lee (1880-1955). All stayed poor - worked as sharecroppers, sawmill workers, or domestic help. None owned land except Jefferson, who inherited James' farm. The cycle of poverty continued for three more generations until WWII factory jobs finally broke it.

u/Illiterate_Life • 1 hour ago
What illiteracy meant: James signed every document with an X. His pension file has a note: "Applicant cannot write his name." In 1920, when he was 77, census taker wrote "Cannot read or write - never attended school." This meant he couldn't read contracts, couldn't check if he was being cheated on crop prices, couldn't even read the Bible. His world was entirely oral - stories, songs, conversations. When he died, all his knowledge of farming, weather, and survival died with him because it was never written down.
