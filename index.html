Eurasian Spring spreads: Turkmenistan sees first protests as Tajik rebels consolidate gains

Regional uprising enters new phase as Turkmen citizens take to streets over economic hardship while Tajik opposition forces expand territorial control

By CNN Staff September 11, 2027 • 8:47 PM EDT

September 11, 2027 — The Eurasian Spring uprising that has already transformed Uzbekistan and plunged Tajikistan into civil war has now spread to Turkmenistan, as hundreds of protesters took to the streets in towns near Turkmenabat on Wednesday, marking the first significant anti-government demonstrations in the gas-rich Central Asian nation.

Turkmenistan: First Sparks of Rebellion

Protests erupted in towns near Turkmenabat, with demonstrators citing economic hardship

The protests, while smaller in scale compared to neighboring countries, represent a significant development in a nation that has maintained strict control over its population through a pervasive security apparatus and personality cult around its leadership.

Protesters chanted:

"Biz iýmäge pulumyz ýetmeýär!" (We can't afford to eat!)

"Zulumy bes ediň" (Stop The Oppression)

Witnesses reported that security forces initially responded with restraint, possibly due to surprise at the sudden outbreak of dissent. However, government officials later characterized the protesters as "foreign-influenced agitators" and warned of severe consequences for future demonstrations.

Key facts about Turkmenistan protests

  • First major protests since 2022 food price demonstrations
  • Turnout lower than expected but organizers planning larger protests for September 13
  • Economic conditions deteriorating despite vast natural gas reserves
  • Government maintains tight control over information flow

The economic grievances driving the protests reflect broader regional issues. Despite possessing the world's fourth-largest natural gas reserves, Turkmenistan has struggled with inflation, food shortages, and unemployment. The situation has been exacerbated by declining gas exports to China and the collapse of the Russian market due to international sanctions.

Organizers have called for larger demonstrations on September 13, with protest leaders using encrypted messaging apps to coordinate activities and avoid government surveillance. Whether these planned protests will materialize remains uncertain given Turkmenistan's history of preemptively arresting potential demonstrators.

Tajikistan: Opposition Consolidates Gains

Rebels seize full control of Istravshan as government forces retreat to Shakhristan

The situation in Tajikistan has evolved dramatically over the past 48 hours, with opposition forces achieving significant territorial gains and the government suffering its most serious setbacks since the conflict began.

Recent developments in Tajikistan

Sept 9-10 Government forces abandon Aksu river positions under rebel pressure
Sept 10 Istravshan falls completely to opposition forces
Sept 11 Government retreats to Shakhristan, last major loyalist stronghold
Current Paschi town split between rebel and government control

The fall of Istravshan represents a major strategic victory for the opposition. The city, which had been contested for days, is now fully under rebel control, giving them a crucial foothold in the western part of the country and access to important transportation routes.

"Istravshan is liberated," declared opposition spokesperson Safarali Khudoyarov in a video statement posted online. "The criminal regime has been driven out, and the people are celebrating their freedom. This is just the beginning. We will not stop until every town and village is free from Rahmon's tyranny."

The government, meanwhile, has been forced to retreat to Shakhristan, a town in central Tajikistan that has remained largely loyal to President Rahmon. Shakhristan has become the regime's last major stronghold, with government forces digging in and preparing for what many expect to be a decisive battle.

Analysis from regional expert:

"The retreat to Shakhristan is significant because it represents the government's acknowledgment that it cannot hold the north. They're consolidating forces for a last stand, but they're increasingly isolated and dependent on a shrinking base of loyal supporters."

The town of Paschi has become a microcosm of the broader conflict, with the front line literally splitting the community in half. Residents report that the eastern part of town remains under government control, while the western portion has been seized by opposition forces. The situation has created a tense standoff, with both sides maintaining checkpoints and neither appearing willing to back down.

Meanwhile, the government has largely succeeded in suppressing protests in other parts of the country, using a combination of security force deployments, arrests, and in some cases, targeted violence against protest leaders. Many demonstrators have fled northward to opposition-held territories, particularly Khujand and Istravshan, creating a growing population of displaced persons.

Context: The Tajik government has reportedly begun planning a major military campaign to retake Istravshan and eventually Khujand, though analysts question whether they have sufficient forces to launch such an offensive while maintaining control over the rest of the country.

Uzbekistan: New Government Implements Reforms

National Provisional Government announces term limits and power restrictions

Uzbekistan's transitional government has moved quickly to implement its first major reforms, announcing significant constitutional changes aimed at preventing the concentration of power that characterized the previous regime.

The National Provisional Government, which took power following the overthrow of President Shavkat Mirziyoyev, has formally declared that future presidents will be limited to a maximum of 10 years in office. Additionally, the office of president will have reduced powers compared to the previous system, with more authority transferred to parliament and regional governments.

Uzbekistan's new reforms

  • Presidential term limit: Maximum 10 years
  • Presidential powers significantly reduced
  • Multiple bills from old regime abolished
  • Power transferred to parliament and regions
  • Constitutional changes being drafted

The reforms represent a dramatic shift from the highly centralized system that existed under Mirziyoyev and his predecessors. Opposition leaders who have joined the transitional government say the changes are designed to prevent any future leader from accumulating the kind of absolute power that characterized the previous regime.

"We are building a new Uzbekistan based on democratic principles and the rule of law," said interim President Abdulla Aripov in a televised address. "The era of unlimited presidential power is over. From now on, Uzbekistan will have a balanced system of government with proper checks and balances."

The transitional government has also abolished a wide range of laws passed under the previous regime, including restrictions on press freedom, assembly rights, and opposition political activity. Many political prisoners have been released, and opposition parties that were previously banned have been legalized.

Next expected developments

International observers have cautiously welcomed the reforms, though they note that the success of Uzbekistan's transition will depend on whether the new government can maintain stability while implementing democratic changes. The country's experience could serve as a model for other nations in the region experiencing similar upheavals.

Attention is now turning to Kazakhstan, which many analysts expect to be the next major focal point of the Eurasian Spring. The country has seen sporadic protests in recent months, and opposition groups have reportedly been emboldened by events in neighboring countries.
