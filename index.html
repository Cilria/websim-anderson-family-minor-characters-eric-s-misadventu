Dr. Robert Louis Robinette Jefferson Jr. (born October 9, 1930) is an American chemist and former professor who taught for nearly five decades. He is known for authoring over 27 papers on catalysis and chemical engineering processes. Born in Florence, Alabama to civil engineer Robert Louis Robinette Jefferson Sr. and Sarah Ann Cook, he was the eldest of four siblings.

Early life and education

Jefferson was born on October 9, 1930, in Florence, Alabama, the eldest child of civil engineer Robert Louis Robinette Jefferson Sr. and homemaker Sarah Ann Cook. His father was a prominent civil engineer originally from Upstate New York who worked on various infrastructure projects throughout the American South during the 1920s and 1930s.

He attended public schools in Florence, Alabama, where he demonstrated exceptional aptitude in mathematics and sciences. In 1948, he left Alabama to attend the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign, receiving his Bachelor of Science in chemistry in 1952. He subsequently pursued graduate studies at Yale University, obtaining his Master of Science in 1954, and later earned his PhD in chemical engineering from Stanford University in 1957.

Academic career

Jefferson began his academic career in 1957 as an assistant professor of chemistry at the University of Illinois. He was promoted to associate professor in 1962 and achieved full professorship in 1967. He taught for nearly 50 years, retiring in 1998 as professor emeritus.

During his tenure, he supervised over 45 doctoral students and 60 master's students. His teaching focused on advanced chemical kinetics, catalysis, and reaction engineering.

Research contributions

Jefferson's research focused primarily on heterogeneous catalysis and chemical reaction engineering. He authored over 27 peer-reviewed papers on topics including:

  • Catalytic cracking of hydrocarbons
  • Zeolite catalyst synthesis
  • Hydrodesulfurization processes
  • Catalyst deactivation mechanisms
  • Reactor design for fine chemicals

His most cited works include the development of the Jefferson-Harris mechanism for catalytic cracking and the Jefferson equation for catalyst deactivation modeling.

Later life

Following his retirement in 1998, Jefferson remained active in professional societies including the American Chemical Society and the North American Catalysis Society. He continued to consult for major chemical companies until 2010.

He married Martha Elizabeth Johnson in 1958, with whom he had three children. As of 2024, he resides in Urbana, Illinois, where he continues to write and occasionally lecture on the history of chemical engineering education.
