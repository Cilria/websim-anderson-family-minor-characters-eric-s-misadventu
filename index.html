Peru's new military ruler declares indefinite martial law, bans all opposition parties

By Rafael Romo, CNN Senior Latin American Affairs Editor | CNN Español

Lima, Peru (CNN) — Just three days after seizing power in a military coup, Peru's self-declared leader Antauro Humala has consolidated absolute control over the Andean nation, issuing a sweeping decree that grants him total authority and formally banning all opposition political parties.

In a nationally televised address Friday evening, Humala announced the immediate implementation of martial law "until the restoration of the Incan Nation is complete," effectively suspending all constitutional rights and establishing what experts describe as Latin America's first full military dictatorship since Pinochet's Chile.

URGENT: Martial law now in effect nationwide. Curfew 6 PM to 6 AM. International flights suspended. Internet blackouts reported in major cities.

"The era of corrupt democracy has ended," Humala declared, wearing full military regalia and flanked by soldiers carrying assault rifles. "Peru will return to its authentic roots as the Incan Nation, free from foreign influence and the treachery of political parties."

The decree, officially titled "Law for National Reorganization and Indigenous Restoration," grants Humala sweeping powers including:

  • Complete control over all branches of government
  • Unlimited authority to rule by decree
  • Banning of all opposition parties and political organizations
  • Suspension of constitutional protections including habeas corpus
  • Establishment of military tribunals for civilians
  • State control of all media outlets

"This is not a temporary measure," Humala stated. "This is the permanent restoration of our ancestral glory. The Incan Nation will rise again, and I will guide it as the Sapa Inca of the new era."

The announcement triggered immediate international condemnation. The United States announced it was "reviewing all diplomatic and economic relations" with Peru, while the Organization of American States called an emergency session for Saturday morning.

"This represents the complete dismantling of democratic institutions in Peru," said former OAS Secretary General Luis Almagro. "The international community cannot stand idly by."

Opposition leaders arrested

Within hours of Humala's announcement, military units began arresting prominent opposition figures. Former President Dina Boluarte, who had been in hiding since the coup, was reportedly captured along with leaders from Peru's major political parties including:

  • Keiko Fujimori, leader of Popular Force
  • Rafael López Aliaga, leader of Popular Renewal
  • Verónika Mendoza, leader of Together for Peru
  • Multiple members of Congress across all parties

State television showed grainy footage of the arrested politicians being loaded into military vehicles, though their current whereabouts remain unknown.

Economic crisis deepens

Peru's economy is in freefall. The sol has lost 35% of its value since the coup, and the Lima Stock Exchange remains closed. Major international companies including mining giants Freeport-McMoRan and Newmont have suspended operations.

Panicked citizens have emptied supermarket shelves, and long lines have formed at banks as people attempt to withdraw savings. The central bank has imposed strict withdrawal limits of 500 soles ($125 USD) per person per day.

Plans for "Incan Restoration"

Perhaps most alarming to international observers is Humala's stated intention to establish what he calls the "restoration of the Incan Nation." According to sources within the military government, plans include:

  • Abolishing the current constitution and replacing it with "Incan law"
  • Eliminating Spanish as an official language and replacing it with Quechua
  • Confiscating properties of those with "colonial surnames"
  • Establishing a new capital in Cusco to replace Lima
  • Creating a theocratic government based on Incan religious principles

"This goes beyond typical authoritarian control," said Dr. Carlos Basombrío, former Peruvian Interior Minister. "Humala appears to be implementing a radical ethno-nationalist project that could destabilize the entire region."

Breaking: CNN has confirmed that internet access is being severely restricted nationwide. Social media platforms including Twitter, Facebook, and WhatsApp are blocked. Mobile networks are experiencing widespread outages.

The United Nations Security Council has scheduled an emergency session for Sunday to address what Secretary-General António Guterres called "a grave threat to international peace and security in the region."
