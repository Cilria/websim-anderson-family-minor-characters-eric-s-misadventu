Saudi nationalism turns darker

Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi's speech marks a dangerous escalation in anti-foreigner rhetoric

By our Middle East correspondent
December 29th 2029

The transformation of Saudi Arabia's anti-foreigner movement from a fringe Telegram group to a mass political force has been remarkable. But Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi's speech on December 29th in Jeddah, delivered to a crowd of over 200,000 supporters, marks a troubling new chapter.

Speaking from a purpose-built stage in the coastal city, Mr al-Harbi, who now leads the newly-formed Arab National Front of Homeland, unveiled a vision that extends far beyond the deportation of foreign workers. His message, delivered in carefully measured tones, nonetheless contained echoes of some of the 20th century's darkest political movements.

"It is essential that Arabs secure the living space they need for the survival of the Arab race and civilization in the future."

Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi, addressing supporters in Jeddah

The phrase "living space" carries uncomfortable historical connotations, though Mr al-Harbi's supporters appear either unaware or unconcerned by such parallels. For them, his message represents a necessary response to what they perceive as decades of economic and cultural displacement.

The crowd's reaction was telling. Chants of "Arabs Above All!" and "Hail Muhammad, The Savior of the Arabs!" suggest a movement that has moved beyond mere policy proposals into the realm of personality cult. The formation of the "Storm Unit" - officially to protect meetings and enforce order - raises inevitable questions about the movement's future direction.

The economic backdrop to this rising nationalism cannot be ignored. Saudi Arabia's economy, despite diversification efforts, continues to struggle with youth unemployment. Official figures put the rate at 32% for those aged 16-29, though economists suggest the real figure may be higher. Against this backdrop, foreign workers - who make up roughly one-third of the kingdom's population - provide an obvious scapegoat.

Yet the movement's growth suggests something deeper than mere economic anxiety. Mr al-Harbi's rhetoric about Arabs being "treated as slaves for over 100 years" resonates with a population that has experienced rapid social and economic change in recent decades. The implicit promise is not just jobs, but a restoration of dignity and cultural primacy.

By the numbers: The Arab National Front of Homeland claims 12 million followers across social media platforms, though independent analysis suggests the active user base is closer to 3-4 million. Telegram remains the primary platform, but the movement has expanded to WhatsApp, Twitter, Facebook and Signal.

The Saudi government's response has been notably restrained. Security forces maintained a visible presence during the Jeddah rally but made no attempt to disperse the crowd. This permissive approach has led to speculation about official sympathy for the movement's goals, if not its methods.

"The authorities are walking a tightrope," says Dr. Kristin Diwan of the Arab Gulf States Institute. "They share the movement's concerns about foreign influence and economic competition, but they fear the genie of mass mobilization once released from the bottle."

The international implications are becoming increasingly serious. India's foreign ministry has issued travel advisories for Indian nationals in Saudi Arabia, while Western embassies report growing anxiety among their citizens. Several multinational companies are reportedly reviewing their Saudi operations, though none have yet announced withdrawals.

Perhaps most concerning is the movement's increasingly sophisticated organizational structure. The Arab National Front of Homeland now operates through a network of semi-autonomous cells across the kingdom, each with its own "Storm Unit" for local enforcement activities. This decentralized structure makes the movement both resilient and unpredictable.

Mr al-Harbi's speech concluded with a ominous warning to his perceived enemies: "The traitors and deceitful liars will pay the price!" The crowd's enthusiastic response suggests that for many Saudis, the time for dialogue has passed. What comes next may be far more confrontational.

The kingdom appears to be at a crossroads. The economic and social pressures that have fueled this nationalist surge show no signs of abating. Meanwhile, the movement's leadership grows increasingly bold, its rhetoric increasingly extreme. The international community watches nervously, hoping for a peaceful resolution but preparing for the possibility of something far more troubling.

Analysis: The comparison to historical nationalist movements is increasingly difficult to ignore. The combination of economic grievance, cultural anxiety and charismatic leadership has proven volatile in other contexts. Saudi Arabia's allies must decide how to respond to an ally that appears to be flirting with political forces that could destabilize the entire region.
