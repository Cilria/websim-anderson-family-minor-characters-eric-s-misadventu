Anti-Indian sentiment surges across the Middle East

Telegram groups like "Sons of Arabs" gain traction as economic grievances fuel xenophobia

Nov 11th 2029 | DUBAI

The Arab world's relationship with its 35m-strong Indian diaspora has taken an ugly turn. Across the Gulf states, where Indians have long formed the backbone of service industries and construction, a wave of anti-Indian sentiment is gathering momentum. The phenomenon is most visible on Telegram, where groups with names like "Sons of Arabs" have amassed hundreds of thousands of followers in recent months.

The largest such group, أبناء العرب (Sons of Arabs), boasts 2.3m subscribers and growing daily. Its members chant slogans that would have been unthinkable just five years ago: "The Arab homeland is for Arabs only!" and "O God, grant victory to the Arabs and punish the Indians." The group's leader, a man known only as "Khaled," told The Economist that his goal is simple: "Kick out the Indians."

"The Arab homeland is for Arabs only! O God, grant victory to the Arabs and punish the Indians."

This surge in xenophobia coincides with a deteriorating economic picture across the region. Saudi Arabia's economy, once buoyed by $100-a-barrel oil, has struggled with prices hovering around $60. The kingdom's Vision 2030 reforms have yet to produce the promised diversification, while youth unemployment remains stubbornly high at 28%. Similar stories play out across the Gulf, where hydrocarbon-dependent economies have been forced to cut subsidies and introduce value-added taxes.

The timing is not coincidental. Governments that once welcomed Indian workers as essential to economic growth now face pressure to provide jobs for their own citizens. Saudi Arabia's Saudisation programme, which mandates quotas for local hires, has been expanded six times since 2025. Yet the private sector still relies heavily on Indian labour, creating friction between official policy and economic reality.

"The rhetoric has become frighteningly mainstream," says Dr. Sarah Al-Mansouri, a political scientist at the American University of Beirut. "What began as fringe Telegram groups now influences policy discussions. Government officials privately tell me they cannot appear soft on 'foreign worker issues' without risking public backlash."

The economic grievances are real enough. Indian workers in the Gulf sent home $89bn in remittances last year—money that might otherwise circulate in local economies. Yet the backlash ignores the essential role these workers play. Indian engineers maintain desalination plants that provide drinking water. Indian doctors staff hospitals that serve Arab citizens. Indian construction workers build the infrastructure that Gulf economies depend upon.

What makes the current wave of anti-Indian sentiment different from previous bouts of xenophobia is its organisation. The Telegram groups operate with military precision, using encrypted messaging to coordinate harassment campaigns. Indian-owned businesses have reported coordinated boycotts. Some groups maintain databases of Indian residents, complete with addresses and employers.

The Indian government has noticed. External Affairs Minister S. Jaishankar raised the issue during his September visit to Riyadh, though officials privately admit they have limited leverage. "These are sovereign nations that can set their own labour policies," says one Indian diplomat who requested anonymity. "Our primary concern is ensuring the safety of our citizens."

Back on Telegram, Khaled shows no signs of moderating his message. In a voice note posted last week, he urged followers to "make life uncomfortable for Indians" through legal means. "We don't need violence," he said. "We need pressure. We need to make them want to leave."

Whether such tactics will succeed remains uncertain. Previous campaigns against foreign workers have fizzled when employers pushed back. Yet the economic backdrop suggests this wave of xenophobia may have staying power. With oil revenues stagnant and populations growing, Gulf states face hard choices about their economic models.

For the 35m Indians who call the Arab world home, the future looks increasingly uncertain. Many have lived here for decades, built businesses, raised children. Now they find themselves cast as economic scapegoats in a region searching for someone to blame for its economic woes. The Telegram groups offer a simple answer: blame the Indians. Whether that answer proves politically effective may determine the fate of millions.
