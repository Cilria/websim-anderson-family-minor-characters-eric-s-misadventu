Islamic Emirate of Uzbekistan Declares Total Victory After Fall of Tashkent

Abdullah Muhammad Abdullayev proclaims complete control as Uzbek government collapses and Karakalpakstan declares independence

By Carlotta Gall and Eric Schmitt | July 25, 2028
Updated 8:47 PM ET, Wed July 25, 2028

TASHKENT, Uzbekistan — The Army of Islam declared complete victory over Uzbekistan on Wednesday, announcing the establishment of the Islamic Emirate of Uzbekistan after its fighters captured Tashkent without resistance, ending a four-month insurgency that has redrawn the map of Central Asia.

Abu Abdullah al-Uzbeki, who revealed his real name as Muhammad Abdullayev, proclaimed himself Emir of the Islamic Emirate in a triumphant address from the steps of the Uzbek Parliament building, where the green and white flag of the new Islamic state was raised for the first time.

[Image: Islamic Emirate fighters raise their flag over Tashkent's main government building]

"By the Grace of Allah, we have achieved final victory," Abdullayev declared to thousands of supporters gathered in the capital's central square. "Allah has blessed us with final victory and has defeated the infidel government and driven these criminal dogs out. Now the Uzbeks shall live the holy life that Allah commanded us. The Mujahideen have achieved the greatest victory for the way of Islam."

Lightning Collapse

The final collapse of the Uzbek government came with stunning speed. After the fall of Termez in late June, the Army of Islam launched a coordinated offensive that saw city after city fall with minimal resistance.

The capture of Tashkent on July 24 marked the culmination of the insurgency. Government forces, demoralized and depleted after months of fighting, either fled or melted away into the civilian population. President Shavkat Mirziyoyev and his inner circle reportedly fled to Moscow aboard a Russian military transport aircraft in the early hours of July 24.

"The speed of the collapse was unprecedented. In less than two weeks, an entire government simply evaporated."

Karakalpakstan Breaks Away

As the Army of Islam consolidated control over the rest of Uzbekistan, the autonomous republic of Karakalpakstan declared independence on July 24, creating a new Central Asian state that controls the vital Amu Darya river delta and significant natural gas reserves.

The Karakalpakstan provisional government, led by former regional governor Atabek Karimov, announced it would seek international recognition and has reportedly opened diplomatic channels with neighboring Kazakhstan and Turkmenistan.

International Recognition

The declaration of the Islamic Emirate has sent shockwaves through the international community. While no country has yet formally recognized the new government, several regional powers including Pakistan and Turkey have indicated they may open diplomatic channels.

Russia, which had conducted limited airstrikes against Army of Islam positions in April and May, has withdrawn its forces from the region and closed its border with Uzbekistan. The Kremlin issued a brief statement calling for "stability and respect for international law" but has not indicated whether it will recognize the new government.

Key Development

The establishment of the Islamic Emirate of Uzbekistan represents the first time an Islamist movement has successfully overthrown a government in Central Asia, creating a new geopolitical reality that will have far-reaching implications for the region.

Abdullayev's Vision

In his address, Abdullayev outlined his vision for the Islamic Emirate, promising to implement a strict interpretation of Sharia law while maintaining stability and economic development. He specifically mentioned plans to change the Uzbek alphabet back to the Persian-Arabic script and to establish an Islamic religious police force.

"We will build an Islamic society that will be a beacon for Muslims everywhere," Abdullayev declared. "But we will also build roads, schools, and hospitals. We will show the world that Islamic governance can bring prosperity and justice."

Humanitarian Crisis

The rapid collapse has created a humanitarian emergency, with hundreds of thousands of civilians displaced and basic services disrupted across much of the country. The World Food Program has warned of impending famine in several regions, while medical supplies have been exhausted in many areas.

Thousands of foreign nationals, including American and European citizens, remain trapped in the country, though the Army of Islam has promised safe passage for those wishing to leave. The United States has evacuated its embassy staff but has not yet announced plans for a larger evacuation effort.

Regional Implications

The fall of Uzbekistan has created a new geopolitical reality in Central Asia. The Islamic Emirate now controls significant natural gas reserves, major transportation corridors including the historic Silk Road routes, and borders with all five Central Asian republics.

Neighboring governments, particularly Tajikistan and Kyrgyzstan, have expressed alarm at the development and have reportedly begun strengthening their border security. Kazakhstan has announced it will host an emergency summit of Central Asian leaders next week to discuss the crisis.

What Comes Next

As the Islamic Emirate consolidates control, attention now turns to how the international community will respond to the new reality in Central Asia. The United Nations Security Council is scheduled to meet in emergency session on Friday, though Russia and China are expected to block any significant action.

For the people of Uzbekistan, the coming months will determine whether Abdullayev's promises of stability and prosperity under Islamic rule will materialize, or whether the country will face the same challenges that have plagued other Islamist governments in the region.

"The world is watching," Abdullayev said in closing his address. "We will show them that the Islamic Emirate of Uzbekistan will be a success, by the Will of Allah."
