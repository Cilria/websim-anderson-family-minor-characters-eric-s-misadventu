Tajikistan slides into civil war as opposition seizes Khujand

Security forces retreat across Aksu river as opposition declares provisional government, while fighting spreads to Dushanbe and Istravshan

By Central Asia Correspondent September 8, 2027

Almaty — Tajikistan has descended into full-scale civil war as security forces retreated from their last positions in Khujand and withdrew across the Aksu river, effectively ceding control of the country's second-largest city to opposition forces who have declared a provisional government.

The dramatic collapse of government authority in the north came after three days of intense fighting that left thousands dead and marked the most significant territorial loss for President Rahmon's regime since protests erupted across the country last week.

Key developments in Tajikistan

  • Khujand: Opposition declares provisional government
  • Aksu river: Government forces retreat to eastern strongholds
  • Istravshan: Armed protesters control 45% of city
  • Dushanbe: Government airstrikes target armories
  • Western Dushanbe: Protesters seize neighborhoods 102 & 103

In Khujand, where the uprising began, opposition leaders announced the formation of a provisional government based in the city center, claiming authority over "liberated territories" across northern Tajikistan. Latif Fayziyev, a former regime official who defected to the opposition, was named head of the provisional government and commander of the newly-formed Tajik National Force.

"The criminal regime of Rahmon has lost all legitimacy," Fayziyev declared in a televised address from what was formerly the regional administration building. "We have formed a provisional government to restore order and prepare for democratic elections. The people have spoken, and they have chosen freedom over tyranny."

"This is no longer a protest movement. This is a revolution. The old order has collapsed, and we are witnessing the birth of a new Tajikistan. The question now is whether the rest of the country will follow Khujand's example."

- Political analyst, Dushanbe

The government's response has been increasingly desperate. In Dushanbe, security forces conducted airstrikes and artillery bombardments targeting armories and police stations in an apparent attempt to prevent weapons from falling into opposition hands. However, the strategy has had limited success, particularly in western neighborhoods where protesters managed to seize control of key facilities.

Western Dushanbe, including neighborhoods 102 and 103, has become a stronghold for opposition forces. Residents report that protesters now control local government buildings and have established checkpoints at major intersections. The area has effectively become an autonomous zone outside government control.

In Istravshan, the situation has deteriorated into urban warfare. Armed protesters, many of whom are former soldiers who defected with their weapons, have seized approximately 45% of the city. Government forces have responded with indiscriminate shelling, but appear unable to dislodge the opposition fighters who have entrenched themselves in residential areas.

Timeline of the civil war escalation

Sept 6 Day of Blood: 3,000+ killed in government crackdown
Sept 7 Army units begin defecting to opposition
Sept 8 AM Security forces retreat from Khujand
Sept 8 PM Opposition declares provisional government
Sept 8 Night Government forces dig in east of Aksu river

The government's retreat to positions east of the Aksu river represents a significant strategic setback. Security forces have begun constructing defensive positions, including sandbag fortifications and reinforced checkpoints, suggesting they are preparing for a prolonged conflict rather than attempting to retake lost territory.

"They are digging in for war," said one local resident who fled across the river. "The army has brought in heavy weapons and is building what looks like permanent fortifications. They know they cannot retake the cities, so they are trying to hold what they still control."

The opposition's military capabilities have grown significantly. The Tajik National Force, as the provisional government calls its armed wing, consists of an estimated 15,000 fighters, including army defectors, armed protesters, and former security personnel. They have captured significant quantities of weapons and equipment, including armored vehicles and artillery pieces.

The economic dimension

The conflict has already disrupted key trade routes through Central Asia. The closure of the main highway between Dushanbe and Khujand has forced commercial traffic to take lengthy detours through Uzbekistan, adding days to delivery times and increasing costs for regional traders.

International observers warn that prolonged conflict could destabilize the entire region, affecting gas pipelines and trade corridors that are vital to the economies of neighboring countries.

Meanwhile, protests continue to spread across the country. In the southern city of Kulob, traditionally a government stronghold, demonstrators have taken to the streets chanting anti-government slogans. Similar scenes have been reported in Khorugh, the capital of the Gorno-Badakhshan autonomous region, where local authorities have been accused of siding with the opposition.

The international community has responded with alarm but limited action. The United Nations has called for an immediate ceasefire and dialogue, while neighboring countries have closed their borders and deployed additional security forces. However, there appears to be little appetite for direct intervention in what has become an increasingly complex civil conflict.

Russia, which maintains a military base in Tajikistan, has issued contradictory statements, initially supporting the government but later calling for a "negotiated settlement" that would include opposition representatives. This shift in tone has been interpreted by analysts as recognition that the Rahmon regime may not survive the current crisis.

Analysis: The situation has evolved from a protest movement to a full-scale civil war. With the opposition controlling major cities and the government reduced to defending isolated strongholds, the conflict appears likely to continue for weeks or months. The question now is not whether Rahmon will fall, but what will replace him and at what cost to the Tajik people.

The provisional government in Khujand faces its own challenges. While it has gained control of significant territory, it lacks international recognition and faces the daunting task of governing while under threat of government counterattacks. Opposition leaders have called for foreign assistance, but so far no country has formally recognized their authority.

As fighting continues to spread and the death toll mounts, the prospect of a negotiated settlement appears increasingly remote. Both sides have suffered heavy casualties and appear committed to achieving victory through force of arms rather than compromise. The result is a country sliding deeper into civil war, with no clear end in sight.

For the people of Tajikistan, the dream of democratic change that inspired the initial protests has given way to the harsh reality of civil conflict. Whether they will emerge from this crisis with the freedom they sought remains an open question, one that will likely be decided not in negotiating rooms but on the battlefields of cities like Khujand and Dushanbe.
