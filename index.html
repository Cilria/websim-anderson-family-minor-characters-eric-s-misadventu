Federal Agents Arrest Prominent Financier Ali Tamer Baykara in Virginia on Fraud Charges

Turkish-born investor accused of running $19 billion Ponzi scheme through shell companies, prosecutors say

By Benjamin Weiser, Matthew Goldstein and William K. Rashbaum
April 2, 2019

The Charges

Ali Tamer Baykara, 61, faces multiple counts of securities fraud, wire fraud and conspiracy.

Alleged scheme: $19 billion Ponzi scheme through shell companies including A.T. Global Capital and Haywood Venture Capital LLC.

Key evidence: Prosecutors say Baykara transferred investor funds to Cayman Islands accounts rather than investing in Asian markets as promised.

PORT HAYWOOD, Va. — Federal agents arrested Ali Tamer Baykara, the Turkish-born financier whose Virginia estate became a gathering place for political and business leaders, on Tuesday morning at his waterfront mansion here, charging him with orchestrating a $19 billion Ponzi scheme that prosecutors described as one of the largest financial frauds in American history.

Mr. Baykara, 61, was taken into custody by agents from the Federal Bureau of Investigation's Richmond field office following a yearlong investigation into his business dealings, according to court papers unsealed Tuesday in federal court in Alexandria, Va.

In an indictment that reads like a financial thriller, prosecutors accused Mr. Baykara of running an elaborate fraud through two shell companies — A.T. Global Capital and Haywood Venture Capital LLC — that he used to embezzle money from investors across Turkey, Russia, Saudi Arabia and Europe, transferring the funds to secret accounts in the Cayman Islands.

Mr. Baykara's estate in Port Haywood, Virginia, where he was arrested Tuesday morning.

Prosecutors said that since 2013, Mr. Baykara had falsely told investors that their money would be invested in high-growth stocks in East Asian and Southeast Asian markets, particularly in technology and infrastructure companies in China, Vietnam and Indonesia. Instead, they said, he used the money to pay earlier investors and to fund his lavish lifestyle, including the purchase of additional properties in Paris, London and the Swiss Alps.

"This was not sophisticated investing — this was sophisticated stealing," said G. Zachary Terwilliger, the United States attorney for the Eastern District of Virginia, at a news conference in Richmond. "Mr. Baykara sold investors a fairy tale about Asian market returns while he was actually running a massive Ponzi scheme."

A Web of Shell Companies

The charges represent a stunning fall for Mr. Baykara, who had cultivated an image as a global financier with connections to world leaders and celebrities. His Port Haywood estate, known as "the Virginia compound," had hosted gatherings that included former President Bill Clinton, various Saudi princes and European business magnates.

But prosecutors said Mr. Baykara's legitimate business activities masked a darker operation. According to the indictment, Haywood Venture Capital LLC, which he established in 2016, was portrayed to investors as a private equity fund focused on emerging Asian markets. In reality, they said, it was a shell company designed to facilitate the movement of funds offshore.

"Mr. Baykara repeatedly lied to shareholders and investors about the nature and performance of Haywood Venture Capital LLC," the indictment states, "creating false financial statements and investment reports while knowing that no actual investments were being made."

The scheme began to unravel last year when several large investors, including a Saudi sovereign wealth fund and a Russian investment group, attempted to redeem their investments. Court papers say Mr. Baykara initially delayed the payments, citing "regulatory issues in Asian markets," but eventually stopped responding entirely.

International Manhunt

Mr. Baykara had been under investigation by multiple agencies, including the Securities and Exchange Commission, the Internal Revenue Service and the F.B.I., for more than a year. Investigators tracked financial transactions across dozens of countries and interviewed more than 200 witnesses.

"This case demonstrates the global reach of financial crime and the importance of international cooperation," said William F. Sweeney Jr., the assistant director in charge of the F.B.I.'s New York field office, which assisted in the investigation.

The arrest came as a surprise to many in Virginia's political and business circles, where Mr. Baykara had been a visible figure for decades. His estate, which spans more than 200 acres along the Chesapeake Bay, had been the site of numerous charitable events and political fundraisers.

Prosecutors said they moved to arrest Mr. Baykara now because they believed he was preparing to flee the country. Recent surveillance showed him meeting with private aviation companies and reviewing properties in countries that do not have extradition treaties with the United States.

Previous Legal Troubles

The charges mark the second major legal battle for Mr. Baykara, who pleaded guilty in 2006 to federal tax charges related to his investment activities. He served 10 months in prison and paid $48 million in back taxes and penalties in what prosecutors at the time described as a "complex financial fraud scheme."

That case was sealed unusually quickly, leading to speculation that Mr. Baykara had cooperated with authorities in other investigations. People familiar with the matter, who spoke on the condition of anonymity, said Tuesday that prosecutors believe Mr. Baykara resumed his fraudulent activities shortly after completing his sentence.

"Some people never learn," Mr. Terwilliger said. "Mr. Baykara had a second chance and he allegedly used it to commit an even larger fraud."

Mr. Baykara appeared briefly in federal court in Alexandria Tuesday afternoon, where he was ordered held without bond. His attorney, Mark J. Geragos, declined to comment on the charges.

If convicted on all counts, Mr. Baykara faces up to 20 years in prison.
