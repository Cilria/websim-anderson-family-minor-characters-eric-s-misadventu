Century-old economist who predicted every recession since 1958 warns of 2027 downturn

Robert Allard Coleman Jr., who turns 100 this month, says economic conditions mirror 2008 warning signs

By Sarah Mitchell | BBC Business Correspondent | 01 July 2026

Robert Allard Coleman Jr., the world's oldest active economist who has correctly predicted every American recession since 1958, has issued his starkest warning yet: the global economy is heading for a significant downturn in early 2027.

In an exclusive interview at his Cambridge, Massachusetts home, Coleman - who celebrates his 100th birthday later this month - told the BBC that current economic conditions bear "disturbing similarities" to those preceding the 2008 financial crisis.

"I've seen this movie before, and it doesn't end well. The warning signs are flashing red: excessive leverage in shadow banking, overvalued assets across multiple sectors, and a dangerous complacency that 'this time is different.'"

Coleman's track record is unmatched in economic forecasting. Since correctly predicting the 1958 recession - when he was just 32 years old - he has accurately called every subsequent downturn, including the 1973 oil crisis, the 1981-82 double-dip recession, the 1990-91 Gulf War recession, the 2000 dot-com crash, and the 2008 financial crisis.

His methodology, developed over seven decades, combines traditional economic indicators with what he calls "human behavioral signals" - patterns of consumer and investor psychology that precede market corrections.

"The economy isn't just numbers on a spreadsheet," Coleman explains, his voice still sharp despite his advanced age. "It's human beings making decisions based on fear, greed, and hope. Right now, I'm seeing the same cocktail of overconfidence and excessive risk-taking that preceded every major downturn of my career."

The economist's concerns center on what he identifies as three critical vulnerabilities: corporate debt levels exceeding those of 2007, a commercial real estate bubble that has "already begun to deflate," and unprecedented levels of speculative investment in emerging technologies.

Coleman, who still maintains an office at Harvard where he graduated in 1946, has been advising policymakers since the Eisenhower administration. His 1958 prediction came just months after he joined the faculty, when he warned that post-war prosperity had created dangerous asset bubbles.

His most famous prediction came in 2006, when he testified before Congress that the housing market represented "the greatest threat to American economic stability since the Great Depression." His warnings were largely dismissed at the time as overly pessimistic.

"In 2006, they called me 'Dr. Doom.' Two years later, they were asking how I saw it coming. The same patterns are emerging now, but they're more widespread and more interconnected than ever before."

Despite his age, Coleman continues to monitor markets daily, relying on a team of assistants who help him analyze data. His current concerns extend beyond American borders, pointing to synchronized bubbles in European and Asian markets.

"This isn't just an American problem anymore," he notes. "The interconnected nature of modern finance means a crisis anywhere becomes a crisis everywhere. When the correction comes - and it will come - it will be global in scope."

The economist's timing prediction - early 2027 - is based on what he calls "the accumulation threshold," a model he developed that tracks when multiple economic stressors simultaneously exceed critical levels.

Current indicators, according to Coleman, show we're approaching this threshold faster than in previous cycles. He points to the Federal Reserve's aggressive interest rate policies, which he argues have created artificial market conditions that cannot be sustained indefinitely.

"The Fed has painted itself into a corner," Coleman argues. "They can't raise rates without triggering a recession, and they can't lower them without reigniting inflation. It's the same trap we saw in the 1970s, but with much higher debt levels."

Coleman's warning comes at a time when most economists remain relatively optimistic about the near-term outlook. The International Monetary Fund recently projected global growth of 3.2% for 2026 and 2027, while major central banks have begun easing monetary policies.

However, Coleman believes these projections fail to account for what he terms "the leverage time bomb" - the unprecedented levels of corporate and government debt accumulated during the low-interest rate era.

"Debt is productive when it funds investment, but dangerous when it funds speculation," he explains. "We've crossed that line. The question isn't whether we'll have a recession, but how severe it will be and whether policymakers have learned anything from 2008."

The economist's age has prompted some to question whether his analysis remains relevant in an era of artificial intelligence, cryptocurrency, and digital banking. Coleman dismisses such concerns, arguing that human nature - and therefore economic cycles - remains fundamentally unchanged.

"Technology changes, but people don't," he says. "Greed, fear, and hubris drive markets today just as they did in 1929. The instruments are different, but the psychology is identical."

Coleman's family has tried to convince him to retire, but he remains committed to his work. "As long as I can think clearly and the data keeps flowing, I have a responsibility to warn people," he insists. "My generation lived through the Depression. We know what real economic pain looks like, and we have a duty to prevent it when possible."

Whether markets will heed his latest warning remains to be seen. History suggests that Coleman's predictions, while initially unpopular, have consistently proven prescient. As he approaches his centenary, the economist remains convinced that his most important warning may be his last.

"I've lived through 17 recessions," Coleman reflects, settling back in his chair. "Each one felt different while it was happening, but they all rhymed. The next one will too, and it's coming sooner than most people think."

For policymakers, investors, and ordinary citizens, the question now is whether to heed the warnings of a man who has never been wrong - or to gamble that this time, somehow, will be different.
