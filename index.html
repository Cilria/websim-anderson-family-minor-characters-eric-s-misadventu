Franklin Jefferson Harland Sr (1836/37-1939) - Last survivor of his Civil War regiment, married his dead brother's widow, lived to 102 with a mystery birth year
892 upvotes • posted by BamaRoots Civil War Researcher

I've been researching my 3rd great-grandfather Franklin Jefferson Harland Sr and found some wild details. He either was born November 11, 1836 or October 22, 1837 (he used both dates throughout his life), died April 18, 1939 at age 102/103. Born in New Union, Alabama to Reverend David W Harland and Elizabeth Ann Jefferson.

Key details I've found:

  • 1861: Enlisted with brothers Edmund, Robert and Alexander - only he and Robert survived
  • Married Alexander's widow Sarah Emma Webb after the war
  • Had 7 children with Sarah until her death in 1899
  • Remarried another widow who died in 1920
  • Worked lumber and farming most of his life
  • Died in Gadsden, AL as "last living veteran of his regiment"

Looking for any military records, especially what happened to brother Edmund and which birth year is correct!

AlabamaArchives • 312 points • 6 hours ago

Found his actual military service records! Franklin J. Harland enlisted May 15, 1861 in Company K, 19th Alabama Infantry Regiment. His brothers Edmund, Robert and Alexander all enlisted the same day.

The birth date mystery is SOLVED: His enlistment papers clearly state November 11, 1836, but his 1921 Confederate pension application says October 22, 1837. He was trying to appear younger to qualify for the pension longer! The 1836 date is correct.

Edmund's fate: He was wounded at the Battle of Shiloh (April 7, 1862), captured, and died at Union Hospital in Savannah, Tennessee on April 15, 1862. He's buried in the Savannah Confederate Cemetery - Plot 47, Grave 3. His personal effects (a pocket knife and family Bible) were sent to Franklin.

Alexander died at the Battle of Chickamauga on September 19, 1863. Franklin was apparently nearby when it happened - there's a letter in the archives from Franklin to their mother describing Alexander's death in detail.

CivilWarBrideResearch • 267 points • 5 hours ago

Found the marriage records! Sarah Emma Webb married Alexander Harland on March 3, 1860 in Etowah County. They had one child, Mary Elizabeth Harland, born January 12, 1861.

After Alexander died at Chickamauga, Franklin married Sarah on December 20, 1863 while on furlough. This was actually pretty common - about 15% of Civil War widows married their deceased husband's brothers. It kept property in the family and provided for the children.

Here's the wild part: Franklin adopted Mary Elizabeth as his own daughter and had her name legally changed to Mary Elizabeth Harland. She grew up thinking Franklin was her biological father until she found her parents' marriage certificate as an adult!

Franklin and Sarah had 7 more children together: David Alexander (1865), William Edmund (1867), Sarah Emma Jr (1869), Robert Franklin (1871), Martha Ann (1873), James Thomas (1876), and Nancy Elizabeth (1878). All lived to adulthood except James who died at age 3 from scarlet fever.

NewUnionHistory • 198 points • 5 hours ago

Found the family farm! Reverend David W Harland owned 320 acres in New Union - specifically the area around what's now County Road 29 and Highway 77. The 1850 slave schedule shows him owning 12 slaves, which explains how the family had relative wealth.

Franklin's childhood home still stands! It's the old white farmhouse at 1276 County Road 29 - now owned by the Smith family. Local records show it was built in 1834 and has been continuously occupied. The current owners confirmed there's a family cemetery on the property with 23 graves including Elizabeth Ann Jefferson Harland who died in 1849.

Reverend David W Harland was quite controversial - he preached pro-slavery sermons citing Biblical justification, but also secretly taught enslaved people to read using the Bible. The contradiction apparently tore the family apart after the war.

LumberHistoryAL • 165 points • 4 hours ago

Found his lumber business records! After the war, Franklin partnered with his surviving brother Robert to form "Harland Brothers Lumber Company" in 1866. They operated sawmills along the Coosa River.

Business was BOOMING - by 1870 they were processing 500,000 board feet annually. Their main customers were the railroads expanding through Alabama. They supplied cross-ties to the Selma & Dalton Railroad and the Alabama & Chattanooga Railroad.

Here's why he switched to farming: The Panic of 1873 destroyed the lumber market. Their mill went bankrupt in 1874 with debts of $12,000 (huge money then). Franklin sold his share to Robert and bought a farm near Gadsden. Robert kept the lumber business going and became quite wealthy - by 1880 he owned three mills and employed 200 men.

Franklin's farm was 160 acres on what is now Steele Station Road. He grew cotton and corn, raised hogs and cattle. The 1880 agricultural census shows he was a successful farmer - produced 150 bales of cotton, 500 bushels of corn, and had 50 head of livestock.

GadsdenGenealogy • 143 points • 4 hours ago

Found his second marriage! After Sarah died in 1899, Franklin married Martha Caroline "Mattie" Thornton on March 15, 1900. Mattie was the widow of John Thornton who died in 1898 - she had 4 children from her first marriage.

Mattie was 20 years younger than Franklin (born 1857). They had no children together, but she helped raise his younger kids. She died in 1920 from influenza during the Spanish Flu pandemic.

Franklin lived his last 19 years as a widower with his son Robert in Gadsden. The 1930 census shows him at age 93 (they used the 1837 birth date) living with Robert's family at 815 Forrest Avenue. He was still listed as "farmer" even though he wasn't actively farming - just owned the land.

His death certificate shows he died of "old age and general debility" at the home of his daughter Nancy Elizabeth Harland Brown. He was either 102 years, 5 months, 7 days OR 101 years, 5 months, 27 days old depending on which birth date you use!

CSARecords • 98 points • 3 hours ago

Found his regiment's complete service record! The 19th Alabama Infantry fought in every major Western Theater battle:

  • Shiloh (where Edmund was mortally wounded)
  • Perryville
  • Murfreesboro
  • Chickamauga (where Alexander died)
  • Chattanooga
  • Atlanta Campaign
  • Franklin
  • Nashville

Franklin was wounded twice - minor wound at Murfreesboro December 31, 1862, and more seriously at Franklin November 30, 1864. He was captured at Nashville December 16, 1864 and spent 4 months in Camp Chase, Ohio prison.

He was released in a prisoner exchange March 1865 and walked home from Mississippi to Alabama. His service record notes "excellent character" and "recommended for promotion" but he never advanced past corporal.

By the 1920s, he was indeed the last surviving member of the 19th Alabama Infantry. The United Confederate Veterans held a special ceremony for him in Birmingham in 1928. He died with full military honors - the Gadsden chapter of the Sons of Confederate Veterans served as pallbearers.
