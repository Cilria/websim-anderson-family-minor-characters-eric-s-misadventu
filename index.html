Coalition Ground Forces Launch Major Offensive Across West Africa

US, French and NATO troops advance on multiple fronts with overwhelming air support, recapturing territory from Islamic Caliphate
By Declan Walsh & Eric Schmitt | The New York Times

Washington — Coalition ground forces have launched a major offensive across West Africa, with American, French and NATO troops advancing on multiple fronts alongside local forces in what military officials describe as the largest multinational ground operation since World War II.

Coalition Advances by Front

  • Ivory Coast: Northern areas recaptured except encircled Korhogo with 1,500 jihadists
  • Nigeria: A1 Highway cut off, Caliphate forces encircled in Yelwa
  • Togo: Kran National Park recaptured, advancing on Dapaong
  • Benin: 3,500 square km recaptured on RNIE2 Highway
  • Senegal: N7 highway cut off, Tambacounda offensive begun
  • Guinea: Siguiri encircled, large Caliphate forces trapped
  • Mali/Niger: Tuareg ANLF rebels recaptured Arlit with coalition support

The offensive, which began on June 30th and has intensified over the past three weeks, has seen coalition forces make significant territorial gains across seven countries, with air power playing a decisive role in supporting ground operations.

"This is the most significant multinational military operation of the 21st century," said General Michael Langley, commander of US Africa Command, in an exclusive interview with The Times. "We have over 63 nations contributing forces, equipment, and intelligence capabilities in a coordinated campaign to dismantle the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel."

Coalition forces from multiple nations have been advancing across West Africa since June 30th

The operation represents a dramatic escalation in the international response to the caliphate's rapid expansion across West Africa, which has seen jihadist forces seize control of multiple capital cities and threaten regional stability.

Ivory Coast Front: Korhogo Encircled

On the Ivory Coast front, coalition forces have made rapid advances, recapturing most of the northern areas that had fallen under caliphate control during the initial jihadist expansion. The key strategic objective remains the city of Korhogo, which is now completely encircled by coalition forces.

"Korhogo represents the last major stronghold of the caliphate in northern Ivory Coast," said French Brigadier General Pierre Lefebvre, commanding coalition forces in the region. "We have approximately 1,500 jihadist fighters trapped inside the city, and we are preparing for what we expect will be a significant battle."

The encirclement of Korhogo follows two weeks of methodical advances by coalition forces, who have used a combination of armored vehicles, special forces, and overwhelming air support to push back caliphate fighters.

"This is the most significant multinational military operation of the 21st century. We have over 63 nations contributing forces, equipment, and intelligence capabilities in a coordinated campaign to dismantle the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel."

Intelligence assessments suggest that the jihadist forces in Korhogo are well-entrenched and have had several weeks to prepare defensive positions, leading coalition planners to expect a potentially protracted siege.

Nigerian Front: Strategic Highway Cut

In Nigeria, coalition forces working alongside Nigerian military units, international volunteers, and local rebel groups have achieved a major tactical success by cutting off the A1 Highway, a crucial supply route that has left caliphate forces encircled in the city of Yelwa.

The operation, which involved coordinated air strikes and ground assaults, has isolated an estimated 800-1,200 jihadist fighters who are now trapped in Yelwa and dependent on air resupply or breakout attempts.

"The cutting of the A1 Highway represents a significant tactical achievement," said Nigerian Army Chief of Staff Lieutenant General Taoreed Lagbaja. "We are now preparing for the next phase of operations, which will focus on the eventual recapture of Sokoto, one of the major cities under caliphate control."

The coalition has deployed significant intelligence assets to monitor the encircled forces, with drone surveillance providing real-time updates on jihadist movements and preparations.

Togo and Benin: Rapid Advances

In Togo, coalition forces have recaptured the Kran National Park and are advancing along the N1 highway toward Dapaong, which serves as the main caliphate stronghold in the country. The operation has been supported by special forces from multiple nations, including German and Belgian units.

Beninese forces, supported by French and American advisors, have made significant gains along the RNIE2 Highway, recapturing nearly 3,500 square kilometers of territory in northern Benin. The advance has been methodical, with coalition forces securing each area before moving forward.

"The operations in Togo and Benin demonstrate the effectiveness of our multinational approach," said General Langley. "Each nation is contributing forces with specific capabilities, and we are seeing the results in terms of territorial gains and enemy casualties."

Senegal and Guinea: Encirclement Operations

In Senegal, coalition forces have cut off the N7 highway and begun the first phase of an offensive designed to gradually isolate Tambacounda, the main caliphate stronghold in the country. The operation has involved significant engineering work to establish forward operating bases and supply lines.

Guinea has seen some of the most intense fighting, with coalition forces gradually pushing back caliphate fighters and now encircling Siguiri, where large numbers of jihadist forces are believed to be trapped. The city has been under siege for several days, with coalition forces conducting precision strikes against defensive positions.

Mali and Niger: Tuareg Alliance

In Mali and Niger, where the governments have effectively collapsed, the coalition has adopted a different approach, providing support to the Azawad National Liberation Front (ANLF), a Tuareg rebel group that has been fighting the caliphate in northern Mali and Niger.

The ANLF, with coalition air support and special forces advisors, has successfully recaptured Arlit in Niger, a key mining town that had been under caliphate control. In Mali, progress has been slower due to the challenging desert terrain, but coalition-backed forces are approaching the RN18 highway.

"The Tuareg forces know this terrain better than anyone," said a senior French officer involved in coordinating with the ANLF. "With our air support and intelligence capabilities, they have been able to achieve significant successes."

Air Power Decisive

Across all fronts, coalition air power has played a decisive role in enabling ground advances. American F-35 fighters, French Rafales, British Typhoons, and German Tornados have conducted thousands of sorties, providing close air support, conducting reconnaissance, and striking caliphate positions.

"The air campaign has been relentless," said General Langley. "We have conducted over 15,000 sorties since the offensive began, and the caliphate has been unable to effectively counter our air superiority."

The air campaign has been supported by a vast array of intelligence assets, including American AWACS aircraft, British Sentinel surveillance planes, and numerous drone platforms that provide real-time intelligence to ground forces.

New Caliph's Strategy

Intelligence assessments indicate that the caliphate's new leader, who emerged after the June 11th airstrike that eliminated most of the senior leadership, has maintained the group's focus on conventional warfare despite the changing tactical situation.

"The new caliph appears to be committed to the same conventional warfare approach that has characterized the group's operations," said Dr. Katherine Zimmerman, a counterterrorism analyst at the American Enterprise Institute. "This has played into coalition strengths, as we have been able to use our advantages in air power and combined arms operations."

The caliphate has continued to attempt to hold territory rather than reverting to insurgent tactics, leading to a series of conventional battles that have generally favored the better-equipped and supported coalition forces.

Challenges Ahead

Despite the significant territorial gains, coalition officials acknowledge that major challenges remain. The caliphate still controls significant territory, particularly in urban areas where they have had time to prepare defensive positions.

"We are making progress, but we are not declaring victory," said General Langley. "The caliphate still has significant capabilities, and we expect that the fighting will become more difficult as we approach their main strongholds."

Coalition planners are particularly concerned about the potential for protracted urban warfare in cities like Korhogo, Tambacounda, and other major caliphate strongholds where jihadist forces have had time to prepare extensive defensive positions.

As the offensive continues into its fourth week, coalition forces are preparing for what many expect will be a long and difficult campaign to completely eliminate the caliphate's territorial control across West Africa.

Coalition Forces by Country

  • United States: 12,500 troops, 200 aircraft
  • France: 8,200 troops, 150 aircraft
  • United Kingdom: 4,800 troops, 120 aircraft
  • Germany: 3,500 troops, 80 aircraft
  • Italy: 2,900 troops, 60 aircraft
  • Spain: 2,200 troops, 50 aircraft
  • Belgium: 1,800 troops, 40 aircraft
  • Netherlands: 1,500 troops, 35 aircraft
  • Other NATO: 8,500 troops
  • Regional forces: 45,000 troops
