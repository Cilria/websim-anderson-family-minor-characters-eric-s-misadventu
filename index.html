WIFE MYSTERY SOLVED: Found his marriage record! Samuel P. Hartland married Sarah Elizabeth McCoy on October 15, 1855 in Berne, Indiana. Here's the fascinating part - Sarah came from a completely different religious background.

Sarah was raised in the Schwenkfelder faith, a tiny German Protestant sect that barely survived persecution in Europe. Her father, Johann "John" McCoy (originally Johann McCoy), was Irish Catholic who converted to Schwenkfelderism after marrying Sarah's German mother, Anna Maria Schlegel.

The marriage caused a HUGE scandal in both communities! The Schwenkfelders were pacifist mystics, while Samuel was a fire-and-brimstone Methodist. Sarah's family disowned her for "marrying beneath our spiritual dignity." She was 19, he was 26.

Found Sarah's handwritten diary from 1855-1857 in the Berne Historical Society archives. She wrote: "My Samuel speaks with such holy fervor that even the hardest hearts melt like wax before him. Mother says I have damned my soul, but I know God's truth when I hear it."

THE COMMUNITY'S PERSPECTIVE - WHAT THE RECORDS DON'T SHOW:

My family has lived in Berne since 1850, and let me tell you, Preaching Sam was both feared and pitied. The old-timers had mixed feelings - they knew he was dangerous, but also that he'd been through hell.

The "Miracle Healings": Despite all the madness, some people swore Samuel actually healed them. Old Mrs. Zimmerman claimed her rheumatism disappeared after Samuel prayed over her. The "Confession Booths": Samuel set up special tents where followers had to confess every sin they'd ever committed. He recorded everything in what he called "The Book of Life." The "Holy Matrimony" Scam: Samuel performed "divine marriages" where he'd pair up couples based on "visions from God." Sometimes these were happy, but often they were disasters. What Finally Happened: After the near-mass-suicide in 1891, the state threatened to bring in the National Guard. Samuel backed down, claiming "God changed His mind about the date." But the commune gradually fell apart. By 1900, most followers had drifted away, either dying off or moving away.
