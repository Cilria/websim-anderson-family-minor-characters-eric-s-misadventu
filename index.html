Robert Franklin Sikes (1904-1962) - From Mississippi cotton fields to Texas shipyards, died exactly one day after his 58th birthday
634 upvotes • posted by MississippiRoots Southern Genealogy

I've been researching my great-grandfather Robert Franklin Sikes and found a fascinating but tragic story. Born November 10, 1904 in Coldwater, Mississippi to Frank William Sikes and Emma Ann Headley. His mother died just two months after his birth - complications from childbirth. His father was apparently an illiterate alcoholic farmer who "never bothered raising his children."

Key details I've uncovered:

  • Mother died January 1905 when Robert was 2 months old
  • 1920: Living with older brother Jefferson Davis Sikes, both working as night watchmen
  • Moved to Quincy, FL and worked in textile mills
  • 1930: Married and moved to Port Arthur, TX to work in shipyards
  • All siblings eventually moved to Port Arthur too
  • Died November 11, 1962 of heart attack - exactly one day after his 58th birthday

Looking for any records about his stepmother, what happened to his father Frank, and why the whole family ended up in Port Arthur!

Found his birth and mother's death certificates! Emma Ann Headley Sikes died January 15, 1905 from "puerperal fever and exhaustion" - classic postpartum infection. She was only 28 years old.

The stepmother mystery SOLVED: Frank William Sikes married Mary Elizabeth "Molly" Thompson on March 3, 1906 - she was just 16 years old (Frank was 34). This was actually illegal under Mississippi law at the time, but rural areas often looked the other way.

Molly was essentially a child bride forced to raise her new husband's infant son. The 1910 census shows her as age 20 with 4-year-old Robert, but here's the tragic part - she died in 1918 during the Spanish Flu pandemic at age 28, the same age as Robert's biological mother.

Frank then married a THIRD time in 1919 to Dora Belle Jenkins, age 19 (Frank was now 47). Dora outlived Frank and actually raised Robert through his teenage years. Robert never referred to her as his mother though - always called her "Miss Dora."

Frank died in 1926 at age 62 from cirrhosis of the liver. His death certificate lists him as "widowed" but he was actually divorced from Dora - she left him in 1924 after he tried to force her oldest daughter (his stepdaughter) into marriage at age 15. The man was absolutely vile.

Local newspaper accounts describe him as "the terror of Coldwater township" and "a disgrace to the community." He was buried in Potter's Field because no church would allow his burial in consecrated ground.

JeffersonDavisResearch • 298 points • 6 hours ago

Found Jefferson Davis Sikes' employment records! He and Robert worked as night watchmen at the Coldwater Cotton Mill starting in 1919. They were paid $1.25 per night to guard the machinery from theft.

Here's why they moved to Quincy, Florida: In 1921, the mill burned down under suspicious circumstances. Jefferson had taken out a $5,000 insurance policy on the mill just 3 months earlier through his brother-in-law who was an insurance agent. The insurance company refused to pay, claiming arson.

The brothers fled to Florida to avoid investigation. They found work at the Quincy Cotton Mill - Jefferson as a loom fixer, Robert as a spinner. Robert actually hated textile work and only lasted 18 months before moving to Texas.

Jefferson stayed in Quincy and eventually owned three textile mills himself by 1940. He died there in 1972, worth over $2 million. He never married but left his entire fortune to his sister's children.

Family rumor was that Jefferson really did burn down the Coldwater mill, but it was never proven. The insurance company eventually settled for $2,000 just to make it go away.

PortArthurShipyards • 245 points • 5 hours ago

Found Robert's employment records at the Port Arthur shipyard! He started as a riveter's helper on March 15, 1930, making $0.65 per hour. The Texas Company (later Texaco) was building oil tankers and needed thousands of workers.

Here's the Port Arthur connection: In 1929, Jefferson Davis Sikes invested in a textile mill in Port Arthur. He sent word to his siblings that Texas was booming and jobs were plentiful. One by one, they all migrated there:

  • Robert: March 1930 (shipyard worker)
  • Sister Mary: June 1931 (married a refinery worker)
  • Brother William: August 1932 (oil field worker)
  • Sister Nancy: October 1933 (married a longshoreman)

Robert worked at the Pennsylvania Shipyards (later Todd Shipyards) for 32 years. He started as a riveter's helper, became a riveter in 1935, then a structural steel worker in 1940. During WWII, he worked 7 days a week building Liberty ships.

His personnel file shows he was an excellent worker - only missed 12 days in 32 years. He retired in 1962 due to heart problems and died exactly 6 months later. His funeral was attended by over 200 shipyard workers - he was apparently very well-liked.

SikesFamilyResearch • 187 points • 4 hours ago

Found Robert's marriage certificate! He married Lillian Mae Thompson (no relation to his stepmother) on June 14, 1930 in Port Arthur. Lillian was from Louisiana and had moved to Texas for work during the Depression.

They had 4 children: Robert Franklin Jr (1931), Lillian Mae (1933), Betty Ann (1935), and James William (1938). All were born in Port Arthur and lived to adulthood.

Lillian worked as a domestic servant for wealthy oil families while Robert built ships. They bought a small house at 1423 Proctor Street in 1938 for $1,200 - Robert built most of it himself using scrap lumber from the shipyard.

Robert Jr followed his father into the shipyards, working there from 1950-1985. The family became part of Port Arthur's working-class aristocracy - three generations of Sikes men built ships that sailed around the world.

Lillian died in 1988, having never remarried. She always said Robert was the only man who ever treated her with respect, unlike her own abusive father. The family still owns the house on Proctor Street - now worth over $150,000.

DeathCertificates • 134 points • 3 hours ago

Found his death certificate details! Robert died at 7:42 AM on November 11, 1962 at St. Mary's Hospital in Port Arthur. The cause was "acute myocardial infarction due to coronary thrombosis" - a massive heart attack.

He had been having chest pains for weeks but refused to see a doctor because he couldn't afford to miss work. On November 10th, his 58th birthday, he worked a full shift at the shipyard despite feeling unwell.

That evening, he celebrated his birthday with his family - had cake, opened presents, went to bed happy. At 6:30 AM the next morning, he woke up gasping for air. He was dead within an hour.

His funeral was held November 14th at St. Paul's Methodist Church. Over 300 people attended - the church had to set up extra chairs outside. He was buried in Greenlawn Memorial Park, Section C, Plot 47.

The cemetery records show his grave is marked with a simple stone that reads: "Robert Franklin Sikes, Nov 10 1904 - Nov 11 1962, Loving Father and Husband." His family couldn't afford more elaborate memorial, but they maintain the grave to this day.
