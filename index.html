John Andrew Phillips

John Andrew Phillips (January 3, 1895 – October 21, 1972) was an American mass murderer who perpetrated the 1956 Phillips Massacre, the deadliest mass shooting in Alabama history. On October 20, 1956, over a six-hour period, Phillips killed 16 people and injured 31 others across multiple locations in Alabama. The attacks were motivated by a civil lawsuit ruling against him for $2,847 in damaged cargo.

Early life

John Andrew Phillips was born January 3, 1895, in Mobile, Alabama, to working-class parents. He served in the U.S. Army during World War I and was honorably discharged in 1919. Phillips worked as a long-haul truck driver for 37 years, primarily transporting produce between Alabama and Georgia.

Civil lawsuit

On October 19, 1956, a Mobile County judge ruled against Phillips in a civil lawsuit brought by the Southern Produce Company. The company alleged Phillips was responsible for $2,847 worth of spoiled tomatoes due to a refrigeration failure in his truck. Phillips had disputed the claim, arguing the refrigeration unit was faulty when he picked up the cargo.

Massacre

On October 20, 1956, beginning at approximately 6:00 AM, Phillips launched a coordinated attack across multiple locations:

Timeline

  • 6:00 AM: Broke into Alabama National Guard armory in Mobile, stealing an MG-42 machine gun and 2,400 rounds of ammunition
  • 6:47 AM: First attack at Rexall Drug Store on 3rd Street - 7 killed, 12 wounded
  • 7:15 AM: Second attack at Western Auto hardware store - 4 killed
  • 7:33 AM: Bus station bombing #1 - 8 killed instantly
  • 7:45 AM: Bus station bombing #2 - 1 responding officer killed
  • 8:20 AM: Returned home, murdered his wife, three children, and two cousins
  • 8:45 AM: Bombed own house with dynamite
  • 9:15 AM - 11:30 AM: Conducted drive-by shootings across three counties
  • 12:15 PM: Apprehended at police roadblock

Aftermath

Police discovered a 47-page journal in Phillips' truck, detailing months of planning. The journal included timetables, escape routes, and target selection criteria. Phillips had been stockpiling weapons and ammunition since the lawsuit began.

Trial and execution

Phillips was charged with 16 counts of first-degree murder. He dismissed his legal counsel two weeks before execution and represented himself at the clemency hearing, stating: "I've been dead since October 20th, 1956. You're just making it official."

He was executed by electric chair at Kilby Correctional Facility on October 21, 1972. His last words were reported as "Should've done it sooner."

Legacy

The Phillips Massacre remains the deadliest mass shooting in Alabama history. The case prompted significant changes in National Guard armory security protocols across the United States.
