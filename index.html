Islamist group threatens to bomb Kaaba and unleash nuclear war in chilling video

"Army of the Last Hour" releases apocalyptic manifesto as they seize another Moroccan city

By Clarissa Ward, Nima Elbagir and Tim Lister | CNN | Updated 12:47 PM ET, Wed March 14, 2029

London (CNN) — The Islamist militant group that seized control of the historic city of Fes has released a chilling video manifesto outlining plans to bomb Islam's holiest site, trigger regional war, and ultimately destroy the world with nuclear weapons and biological agents.

The video, released early Wednesday morning, marks a terrifying escalation in the crisis that has gripped Morocco since the "Army of the Last Hour" took control of Fes and eastern Morocco three days ago. The group has now also seized the coastal city of Nador after a brief battle with security forces.

Warning: This article contains disturbing content and apocalyptic threats that may be upsetting to readers.

In the 47-minute video, the group's leader Abu Umayr al-Mahdi declares: "Judgment Day has arrived. The end is coming, the final battle has begun, the forces of Satan will be defeated, and all of humanity will suffer in hellfire for their disobedience to Allah."

"The final battle against the forces of evil must begin, and in this case, if we do not win, the world will burn in the fires of hell and all forms of life will be destroyed."

The video, which CNN has verified but chosen not to broadcast in full due to its graphic content, outlines a four-stage apocalyptic plan that has horrified intelligence officials and religious leaders worldwide.

The group's apocalyptic plan:

Stage 1: Bomb the Kaaba in Mecca as punishment for Muslims' "disobedience"
Stage 2: Unleash chaos in the Middle East to trigger all-out war between states
Stage 3: Deploy dirty bombs against "governments of Satan" worldwide
Stage 4: Launch nuclear weapons and biological agents to destroy all life

The Kaaba, the cube-shaped building at the center of Islam's most sacred mosque in Mecca, is the holiest site in Islam. The threat to destroy it has sent shockwaves through the Muslim world, with Saudi Arabia immediately calling for an emergency summit of Islamic nations.

"This represents an unprecedented level of extremist ideology," said Dr. Karen Armstrong, a leading expert on comparative religion. "Even groups like ISIS and al Qaeda have never threatened to destroy the Kaaba. This goes beyond anything we've seen before."

The video shows al-Mahdi surrounded by armed followers in what appears to be a fortified position within Fes' ancient medina. He claims the group has already acquired "the means of our final victory" and warns that "the countdown has begun."

Intelligence sources tell CNN the group appears to have sophisticated knowledge of weapons systems and has likely received external support. The threat to use dirty bombs — conventional explosives combined with radioactive material — has prompted security alerts across Europe and the United States.

"We're treating this as a credible threat," said a senior U.S. intelligence official who spoke on condition of anonymity. "The group has demonstrated tactical capability, and their apocalyptic ideology means traditional deterrence may not apply."

The seizure of Nador, a strategic port city on the Mediterranean coast, gives the group control over a major shipping route and brings them closer to Europe. Spanish authorities have deployed additional forces to the North African enclaves of Ceuta and Melilla, while France has raised its terrorism alert to the highest level.

Inside Morocco, panic is spreading. Thousands continue to flee the affected areas, while the government appears paralyzed by the speed of the militants' advance. The group's control of telecommunications infrastructure in captured areas has made it difficult to assess the true scope of the humanitarian crisis.

"This is no longer just a Moroccan problem," said Dr. H.A. Hellyer, a senior fellow at the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace. "The group's apocalyptic ideology and global ambitions make this an international security crisis of the highest order."

The video has prompted an extraordinary response from religious leaders across faiths. The Grand Imam of Al-Azhar in Cairo called the group "the enemies of God," while Pope Francis prayed for "deliverance from this madness." Jewish, Christian, and Muslim leaders have issued a joint statement condemning the "perversion of religious belief."

However, there are concerns that the group's extreme ideology might attract followers among those who believe the world is approaching its end. Intelligence agencies report increased chatter on extremist forums, with some fringe groups expressing support for the apocalyptic vision.

The crisis has also raised difficult questions about how to respond to an enemy that actively seeks death and destruction. Traditional counterterrorism strategies assume opponents want to survive and achieve political goals, but the Army of the Last Hour appears to seek only apocalypse.

"We're dealing with a death cult that wants to end the world," said a European intelligence official. "How do you deter someone who believes dying is victory and killing is salvation?"

As the sun sets on another day of crisis, the ancient city of Fes remains under militant control, while the world grapples with an enemy that seems to have emerged from humanity's darkest nightmares. The group's video ends with al-Mahdi's chilling promise: "Soon you will see the black banners of the end times flying over every capital, and then you will know that the hour is near."

For a world already grappling with multiple crises, the emergence of a group that actively seeks to end human civilization represents a new and terrifying threshold. The coming days will test the international community's ability to confront an enemy that appears to have no interest in negotiation, compromise, or even survival.
