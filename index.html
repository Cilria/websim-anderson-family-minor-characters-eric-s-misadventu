Arab State leader orders invasion of Yemen as 400,000 troops cross border

Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi declares military operations against Yemen will begin at 14:00 GMT+3 as regional conflict erupts

By Nic Robertson, CNN International Diplomatic Editor
March 1, 2030 - Updated 2:45 AM GMT+3

BREAKING Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi, the leader of the self-declared Arab State, has issued formal orders to his generals for military operations against Yemen to begin at 14:00 GMT+3, with approximately 400,000 troops already crossing the border and rapidly capturing border towns, according to multiple sources on the ground.

The invasion order, delivered to General Khalid Mohammed and General Turki bin Abdullah, commanders of the First and Second Regiments, marks a dramatic escalation that has transformed what began as domestic ethnic cleansing into a full-scale regional military conflict, prompting urgent international response.

"Military operations against Yemen will begin at 14:00 GMT+3, and orders will be issued to General Khalid Mohammed and General Turki bin Abdullah, commanders of the First and Second Regiments. Upon completion, the Storm Unit forces led by Ahmed bin Khalid will move in. Glory to the Arab State, victory to Arab fascism, the Arab race, and the great national awakening."

Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi, leader of the Arab State

According to military sources who spoke to CNN on condition of anonymity, the Saudi National Guard has been reformed into an elite "Storm Front" regiment, with troops beginning their march toward Sana'a and reaching the first major urban centers along the primary invasion route.

The scale of the military operation—estimated at 400,000 troops—represents one of the largest military mobilizations in modern Middle Eastern history, surpassing even the 1990-1991 Gulf War in terms of ground forces deployed.

"This is not simply a border incursion or limited military action," said Dr. Lina Khatib, director of the Middle East and North Africa programme at Chatham House. "We're witnessing what appears to be a full-scale invasion with the objective of regime change or complete territorial conquest."

Key developments from al-Harbi's invasion order:
• Military operations against Yemen to begin at 14:00 GMT+3
• Approximately 400,000 troops crossing the border
• Saudi National Guard reformed into elite "Storm Front" regiment
• Rapid capture of border towns and villages reported
• Troops advancing toward major urban centers en route to Sana'a

According to eyewitness accounts from border regions, Yemeni forces have offered limited resistance, with most defensive positions overwhelmed by the sheer scale of the advancing forces. Local residents report columns of armored vehicles and troop carriers stretching for kilometers.

The international community has responded with alarm to these developments. The United Nations Security Council is holding an emergency session Saturday morning, while the United States State Department has called for "immediate de-escalation" and warned of "serious consequences for regional stability."

"We are deeply concerned by the scale of military operations and the potential for significant civilian casualties," said UN Secretary-General in a statement. "All parties must respect international humanitarian law and protect civilian infrastructure."

Iran, which has historically supported various factions in Yemen, has put its military forces on heightened alert along the Persian Gulf. Iranian state media reported that Supreme Leader Ayatollah Khamenei convened an emergency meeting of the Supreme National Security Council within hours of the invasion announcement.

Turkey, which has maintained military presence in Qatar and other regional locations, has called for an emergency meeting of the Organization of Islamic Cooperation. Turkish President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan stated that "the region cannot afford another devastating conflict" and called for immediate diplomatic intervention.

The economic implications of the invasion have already begun to ripple through global markets. Oil prices surged by 12% in trading following the announcement, while major international companies with operations in the region began reviewing their exposure to what analysts are calling an increasingly unstable situation.

Perhaps most significantly, al-Harbi's explicit rejection of international law and humanitarian norms marks a fundamental departure from even the most authoritarian regional governments, which have typically maintained at least nominal commitment to international institutions.

"This represents a qualitative leap in the region's political dynamics," said Dr. Khatib. "We're witnessing the emergence of an explicitly expansionist movement that rejects the international order and appears committed to territorial conquest."

The Arab State leader appeared to anticipate international criticism, declaring in his orders: "Glory to the Arab State, victory to Arab fascism, the Arab race, and the great national awakening."

Human rights organizations have expressed particular alarm about the potential for civilian casualties, given Yemen's ongoing humanitarian crisis and the presence of approximately 20 million civilians in areas potentially affected by military operations.

As al-Harbi's forces continue advancing toward Sana'a, many analysts are drawing comparisons to previous regional conflicts, though few have reached the scale of what appears to be unfolding across Yemen's borders.

The crowd's response—nearly one million supporters attending his recent speeches—suggested that al-Harbi's radical vision has found significant support among segments of the Saudi population, raising urgent questions about regional stability and the potential for wider conflict in the coming days.
