Uzbekistan president flees as army defects; 300,000 protesters seize Tashkent

Shavkat Mirziyoyev leaves country as military refuses orders and joins demonstrators demanding regime change

By Najmeh Bozorgmeh in Istanbul and Ben Hall in London September 2, 2027

Tashkent — Uzbekistan's president Shavkat Mirziyoyev has fled the country after the army refused to fire on protesters and instead marched on the presidential palace, bringing an abrupt end to his 12-year rule and ushering in what opposition groups are calling a new era for the Central Asian nation.

An estimated 300,000 demonstrators flooded the streets of Tashkent on Thursday, the largest protest in the former Soviet republic's history, demanding the resignation of Mr Mirziyoyev following days of violent crackdowns that left hundreds dead across the country.

Timeline of regime collapse

7:00 PM Uzbek army refuses orders, marches to presidential palace
7:15 PM Flight tracking shows presidential plane leaving Tashkent
8:00 PM National Provisional Government declares takeover
8:30 PM Celebrations erupt across major cities

At approximately 7:00 PM local time, soldiers from the Uzbek armed forces, who had been deployed to suppress the protests, instead laid down their weapons and joined the demonstrators. Witnesses reported scenes of jubilation as military vehicles were cheered by crowds who had spent days under threat of violence.

Flight tracking data showed a government aircraft, believed to be carrying Mr Mirziyoyev and senior officials, departing Tashkent airport shortly after 7:15 PM. The aircraft's destination was not immediately clear, though speculation centred on Russia or one of the neighbouring Central Asian states.

"The army has chosen the people over the dictator," said one protester in Tashkent's central square. "We are finally free."

By 8:00 PM, opposition leaders had announced the formation of a National Provisional Government, vowing to dismantle what they described as the "criminal regime" of Mr Mirziyoyev and implement sweeping democratic reforms.

"The era of corruption, repression and economic mismanagement is over," said a spokesperson for the provisional government in a televised address. "We will establish a democratic Uzbekistan that respects human rights and the rule of law."

Key figures in transition

  • Shavkat Mirziyoyev: President since 2016, fled country Thursday evening
  • National Provisional Government: Led by opposition coalition
  • Army leadership: Refused orders to suppress protests
  • International response: Monitoring situation closely

The speed of the regime's collapse stunned observers, coming just 24 hours after security forces had opened fire on protesters in several cities. The army's defection appears to have been triggered by orders to use live ammunition against unarmed civilians, with several military commanders reportedly refusing to carry out what they termed "illegal orders."

As news of the president's departure spread, celebrations broke out across Uzbekistan. In Tashkent, protesters who had spent days demanding change now found themselves in control of the capital. Similar scenes were reported in Samarkand, Bukhara and other major cities.

The provisional government has promised immediate reforms including the release of political prisoners, the establishment of an independent judiciary, and preparations for democratic elections within six months. They have also pledged to investigate the violence of recent days and hold those responsible to account.

The international community has reacted cautiously to the developments, with Western governments calling for calm and a peaceful transition while regional powers expressed concern about stability in Central Asia. The United States embassy in Tashkent issued a statement urging all parties to respect democratic principles.

China, which has significant economic interests in Uzbekistan through its Belt and Road Initiative, called for "stability and continuity" while Russia, traditionally influential in the region, said it was monitoring the situation closely.

The collapse of Mr Mirziyoyev's government represents the most significant political upheaval in Central Asia since the fall of the Soviet Union. Uzbekistan, with its 36 million population and strategic location, is crucial to regional stability and energy security.

Opposition groups have been quick to reassure foreign investors and trading partners that contracts and agreements will be honoured, seeking to prevent economic chaos during the transition. The Uzbek currency, the som, which had plummeted in recent months, showed signs of stabilising on Friday.

However, significant challenges lie ahead for the provisional government. They must manage a transition to democracy in a country with no recent experience of pluralistic politics, while addressing the economic grievances that sparked the protests and maintaining stability in a volatile region.

"This is just the beginning," said one opposition leader. "We have removed a dictator, but building a democracy will require patience, wisdom and the support of the international community."

As Tashkent returned to calm on Friday morning, with residents cleaning up debris from days of protests, the mood was one of cautious optimism mixed with uncertainty about what comes next. For the first time in decades, Uzbeks are contemplating a future without the authoritarian rule that has defined their recent history.

Analysis

The rapid collapse of Mirziyoyev's regime follows a pattern seen in other post-Soviet states where security forces ultimately refuse to fire on their own people. The army's defection was the decisive moment.
