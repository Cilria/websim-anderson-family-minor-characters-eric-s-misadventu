Katrakis declares Greek National Front sole legal party, assumes title of "Leader"

By Nic Robertson and Elinda Labropoulou February 27, 2027 4:47 PM EET Athens, Greece
LATEST: European Union announces immediate sanctions, NATO suspends Greece's membership pending review
Nikolaos Katrakis addresses parliament after passing bills establishing one-party rule and assuming the title of "Leader" (CNN)

Greek Prime Minister Nikolaos Katrakis completed his transformation from elected leader to absolute ruler Thursday, signing two bills that abolished all political parties except his Greek National Front and formally renamed his position from "Prime Minister" to simply "Leader."

The move marks the culmination of a three-week power grab that began with emergency decrees and ended with what historians are calling the most complete democratic collapse in Europe since the 1930s.

Greece's New Reality

One-Party State

Greek National Front becomes the only legal political party. All other parties dissolved effective immediately. Membership in any opposition organization punishable by up to 10 years imprisonment.

Leadership Cult

Position renamed from "Prime Minister" to "Leader" - same title used by Franco, Mussolini, and Hitler. Katrakis now holds absolute executive, legislative, and judicial authority.

Complete Control

Combined with the Enabling Bill passed earlier this month, Katrakis now rules by decree, controls all security forces, has suspended constitutional protections, and can ignore court rulings.

"Today marks the rebirth of a strong, unified Greece," Katrakis declared from the parliament podium, his voice echoing through a chamber half-empty after opposition MPs either fled or were arrested. "The era of weak coalition governments and foreign interference is over. I am your Leader, and the Greek National Front is your only legitimate voice."

The transformation was swift and methodical. Within hours of the bills' passage, state television began referring to Katrakis exclusively as "the Leader," while his image appeared on every channel alongside the Greek National Front flag.

"This is it - he's gone mask off. Katrakis has completed his metamorphosis from populist politician to full dictator in less than a month."

The single-party decree, titled the "National Unity and Security Act," formally dissolves all existing political parties and declares the Greek National Front as the "sole legitimate representative of the Greek people's will." The law criminalizes membership in any other political organization, with penalties ranging from five to fifteen years imprisonment.

Simultaneously, the "Executive Restructuring Act" eliminates the traditional title of "Prime Minister" - a position dating back to 1833 - replacing it with the more ambiguous title of "Leader." The law's preamble explicitly states this change reflects "the new reality of direct, personal leadership over all aspects of governance."

The final pieces fall into place

These latest moves complete Katrakis's consolidation of power, building upon the foundation laid by the Enabling Bill earlier this month. That legislation already granted him authority to pass laws without parliament, suspend protests, control all police forces, and ignore court rulings.

"Katrakis has achieved in three weeks what took Hitler three years," said Professor Ruth Ben-Ghiat, an expert on authoritarianism at New York University. "The speed is unprecedented in modern European history. He's essentially created a textbook totalitarian state in under a month."

The transformation was immediately visible. Within minutes of signing the bills, Katrakis's official Twitter account changed its bio from "Prime Minister of Greece" to "Leader of the Greek people." State television began playing footage of Katrakis interspersed with images of ancient Greek warriors, while the national anthem was replaced with the Greek National Front's party song during official broadcasts.

International isolation

The international response was swift and unified. European Commission President immediately announced that Greece would be suspended from the EU's decision-making processes, while NATO Secretary General confirmed Greece's membership in the alliance was "under urgent review."

"Greece has ceased to be a democracy," the Commission President said in Brussels. "We cannot conduct business as usual with a regime that has abolished political pluralism and basic human rights."

The United States announced it was "reevaluating all aspects of our bilateral relationship," while the State Department issued a travel advisory warning Americans that "Greece's legal framework no longer provides basic protections for foreign nationals."

Financial markets reacted with panic. The Athens Stock Exchange suspended trading after stocks fell 23% in the first hour, while the euro dropped to its lowest level against the dollar since 2022. Greek bond yields spiked to levels not seen since the 2015 debt crisis, effectively shutting Greece out of international capital markets.

The opposition crushed

Opposition leaders who haven't already fled are either in hiding or under arrest. Former Prime Minister Alexis Tsipnas reportedly escaped to Turkey via speedboat, while communist party leader Dimitris Koutsoumbas was detained at Athens International Airport attempting to board a flight to Brussels.

"They've won," said one opposition MP who requested anonymity, speaking from an undisclosed location. "There's no parliament, no courts, no press, no opposition. It's over."

The few remaining independent journalists have either gone underground or fled. State television now broadcasts continuous footage of Katrakis's speeches, while radio stations have been instructed to play only "patriotic music" and news bulletins from the official state news agency.

Universities have been ordered to suspend classes indefinitely, while school curricula are being rewritten to emphasize "national unity and the Leader's vision for Greece's future."

A nation transformed

The speed of Greece's transformation has left even seasoned observers stunned. In less than seven weeks, the country has gone from a functioning democracy to a one-party state with absolute rule concentrated in the hands of one man.

"Katrakis hasn't just destroyed Greek democracy - he's created a template for how quickly democratic institutions can be dismantled when you have a determined leader, a parliamentary majority, and a manufactured crisis," said Professor Cas Mudde, an expert on European politics at the University of Georgia.

The streets of Athens were eerily quiet Thursday night. Protesters who might have demonstrated were either arrested under the protest ban or too frightened to gather. The few pedestrians hurried home before the 8 PM curfew, while police vehicles patrolled nearly empty streets.

"This is what the end of democracy looks like," said an elderly Athenian, speaking from her balcony as sirens wailed in the distance. "Not with a bang, but with a series of bills passed in parliament while the world watches in horror."

As night fell, Katrakis addressed the nation from the steps of the parliament building, flanked by party militias in the Greek National Front's signature black uniforms.

"The old Greece is dead," he declared. "Tomorrow, a new Greece begins - united under one party, one people, one Leader."

The crowd of supporters - many reportedly transported in by party buses - chanted "Leader! Leader!" as fireworks exploded overhead, marking what historians may record as the day European democracy suffered its most significant defeat since the 1940s.
