James William Hatchell (1909-1951): Tobacco farmer from Muck Cross, VA - suicide in 1951

Virginia Tobacco Farming Depression Era Suicide Posted by u/VirginiaTobaccoRoots • 6 hours ago

I've been researching my great-grandfather James William Hatchell (1909-1951) and found some disturbing details. He was a tobacco farmer in Muck Cross, Mecklenburg County, Virginia his whole life. Here's what I have:

Born: April 6, 1909 in Muck Cross, Mecklenburg County, VA
Died: July 10, 1951 (age 42) - suicide by gunshot
Parents: John Hatchell (1879-1949) and Mary Elizabeth Morgan (1884-1965)

He was third-oldest of 9 kids and married Elizabeth Jane Bower. They had 5 children - 4 daughters and 1 son. He worked tobacco all his life according to census records. The death certificate says he shot himself at 2:45 AM because he was "depressed over thinking he had cancer." His obituary was just one paragraph - no services mentioned, just that he "died near his home" and was survived by kin.

This was 1951 rural Virginia - mental health was taboo and tobacco farming was dying. I can't find anything about Elizabeth after his death or what happened to the kids. Did she remarry? Did the kids stay in Muck Cross? Any advice on finding more about this family tragedy would help.

🏅 u/TobaccoFarmingHistorian • 4 hours ago
Tobacco farming context for 1951: This was the absolute worst time to be a Virginia tobacco farmer. The 1950 crop had disastrous prices due to overproduction. Many farmers were in debt to the federal government for crop loans they couldn't repay. The Mecklenburg County Extension Office records show 43% of tobacco farmers lost their farms 1950-1952. Combine that with health fears (rural areas had no real cancer treatment) and you can see why suicide rates among farmers spiked.

u/MentalHealthHistorian • 3 hours ago
The "thinking he had cancer" detail is heartbreaking. In rural 1951 Virginia, "cancer" was often a catch-all diagnosis for any unexplained illness. Many farmers feared cancer because they'd seen friends waste away from what they thought was cancer but was actually undiagnosed tuberculosis, heart disease, or just exhaustion. The stigma was so severe that some families claimed suicide victims had "heart trouble" instead.
